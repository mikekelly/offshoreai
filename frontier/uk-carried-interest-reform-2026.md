# UK Carried Interest Reform — New Regime In Force 6 April 2026

## What this is

A fundamental rewrite of UK carried-interest taxation,
moving from a CGT-based regime to an **income-tax-based
regime with a partial exclusion**. Announced at the
Autumn Budget 2024, draft legislation July 2025,
Finance Bill 2025-26 published November 2025, **in force
6 April 2026** — one month before this file's `as_of`
date.

This is the **single biggest live PE-tax topic** for any
adviser working with UK-resident PE professionals. Crown
Dependencies practitioners are seeing daily questions
about restructuring positions, vehicle choice, and
inter-jurisdictional planning in response.

## Where it stands (as of 2026-05-18)

**In force from 6 April 2026.** All carried interest
arising on or after that date is taxed under the new
regime. Carried interest arising before that date
continues under the prior regime.

### Pre-6 April 2025 (historical)

CGT-based regime; effective rate around **28%** for
additional-rate payers.

### 6 April 2025 – 5 April 2026 (transition)

CGT increased to a **flat 32%**.

### From 6 April 2026 (current)

- Carried interest treated as **income** for income-tax
  purposes;
- Subject to **income tax + Class 4 NICs**;
- For **qualifying carried interest**, a **72.5% multiplier**
  applies (only 72.5% of the carry is brought into
  charge);
- Effective rate for an additional-rate (45%) taxpayer:
  approximately **34.1%** on qualifying carry;
- **Non-qualifying** carry: full income-tax + NICs,
  effective rates up to **47%**.

The 72.5% multiplier was a meaningful concession from
the original Autumn Budget 2024 design (which had
contemplated a less generous discount). Industry
representations through 2025 secured the current
parameters.

## What the qualifying / non-qualifying split means

The regime distinguishes:

- **Qualifying** carry — meets defined conditions (the
  partner is performing genuine investment-management
  activity, the fund meets specified characteristics,
  etc.);
- **Non-qualifying** carry — falls outside.

The qualifying definition has been **tightened** vs
prior drafts — Kirkland & Ellis (Dec 2025) flagged
specific narrowing. The government dropped additional
**access conditions** (such as a minimum-investment-by-
partner requirement) in the final legislation,
recognising the need to balance fairness with
international competitiveness.

For most established UK-resident PE partners working at
mature sponsors, **qualifying** status is the default
outcome. For new entrants, secondary-market participants,
and certain non-traditional structures, the analysis
needs care.

## What practitioners are doing about it

### Pre-6 April 2026 crystallisation

Through Q1 2026, many partners **accelerated carry
realisations** to land under the 32% CGT regime rather
than the new income regime. This involved:

- **Distributions** of accumulated carry by 5 April 2026;
- **Disposals** of carry interests where commercially
  feasible;
- **Restructurings** to lock in the pre-6 April basis.

### Post-6 April 2026 structuring

Active areas of advice:

- **Non-UK residence** planning for UK partners
  considering relocating to mitigate the new charge —
  but **substantial substance** required and the
  Statutory Residence Test bites quickly;
- **Crown Dependencies** as a residence destination for
  partners — Jersey HVR / Guernsey Open Market /
  Isle of Man residency programs are being marketed
  more actively for PE partners;
- **Trust** structures to defer or restructure carry
  receipts;
- **Carry-as-co-invest** distinction is now more
  acutely material — co-invest economics remain CGT-
  treated;
- **Family-office structures** for spinouts and
  generational transfer.

### Sponsor-side response

Sponsors are:

- **Documenting** carry arrangements more carefully to
  preserve qualifying status;
- **Reviewing** GP / LP economics for tax-efficient
  allocations to non-UK partners;
- **Adjusting** carry vesting and forfeiture mechanics;
- **Communicating** to LPs that gross-of-tax economics
  may need re-pricing.

## Crown Dependencies angle

Why this matters specifically for Jersey / Guernsey /
Isle of Man PE practitioners:

### Residency planning for UK partners

- **Jersey HVR (2(1)(e))** — bespoke high-value-residency
  route; tax-rate cap; specific eligibility
- **Guernsey Open Market** — house-purchase-based
  residency; standard 20% tax;
- **IoM tax cap** — annual cap on individual income tax;

For a UK partner facing a 34-47% UK income-tax charge on
carry, moving Crown Dependencies tax residence can be
materially attractive. But:

- **UK Statutory Residence Test** has stringent
  conditions for leaving;
- **Tail liability** on carry that economically accrued
  while UK-resident — even if realised after departure;
- **Substance** in the destination is essential;
- **Specific** anti-avoidance provisions in the new
  legislation around residence changes.

### GP-entity location

Crown Dependencies-domiciled GPs (Jersey companies,
Guernsey companies acting as GPs of Jersey / Cayman ELP
funds) may become **more attractive** as the carry-
allocation entity for non-UK partners — though the
**UK look-through / situs** rules continue to apply
where UK-resident partners receive the economic benefit.

### Fund-vehicle implications

- **Jersey JPF** and **Guernsey PIF** structures
  unchanged at the fund level;
- **Cayman ELP** unchanged;
- **Carry allocation** at the GP entity is the focal
  point — partnership profit allocation and carry
  vesting are where the new rules bite.

### Investor relations

LPs are asking sponsors:

- "How does your post-6 April 2026 cost base compare to
  pre-reform?"
- "Have you re-priced economics in response?"
- "What's your retention plan for key UK-based
  partners?"

## What to watch for next

- **HMRC guidance** continuing to issue (manual updates
  through 2026);
- **Edge-case rulings** for non-traditional structures
  (secondaries managers, fund-of-funds, infrastructure);
- **Tribunal cases** as positions are tested — first
  appeals likely 2027-2028;
- **OECD / EU peer reform** — other jurisdictions may
  follow;
- **Crown Dependencies competitive response** — formal
  marketing of HVR / residency programs to PE partners.

## Pitfalls

- **Treating** the 72.5% multiplier as a fixed cliff —
  the qualifying / non-qualifying boundary is fact-
  sensitive;
- **Assuming** UK departure shields against UK carry
  charges — the tail liability is real;
- **Misclassifying** co-invest as carry (or vice versa)
  — taxation is now materially different;
- **Sponsor documentation** that doesn't clearly
  establish qualifying status for each partner;
- **Cross-border** with French / German / Italian
  partners — each jurisdiction's reform pipeline
  diverges.
