## How to read each slide

- **Headline** — the line that does 80% of the work; the only thing many will read.
- **Body** — the spare on-slide copy (keep it short; the presenter says the rest).
- 🖼 **Visual** — a placeholder describing the imagery to design.
- ✍️ **Lever** — which copywriting technique the slide leans on, so you can tune it.

The deck runs as one story: **Attention** (slides 1–2) → **Interest** (3–5) →
**Desire** (6–11) → **Action** (12–15). Each slide should leave a small open loop the
next one closes.

## Slide 1 — Cover

**Headline:** Six of every seven pounds in your business is human time.

**Body:** OffshoreAI — managed compliance loops for Jersey trust companies.
*(logo · presenter · date)*

🖼 **Visual:** A quiet, high-end Jersey trust office after hours — one desk lamp
still on, a single analyst dwarfed by banker's boxes and paper CDD files. Restrained,
expensive, a little lonely. (Not a glossy "AI" render — credibility starts here.)

✍️ **Lever:** Tribe-call + open loop. It names *their* economics without selling
anything yet (Trojan horse — product stays hidden). The unanswered question —
*so what?* — pulls them to slide 2.

---

## Slide 2 — The punch

**Headline:** The most expensive work on the island is still done by hand.

**Body:** £1 of software, £6 of people. Everyone's racing to capture the £7 with AI.
Almost no one has said *how*.

🖼 **Visual:** The "$1 : $6" made physical — a single coin beside a thick stack of
six payslips (or six suited figures). Make the ratio *felt*, not stated.

✍️ **Lever:** Make-numbers-physical + curiosity gap. "No one has said how" is the
open loop the whole deck pays off. (Punchier B2C alt: *"Your blender makes great
slop." — too cute for this room; hold it.*)

---

## Slide 3 — The maze (get them nodding)

**Headline:** You already know where the hours go.

**Body:** One person pulls the documents. Another unpicks the ownership. Another
screens. Another drafts the file. Another reviews it. Another chases the client for
the one missing page. By the time it ships, the risk picture has already moved.

🖼 **Visual:** A single CDD onboarding rendered as a tangled relay — batons dropped
between six pairs of hands, a clock ticking in the corner.

✍️ **Lever:** Rule of consistency — open with something they'll nod *yes* to before
any pitch. Rhythm: short, repeated "Another…" beats, then one long sentence to land
it.

---

## Slide 4 — Absolve the guilt

**Headline:** Bolting AI onto that maze just makes faster paperwork.

**Body:** Cheaper drafts. More variants. The same fragile process underneath. That's
not a failure of effort — it's the wrong unit of value. The tools you've been sold
automate *tasks*. The cost lives in the *process*.

🖼 **Visual:** A shiny "AI" button stuck onto the same tangled relay from slide 3 —
nothing untangled, just moving faster. Same maze, new paint.

✍️ **Lever:** Failed-solution / absolve-guilt. Removes blame ("not your fault, you
had the wrong mechanism"), which opens them to a new frame on slide 5.

---

## Slide 5 — The reframe

**Headline:** Stop buying tasks. Start owning loops.

**Body:** A **loop** is the whole job, end to end — inputs in, a finished, defensible
outcome out — that gets smarter every time it runs. Not a tool your team operates. A
process that operates, with your team in command of it.

🖼 **Visual:** The tangled relay of slide 3 redrawn as one clean closed circle:
inputs → work → outcome → learning → back to inputs. Order replacing chaos.

✍️ **Lever:** Spin-the-known + the core idea reveal. Still no product — we've sold the
*concept* first (build desire before the thing).

---

## Slide 6 — How a loop is built

**Headline:** Three layers. You stay on top.

**Body:**
- **The loop** owns the outcome.
- **The agents** do the legwork inside it — research, screening, drafting, QA.
- **You** keep what only you can: judgment, client trust, and the sign-off.

The machine does the maze. The human does the decision.

🖼 **Visual:** A clean three-tier stack — *Managed loop* (top), *Specialist agents*
(middle), *Human judgment* (base, drawn as the foundation, not an afterthought).

✍️ **Lever:** Pre-empts the deepest fear in this room ("does AI take the decision?")
*before* it's voiced — and answers *no*. Customer-as-hero: they sit on top.

---

## Slide 7 — The wedge

**Headline:** Start with the file that costs you the most: CDD onboarding.

**Body:** One loop, one outcome — a complete, audit-ready CDD file.

```
client docs · structure · source-of-wealth
        ↓   research → ownership unpicked, structure mapped
        ↓   screening → sanctions / PEP / adverse media
        ↓   corpus → the JFSC AML/CFT requirements for this exact entity
        ↓   assembly → drafted file + risk rating
        ↓   YOUR MLRO reviews and signs        ← the decision stays human
        ↓   the loop remembers what you changed
```

🖼 **Visual:** The slide-5 circle, now labelled as the CDD loop, with the **MLRO
sign-off** step highlighted as a gate the work must pass through.

✍️ **Lever:** Concrete > abstract. Pick the single highest-pain, most-measurable job
so the value is undeniable. The highlighted human gate handles the regulatory
objection visually.

## Slide 8 — The part that can't be faked

**Headline:** Every answer traces to a cited, dated source.

**Body:** Generic AI guesses about offshore law. Confidently. Wrongly. Ours is built
on a research-grade corpus that separates statute from regulator guidance from
commentary — and tells you the date it was last checked. An answer you can put in
front of the JFSC.

🖼 **Visual:** A drafted CDD conclusion with live citation chips beside each
assertion — *[Trusts (Jersey) Law 1984, Art. X · verified 2026-05]* — versus a
generic chatbot's unsourced paragraph. Side by side.

✍️ **Lever:** Proof-beats-adjectives. Don't *say* "accurate" — *show* the receipts.
This is the slide that converts a sceptic.

## Slide 9 — How it lands in your firm

**Headline:** We don't email you a login. We come and build it in.

**Body:** A forward-deployed engineer works inside your firm for `[[N weeks]]`, wires
the loop to your systems, and turns *how you actually do it* into the loop's playbook.
You finish the deployment owning a working process — not a tool you have to staff and
figure out.

🖼 **Visual:** An engineer at a desk *inside* the trust office (same room as the
cover), screen showing the loop, a compliance officer beside them — partnership, not
handover.

✍️ **Lever:** Differentiator + objection pre-empt ("will this just sit unused like the
last tool?" → no, we deploy it). Customer-as-hero, OffshoreAI-as-guide.

**Headline:** Every file you run makes the next one faster.

**Body:** Each deployment teaches the system patterns it keeps. Your firm's knowledge

🖼 **Visual:** A rising curve — *cost per file* falling as *files run* climbs — with a

✍️ **Lever:** Future-pacing + the moat, in plain words. Open loop → "so what does it
cost / what does it save?" (slides 11–12).

## Slide 12 — What it costs

**Headline:** Priced against the work it removes — not as another subscription.

**Body:**
- **Deployment** — we build the loop into your firm.
- **Platform** — the maintained corpus, skills, and regulatory updates.
- **Usage** — per entity, or per file run. You pay as it earns.

A fraction of the cost it takes out. `[[Indicative figures TBC.]]`

🖼 **Visual:** Three clean tiles (Deployment · Platform · Usage), and a simple bar:
*cost of the loop* sitting well below *cost of the hours today*.

✍️ **Lever:** Build-desire-around-price — anchor to the labour saved so the price reads
as a bonus, not a bill. Avoid per-seat framing entirely.

## Slide 13 — "I know what you're thinking"

**Headline:** The three questions you're already asking.

**Body:**
- **"Could we just build this ourselves?"** You could hire for it — and maintain a
  research-grade legal corpus forever. That's our full-time job, not a side project
  for your compliance team.
- **"Where does our client data go?"** Your client data stays yours, walled off. Only
  the *regulatory* learning is shared — never a beneficial owner's details.
- **"Does the machine make the call?"** Never. Your MLRO signs. The regulator's
  accountable person is still a person.

🖼 **Visual:** Three plain cards, each a question answered in one calm line. No
graphics — restraint signals confidence.

✍️ **Lever:** Objection-handling inside the copy (the *8 Mile* move) — name them
before the buyer can, defuse with a straight answer, not a boast.

---

## Slide 14 — The ask

**Headline:** One loop. One firm. Let's prove it on real files.

**Body:** A scoped pilot of the CDD onboarding loop, on your own onboardings, with
your MLRO in the chair. A clear before-and-after at the end. No platform-wide
commitment to find out if it works.

🖼 **Visual:** A short three-step path — *Scope → Deploy → Measure* — with a calendar
window for the pilot.

✍️ **Lever:** Foot-in-the-door — ask for the small yes (a pilot), not the big one.
Lowers the risk of the first commitment with a slow, conservative buyer.

---

## Slide 15 — Close / the bigger picture

**Headline:** Prove it in Jersey. Then it travels.

**Body:** A loop that works here works in Guernsey, the Isle of Man, the BVI, Cayman.
The firms that own their loops first won't be caught. The question isn't whether this
arrives. It's who's running it when it does.

🖼 **Visual:** Jersey lit on a map, with Guernsey / IoM / BVI / Cayman glowing next in
sequence — the roadmap as a horizon.

✍️ **Lever:** Vision close + gentle urgency (earned, not manufactured). Leaves them
with the open loop that matters: *be first, or be caught.*

## Open question for the real build

Before this becomes slides: decide the **emotional register**. This sketch sits
deliberately on the *credible* end (the personas demand it). There's room for one or
two sharper, frame-breaking lines (slides 2 and 4) if a specific audience can take it —
but test that against a real TCSP reader before dialling up the heat. With this room,
under-selling beats over-selling.
