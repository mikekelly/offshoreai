**Voisinage** is a Norman customary doctrine specific to Jersey law that governs the relationship between neighbouring landowners. It requires each owner to use their property in a way that does not cause unreasonable harm to neighbours.

## What it is

Voisinage is a centuries-old customary principle preserved in Jersey's legal system (though largely abandoned in other modern legal systems). It is based on civil-law traditions and differs from English nuisance law, though the two often overlap in practical outcome. The key requirement is that a property owner must not cause excessive harm to neighbours, use their property unreasonably, or exceed the normal inconveniences of neighbourhood living without adequate justification.

## Does it apply to your neighbour problem?

**Yes, it very likely does apply** if your issue involves:

- Excessive noise, smells, smoke or fumes from a neighbouring property
- Vibrations from machinery or construction
- Surface water or drainage issues affecting your property
- Overhanging tree branches or roots causing interference
- Light obstruction
- Livestock, agricultural activities, or other commercial operations generating nuisance
- Inter-unit problems in apartment buildings (noise, smells from common parts or neighbouring units)

The crucial test is whether the neighbour's activity causes harm that exceeds "ordinary inconveniences of neighbourhood" without adequate justification. Unlike English nuisance law, voisinage can apply even without proving the neighbour acted with fault—it focuses on whether the use of their property is objectively causing excessive harm.

## Available remedies

If voisinage applies to your situation, the Jersey Royal Court can grant:
- An injunction to stop or restrict the activity
- Damages for harm caused
- Specific performance requiring preventative measures

## Getting advice

Given voisinage's customary-law foundation and Jersey-specific procedural requirements, you should engage a Jersey advocate experienced in voisinage claims. The practical approach typically involves documenting the interference, attempting mediation first, and, if necessary, pursuing an Order of Justice through the Royal Court.
