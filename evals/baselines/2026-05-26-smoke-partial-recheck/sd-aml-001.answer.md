## The difference between Terrorism Law Article 19 and Article 21 SAR duties

Both are SAR (failure-to-disclose) offences under the **Terrorism (Jersey) Law 2002**, triggered by suspicion of an offence under Articles 15, 16 or 16A (terrorism-financing offences) — but they target different populations and apply different tests.

### Article 19 — general duty
- Applies to **anyone** carrying on a trade, profession, business or employment.
- The mental element is **subjective**: the person must actually "believe or suspect" terrorism-financing involvement, based on information that came to their attention through their work.
- Defence: **reasonable excuse**.
- It is the CFT analogue of the POCL Article 34A general SAR duty.

### Article 21 — financial-institution duty
- Applies only to persons carrying on a **regulated business** as defined in Schedule 2 to the Proceeds of Crime (Jersey) Law 1999.
- The mental element adds an **objective limb**: the person commits the offence if they know, suspect, **or have reasonable grounds for knowing or suspecting**. This catches **negligent failure** to spot suspicion — facts that ought to have aroused suspicion in a regulated business but did not.
- Defence: reasonable excuse, including a legal professional privilege carve-out.
- This is the practical compliance trigger driving the JFSC AML/CFT Handbook's CDD, monitoring and SAR disciplines for CFT.

### The key contrast
Article 19 catches **actual subjective suspicion** across the whole economy; Article 21 layers a stricter, objective "reasonable grounds" test onto **regulated-sector firms** alone — so a regulated trust company business or bank can commit the Article 21 offence even where it did not in fact suspect, if a reasonable regulated business would have. Both require disclosure to the FIU as soon as reasonably practicable.
