**Voisinage** is Jersey's customary-law "good neighbourhood" doctrine, inherited from Norman customary law (Le Geyt, Pothier) and preserved by the Royal Court. In substance: a landowner must not use their property in a way that causes a neighbour harm exceeding the ordinary inconveniences of neighbourhood, without adequate justification (`knowledge/jersey/property/voisinage.md`).

How it differs from English nuisance — useful to know because Jersey advocates routinely warn against importing the English test wholesale:

- voisinage can apply on an **objective** basis (fault is not always required), where English nuisance is fault-shaped;
- it isn't tied to the "smell, smoke, noise" enumerated categories;
- it sits on a distinct customary-law footing, not on tort.

**Does it apply to your neighbour problem?** It plausibly applies if your complaint falls into the typical categories the corpus lists — excessive noise, smells, smoke/fumes, vibrations from machinery or construction, surface-water or drainage issues, tree roots or overhanging branches, light obstruction — and if the interference exceeds ordinary neighbourhood inconvenience. Common defences are reasonable use, statutory authority (e.g. planning consent — though planning permission does **not** by itself displace voisinage), claimant consent/acquiescence, and (limited) prescription. Remedies in the Royal Court are injunction, damages, and specific performance of preventative measures.

Practical first steps the corpus recommends: keep a contemporaneous log/photos/recordings, write to the neighbour, try mediation, and engage a Jersey advocate familiar with voisinage before going to an Order of Justice.
