## How Article 9 handles foreign forced-heirship

Article 9 of the **Trusts (Jersey) Law 1984** is the firewall: it directs the Royal Court to determine questions of trust validity, the settlor's capacity, administration, trustees' powers, and beneficial rights **by Jersey law alone**, and to disregard any rule of foreign law that would otherwise apply. Article 9 owes its current strength to the **Trusts (Amendment No. 4) (Jersey) Law 2006**.

The forced-heirship-defeating mechanics sit in four interlocking paragraphs:

- **Article 9(1)** — the headline choice-of-law rule: validity, capacity, administration, beneficial rights, etc. *"shall be determined in accordance with the law of Jersey and no rule of foreign law shall affect such question."*
- **Article 9(2)** — the determination proceeds *without consideration of whether any foreign law prohibits or fails to recognise trusts*, **and** *without consideration of whether the trust avoids or defeats rights conferred by foreign law*. This is the operative anti-forced-heirship language: a transfer into a Jersey trust is not voidable in Jersey merely because it defeats a French *réserve héréditaire*, an Italian *legittima*, a German *Pflichtteilsergänzung* clawback, or a Sharia compulsory-shares regime.
- **Article 9(3A)** — Jersey conflict-of-laws rules (other than Article 9 itself) do not apply to Article 9 questions, so a *renvoi* argument cannot bounce the question back to the foreign law.
- **Article 9(4)** — strong-form firewall against foreign judgments: a foreign court order inconsistent with Article 9 is **unenforceable in Jersey**. Even if a civil-law court has notionally returned the trust assets to the estate to compute the reserve, that judgment cannot be enforced in Jersey.

### What the firewall does NOT do

Five real-world limits run through the practitioner discipline:

1. **Article 9(2A) savings** — Article 9 will not validate a disposition where the settlor had no ownership or power to dispose, does not override foreign laws governing formalities of property transfer, and does not cure a testamentary disposition invalid under the testator's domicile. The firewall cannot turn theft into title, override the *lex situs* for foreign real estate, or rescue an invalid will.
2. **Jersey-domiciled settlors** — Article 9(3) preserves **Jersey's own *légitime*** where the settlor is Jersey-domiciled. A Jersey-domiciled settlor cannot escape Jersey customary forced-heirship by settling into a Jersey trust.
3. ***In personam* jurisdiction over a foreign-resident beneficiary** — Article 9 binds the Royal Court and Jersey-located assets. It does not bind the foreign court, which may order a resident beneficiary to act in relation to trust property. (Compare *Crociani*, on the limits of Jersey exclusive-jurisdiction clauses.)
4. **Asset location** — assets sited in the foreign jurisdiction can be reached by the foreign court directly; the firewall cannot defend what is not in Jersey or held by a Jersey-located intermediary.
5. **Sham, capacity, public policy** — a sham trust (failing the Article 2 existence test) gets no firewall protection; the settlor's *personal-law* capacity to dispose may still constrain the underlying transfer; and Jersey public policy is a residual safety valve in egregious cases.

### Drafting reinforcement

Couple the firewall with an express choice of Jersey proper law (engaging Article 4), and complement it with **Article 9A reserved-powers protection** so that *donner et retenir ne vaut* / substance-over-form attacks on settlor-controlled structures do not gain traction (Article 9 itself disapplies the *donner et retenir ne vaut* rule within the trust context). Where the family is exposed to a forced-heirship jurisdiction, the corpus's drafting note is to layer **parallel substantive lifetime planning** in that jurisdiction (a notarial *pacte de famille*, for example) rather than relying on the firewall in isolation.
