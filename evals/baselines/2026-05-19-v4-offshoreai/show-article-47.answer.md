## A naming note up front

A trustee decision is set aside under **Article 47G**, not Article 47B. The cluster works like this: **Article 47B** is the *interpretation* provision (it defines "mistake" widely for the regime); **Article 47E** sets aside a *transfer of property to* a trust; **Article 47G** sets aside an *exercise of a fiduciary power* (this is the trustee-decision route). All sit in the **Articles 47B–47H** regime inserted by the **Trusts (Amendment No. 6) (Jersey) Law 2013** [`knowledge/jersey/trusts/article-47-set-aside.md`].

## The procedure to set aside a trustee decision (Article 47G)

**Who applies.** The trustee itself, a protector, a beneficiary, or another person with appropriate standing.

**Vehicle.** A **Representation** in the **Royal Court, Samedi Division**, joining as respondents the persons affected — the trustee that took the impugned decision, the beneficiaries who received (or would have received) property, and any third-party recipient [`knowledge/jersey/use-cases/trust-officer/article-47-set-aside-for-mistake.md`].

**Evidence.** Affidavit evidence of the decision-maker's state of mind at the relevant time, supported by **contemporaneous documentary evidence** — file notes, board minutes, adviser e-mails, the advice given. A reconstruction affidavit years after the event is weak [`knowledge/jersey/use-cases/trust-officer/article-47-set-aside-for-mistake.md`].

**The three-limb test** the Royal Court applies (formulated in *In re S & T Trusts* [2015] JRC259):

1. **There was a mistake** within the wide Article 47B definition (the effect of the transaction; its consequences, including tax consequences; any advantage believed to be gained; or any fact existing at the time).
2. **But for the mistake, the power-exercise would not have been made** in the form it was.
3. **The mistake was of so serious a character that it would be just** for the Court to make a declaration.

A failure on any one limb defeats the application; limb 2 is the limb most commonly lost in practice [`knowledge/jersey/trusts/article-47-set-aside.md`].

**Effect of a successful declaration.** The exercise is declared **voidable** and unwound — typically *ab initio* — restoring the position immediately before the power was exercised. Third parties who took in good faith and for value are typically protected. Foreign-tax unwinding depends on whether the foreign revenue authority recognises the rescission (HMRC has historically accepted Jersey set-aside for IHT/CGT purposes; US IRS practice is more cautious) [`knowledge/jersey/trusts/article-47-set-aside.md`].

**Common pitfalls.** Partial-rescission relief that amounts to a re-writing of history will be refused (*In re M Settlement*); a disappointed *future* tax advantage (as opposed to an unforeseen *charge*) tends not to qualify [`knowledge/jersey/trusts/article-47-set-aside.md`].

## How Jersey is wider than *Pitt v Holt*

The English Supreme Court in *Pitt v Holt; Futter v Futter* [2013] UKSC 26 drew a line between a **"mistake"** (an erroneous belief about a relevant fact or law — capable of grounding rescission) and **"mere causative ignorance"** or **"inadequate deliberation"** — generally not [`knowledge/jersey/trusts/article-47-set-aside.md`].

The Royal Court has **rejected that distinction under the statutory regime**: in Jersey, *the manner of the mistake is irrelevant* — the Court asks only whether there was *any* mistake within Article 47B's wide statutory definition. Practical consequence: a Jersey mistake claim is **easier to make out** than its English counterpart, which is why English advisers sometimes prefer a Jersey-law trust where the principal substantive risk to be hedged is a future-tax-rule consequence the adviser cannot yet see clearly [`knowledge/jersey/trusts/article-47-set-aside.md`; `knowledge/jersey/use-cases/trust-officer/article-47-set-aside-for-mistake.md`].
