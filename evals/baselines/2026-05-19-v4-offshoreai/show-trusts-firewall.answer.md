## How Article 9 handles foreign forced-heirship

**The core mechanic.** Article 9(1) of the Trusts (Jersey) Law 1984 directs the Royal Court to determine questions of validity, capacity, interpretation, administration, trustee powers, and beneficial rights of a Jersey trust **"in accordance with the law of Jersey, and no rule of foreign law shall affect such question."** Article 9(2) reinforces this by requiring the determination to proceed **without consideration of whether the trust "avoids or defeats rights, claims, or interests conferred by any foreign law"** — which is the specific language that disposes of forced-heirship attacks. (`knowledge/jersey/trusts/firewall.md`)

**Worked illustration.** A settlor with children resident in (e.g.) France or Italy may face local *réserve héréditaire* / *légitime* rules reserving a fixed share of the estate to specific heirs. After the settlor's death, the heirs may attack a lifetime transfer into the Jersey trust as a fraudulent disposition that should notionally be returned (*rapport*) for calculation of the reserve. Article 9 instructs the Royal Court not to give effect to those rules. German *Pflichtteilsergänzung* claw-back is treated the same way. (`knowledge/jersey/trusts/firewall.md`)

**The judgment-blocking layer.** If the forced-heirship court actually rules — declaring the transfer void or ordering restitution — Article 9(4) renders that foreign judgment **unenforceable in Jersey** to the extent it conflicts with Article 9. Article 9(3A) blocks a renvoi argument: Jersey conflict-of-laws rules (other than Article 9 itself) do not apply, so a litigant cannot route around Article 9 by arguing Jersey choice-of-law sends the question back to the foreign forced-heirship law. (`knowledge/jersey/trusts/firewall.md`)

**The savings — what Article 9 does *not* do.** Article 9(2A) preserves three things: (i) it cannot validate a disposition where the settlor lacked ownership / power to dispose, (ii) it does not displace foreign rules on ownership or formalities of property transfer (so local-law formalities for real estate remain live), and (iii) it does not validate testamentary dispositions invalid under the testator's domiciliary law. So Article 9 protects against forced-heirship *as a substantive constraint on the trust*, not against personal-law defects in the underlying transfer. (`knowledge/jersey/trusts/firewall.md`)

**Domicile carve-out.** Article 9(3) preserves Jersey's own *légitime* where the **settlor is domiciled in Jersey** — a Jersey-domiciled settlor cannot use a Jersey trust to escape Jersey forced-heirship. The firewall is for foreign forced-heirship attacking a foreign-domiciled settlor's Jersey trust. (`knowledge/jersey/trusts/firewall.md`)

**Practical limits.** The firewall binds the Royal Court and Jersey-located assets — it does not bind a foreign court that has personal jurisdiction over the trustee or a beneficiary, and it cannot reach assets located in the forced-heirship jurisdiction. The standard practitioner response is to (a) place a clean Jersey-law proper-law clause in the deed to engage Article 9, (b) avoid clauses that re-import foreign succession rules through the back door, and (c) pair the trust with substantive lifetime planning (e.g., a notarial *pacte de famille*) in the forced-heirship jurisdiction rather than relying on the firewall alone. A sham trust failing the Article 2 existence test does not engage the firewall at all. (`knowledge/jersey/trusts/firewall.md`)

**Statutory provenance.** The current Article 9 form derives from the **Trusts (Amendment No. 4) (Jersey) Law 2006**, which strengthened the firewall by extending it to inconsistent foreign judgments (Article 9(4)) and clarifying the capacity position. (`knowledge/jersey/trusts/firewall.md`)
