Under **Article 115 of the Companies (Jersey) Law 1991**, a director may approve a distribution — which includes both cash dividends and buyback payments (under the Article 114 definition) — only if the directors are able to make a **solvency statement** confirming a two-limb **cash-flow solvency test**:

1. **At the time** of the distribution, the company is solvent — i.e., able to pay its debts as they fall due; and
2. **Post-distribution**, the company will remain able to pay its debts as they become due — a forward-looking assessment, typically over a **12-month minimum horizon**, taking into account existing debts and maturities, contingent and prospective liabilities, operating cash-flow, and the direct liquidity impact of the distribution itself.

All directors must sign the statement, usually accompanied by a statutory declaration. A knowingly false statement, or a distribution made without the statement, is **unlawful under Article 115A** — members must repay and directors face personal liability. Procedural deficiencies can sometimes be cured by a **court order under Article 115ZA** where the substantive solvency position was actually met.

The **Jersey distinctive**: this is a **cash-flow** test, not the English "accumulated realised profits less realised losses" test in CA 2006 s.830. A loss-making or newly-formed Jersey company can lawfully distribute if it is cash-flow solvent — one of the most-cited reasons for using a Jersey TopCo in cross-border groups.
