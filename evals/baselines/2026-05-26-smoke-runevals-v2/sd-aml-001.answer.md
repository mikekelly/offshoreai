## Article 19 vs Article 21 — Terrorism (Jersey) Law 2002 SAR duties

The two Articles impose **parallel but differently-scoped** SAR duties for terrorism financing.

### Article 19 — general SAR duty
Applies to **anyone** to whom the information comes in the course of a **trade, profession, business or employment**. Triggered by **actual subjective belief or suspicion** that another person has committed an offence under Articles 15, 16, or 16A. Failure to disclose to the FIU as soon as reasonably practicable is an offence, subject to a **reasonable excuse** defence. (`knowledge/jersey/aml-cft/terrorism-article-19.md`)

### Article 21 — failure to disclose, financial institutions
Applies only to persons in **regulated business** (as defined in Schedule 2 to POCL 1999). Adds an **objective limb**: the offence is triggered where the person knows, suspects, **or has reasonable grounds for knowing or suspecting** the predicate offences — catching **negligent failure** to spot suspicion that Article 19 would not reach. Reasonable excuse remains a defence (including legal professional privilege carve-out). (`knowledge/jersey/aml-cft/terrorism-article-21.md`)

### The key difference in one line
Article 19 catches **actual subjective suspicion** across any business context; Article 21 adds the **objective "reasonable grounds" test** for regulated-sector firms, making it the practical compliance trigger behind the JFSC AML/CFT Handbook's CDD, monitoring, and SAR disciplines.

Article 21 is the CFT analogue of POCL Article 34A; Article 22 governs the discloser's civil-liability immunity, and tipping-off after either disclosure sits in Article 35 (not Article 23).
