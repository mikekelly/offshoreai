## What a Parish Hall Enquiry is

A **Parish Hall Enquiry** is a distinctively Jersey **pre-court forum**, presided over not by a judge but by a **Centenier** — an *elected, unpaid honorary police officer* of the parish where the alleged offence happened. It's the route through which the **majority of routine Jersey motoring offences are resolved without ever reaching the Magistrate's Court**. There is no direct equivalent in the UK or other common-law systems — it's not a fixed-penalty notice and not a court, but a hybrid with features of both (`knowledge/jersey/road-traffic/parish-hall-enquiries.md`).

At the enquiry the Centenier reviews the police evidence, decides whether there's enough to charge, judges whether the public interest needs a full prosecution, engages with you directly, and can issue a **caution, a written fine of up to £200 per offence, or a referral to the Magistrate's Court**. The whole thing is informal — typically 15–30 minutes, no robes, no transcripts, no advocate's address — and usually resolved the same day (`knowledge/jersey/road-traffic/parish-hall-enquiries.md`).

A crucial limit: the Centenier can **only** fine or caution you if **you admit the offence and accept the proposed penalty**. If you dispute it, the matter must go to court (`knowledge/jersey/road-traffic/parish-hall-enquiries.md`).

## When your case goes there

Routine, lower-level motoring matters are the typical Parish Hall fare. Examples that **can** be handled at Parish Hall (`knowledge/jersey/road-traffic/parish-hall-enquiries.md`):

- Minor speeding (under 15 mph over the limit)
- Mobile phone offences
- Failing to display an insurance disc
- Some traffic-light offences
- Minor careless driving (in some cases)
- Parking offences

The schedule of Parish-Hall-treatable offences is maintained by the Law Officers' Department and reviewed periodically.

## When it does NOT go there

The Parish Hall route is **excluded** — the case goes directly to Magistrate's Court by summons — for the more serious matters (`knowledge/jersey/road-traffic/parish-hall-enquiries.md`):

- Speeding 15 mph or more over the limit
- Drink-driving (any reading) or drug-driving
- Dangerous driving (Article 23 RTJL)
- Causing death or serious injury by driving
- Driving while disqualified
- Failing to stop/report after an accident (in serious cases)
- Any offence carrying **mandatory disqualification**
- Anything where the Centenier judges the £200 ceiling inadequate

It also won't proceed at Parish Hall if **you dispute the allegation** — even an otherwise-eligible minor offence must be referred to court if you decline to admit it.

## Your options and a few cautions

You always have the **right to opt out**: you can decline to admit the offence and **demand the case be heard at Magistrate's Court instead**, at any point, and you can appeal a Parish Hall outcome by requesting a Magistrate's Court re-hearing. You're entitled to advance notice of the offence, to bring a friend/family member/advocate, and to a copy of the Centenier's record (`knowledge/jersey/road-traffic/parish-hall-enquiries.md`).

Worth knowing: a Parish Hall outcome **isn't a formal criminal conviction** for most purposes, but it **is recorded** and **can lead to licence endorsement** for relevant offences — so it's not consequence-free. The corpus flags real pitfalls: admitting guilt without advice when a defence might exist or where endorsement matters for insurance; and assuming the Centenier's fine will always be lower than a court's (it isn't necessarily) (`knowledge/jersey/road-traffic/parish-hall-enquiries.md`).
