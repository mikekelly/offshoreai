Here's how Guernsey's image-rights register works, drawn from the corpus.

## The core idea

Guernsey is the **only jurisdiction in the world** with a *statutory image-rights register* for individuals, created by the **Image Rights (Bailiwick of Guernsey) Ordinance 2012**. Where most legal systems treat your image/persona as a fragmented bundle of tort claims (passing off, defamation, breach of confidence in the UK; state-by-state "rights of publicity" in the US; tort-based personality rights in France/EU), Guernsey converts it into a single **registrable property right** with statutory backing (`knowledge/guernsey/image-rights-register.md`).

That property-right framing is what matters for you as a celebrity. Because it is *property*, your registered image is:

- **Transferable** — can be assigned or licensed;
- **Inheritable** — passes on death;
- **Enforceable** against unauthorised use; and
- **Mortgageable** — can be used as collateral in some cases.

## What you can register

You register a **"personnage"**, which can cover your name (real, professional, or stage), voice, signature, image/likeness, distinctive appearance characteristics, mannerisms, and other identifying attributes. Both natural persons and fictional/non-real characters can be associated with a personnage (so it also works for animated characters or brand mascots) (`knowledge/guernsey/image-rights-register.md`).

## The registration process

Registration is filed with the **Guernsey Intellectual Property Office**, undergoes examination and publication, and is granted after an opposition period. It is **renewable every 10 years** (with protection running to the life of the person plus 100 years for some categories), appears **publicly** on the register, and carries **modest fees** compared with commercial trademark filings (`knowledge/guernsey/image-rights-register.md`).

## Licensing and structuring — your main use case

For protecting *and licensing*, the register is most valuable as the **asset base** for licensing arrangements rather than as a standalone enforcement tool. The typical structure combines:

- **A trust** — image rights settled into a Guernsey trust; or
- **A holding company** — image rights owned by a Guernsey (or other) company that licenses their use;
- **Royalty flow** — licensing fees paid up to that holding company/trust;
- **Estate planning** — succession of the image rights potentially outside your home jurisdiction's succession regime (subject to conflict-of-laws rules).

Licensing agreements are typically drafted with **Guernsey-law governing law and forum** (`knowledge/guernsey/image-rights-register.md`).

## Enforcement and its limits

A registered personnage gives you **standing in Guernsey courts** and statutory remedies — injunction, damages, account of profits, and delivery up of infringing material. The honest limitation the corpus flags: the regime is **sui generis**, so **foreign courts may not give it direct effect**. Against infringers operating outside Guernsey, you generally rely on local-jurisdiction enforcement (trademark, passing off, publicity-rights claims) and use the Guernsey registration as **evidence of a proprietary interest** to support that foreign litigation. Note also that registration is **public**, which may not suit individuals wanting privacy (`knowledge/guernsey/image-rights-register.md`).
