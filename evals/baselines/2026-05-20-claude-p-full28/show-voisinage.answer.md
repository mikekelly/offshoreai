## What voisinage is

**Voisinage** is a Jersey **Norman customary-law doctrine of "good neighbourhood."** It requires every landowner to use their property in a way that does not cause **unreasonable harm** to neighbouring owners. It survives in Jersey having been abandoned in most other modern legal systems, and it's applied by the Royal Court. (`knowledge/jersey/property/voisinage.md`)

It overlaps with English nuisance in result but is distinct in foundation and reach:
- it can apply **without fault** — there are objective/strict-liability elements, so you don't necessarily need to prove the neighbour was negligent or malicious;
- it isn't tied to the enumerated "smell, smoke, noise" categories of English nuisance, so a **broader range of activities** can be caught;
- it rests on a specific Jersey customary-law foundation (Le Geyt, Pothier, Royal Court jurisprudence — e.g. *Rockhampton Apartments* is named). (`knowledge/jersey/property/voisinage.md`, `knowledge/jersey/contract/delit-tort.md`)

## Does it apply to *your* neighbour problem?

I can't tell you that — the corpus describes the doctrine, not your facts. What it does give you is the **test to apply.** Voisinage liability requires (`knowledge/jersey/property/voisinage.md`):

1. **Harm** that is objectively identifiable;
2. **caused by** the neighbour's use of their property;
3. that **exceeds the ordinary inconveniences** of living next to someone; and
4. is **without adequate justification** or proportionate purpose.

If your problem fits that shape, it's plausibly a voisinage matter. The corpus lists the kinds of complaints that typically qualify: excessive **noise**, **smells** (livestock/cooking), **smoke or fumes**, **vibrations**, **surface water/drainage**, **tree roots or overhanging branches**, and **light obstruction**. It also flags context-specific treatment for rural/agricultural, construction (often just temporary inconvenience unless excessive/prolonged), restaurant/commercial, and apartment/inter-unit situations. (`knowledge/jersey/property/voisinage.md`)

Things that can defeat a claim: the neighbour's **reasonable use** of their land, your **consent or acquiescence**, **statutory authority** for the activity, a valid **servitude** (easement) permitting it, or (limited) prescription. Note two important points: a neighbour having **planning permission does not exclude voisinage**, and a valid servitude isn't overridden by voisinage but **excessive use of a servitude can still breach it.** (`knowledge/jersey/property/voisinage.md`)

If it does apply, the Royal Court can grant an **injunction**, **damages**, or order **preventative measures**. The usual route is correspondence → mediation → an Order of Justice if litigation is needed, often with expert (acoustic/environmental) evidence and a site visit. Practical first steps the corpus recommends: **document the interference** (logs, photos, recordings), keep a **correspondence trail**, **engage experts early**, **try mediation first**, and **instruct a Jersey advocate familiar with voisinage** — not least because a common pitfall is wrongly assuming the English nuisance test applies wholesale. (`knowledge/jersey/property/voisinage.md`)

## Two caveats for you

- **This is the doctrine, not legal advice on your situation.** Whether it "really applies" turns on your specific facts measured against the four-element test above — that's a judgment for a Jersey advocate, not something the corpus can resolve.
