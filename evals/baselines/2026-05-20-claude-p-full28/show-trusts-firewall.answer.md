## How the Article 9 firewall works against foreign forced-heirship

**Article 9 of the Trusts (Jersey) Law 1984** is the "firewall." Its core instruction to the Royal Court is that questions about a Jersey trust's validity, interpretation, the settlor's capacity, administration, trustees' powers, and beneficial rights are decided by **Jersey law alone** — "no rule of foreign law shall affect such question" (Article 9(1)). Its current shape comes from the Trusts (Amendment No. 4) (Jersey) Law 2006, which strengthened it to reach foreign judgments. (`knowledge/jersey/trusts/firewall.md`)

**The forced-heirship problem it solves.** A settlor with children resident in a civil-law jurisdiction (France, Italy, Germany, etc.) faces local *réserve héréditaire* / *légitime* rules reserving a fixed share of the estate to certain heirs. After the settlor dies, those heirs may attack a lifetime transfer into a Jersey trust as a "fraudulent disposition" that should be notionally clawed back into the estate to calculate the reserve (e.g. the German *Pflichtteilsergänzung* clawback). Article 9 refuses to give effect to those rules in Jersey. (`knowledge/jersey/trusts/firewall.md`)

**The mechanism, paragraph by paragraph** (`knowledge/jersey/trusts/firewall.md`):

- **9(1)** — the headline choice-of-law rule: Jersey law governs the listed questions; foreign law does not affect them.
- **9(2)** — the heart of the firewall: the determination proceeds *without* considering whether a foreign law refuses to recognise trusts, and *without* considering whether the trust defeats rights conferred by foreign law. So even if the foreign jurisdiction treats the disposition as a fraud on forced-heirship, Jersey will not adopt that view.
- **9(2A)** — savings: the firewall cannot validate a disposition where the settlor had no ownership/power to dispose, cannot override foreign laws on ownership or transfer formalities (e.g. for foreign real estate), and cannot cure a will invalid under the testator's domicile. It "cannot turn theft into title."
- **9(3)** — Jersey's *own légitime* still applies if the settlor is **domiciled in Jersey**; a Jersey-domiciled settlor cannot escape Jersey forced-heirship by settling into a Jersey trust.
- **9(3A)** — Jersey conflict-of-laws rules (other than Article 9) don't apply, blocking a *renvoi* argument that sends the question back to foreign law. Article 9 is self-contained.
- **9(4)** — the strong-form firewall: **foreign judgments inconsistent with Article 9 are unenforceable in Jersey**, even a foreign ruling that the transfer was invalid.
- **9(5)–(7)** — disapply the customary rule *donner et retenir ne vaut* (complementing reserved-powers planning), and define "foreign," "heirship rights," "*légitime*," and "personal relationship."

**Limits the reader must keep in mind** (`knowledge/jersey/trusts/firewall.md`):
- The firewall binds the **Royal Court and Jersey-located assets** — not foreign courts. A foreign court with *in personam* jurisdiction over a resident beneficiary can order that person to act regarding trust property.
- **Asset location**: assets sitting in the foreign jurisdiction can be reached directly there.
- **Jersey-domiciled settlor**: Jersey *légitime* still bites (9(3)).
- **Sham/Article 2 failure**: if the settlor in substance retained beneficial ownership, there is no trust and the firewall never engages.
- **Public policy**: a residual safety valve can override Article 9 in egregious cases.

**Practical drafting points**: make an express choice of Jersey proper law (engaging Article 4 so Article 9 applies); avoid clauses that re-import foreign succession rules "by the back door"; and where a forced-heirship jurisdiction is in play, consider parallel substantive lifetime planning there (e.g. a notarial *pacte de famille*) rather than relying on the firewall alone. Article 9A (reserved powers) reinforces the structure by ensuring the settlor's retention of powers doesn't invalidate the trust. (`knowledge/jersey/trusts/firewall.md`)
