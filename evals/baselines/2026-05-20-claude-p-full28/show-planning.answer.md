## Do you need planning permission for a rear extension on your Jersey house?

**Short answer: it depends on the size and location of the extension.** Jersey's planning framework grants automatic permission ("permitted development") for minor works, but rear extensions only qualify if they stay within defined limits.

**When you likely do *not* need planning permission**

A typical single-storey rear extension can fall within the **permitted-development** classes granted automatically by the **Planning and Building (General Development) (Jersey) Order 2011** ("GD Order 2011"), provided it stays within prescribed limits on floor area, depth, and height. For a typical Jersey-sized property, a small single-storey rear extension (the corpus gives ~3–4m projection as an indicative example) "may be permitted development." (`knowledge/jersey/planning/permitted-development.md`, `knowledge/jersey/planning/extensions-and-conversions.md`)

**When you *do* need a full planning application**

- **Anything above the GD Order thresholds** — exceeding any single condition (depth, height, area, position) pushes the work back into the standard application route. (`knowledge/jersey/planning/permitted-development.md`)
- **Two-storey extensions** — these *always* require an application under current rules. (`knowledge/jersey/planning/extensions-and-conversions.md`)
- **Conservation areas** — typically need a full application even if the extension is within the size thresholds, because permitted-development rights are restricted there. (`knowledge/jersey/planning/extensions-and-conversions.md`, `knowledge/jersey/planning/permitted-development.md`)
- **Listed buildings** — listed-building consent is *always* required, and it is separate from (and additional to) planning permission. (`knowledge/jersey/planning/extensions-and-conversions.md`, `knowledge/jersey/planning/permitted-development.md`)
- **Special landscape zones** (Coastal National Park / Green Zone) or where an **Article 31 direction** has removed permitted-development rights — also restricted. (`knowledge/jersey/planning/permitted-development.md`)

**Important caveats even if planning permission isn't needed**

1. **Building approval is separate.** Even where planning permission is not required, you must still comply with the **Building Bye-Laws 2007** (technical building approval). Both are needed. (`knowledge/jersey/planning/permitted-development.md`, `knowledge/jersey/planning/extensions-and-conversions.md`)
2. **Boundary distance matters** — building close to a boundary can trigger neighbour-rights issues. (`knowledge/jersey/planning/extensions-and-conversions.md`)
3. **Restrictive covenants** on your title are a private-law matter and can prohibit works even where planning law permits them. (`knowledge/jersey/planning/permitted-development.md`, `knowledge/jersey/planning/extensions-and-conversions.md`)

**The recommended way to be sure**

Don't assume "small" or "modest" means permitted — the corpus repeatedly flags that the GD Order limits are often more restrictive than intuition suggests, and treating works as permitted when they aren't risks enforcement (including being required to reverse the works at your own cost). If there is any doubt, you can apply for a **Certificate of Lawful Development**, which confirms the works fall within permitted development at a lower fee than a full application, and you can use the planning department's free pre-application advice. (`knowledge/jersey/planning/permitted-development.md`, `knowledge/jersey/planning/extensions-and-conversions.md`)

A note on confidence: both source files are marked `status: draft`, and the practical thresholds (e.g. the 3–4m figure, area limits) are described as "typical"/"indicative" — the corpus directs you to check the current GD Order 2011 for the exact wording and conditions rather than relying on a fixed number.
