## Drug-driving in Jersey — current rules and what's coming

**The rules today (as of May 2026)**

Drug-driving in Jersey is currently prosecuted on an **impairment basis** under the Road Traffic (Jersey) Law 1956. There is no numeric "over the limit" shortcut — the police must prove the driver was *actually unfit* to drive properly because of drug use, using objective evidence (poor vehicle control, slurred speech, failed field sobriety tests, plus drugs detected in blood or urine). This is a harder evidential test than drink-driving's mechanical "over the limit" rule.

Critically, Jersey does **not** yet have:
- statutory drug-concentration limits (no equivalent of the English specified-limit regime);
- police powers to conduct roadside drug tests (only impairment-based field tests);
- per se offences (being over a numeric limit regardless of impairment).

The offence is **substance-neutral**: impairment by any drug counts — controlled drugs (cannabis, cocaine, MDMA, heroin), prescription medication (sedatives, opioids, benzodiazepines), over-the-counter medicines with warnings, and drug/alcohol combinations. Holding a lawful prescription is **not** a defence if the impairment is real.

Procedure runs: stop (States or Honorary Police, usually on observed signs) → field sobriety tests → arrest on reasonable suspicion → blood/urine sample at the station → charge on impairment evidence plus drug presence → Magistrate's Court.

Sentencing tracks drink-driving: mandatory disqualification (typically 12+ months for a first offence), a fine up to £2,000, imprisonment up to 6 months, licence endorsement, with aggravation for accidents, others present, or refusal to cooperate.

**What's coming**

In **February 2026**, States representatives proposed legislation to bring Jersey closer to the UK model. The proposal would:
- introduce **statutory drug limits** for controlled and some prescription substances;
- give police **roadside testing powers** (swab tests, similar to UK drug-wipes);
- create **per se offences** (being over the limit is itself the offence, no impairment proof needed);
- impose **mandatory disqualification** on conviction.

As of May 2026 this is **not yet enacted** — the States Assembly timetable suggests possible introduction during 2026 or 2027. Until then, the impairment-based regime continues to apply.
