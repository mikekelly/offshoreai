## Why Bermuda

Bermuda is the world's leading domicile for captive insurance — it has the largest captive market globally, the deepest reinsurance and specialty-insurance professional infrastructure (brokers, actuaries, insurance managers, specialist legal/accounting firms), and a mature regulatory regime under the **Insurance Act 1978** administered by the BMA. ([knowledge/bermuda/insurance.md](knowledge/bermuda/insurance.md), [knowledge/bermuda/insurance-classes.md](knowledge/bermuda/insurance-classes.md))

Two structural advantages matter especially for a multinational:

- **Solvency II equivalence** — Bermuda's regime (the Bermuda Solvency Capital Requirement) is recognised by the EU as equivalent, so Bermuda vehicles reinsuring EU cedants benefit from reduced collateral requirements. ([knowledge/bermuda/insurance-classes.md](knowledge/bermuda/insurance-classes.md), [knowledge/bermuda/insurance.md](knowledge/bermuda/insurance.md))
- **NAIC qualified jurisdiction** — for US-facing risk, Bermuda is a qualified jurisdiction, again allowing reduced collateral when reinsuring US cedants. ([knowledge/bermuda/insurance.md](knowledge/bermuda/insurance.md))

The corpus notes the trade-off: relative to lighter-touch domiciles, Bermuda carries higher BMA scrutiny and real substance requirements. By contrast, Cayman is the choice for **healthcare** captives and Guernsey for **European-parent** captives wanting an EU-proximate (PCC-based) structure; Bermuda is typically chosen for the **largest** captives, catastrophe reinsurance, and ILS. ([knowledge/bermuda/insurance-classes.md](knowledge/bermuda/insurance-classes.md))

## Which class

This depends on whose risks the captive will write. For a multinational group, the relevant general-business (P&C) classes are:

- **Class 1 — pure single-parent captive.** This is the right class if the captive insures **only the risks of your own group** (related-party risks). It is the simplest category and the lowest regulatory tier, typical for a large corporate self-insuring its own risks. ([knowledge/bermuda/insurance-classes.md](knowledge/bermuda/insurance-classes.md), [knowledge/bermuda/insurance.md](knowledge/bermuda/insurance.md))
- **Class 2 — group / multi-owner captive.** Use this if the captive is multi-parent (e.g. a joint venture or trade-association structure) **or** if it will write group risks plus a limited amount of unrelated third-party business. ([knowledge/bermuda/insurance-classes.md](knowledge/bermuda/insurance-classes.md))
- **Class 3 — captive writing significant unrelated business.** Relevant only if you intend to write substantial third-party (commercial) business beyond the group, up to specified thresholds. ([knowledge/bermuda/insurance-classes.md](knowledge/bermuda/insurance-classes.md))

If your group has multiple distinct programmes or lines you want kept legally separate, the corpus also flags running the captive through a **Segregated Accounts Company (SAC)** for multi-line / multi-programme structures. ([knowledge/bermuda/insurance-classes.md](knowledge/bermuda/insurance-classes.md))

**Bottom line:** for a multinational group self-insuring its own risks, the default is **Class 1** (pure single-parent). Step up to **Class 2** if there are multiple unrelated owners or some limited third-party business, and to **Class 3** only if you plan significant unrelated commercial business. The corpus's stated principle is to pick the *lowest class consistent with your business* to minimise regulatory overhead. ([knowledge/bermuda/insurance-classes.md](knowledge/bermuda/insurance-classes.md))
