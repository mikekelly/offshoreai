On **15 April 2026**, the **Residential Tenancy (Jersey) Amendment Law 2025** came into force, the most significant overhaul of Jersey's residential-tenancy regime in over a decade. It amends the underlying **Residential Tenancy (Jersey) Law 2011 (RTJL)**. The key changes:

**Tenancy form**
- Initial fixed terms are now **capped at 3 years** (down from the prior 9-year ceiling), and **successive fixed terms are prohibited** — an expired fixed term either ends outright or converts automatically to a periodic tenancy.

**Ending a tenancy**
- A **tenant** may now terminate **any** tenancy — including a fixed term — on **one month's written notice**, so fixed-term "lock-in" no longer binds tenants.
- A **landlord** ending a **periodic** tenancy must either justify it on a permitted ground (breach, sale, owner-occupation, redevelopment) or give **12 months' notice** for a no-fault termination. This replaced the old 3-month no-fault notice; 3 months' notice is now only available on a justified ground.

**Rent**
- For periodic tenancies, rent may be raised **only once per year**, by **no more than the Jersey Retail Prices Index (RPI)**, with **at least 2 months' written notice**, unless the rent is "significantly below market rent" (a narrow, challengeable carve-out).

**New forum**
- A new independent **Rent Tribunal** was established to hear rent-increase disputes (whether an increase exceeds RPI, whether the below-market carve-out applies, the once-yearly rule, and notice compliance). It does **not** cover social housing, employment-linked accommodation, or eviction/breach disputes.

**Fees**
- Tenancy agreements must clearly identify **all fees** chargeable to the tenant; hidden fees are unenforceable and may be an offence.

**Transitional treatment** (as at 15 April 2026): new tenancies fall fully under the new regime; pre-existing **periodic** tenancies convert immediately to the new rules; pre-existing **fixed-term** tenancies continue under the old law until expiry and then convert; any successive-fixed-term clause becomes unenforceable at the current term's expiry.

**What did not change:** the Petty Debts Court keeps exclusive jurisdiction over eviction/breach disputes, the Viscount still enforces eviction orders, and the condition-report regime, Tenants' Deposit Scheme, lodging-house exclusion, and 9-year lease ceiling are all unchanged.
