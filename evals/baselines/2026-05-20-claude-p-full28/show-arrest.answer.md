## If you've been arrested in Jersey — your rights

Jersey criminal procedure is governed by the **Police Procedures and Criminal Evidence (Jersey) Law 2003** (PPCE 2003) and its Codes of Practice. Note that both Jersey States Police and the Honorary Police (Centeniers, Vingteniers, Constable's Officers) have full and legally identical arrest powers.

**At the moment of arrest**, the officer must tell you that you are under arrest, state the offence, identify themselves, and caution you: *"You are not obliged to say anything unless you wish to do so, but what you say may be put into writing and given in evidence."* They may use no more force than necessary and may search you for weapons or evidence. If these requirements aren't met, the arrest may be unlawful — which can lead to exclusion of evidence and civil claims for wrongful arrest. (`knowledge/jersey/crime-fraud/arrest.md`)

**Once at the police station**, you have these core rights under PPCE and its Codes (`knowledge/jersey/crime-fraud/rights-at-police-station.md`):

- **Free legal advice** — you can consult a solicitor or advocate at any time, free, confidentially, 24/7 via the duty-solicitor scheme. Interviews should normally not begin until you've had the chance for advice if you want it. This can only be delayed in very serious cases, on specific statutory grounds, authorised at superintendent level. If you decline advice but change your mind mid-interview, questioning must stop.
- **Inform someone of your arrest** — one person told of your arrest and location, at public expense. Can be delayed (inspector-level authorisation) in serious cases.
- **Consular notification** if you're a foreign national.
- **Right to silence** — you need not answer questions. Notably, Jersey is narrower than post-2003 England: no adverse inference can typically be drawn from silence at a police-station interview alone.
- **A copy of the custody record** (the full log of your detention) — sight of it during detention on request, and a copy on release.
- **A written notice of rights** in your language on arrival.
- **An interpreter** at public expense if needed before interview.
- **An appropriate adult** if you are under 18, mentally vulnerable, or have significant learning difficulties.
- **Medical attention** if unwell or injured.
- **Adequate physical conditions** — meals, water, toilet access, and typically a continuous 8 hours' rest in any 24-hour period.

**Interviews** must be openly audio-recorded (Code E), free of oppression or intimidation, with breaks at appropriate intervals, and you must be fit to be interviewed. (`knowledge/jersey/crime-fraud/rights-at-police-station.md`)

**Detention time limits** (`knowledge/jersey/crime-fraud/rights-at-police-station.md`, and summarised in `knowledge/jersey/crime-fraud/index.md`):

| Stage | Limit | Authorised by |
|---|---|---|
| Initial detention | 24 hours | Custody officer |
| First extension (serious offences) | up to 36 hours | Inspector or higher |
| Royal Court warrant of further detention | up to 96 hours total | Royal Court (Magistrate) |
| Beyond 96 hours | Charge or release required | — |

Detention beyond these limits is unlawful and grounds for a habeas corpus / wrongful-imprisonment claim.

**Bail** — there is a general presumption in favour of bail (anchored in ECHR Article 5 via the Human Rights (Jersey) Law 2000). The custody officer can release you with or without charge subject to a return date, or bail you to court; a court can grant bail pending trial. Bail can be refused only on specific grounds (e.g. flight risk, risk of further offences, witness interference) and conditions (residence, curfew, reporting, surety, passport surrender) may be imposed instead of refusal. (`knowledge/jersey/crime-fraud/bail.md`)

**If your rights are breached**, note the details, tell your solicitor at the first opportunity, complain through the formal route after release, and seek exclusion of any tainted evidence at trial. (`knowledge/jersey/crime-fraud/rights-at-police-station.md`)

**Practical steps the corpus recommends**: stay calm, identify yourself truthfully, don't resist physically (challenge an unlawful arrest later through proper channels), don't answer substantive questions before legal advice, ask for a solicitor immediately, use your call to inform someone, note times and names, and don't sign anything without advice. (`knowledge/jersey/crime-fraud/arrest.md`)

A related note: minor offences are often diverted to a **Parish Hall Enquiry** before a Centenier rather than going to court — see `knowledge/jersey/road-traffic/parish-hall-enquiries.md` (referenced from the index, not read here).
