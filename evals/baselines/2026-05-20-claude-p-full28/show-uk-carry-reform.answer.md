## UK carried-interest reform — what changes from 6 April 2026

**The change is a move from a CGT-based regime to an income-tax-based regime with a partial exclusion** (`knowledge/frontier/uk-carried-interest-reform-2026.md`):

- **Before 6 April 2025:** CGT-based, effective rate ~28% for additional-rate payers.
- **6 April 2025 – 5 April 2026 (transition):** flat 32% CGT.
- **From 6 April 2026:** carry is treated as **income**, subject to income tax + Class 4 NICs. For **qualifying** carry a **72.5% multiplier** applies (only 72.5% is brought into charge), giving an effective rate of ~**34.1%** for a 45% additional-rate taxpayer. **Non-qualifying** carry is taxed in full — effective rates up to **47%**.

The 72.5% multiplier was a concession secured through 2025 industry representations, more generous than the original Autumn Budget 2024 design. The **qualifying/non-qualifying split** turns on defined conditions (genuine investment-management activity, fund characteristics). The qualifying definition was tightened versus earlier drafts, though the government dropped proposed access conditions (e.g. a minimum-partner-investment requirement) for competitiveness reasons. For most established UK-resident partners at mature sponsors, qualifying status is the default; new entrants, secondaries participants, and non-traditional structures need careful analysis.

## What PE partners in the Crown Dependencies should do

**Pre-6 April crystallisation (now largely past):** through Q1 2026 many partners accelerated carry realisations — distributions before 5 April 2026, disposals where feasible, restructurings — to lock in the 32% CGT basis rather than the new income regime (`knowledge/frontier/uk-carried-interest-reform-2026.md`).

**Post-6 April structuring and the Crown Dependencies angle:**

- **Residency planning for UK partners** — the CD residency routes are being marketed more actively: **Jersey HVR (2(1)(e))** with its bespoke tax-rate cap, **Guernsey Open Market** (house-purchase-based, standard 20%), and the **Isle of Man tax cap**. For a partner facing a 34–47% UK charge, relocating CD tax residence can be materially attractive — **but** the corpus flags serious caveats: the UK Statutory Residence Test makes leaving genuinely hard, there is **tail liability** on carry that economically accrued while UK-resident (even if realised after departure), real **substance** in the destination is essential, and the new legislation contains **specific anti-avoidance provisions** around residence changes. Do not assume departure shields against UK carry charges.

- **GP-entity location** — CD-domiciled GPs (Jersey/Guernsey companies acting as GP of Jersey or Cayman ELP funds) may become more attractive as the carry-allocation entity for *non-UK* partners, though UK look-through/situs rules still apply where a UK-resident partner receives the economic benefit.

- **Fund-vehicle level is unchanged** — Jersey JPF, Guernsey PIF and Cayman ELP are unaffected at the fund level; the action is at the **GP carry-allocation entity** (partnership profit allocation, carry vesting/forfeiture).

- **Carry-vs-co-invest distinction is now acutely material** — co-invest economics remain CGT-treated, so misclassifying one as the other is a real pitfall. The Jersey carry-vehicle structure itself (Jersey LP holding the carry interest in the main fund, with vesting/forfeiture, good-/bad-leaver terms) is well established, and the corpus stresses that **home-jurisdiction tax of the recipients is the dominant design consideration** — carry-vehicle structuring must integrate with each member's home-jurisdiction position (`knowledge/jersey/use-cases/fund-counsel/carry-vehicle.md`).

- **Other tools:** trust structures to defer/restructure receipts, and family-office structures for spinouts and generational transfer.

**Sponsor-side actions:** document carry arrangements carefully to preserve qualifying status, review GP/LP economics for tax-efficient allocation to non-UK partners, adjust vesting/forfeiture mechanics, and prepare to re-price gross-of-tax economics for LPs (who are now asking about post-reform cost base and key-partner retention plans).

**Pitfalls to avoid** (`knowledge/frontier/uk-carried-interest-reform-2026.md`): treating the 72.5% multiplier as a fixed cliff (the qualifying boundary is fact-sensitive); assuming UK departure removes UK carry charges; misclassifying co-invest as carry; sponsor documentation that fails to establish qualifying status per partner; and cross-border complications where French/German/Italian partners are involved (each jurisdiction's reform pipeline diverges).

**Watch next:** continuing HMRC manual guidance through 2026, edge-case treatment for secondaries/fund-of-funds/infrastructure, first tribunal cases likely 2027–2028, and a possible formal CD competitive response marketing residency programs to PE partners.
