## Setting aside a trustee decision for mistake under the Jersey regime

**It's a statutory jurisdiction, not a common-law one.** Jersey's mistake regime lives in **Articles 47B–47H of the Trusts (Jersey) Law 1984**, inserted by the **Trusts (Amendment No. 6) (Jersey) Law 2013**. The amendment put the Royal Court's previously case-law-developed approach onto a statutory footing and deliberately replaced the *Hastings-Bass* doctrine (which Jersey had already taken further than England).

**Which Article applies to a *trustee decision*.** The Article cluster is split by what the mistake attaches to:
- **Article 47G** — set-aside of the **exercise of a fiduciary power**. This is the one for a trustee decision (e.g., an appointment or distribution of capital the trustee would not have made had it appreciated a tax consequence; a protector's consent given under a misapprehension). Relief reverses the power-exercise and restores the position immediately before it.
- **Article 47E** — set-aside of a **transfer of property *into* a trust** (the settlor-mistake pattern, the most-used Article).
- **Article 11** — a mistake going to the settlor's very intention to settle, which invalidates the *trust itself* — a distinct and mutually exclusive door.

(Note the naming trap: "Article 47" alone is ambiguous — bare Article 47 is the old variation-of-trusts jurisdiction. Cite the sub-Article.)

**What counts as a "mistake" (Article 47B).** Article 47B defines mistake widely: a mistake as to the **effect** of a transaction, its **consequences** (expressly including tax consequences), any **advantage** the person believed they'd gain, or any **fact existing before or at the time** of the disposition or power-exercise. Testamentary dispositions are excluded.

**The three-limb test** (formulated in *In re S & T Trusts* [2015] JRC259 and applied since) — the applicant must show:
1. there was a mistake within the wide Article 47B definition;
2. **but for** the mistake, the power would not have been exercised in the form it was; and
3. the mistake was **so serious that it would be just** for the Court to make a declaration.

Failure on any limb defeats the application; limb 2 is the most common failure point (showing the trustee would not have acted *at all*, versus acting *differently*).

**Procedure.** Application is by **Representation in the Royal Court (Samedi Division)**, supported by affidavit evidence of the relevant state of mind at the time, joining affected persons as respondents (the trustee that took the decision, beneficiaries, and any third party who received property). Contemporaneous documentary evidence — file notes, board minutes, advice given — is critical; a late reconstruction affidavit is weak. The applicant is typically the trustee in a 47G case. A declaration usually voids the transaction *ab initio*, but good-faith-for-value third-party rights are typically protected, and crystallised foreign tax consequences only unwind to the extent the foreign revenue recognises the rescission (HMRC historically has for IHT/CGT; the IRS is more cautious). Where the right remedy is a remedial *direction* rather than a set-aside, the **Article 51** directions jurisdiction may be the better vehicle.

## How Jersey is wider than *Pitt v Holt*

In ***Pitt v Holt; Futter v Futter* [2013] UKSC 26** the English Supreme Court drew a distinction between a true **"mistake"** (an erroneous belief about a relevant fact or law), which can ground rescission, and **mere causative ignorance / inadequate deliberation**, which generally cannot.

Under the statutory regime the **Royal Court has rejected that distinction**: the only question is whether there was *any* mistake within the wide Article 47B definition — **the *manner* of the mistake is irrelevant**, so the English fact/law/ignorance gradations don't apply. That makes a Jersey mistake claim **easier to make out** than its English counterpart, and is the policy reason an adviser may prefer a Jersey-law trust where the principal risk is future application of a tax rule that can't yet be foreseen. Two practical limits remain, though: the Court is reluctant to treat the loss of a hoped-for *future* tax advantage (as opposed to an unforeseen *charge*) as a qualifying mistake, and it resists **partial rescissions** that amount to re-writing history rather than unwinding (*In re M Settlement*).
