Under the **[Companies (Jersey) Law 1991][cjl]**, a director approving a dividend or share buy-back must apply the **cash-flow solvency test** in **Article 115**, supported by a **solvency statement** mechanism that parallels **Article 61A** (used for capital reductions).

## The two-limb test (Article 115)

1. **Solvency at the time** — the company is solvent (cash-flow standard) at the moment of the distribution; and
2. **Forward-looking solvency** — after the distribution, the company **will remain able to pay its debts as they become due**.

The horizon is typically **12 months minimum**, and the assessment must take into account existing debts and their maturity profile, **contingent and prospective liabilities**, post-distribution operating cash-flow, and the direct liquidity impact of the distribution itself.

## Mechanics — the solvency statement

The Article 61A solvency-statement form is used:

- **Every director** must sign (a dissenting director must not sign — if any director cannot sign, this route is unavailable);
- It must be **accompanied by a statutory declaration** sworn before a Commissioner for Oaths;
- A **knowingly false statement** exposes the signing directors to **personal liability** for the company's debts (potentially the full amount of the distribution), criminal offence, and possible disqualification.

## The Jersey distinctive

This is a **cash-flow** test, not the English "**profits available for distribution**" test (CA 2006 s.830). A loss-making or newly-formed Jersey company with no accumulated profits can still pay a dividend or buy back shares if it is cash-flow solvent — one of the most-cited reasons for choosing a Jersey TopCo.

## Consequences of getting it wrong

A distribution made without a valid solvency statement (or on a knowingly-false one) is **unlawful** under **Article 115A**: members must repay, directors face personal liability, and the distribution may be unwound. **Article 115ZA** allows the Royal Court to make a retrospective order treating a distribution as compliant where the substantive solvency position was satisfied but procedural compliance was technically deficient.
