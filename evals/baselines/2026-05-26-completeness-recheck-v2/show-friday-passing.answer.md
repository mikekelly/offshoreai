## Why Friday afternoon

In Jersey, a sale of real property does not complete by the parties signing a deed in their lawyers' offices — the **contract is "passed" before the Royal Court**, sitting in its **Samedi Division**. The Samedi Division is the Court's civil day-to-day forum, named after its historical **Saturday** sittings (*samedi* = Saturday in French); contract passing for real property is now the **Friday afternoon** practitioner convention. The parties (almost always through their lawyers acting under powers of attorney) appear, the contract is read or referenced, the Greffier records it, and **title passes at that Court act** — possession typically the same day.

The procedure is a **Norman customary-law inheritance**: the Royal Court has historically been the repository of real-property records, and a public Court act gives certainty about title transitions. Jersey conveyancing is therefore unlike English conveyancing, which is largely a private exchange-and-completion process — the *fact* of the transaction in Jersey is public (parties' identities; the Act of Court), even though specific terms like price typically are not.

A few practitioner notes worth knowing:

- **You usually don't attend in person.** Your lawyer appears as your attorney under a power of attorney drafted for the passing — routine for non-resident buyers and sellers.
- **Pre-passing work matters.** Title investigation through the Public Registry, planning / environmental / tax searches, and any hypothec (Jersey real-property security) arrangements with your lender are all done before the Friday slot.
- **It's not just sales.** Hypothec-only contracts (taking out a secured loan against an existing property), long leases, gifts, and certain inheritance transfers are also passed before the Royal Court, with procedural variations for each.

**Caveat:** the corpus file directly describing contract passing is `draft` status, and although three corpus files reference the Friday-afternoon convention as practitioner reality, none gives a fully verified historical explanation of *why Friday* specifically rather than another weekday — the Samedi name itself traces to historical Saturday sittings, not Friday. Treat the Friday-afternoon timing as the strong contemporary convention and verify against your conveyancing lawyer for any specific transaction.
