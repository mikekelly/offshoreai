## The Article 9 firewall vs. foreign forced heirship

Article 9 of the **Trusts (Jersey) Law 1984** is Jersey's choice-of-law "firewall." It tells the Royal Court to decide questions about a Jersey trust — validity, settlor capacity, interpretation, administration, trustees' powers, and beneficial rights — **solely under Jersey law**, and instructs the Court to disregard any rule of foreign law (Article 9(1)) (`jersey/trusts/firewall.md`).

The mechanics that bite on forced heirship specifically:

- **Article 9(2)** — the Court decides the question **without considering** whether foreign law refuses to recognise trusts or whether the trust "avoids or defeats" rights conferred by foreign law. So a French *réserve héréditaire* claim or a German *Pflichtteilsergänzung* clawback against a lifetime transfer into a Jersey trust gets no traction in Jersey.
- **Article 9(4)** — foreign judgments inconsistent with Article 9 are **unenforceable in Jersey**. A forced-heirship judgment from the civilian forum cannot be enforced against trust assets in Jersey.
- **Article 9(3A)** — Jersey conflict-of-laws rules (other than Article 9 itself) do not apply, blocking a *renvoi* argument back to the foreign succession law.
- **Article 9(3)** — Jersey's own *légitime* (customary forced heirship for spouse/children of the movable estate) still applies if the **settlor was Jersey-domiciled**. The firewall doesn't let a Jersey domiciliary escape Jersey legitime by settling a Jersey trust.

**Important savings (Article 9(2A))**: the firewall does **not** validate a disposition where the settlor never had ownership/power to dispose, does not override foreign rules on the formalities of property transfer (so non-Jersey real estate is still governed by the *lex situs* on transfer), and does not cure a will that is invalid under the testator's domicile.

**Practical limits** the corpus flags:

1. The firewall binds the Royal Court and Jersey-located assets; a foreign court may still act **in personam** against a beneficiary resident in that forum, or against trust assets physically located abroad.
2. Drafting that re-imports the foreign succession regime (e.g., "trustee to distribute according to the laws of settlor's home jurisdiction") can undercut the firewall by the back door — express choice of Jersey law as the proper law (engaging Article 4) is the baseline.
3. A trust that fails the Article 2 existence test (a sham) is not protected — Article 9 protects a trust from foreign-law attack, not non-trusts.
4. Article 9A on reserved powers complements the firewall — it neutralises *donner et retenir ne vaut* and substance-over-form attacks that would otherwise pair with forced-heirship arguments against reserved-powers settlors.
