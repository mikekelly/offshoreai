## Article 47B and the set-aside procedure

A small but important clarification first: **Article 47B itself doesn't contain a "set-aside procedure"** — it is the *interpretation* Article for the modern mistake regime, which is the cluster of **Articles 47B–47H** of the Trusts (Jersey) Law 1984 (inserted by the Trusts (Amendment No. 6) (Jersey) Law 2013). The operative set-aside provisions are:

- **Article 47E** — set-aside of a **transfer or disposition of property to a trust** made under mistake; on a successful application the Court declares it voidable and property returns to the transferor.
- **Article 47G** — set-aside of an **exercise of a fiduciary power** (e.g. a trustee's appointment of capital, a protector's consent) made under mistake.
- **Article 47B** — supplies the **wide statutory definition of "mistake"**: a mistake as to (i) the *effect* of a transaction, (ii) its *consequences* (expressly including tax consequences), (iii) any *advantage* the person believed they would obtain, or (iv) any *fact existing before or at the time of* the disposition or power-exercise. Testamentary dispositions are excluded.

### The three-limb test the Royal Court applies

Per *In re S & T Trusts* [2015] JRC259 and the line of authority following it, the applicant must show:

1. There was a **mistake** within the wide Article 47B definition;
2. **But for** the mistake, the disposition or power-exercise would not have been made in the form it was; and
3. The mistake was **so serious that it would be just** for the Court to grant relief.

Failure on any limb is fatal; limb 2 (but-for causation, rather than merely a different transaction) is the most common pitfall.

### Procedure in practice

- Application by **Representation** in the **Royal Court, Samedi Division**, supported by affidavit evidence going to the applicant's state of mind at the relevant time.
- Applicants are typically the **settlor** (47E) or the **trustee** (47G); beneficiaries and others with standing may also apply.
- **Contemporaneous documentary evidence** of the mistake (file notes, advice given, correspondence) is critical — a reconstruction affidavit years later is weak.
- Respondents include those affected — trustee, beneficiaries, third-party recipients.
- Effect of a successful declaration is normally *ab initio* voidness, with third-party good-faith-for-value rights protected; cross-border tax effects depend on whether the foreign revenue authority recognises the Jersey rescission (HMRC historically does; the US IRS is more cautious).

### How Jersey is wider than *Pitt v Holt*

The UK Supreme Court in *Pitt v Holt; Futter v Futter* [2013] UKSC 26 drew a distinction between a true **"mistake"** (which can ground rescission) and **"mere causative ignorance" / "inadequate deliberation"** (which generally cannot). The Royal Court has, applying the **statutory** regime, expressly **rejected** that distinction:

> "Under Jersey law, when considering applications under Article 47E, the jurisdiction to give statutory relief requires the Court to decide whether there was any mistake; in Jersey, the manner of the mistake is irrelevant and it is therefore inappropriate to make the distinctions applied under English law."

The practical consequences:

- A Jersey claim is **easier to make out** than its English equivalent — "we didn't think about that" can qualify.
- Jersey's regime is therefore commonly preferred where the principal substantive risk is **future application of a tax or other rule the adviser cannot yet see clearly**.
- But the Royal Court still polices the boundary: a **commercial misjudgement**, a **disappointed expectation of a *future* tax advantage** (as distinct from an unforeseen *charge*), or a request for **partial rescission / re-writing of history** will not be granted — see *In re M Settlement* and the Walkers / Carey Olsen commentaries.
