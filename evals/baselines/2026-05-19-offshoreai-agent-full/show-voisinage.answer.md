## What voisinage is

**Voisinage** ("good neighbourhood") is a Norman customary-law doctrine, preserved in Jersey though abandoned in most modern systems, that obliges a landowner to use their property in a way that does **not cause unreasonable harm** to neighbours. It is enforced by the Royal Court and is **distinct from English nuisance** — though the two often overlap in result, voisinage has its own customary foundation (Le Geyt, Pothier, and modern Royal Court jurisprudence) and can apply without fault (`jersey/property/voisinage.md`).

## Whether it applies to your problem

To bring a voisinage claim, you need to be able to show:

1. **Harm** that is objectively identifiable;
2. **Caused by** the neighbour's use of their property;
3. **Exceeding the ordinary inconveniences** of neighbourhood living; and
4. **Without adequate justification** (e.g. statutory authority, valid servitude, or reasonable use).

Typical fact patterns the doctrine captures include excessive noise, smells, smoke/fumes, vibrations, surface-water/drainage issues, tree roots and overhanging branches, and light obstruction. Construction noise and dust are usually treated as temporary inconvenience, but **excessive or prolonged** disturbance can cross the line. Crucially, the fact that your neighbour has planning permission does **not** by itself defeat a voisinage claim, though regulatory compliance is evidence of reasonable use (`jersey/property/voisinage.md`).

## Practical first steps the corpus recommends

- Document the interference (logs, photographs, recordings).
- Build a correspondence trail with the neighbour.
- Consider mediation as a first step — it is often pursued before litigation.
- If escalation is needed, the claim proceeds by Order of Justice in the Royal Court, often with expert evidence (acoustic, environmental) and a site visit.
- Instruct a Jersey advocate familiar with voisinage — assuming the English nuisance test applies wholesale is a flagged pitfall (`jersey/property/voisinage.md`).
