## What changed for Jersey residential tenancies on 15 April 2026

The **Residential Tenancy (Jersey) Amendment Law 2025** came into force on 15 April 2026 — described in the corpus as the most significant Jersey residential-tenancy reform in over a decade. From that date, the post-amendment regime applies to new tenancies, with specific transitional rules for existing ones.

**Headline changes:**

- **Fixed terms capped at 3 years.** The initial fixed term of a residential tenancy may not exceed three years, down from the prior nine-year ceiling above which a lease becomes a Royal-Court-passed contract of lease. Successive fixed terms are prohibited — at expiry the tenancy either ends outright or converts automatically to a periodic tenancy.

- **Tenant exit on one month's notice.** A tenant may terminate any residential tenancy — including a fixed term — on one month's written notice. Landlords can no longer rely on fixed-term "lock-in" arguments.

- **Landlord termination requires justification or 12 months' notice.** To end a periodic tenancy, a landlord must either justify on a permitted ground (breach, sale, owner-occupation, redevelopment — three months' notice) or give 12 months' "no-fault" notice. This replaces the prior three-month no-fault notice.

- **RPI-capped, once-yearly rent reviews.** Rent on a periodic tenancy may be increased only once per year, by no more than the Jersey Retail Prices Index, with at least two months' written notice. A narrow "significantly below market rent" carve-out exists but is challengeable.

- **New independent Rent Tribunal.** Handles disputes over RPI-cap compliance, the once-yearly rule, the below-market carve-out, and the 2-month notice rule. It does *not* handle eviction, breach, social housing rent, or employment-linked accommodation — eviction and breach remain with the Petty Debts Court (with Viscount enforcement).

- **Strengthened written-form rules.** Tenancy agreements must clearly identify all fees chargeable; hidden fees are unenforceable and may constitute an offence.

**Transitional treatment at 15 April 2026:** new tenancies fall fully under the new regime; pre-existing *periodic* tenancies convert immediately (no grandfathering); pre-existing *fixed-term* tenancies continue under the old law until expiry, then flip; successive-fixed-term clauses become unenforceable at the next expiry.

**What did *not* change:** the Petty Debts Court / Viscount eviction pathway, the 2014 condition-report regime, the Tenants' Deposit Scheme, the lodging-house exclusion, and the 9-year contract-of-lease ceiling.
