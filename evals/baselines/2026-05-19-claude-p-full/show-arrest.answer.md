# Your rights on arrest in Jersey

Jersey criminal procedure is governed by the **Police Procedures and Criminal Evidence (Jersey) Law 2003** ("PPCE 2003") and its Codes of Practice. Here is what you are entitled to.

## At the moment of arrest

The arresting officer must (`jersey/crime-fraud/arrest.md`):

- **Tell you that you are under arrest** and **state the offence**.
- **Caution** you in these terms: *"You are not obliged to say anything unless you wish to do so, but what you say may be put into writing and given in evidence."*
- **Identify themselves** (uniform / warrant card).
- **Use no more force than necessary** — force must be reasonable, proportionate, and necessary.

Failure to comply with these requirements may render the arrest unlawful and give grounds for excluding subsequent evidence, civil claims (wrongful arrest, false imprisonment), and complaints. The same powers and duties apply whether you are arrested by the States of Jersey Police or by the **Honorary Police** (Centeniers and other parish officers) — an arrest by an honorary officer is legally identical (`jersey/crime-fraud/arrest.md`).

A search incident to arrest must be proportionate; any strip search must be by a same-sex officer, in private, and recorded (`jersey/crime-fraud/arrest.md`).

## After arrest — at the police station

You must be taken to a police station "as soon as practicable" and presented to a **custody officer**, who independently decides whether your detention is lawful (`jersey/crime-fraud/arrest.md`).

The PPCE 2003 Codes of Practice give you the following rights at the station (`jersey/crime-fraud/rights-at-police-station.md`):

1. **Free legal advice.** You can consult a solicitor or advocate at any point — free of charge under the duty-solicitor scheme, available 24/7, confidential, and normally available **before** any interview. The right can only be **delayed** in very serious cases on specific statutory grounds and on authority of a superintendent or higher. If you initially decline advice and later change your mind, questioning must stop and you must be given the chance to consult a solicitor.
2. **Right to inform one person of your arrest** — family member, friend or other — at public expense, as soon as practicable. Can be delayed in serious cases on inspector-level authority.
3. **Consular notification** — if you are a foreign national, your consulate or High Commission can be informed; some countries have automatic notification arrangements.
4. **Right to silence.** You are not obliged to answer questions. The caution is read again before any formal interview. The corpus notes that, unlike post-2003 England, Jersey is narrower on drawing adverse inferences from silence at police-station interview.
5. **Custody record.** A comprehensive log of times, reasons for detention, reviews, solicitor visits, medical examinations, searches, property, meals, rest and interviews is kept. You may see it on request during detention and are entitled to a **copy on release**.
6. **Written notice of rights** in your language on arrival at the station.
7. **Interpreter** at public expense if you do not speak English well enough; interview cannot start without one (save in narrowly defined urgent cases).
8. **Appropriate adult** for juveniles under 18, mentally vulnerable persons, or those with significant learning difficulties — interview cannot normally proceed without one.
9. **Medical attention** if unwell, injured, or needing medication, with events recorded in the custody record.
10. **Adequate physical conditions** — meals, drinking water, toilet access, heat/light, and typically **a continuous 8-hour rest period in any 24 hours**.

## Interview safeguards

Interviews are audio-recorded under **Code E**. Oppression (raised voices, intimidation, prolonged questioning) is prohibited. The caution is given at the start of each session. You can have your solicitor present, and you must be **fit to be interviewed** — police must not interview someone too intoxicated, unwell or distressed to follow (`jersey/crime-fraud/rights-at-police-station.md`).

## How long you can be held

From `jersey/crime-fraud/rights-at-police-station.md` and `jersey/crime-fraud/index.md`:

| Stage | Limit | Authorised by |
|---|---|---|
| Initial detention | **24 hours** | Custody officer |
| First extension (serious offences) | up to **36 hours** | Police inspector or higher |
| Royal Court warrant of further detention | up to **96 hours total** | Royal Court (Magistrate) |
| Beyond 96 hours | **Charge or release** required | — |

Detention beyond these limits is unlawful and provides grounds for a habeas corpus / wrongful imprisonment claim.

## Searches and samples at the station

Strip searches by same-sex officer, in private, with records. **Intimate samples** (blood, urine, etc.) require specific consent or a warrant. **Non-intimate samples** (fingerprints, mouth swabs) are permissible in defined circumstances (`jersey/crime-fraud/rights-at-police-station.md`).

## If your rights are breached

Note the time and details if possible, tell your solicitor at first opportunity, complain through the formal route after release. Material breaches can be used to seek exclusion of evidence at any trial, and serious breaches may support civil claims (`jersey/crime-fraud/rights-at-police-station.md`).

## Practical checklist

From `jersey/crime-fraud/arrest.md` and `jersey/crime-fraud/rights-at-police-station.md`:

- Stay calm and don't physically resist — challenge an unlawful arrest later through proper channels.
- Identify yourself truthfully.
- **Ask for a solicitor immediately** and do not answer questions before legal advice.
- Use your phone call to inform someone of your arrest.
- Don't sign anything without legal advice.
- Read the custody record carefully and keep the written notice of rights and your copy of the custody record on release.
