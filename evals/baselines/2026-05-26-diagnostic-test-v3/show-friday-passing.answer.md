The Friday-afternoon timing is the **Royal Court's scheduling convention for the property docket**, sitting on top of the **legal requirement** that all Jersey real-property contracts must be passed before the Royal Court.

## The legal requirement (not the Friday bit)

Jersey is one of the few common-law-adjacent jurisdictions where **title to land does not transfer by private deed**. The buyer and seller (or, more usually, their advocates acting under power of attorney) must **physically appear before the Royal Court's Samedi Division**, the contract is read or referenced, **two Jurats sit to witness**, the Greffier signs and records the act, and title passes at that moment. This is a **Norman customary-law inheritance**: the Royal Court is the public repository of real-property transactions, which is why Jersey conveyancing is more transparent than English conveyancing (parties' identities appear in the Acts of Court, though the price typically does not). The Samedi Division, despite its name (from historical *Saturday* sittings), now sits most weekdays for civil business. ([`knowledge/jersey/property/contract-passing.md`](knowledge/jersey/property/contract-passing.md); [`knowledge/jersey/property/real-property-overview.md`](knowledge/jersey/property/real-property-overview.md); [`knowledge/jersey/legal-system/royal-court.md`](knowledge/jersey/legal-system/royal-court.md))

## Why Friday afternoon specifically

The corpus records Friday-afternoon passings as the **settled modern practice** — sale contracts, long leases, hypothecs, and flying-freehold transfers all routinely pass at the Friday-afternoon sitting, with two Jurats in attendance. ([`knowledge/jersey/property/commercial-leases.md`](knowledge/jersey/property/commercial-leases.md); [`knowledge/jersey/property/flying-freeholds.md`](knowledge/jersey/property/flying-freeholds.md); [`knowledge/jersey/contract/force-majeure.md`](knowledge/jersey/contract/force-majeure.md))

The corpus **does not anchor the Friday-afternoon slot in any statute** — it is a Royal Court timetabling convention by which the conveyancing docket is grouped into one weekly sitting (efficient for the Jurats, the Greffier, advocates carrying multiple completions, and lenders coordinating hypothec registration). If the Court doesn't sit on a given Friday — a bank holiday, the historical court-closure scenario discussed under force-majeure — a property contract scheduled to pass cannot complete that week, which is why Friday-passing timing has occasionally itself featured in force-majeure arguments.

## What that means in practice for you

Your transaction is on a Friday afternoon because **completion = the Royal Court act**, not the exchange of signed documents, and the Court bundles property passings into the Friday slot. Make sure your advocate holds a properly drafted power of attorney if you can't attend personally, that funds and the hypothec paperwork are in place by Friday morning, and that the contract has been agreed in advance — the Court reads it, it does not negotiate it.
