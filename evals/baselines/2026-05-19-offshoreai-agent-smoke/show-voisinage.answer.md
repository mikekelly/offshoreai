## What voisinage is

**Voisinage** is a **Norman customary doctrine** of "good neighbourhood" preserved in Jersey law (abandoned in most other modern systems). It requires each landowner to use their property in a way that does not cause **unreasonable harm** to neighbours — harm that exceeds the ordinary inconveniences of neighbourhood, without adequate justification. It is enforced by the Royal Court, with roots in Le Geyt, Pothier, and modern Royal Court jurisprudence. It overlaps with English nuisance in result but is **doctrinally distinct**: voisinage can apply without fault and is not limited to enumerated categories like smell/smoke/noise (`jersey/property/voisinage.md`).

## Does it apply to your neighbour problem?

The corpus identifies these as **typical voisinage claims**:

- Excessive noise from a neighbouring property
- Smells from livestock or other activities
- Smoke or fumes
- Vibrations from machinery or construction
- Surface water / drainage issues
- Tree roots or overhanging branches
- Light obstruction (subject to specific Jersey rules)
- Inter-unit issues in apartments (noise, smells, common parts)
- Restaurant / commercial-premises issues (kitchen smells, late-night noise, refuse/pest)

To bring a claim you would need to show: (1) objectively identifiable **harm**, (2) **caused by** the neighbour's use of their property, (3) **exceeding** ordinary neighbourhood inconvenience, (4) without adequate **justification**. Available defences include reasonable use, consent/acquiescence, statutory authority, and (limited) prescription. Note that **planning permission does not exclude voisinage** — your neighbour cannot defeat a claim just by pointing to consents — though regulatory compliance may be evidence of reasonable use.

I can't tell you whether it applies to **your** specific problem without knowing what the interference is. If you share the facts (what the neighbour is doing, how it affects you, how long it's been going on, whether you've corresponded with them), I can map them against the threshold above. The corpus does not contain a "complaint triage" tool — applying the doctrine to a specific fact pattern is a Jersey advocate's job.

## Practical next steps the corpus recommends

- Document the interference (logs, photographs, recordings)
- Open a correspondence trail with the neighbour
- Consider mediation as a first step
- Engage experts early where relevant (acoustic, environmental)
- Engage a Jersey advocate familiar with voisinage before issuing an Order of Justice

## Common pitfalls flagged

- Assuming the English nuisance test applies wholesale (it does not)
- Missing the customary-law foundation
- Failing to characterise the harm specifically
- Ignoring statutory authority defences
- Underestimating Royal Court costs
