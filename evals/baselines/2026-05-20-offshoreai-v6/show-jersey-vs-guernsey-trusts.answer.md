## Short answer

For most cross-border family-wealth structures, Jersey and Guernsey trusts are **competitive substitutes** — both descend from a shared Norman customary heritage, both recognise discretionary/fixed-interest/purpose trusts, both allow settlor-reserved powers, both have a statutory firewall, both permit perpetual duration, and both run a Royal Court supervisory jurisdiction with final appeal to the Privy Council (`knowledge/guernsey/trust-law-differences-from-jersey.md`). So the choice usually turns on a handful of differentiators rather than a categorical "one is better."

## When to lean Jersey

- **Maximum forced-heirship / matrimonial-claim defence.** Jersey's **Article 9 firewall** (Trusts (Jersey) Law 1984, as strengthened by the 2006 Amendment No. 4) is the gold standard. Article 9(1) directs Jersey law alone to govern validity, capacity, administration and beneficial rights; Article 9(2) disregards foreign non-recognition of trusts and forced-heirship attacks; and critically **Article 9(4) makes foreign judgments inconsistent with the Article unenforceable in Jersey** (`knowledge/jersey/trusts/firewall.md`). Guernsey's equivalent (s.14 TGL 2007) is comparable in protection but **less explicit on foreign-judgment non-recognition**, so aggressive-defence structures still marginally prefer Jersey (`knowledge/guernsey/trust-law-differences-from-jersey.md`).
- **Depth of case law on setting aside trustee decisions for mistake.** Both jurisdictions have codified a statutory mistake regime wider than the English *Pitt v Holt* approach, but **Jersey's Article 47B–J jurisprudence is more developed** (the *Re S&T Trusts* line) (`knowledge/guernsey/trust-law-differences-from-jersey.md`).
- **Reserved-powers / settlor-control structures** (e.g. US-grantor-trust planning, founder/patriarch control). Jersey's **Article 9A** sets out a wide, specific statutory menu — revocation, variation, advancement, trustee direction, beneficiary appointment, change of proper law — and confirms reservation neither invalidates the trust nor makes the settlor a trustee (`knowledge/jersey/trusts/reserved-powers.md`). Guernsey has broadly equivalent provisions; the menus differ in detail but you can achieve equivalent outcomes either side.
- **Trust-company industry depth** — Jersey's TCB industry is the larger of the two (`knowledge/guernsey/trust-law-differences-from-jersey.md`).

## When to lean Guernsey

- **Cell structures for trustee/fund vehicles** — Guernsey offers both **PCC and ICC** (it invented the PCC in 1997), versus Jersey's PCC only (`knowledge/guernsey/trust-law-differences-from-jersey.md`; `knowledge/CROSS-JURISDICTIONAL-MAP.md`).
- **Combination with an image-rights register** — Guernsey is the only jurisdiction in the world with one, relevant if the family has commercially valuable personalities (`knowledge/CROSS-JURISDICTIONAL-MAP.md`).
- **Fund-trust combinations using a PIF** rather than Jersey's JPF, where the rest of the structure is already Guernsey-anchored (`knowledge/guernsey/trust-law-differences-from-jersey.md`).

## The practical tie-breakers

Where the two are genuine substitutes, the corpus says the real decision usually rests on non-legal factors: **sponsor familiarity** with one regime, **existing service-provider relationships**, **group/administrator infrastructure location**, and **specific asset or family considerations** (`knowledge/guernsey/trust-law-differences-from-jersey.md`).
