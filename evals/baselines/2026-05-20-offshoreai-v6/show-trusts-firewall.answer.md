## How the Article 9 firewall handles foreign forced heirship

**The core rule.** Article 9 of the Trusts (Jersey) Law 1984 directs the Royal Court to determine every question about a Jersey trust — validity, the settlor's capacity to settle, interpretation, administration, trustees' powers, and beneficial rights — **solely under Jersey law**, and to refuse to give effect to any rule of foreign law that would constrain the settlor's freedom to settle (`knowledge/jersey/trusts/firewall.md`, Art. 9(1)). Critically, Article 9(2) requires that determination to proceed **without consideration of whether the trust defeats rights conferred by foreign law** — so even where a civil-law jurisdiction treats a lifetime transfer into the trust as a fraud on the *réserve héréditaire*, Jersey will not apply that view (`knowledge/jersey/trusts/firewall.md`, Art. 9(2)).

**Applied to a forced-heirship claim.** When a settlor dies and forced heirs in their home jurisdiction (France's *réserve héréditaire*, Italy's *legittima*, Spanish civil-code shares, Shari'a prescriptive shares) claim a fixed slice of the trust assets, the Jersey-law position is that those heirs have **no entitlement** to the trust property, the trustee is **not obliged** to distribute to satisfy the claim, and a foreign judgment founded on forced heirship will **not be recognised** in Jersey (Art. 9(4)) (`knowledge/jersey/use-cases/trust-officer/forced-heirship.md`). The same logic defeats foreign clawback/anti-avoidance regimes such as German *Pflichtteilsergänzung* (`knowledge/jersey/trusts/firewall.md`).

**Supporting machinery.** Two further provisions reinforce this: Article 9(3A) excludes Jersey's own conflict-of-laws rules (preventing a *renvoi* argument that bounces the question back to the foreign law), and Article 9(4) renders inconsistent foreign judgments unenforceable in Jersey (`knowledge/jersey/trusts/firewall.md`).

**The firewall is not self-executing or absolute.** In practice the trustee must *actively assert* Article 9 — typically by applying for **Article 51 Royal Court directions** confirming the Jersey-law position before distributing or resisting a claim (`knowledge/jersey/use-cases/trust-officer/forced-heirship.md`). And the firewall binds only the Royal Court and Jersey-located assets. Its real-world limits are:

- **Asset location** — assets sited in the foreign jurisdiction can be reached by the foreign court directly (`knowledge/jersey/trusts/firewall.md`).
- **Personal jurisdiction** — a foreign court with *in personam* jurisdiction over a settlor or beneficiary can make orders personal to that person (and back them with contempt sanctions); those don't bind the Jersey trustee, but the pressure can drive practical outcomes (`knowledge/jersey/use-cases/international-lawyer/article-9-firewall.md`).
- **Settlor domiciled in Jersey** — Article 9(3) preserves Jersey's *own* légitime where the settlor is Jersey-domiciled; a Jersey-domiciled settlor cannot use a Jersey trust to escape Jersey forced heirship (`knowledge/jersey/trusts/firewall.md`).
- **Capacity, ownership, formalities, sham, public policy** — Art. 9(2A) saves foreign ownership/formality rules and won't cure a want of capacity or title; a sham trust isn't protected because it isn't a trust at all; and public policy is a residual override (`knowledge/jersey/trusts/firewall.md`).

**Drafting points.** Make an express choice of Jersey proper law (engaging Article 4 so the firewall applies); avoid clauses that re-import foreign succession rules through the back door; and where exposure is real, consider parallel substantive lifetime planning (e.g. a notarial *pacte de famille*) in the home jurisdiction rather than relying on the firewall alone (`knowledge/jersey/trusts/firewall.md`).
