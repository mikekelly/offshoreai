## 1. The wrapper — a Jersey discretionary trust with an express Jersey-law choice

Use a Jersey **discretionary trust** as the central wealth wrapper, with **Jersey law expressly declared as the proper law**. That explicit choice is what engages the **Article 9 firewall** — the single most important provision for forced-heirship defence. Article 9(1) directs the Royal Court to apply *only* Jersey law to the trust's validity, the settlor's capacity, administration, the trustee's powers, and beneficial rights; Article 9(2) requires the court to disregard whether any foreign law "avoids or defeats rights, claims or interests conferred by any foreign law" — i.e. Italian *legittima* (`knowledge/jersey/trusts/firewall.md`, Art. 9(1)–(2); `knowledge/jersey/use-cases/family-office-adviser/worked-example-international-family-wealth.md`, Steps 1–2).

Drafting discipline matters: avoid any clause requiring the trustee to distribute "in accordance with the laws of succession of the settlor's home country" — that re-imports forced heirship by the back door and undercuts the firewall (`knowledge/jersey/trusts/firewall.md`, "Practical drafting").

## 2. The forced-heirship firewall — why Article 9 defeats the Italian *legittima*

Italy reserves indefeasible shares for the spouse and children and allows heirs to **claw back** lifetime gifts that defeated those reserved shares. The firewall answers each vector:

- Jersey law alone governs validity, capacity and beneficiary rights — Italian forced-heirship rules are not applied (Art. 9(1)–(2));
- **A foreign judgment inconsistent with Article 9 — e.g. an Italian clawback order — is unenforceable in Jersey** (Art. 9(4)). This is the strongest single feature of the Jersey firewall;
- the customary rule *donner et retenir ne vaut* is disapplied, which supports the reserved-powers layer below (`knowledge/jersey/trusts/firewall.md`, Art. 9(4)–(7); worked example Step 5).

If heirs do attack, the trustee can seek **Article 51 directions** from the Royal Court for an authoritative Jersey-court confirmation of the firewall position (worked example Step 5).

**Be candid about the limits.** The firewall binds the Royal Court and protects Jersey-located assets; it does **not** bind a foreign court exercising *in personam* jurisdiction over an Italian-resident beneficiary, and it cannot protect assets physically located in the forced-heirship jurisdiction. It also will not help if the settlor is Jersey-domiciled (Art. 9(3) preserves Jersey *légitime*), nor will it save a trust that is a sham under Article 2 (`knowledge/jersey/trusts/firewall.md`, "Limits"; `knowledge/jersey/use-cases/international-lawyer/article-9-firewall.md`). So the corpus recommends **parallel substantive lifetime planning in Italy** rather than relying on the firewall alone.

## 3. Settlor control — Article 9A reserved powers

To let Mr Rossi retain comfort without invalidating the trust, reserve powers under **Article 9A**, which confirms that reserving a defined menu of powers does not affect validity and the trust takes immediate effect (Art. 9A(1)). Typical reservations: a consent power over specified trustee actions, power to add/remove beneficiaries, and power to remove and replace trustees. A trustee acting on a reserved-power direction commits no breach of trust (Art. 9A(3)), and reserving powers does not make the settlor a trustee (Art. 9A(3A)) (`knowledge/jersey/trusts/reserved-powers.md`; worked example Step 2).

Drafting cautions from the corpus: list the powers exactly rather than using catch-alls (so trustee protection is clear), specify the mechanism of exercise, and **provide for succession of the powers on the settlor's death or incapacity** — otherwise they lapse. Pair it with a non-binding **letter of wishes** (`knowledge/jersey/trusts/reserved-powers.md`, "Drafting practice").

## 4. The underlying holding layer — asset-class SPVs

Insert underlying companies between the trust and the assets rather than holding investments directly. The corpus matches vehicle to asset class (worked example Steps 3 & 7):

- **EU operating-company shares** — Luxembourg or Jersey holdco for treaty access;
- **US public equities** — held through a **non-US blocker** (Cayman or BVI BC) so the US-situs assets are not owned directly by Mr Rossi, avoiding US estate-tax exposure (a non-US-domiciliary's US-situs threshold is only $60,000);
- **Swiss bank portfolio** — held by the underlying company;
- **Caribbean villa** — a **BVI BC** owning the property directly, to avoid Caribbean estate tax on death.

## 5. Governance — PTC option

As the structure matures, a **Private Trust Company** (a Jersey company whose sole purpose is to act as trustee of the family's trusts, typically owned by a purpose trust) can replace the professional TCB trustee, putting family members and advisers on the board while preserving JFSC-regulated administration (`knowledge/jersey/use-cases/family-office-adviser/worked-example-international-family-wealth.md`, Step 4; the file cross-refers `private-trust-company.md` and `when-ptc.md`).

## 6. The reporting / tax overlay — do not skip

The structure carries substantial ongoing transparency and foreign-tax obligations the corpus flags explicitly (worked example Steps 6–8):

- **CRS** reporting to controlling persons' home jurisdictions (Italy, UK, possibly US); **FATCA** if any US person appears; **DAC6** for reportable cross-border arrangements;
- the **UK-resident child** creates UK tax exposure — possible excluded-property treatment, 10-year-anniversary IHT charges, income/capital matching on distributions, and **UK Trust Registration Service** registration — needing specialist UK advice;
- **Italian declarations** of the trust must be made carefully, as the Italian authorities may otherwise challenge the structure retrospectively.

## Why Jersey specifically

The corpus's own summary: Article 9 gives the most explicit forced-heirship defence (notably 9(4) on foreign judgments), Article 9A gives a wide reserved-powers menu, the Article 47B mistake regime is insurance against future structural surprises, TCB regulation is robust, and the trust/underlying companies generally pay 0% Jersey tax (`knowledge/jersey/use-cases/family-office-adviser/worked-example-international-family-wealth.md`, "Why Jersey").
