## Drug-driving in Jersey

### The rules today (as of May 2026)

Drug-driving is prosecuted on an **impairment** basis under the Road Traffic (Jersey) Law 1956. Police must prove the driver was *unfit to drive* because of drug use, using objective evidence — poor control, slurred speech, failed field sobriety tests, plus blood/urine confirming drug presence. There is **no per se "over the limit" offence** as there is for alcohol.

What Jersey does **not** currently have:
- Statutory drug-concentration limits (no equivalent of the English specified-limit regime);
- Roadside drug-testing powers (swabs/wipes) beyond impairment-based field tests;
- Per se offences (being over a numeric limit regardless of actual impairment).

Because prosecution turns on proving impairment, it is a harder evidential test than drink-driving's mechanical limit. The offence is **substance-neutral** — controlled drugs, prescription medication, OTC medicines, and drug/alcohol combinations all count; "I have a prescription" is no defence to genuine impairment. Sentencing tracks drink-driving: mandatory disqualification (typically 12+ months first offence), a fine up to £2,000, imprisonment up to 6 months, and endorsement. ### What's coming

In **February 2026**, States representatives proposed legislation to bring Jersey closer to the UK model:
- **Statutory drug limits** for controlled and some prescription substances;
- **Police roadside testing powers** (swab tests akin to UK drug-wipes);
- **Per se offences** — being over the limit becomes the offence in itself;
- **Mandatory disqualification** on conviction.

The proposal is **not yet enacted**. The States Assembly timetable points to possible introduction during 2026 or 2027; until then, the impairment-based regime continues to apply. ([knowledge/jersey/road-traffic/drug-driving.md](knowledge/jersey/road-traffic/drug-driving.md))
