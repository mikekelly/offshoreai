The **Residential Tenancy (Jersey) Amendment Law 2025** came into force on **15 April 2026** — the most significant Jersey residential-tenancy reform in over a decade, amending the Residential Tenancy (Jersey) Law 2011.

## What changed

**Fixed terms capped at 3 years.** The initial fixed term may not exceed 3 years (down from the prior 9-year ceiling), and **successive fixed terms are prohibited** — a tenancy ends or converts automatically to periodic at expiry.

**Tenant exit on one month's notice.** A tenant may terminate *any* residential tenancy on one month's written notice, including a fixed term — no more fixed-term lock-in.

**Landlord no-fault termination route changed.** To end a periodic tenancy a landlord must either justify it on a permitted ground (breach, sale, owner-occupation, redevelopment) or give **12 months' notice** without justification. This replaces the old 3-month no-fault notice; 3 months' notice now only works on a justified ground.

**Rent caps.** Rent on periodic tenancies can rise only once per year, by no more than the Jersey RPI, with at least 2 months' written notice — subject to a narrow "significantly below market rent" carve-out.

**New Rent Tribunal.** An independent tribunal now hears rent-increase disputes (RPI breaches, the market-rent carve-out, the once-yearly rule, notice compliance). It does *not* cover social housing, employment-linked accommodation, or eviction/breach (which stay with the Petty Debts Court).

**Strengthened written-form rules.** Agreements must disclose all tenant-chargeable fees; hidden fees are unenforceable and may be an offence.

## Transitional treatment (at 15 April 2026)
- **New tenancies** → full post-amendment regime.
- **Pre-existing periodic** → converts immediately (rent caps, 12-month notice, etc.).
- **Pre-existing fixed-term** → continues under old law until expiry, then post-amendment rules apply.
- **Successive-fixed-term clauses** → become unenforceable at expiry, converting to periodic.

## What did *not* change
Petty Debts Court eviction/breach jurisdiction, Viscount enforcement, the 2014 condition-report regime, the Tenants' Deposit Scheme, the lodging-house exclusion, and the 9-year contract-of-lease ceiling.
