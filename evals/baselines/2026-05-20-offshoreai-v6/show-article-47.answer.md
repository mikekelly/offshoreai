## The procedure

A few naming points first. The "Article 47B" label is shorthand for the **Articles 47B–47H mistake regime**, introduced by the **Trusts (Amendment No. 6) (Jersey) Law 2013** to put the Royal Court's prior case-law approach on a statutory footing (and to displace *Hastings-Bass*). Article 47B itself is the **interpretation/definition** provision; the operative set-aside Articles are 47E and 47G (`knowledge/jersey/trusts/article-47-set-aside.md`).

For a **trustee decision** specifically — i.e. an exercise of a fiduciary power — the relevant door is **Article 47G** (47E governs transfers of property *into* a trust). The procedure:

1. **Forum and form.** Application is by **Representation** in the Royal Court, Samedi Division, supported by affidavit evidence of the relevant person's state of mind at the time. Standing: a trustee, protector, beneficiary or other appropriate person (`knowledge/jersey/trusts/article-47-set-aside.md`).
2. **What "mistake" means (Article 47B).** The definition is deliberately wide, covering a mistake as to the **effect** of the transaction, its **consequences** (expressly including tax consequences), any **advantage** the person believed they would gain, or any **fact** existing before or at the time of the power-exercise (`knowledge/jersey/trusts/article-47-set-aside.md`).
3. **The three-limb test** (per *In re S & T Trusts* [2015] JRC259): (i) there was a mistake within the Article 47B definition; (ii) **but for** the mistake the power would not have been exercised as it was; and (iii) the mistake was **of so serious a character that it would be just** for the Court to grant relief. Failure on any limb defeats the application — limb (ii) is the most common to fail (`knowledge/jersey/trusts/article-47-set-aside.md`).
4. **Effect of a declaration.** The Court declares the exercise **voidable**; relief unwinds the power-exercise and restores the position immediately prior (typically void *ab initio*, with Court flexibility). Good-faith-for-value third-party rights are generally protected; cross-border tax consequences only unwind to the extent the foreign revenue recognises the rescission (HMRC has historically accepted it for IHT/CGT; the IRS is more cautious) (`knowledge/jersey/trusts/article-47-set-aside.md`).

Note the boundary cases: a mistake going to the very intention to settle is dealt with under **Article 11** (not 47C–H), and where the remedy sought is a direction to the trustee rather than an unwinding, an **Article 51** directions application may be the right vehicle (`knowledge/jersey/trusts/article-47-set-aside.md`).

## How Jersey is wider than *Pitt v Holt*

In ***Pitt v Holt; Futter v Futter* [2013] UKSC 26** the English Supreme Court distinguished a true **mistake** (which can ground rescission) from **mere causative ignorance** or **inadequate deliberation** (which generally cannot). Under the Jersey statutory regime the Royal Court has **rejected that distinction**: the jurisdiction simply asks whether there *was* a mistake — "the manner of the mistake is irrelevant," so the English mistake/ignorance line is inappropriate in Jersey (`knowledge/jersey/trusts/article-47-set-aside.md`). The practical upshot is that a "we didn't think about that" position is more likely to be remediable in Jersey than in the English High Court — though the Court still won't permit a re-writing of history (e.g. partial rescission was refused in *In the Matter of the M Settlement*) (`knowledge/jersey/trusts/article-47-set-aside.md`).
