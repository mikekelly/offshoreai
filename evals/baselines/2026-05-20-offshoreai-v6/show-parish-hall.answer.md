A **Parish Hall Enquiry** is a distinctively Jersey **pre-court forum**, presided over by a **Centenier** — an elected, unpaid honorary police officer of the parish where the alleged offence took place. It is the route by which the **majority of routine Jersey motoring offences are resolved without ever reaching the Magistrate's Court**. There is no direct equivalent in the UK or other common-law systems — it is not a UK fixed-penalty notice, not a Scottish fiscal fine, and not a court (`knowledge/jersey/road-traffic/parish-hall-enquiries.md`).

At the enquiry the Centenier reviews the evidence, decides whether there is a case and whether the public interest requires prosecution, and can then **caution**, issue a **written fine of up to £200 per offence**, or **refer the matter to the Magistrate's Court**. Crucially, the Centenier can only fine or caution if you **admit** the offence and **accept** the proposed penalty; if you dispute it, the case must go to court (same file).

## When your case goes there

Routine, lower-level motoring matters are typical Parish Hall fare. Examples that **can** be dealt with at Parish Hall:

- Minor speeding (under 15 mph excess)
- Mobile phone offences
- Failing to display an insurance disc
- Some traffic-light offences
- Some minor careless driving
- Parking offences

The schedule of treatable offences is maintained by the Law Officers' Department.

## When it does *not* go there

The Parish Hall route is **excluded** — and the case goes straight to the Magistrate's Court by summons — for:

- Speeding **15 mph or more** over the limit
- Drink-driving (any reading) and drug-driving
- Dangerous driving (Article 23 RTJL 1956)
- Causing death or serious injury by driving
- Driving while disqualified
- Failing to stop/report after an accident (serious cases)
- **Any offence carrying mandatory disqualification**
- Anything where the Centenier judges the £200 ceiling inadequate

## Two things worth knowing

- **You can always opt out.** A defendant can decline to admit and **demand the case be heard at the Magistrate's Court** at any point — and can appeal a Parish Hall outcome by requesting a Magistrate's Court re-hearing.
- **It still has consequences.** A Parish Hall outcome is recorded and can lead to **licence endorsement** for relevant offences, even though it does not formally count as a court criminal conviction for most purposes. Don't assume "no court" means "no consequences" — and consider getting advice before admitting, since endorsement can matter for insurance.
