**No — you cannot "just" evict in Jersey, and self-help is unlawful.** Non-payment of rent is a **breach** under the Residential Tenancy (Jersey) Law 2011, which triggers a statutory procedure that you must follow before you can retake possession.

## The procedure

1. **Serve a written breach notice.** Identify the unpaid rent (which periods, how much), and require remedy within **at least 7 days** (statutory minimum — you can give longer, not less). Sign it, deliver per the agreement or by personal service / recorded post.
2. **Wait the 7 days.** If the tenant pays the arrears in full within the window, the breach is remedied and the tenancy continues. A partial payment is not remedy unless you accept it as such.
3. **Apply to the Petty Debts Court** for an order terminating the tenancy and evicting the tenant (and, optionally, a money judgment for the arrears). The Petty Debts Court has **exclusive original jurisdiction** — not the Royal Court.
4. **The hearing.** The court checks the breach is made out, the notice was procedurally compliant, and considers tenant defences (set-off for unaddressed disrepair, discrimination, procedural defects) and vulnerability (children, Income Support, mental health). It may grant the order with a **stay of execution** — commonly 14–28 days, sometimes longer for families with minor children.
5. **Enforcement by the Viscount.** Once any stay expires, you apply for the Viscount to execute the eviction. The Viscount is the **only person lawfully entitled** to take possession from a resisting tenant — not you, not a private bailiff. The Viscount attends, removes the tenant (with force if necessary), inventories and stores possessions, sells items of realisable value to recover fees, and disposes of unclaimed items after 15 days.

**Realistic timeline:** typically **2–6 weeks** from order to possession with no stay, **6–10 weeks** with a standard 28-day stay, longer if contested or appealed.

## What you must not do

Changing the locks, cutting off utilities, removing the tenant's possessions, or entering without consent are all unlawful — several are criminal offences under the RTJL and expose you to damages claims and a harassment offence in their own right.

## Watch-outs

- A **defective notice** (wrong period, wrong service, wrong addressee) is fatal to the application — get it right.
- If the tenant has a **set-off claim** (e.g. you have ignored repair obligations under the Public Health and Safety (Rented Dwellings) (Jersey) Law 2018), that can reduce or extinguish the arrears claim.
- An eviction that follows a protected act — a discrimination complaint, a repair demand, a report to authorities — is highly contestable as retaliatory.
- Budget for **Viscount's fees** (recoverable from the tenant in principle, often borne by the landlord in practice if the tenant is judgment-proof).
