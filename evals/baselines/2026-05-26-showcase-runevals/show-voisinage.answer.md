**Voisinage** is a Norman customary-law doctrine preserved in Jersey but abandoned in most modern legal systems. It requires each landowner to use their property in a way that doesn't cause **unreasonable harm** to neighbours — the "good neighbourhood" duty. The Royal Court still applies it today, distinct from (though overlapping with) English nuisance.

## The test

To bring a voisinage claim, you broadly need:

- **harm** that is objectively identifiable;
- **caused by** the neighbour's use of their property;
- **exceeding** the ordinary inconveniences of neighbourhood; and
- **without** adequate justification.

Notably, voisinage can apply **without fault** (it has an objective character), and it's **not** limited to the English nuisance categories of "smell, smoke, noise" — so the range of activities it can capture is broader.

## Typical fact patterns it covers

Noise, smells, smoke/fumes, vibrations, surface water/drainage, tree roots and overhanging branches, light obstruction, livestock issues, prolonged construction nuisance, kitchen/refuse issues from commercial neighbours, inter-unit noise in flying-freehold apartments. ## Does it apply to *your* problem?

I can't tell you that without knowing what the neighbour is actually doing. The corpus flags a few specific pitfalls to watch:

- **Planning permission does not exclude voisinage** — a neighbour saying "I have permission" isn't a complete defence;
- **Regulatory compliance** may be evidence of reasonable use but isn't a shield;
- **Valid servitudes** (easements) can permit specific activities — but excessive use of a servitude can still breach voisinage;
- **Defences** include reasonable use, the claimant's consent/acquiescence, statutory authority, and (in limited cases) prescription.

Procedurally the usual route is: log the interference (photos, recordings, dates), correspond with the neighbour, attempt mediation, then if needed an Order of Justice to the Royal Court — site visits and expert evidence (acoustic, environmental) are common.
