## Guernsey's Image-Rights Register — how it works

Guernsey is **the only jurisdiction in the world** with a statutory image-rights register for individuals, created by the **Image Rights (Bailiwick of Guernsey) Ordinance 2012**. It converts what most legal systems treat as a patchwork of tort claims (passing off, defamation, US state "right of publicity") into a **registered property right** — transferable, inheritable, enforceable, and in some cases mortgageable [`knowledge/guernsey/image-rights-register.md`].

### What you can register — the "personnage"

A registered *personnage* can cover your name (real, professional, or stage), voice, signature, image/likeness, distinctive appearance features, mannerisms, and other personality-identifying attributes. Both natural persons and fictional characters can be associated with a personnage, which also opens the door to mascots and animated characters.

### Registration mechanics

- Filed with the **Guernsey Intellectual Property Office**;
- Subject to examination, publication and an opposition period;
- **Renewable every 10 years**, with protection running for the life of the person plus 100 years for certain categories;
- Modest fees relative to commercial trade-mark filings;
- The registration itself is **public**.

### What it gives you

Once registered, you have standing in Guernsey courts to enforce against infringement, with statutory remedies — injunction, damages, account of profits, and delivery up of infringing material. Importantly, the registered right is the **asset base** for licensing arrangements: you can grant licences, attach Guernsey-law governing-law and forum clauses, and use the registration as evidence of proprietary interest when pursuing infringers abroad.

### Wealth-structuring overlay (the usual celebrity pattern)

The register is most powerful when combined with a holding structure:

- **Guernsey trust** holding the personnage, with the celebrity as settlor and family as beneficiaries;
- **Holding company** (Guernsey or other) owning the rights and licensing them to commercial users;
- **Royalty flow** routed to that holding entity, with downstream distribution per the trust;
- **Succession planning** — image rights pass outside your home-jurisdiction succession regime, subject to applicable conflict-of-laws rules.

### Honest limitations to factor in

- **Foreign-court recognition is the main practical question** — the Guernsey regime is *sui generis* and foreign courts may not give it direct effect. In practice, you combine it with local-jurisdiction enforcement (trade marks, passing off, right-of-publicity claims in places like California/New York);
- It's strongest as the **asset base for licensing**, less so as a standalone enforcement tool against an infringer wholly outside Guernsey;
- The register is **public** — if privacy is a priority, calibrate what you file accordingly.
