## Article 47B procedure — set-aside for mistake under the Trusts (Jersey) Law 1984

A note on labelling first: "Article 47" is ambiguous. The mistake jurisdiction is the **Articles 47B–47H regime**, introduced by the **Trusts (Amendment No. 6) (Jersey) Law 2013** to put the Royal Court's case-law-developed mistake jurisdiction on a clear statutory footing. Cite the sub-Article in formal work.

**The two operative doors:**
- **Article 47E** — set-aside of a transfer or disposition of property *to* a trust (the typical settlor case, often discovering an unforeseen tax consequence).
- **Article 47G** — set-aside of an exercise of a fiduciary power *by* a trustee, protector, settlor or other power-holder.

**Article 47B — the wide statutory definition of "mistake"** covers a mistake as to: the **effect** of the transaction; its **consequences** (expressly including tax consequences); any **advantage** the person believed they would gain; and any **fact existing before or at the time of** the disposition or power-exercise. Testamentary dispositions are excluded.

**The three-limb test** (per *In re S & T Trusts* [2015] JRC259) — applicant must show all three:

1. There was a mistake within the wide Article 47B definition;
2. But for the mistake, the transfer or power-exercise would not have been made in the form it was;
3. The mistake was of so serious a character that it would be just for the Court to grant the declaration.

Limb 2 is the most common failure point — applicants struggle to show they would not have entered the transaction *at all*, as opposed to a *different* transaction.

**Procedure:** application is by **Representation** in the Royal Court, Samedi Division, supported by **affidavit evidence of the state of mind at the relevant time**. Contemporaneous documentary evidence (file notes, advice given, correspondence) is critical — a reconstruction affidavit years after the event is weak. Effect of a successful declaration is normally *void ab initio*, with third-party rights acquired in good faith and for value typically protected.

## How Jersey is wider than *Pitt v Holt*

The English Supreme Court in *Pitt v Holt; Futter v Futter* [2013] UKSC 26 drew a distinction between a true **"mistake"** (an erroneous belief about a fact or law — rescindable) and **"mere causative ignorance" or "inadequate deliberation"** (generally not).

Under Article 47, the Royal Court has **rejected** that distinction. The Court treats the question as simply whether there *was any mistake* within the statutory definition — **"the manner of the mistake is irrelevant"** — so it is inappropriate to import the English *Pitt v Holt* taxonomy. The consequence: a future "we didn't think about that" position is more likely to be remediable in Jersey than in the English High Court, which is a load-bearing feature of the Jersey trust as a wealth-structuring vehicle.

Two caveats worth flagging:
- Walkers' recent-cases survey notes the Royal Court's reluctance to treat a **disappointed expectation of a future tax advantage** (as distinct from an unforeseen tax *charge*) as qualifying.
- *In the Matter of the M Settlement* — Royal Court declined a partial rescission on the basis that the proposed relief was a re-writing of history rather than an unwinding.
