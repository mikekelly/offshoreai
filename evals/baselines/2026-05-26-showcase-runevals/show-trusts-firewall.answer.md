The Jersey trust **firewall** is **Article 9 of the Trusts (Jersey) Law 1984**, in its strengthened post-2006 form. It is a self-contained choice-of-law rule designed to neutralise foreign forced-heirship attacks on a Jersey trust, but with real limits.

## How the firewall blocks forced-heirship

**Article 9(1)** instructs the Royal Court to apply **Jersey law and only Jersey law** to questions about a trust's validity, the settlor's capacity to settle, administration, trustees' powers, foreign-court exercises of variation powers, and beneficial rights. *"No rule of foreign law shall affect such question."*

**Article 9(2)** is the operative heart against forced heirship: the determination proceeds **without consideration of whether the trust avoids or defeats rights, claims, or interests conferred by any foreign law**. So a child claiming *réserve héréditaire* in France, *legittima* in Italy, or *Pflichtteilsergänzung* clawback in Germany cannot persuade the Royal Court to notionally return lifetime transfers into the Jersey trust to the deceased settlor's estate for reserve calculation.

**Article 9(3A)** disapplies Jersey's ordinary conflict-of-laws rules to the Article 9 questions, blocking a *renvoi* argument that the issue should be sent back to the foreign law of the settlor's domicile.

**Article 9(4)** — the strong-form firewall — makes **foreign judgments inconsistent with Article 9 unenforceable in Jersey**. So even if a French or Italian court has ruled that the disposition into the trust is reducible to satisfy a reserve, that judgment cannot be enforced against trust assets in Jersey.

## Limits — where the firewall does not protect

1. **Settlor domiciled in Jersey.** Article 9(3) preserves **Jersey's own** *légitime* rules where the settlor is Jersey-domiciled. A Jersey-domiciled settlor cannot use a Jersey trust to escape Jersey forced-heirship.
2. **Foreign personal jurisdiction.** Article 9 binds the Royal Court, not the foreign court. A foreign forum with *in personam* jurisdiction over a beneficiary resident there can order that beneficiary to take steps in relation to trust property.
3. **Asset location.** The firewall cannot protect trust assets physically located in the forced-heirship jurisdiction — those can be reached directly by the foreign court.
4. **Article 9(2A) savings.** Article 9 cannot validate a disposition where the settlor had no ownership or no power to dispose, cannot override foreign formalities for property transfer (notably real estate), and cannot validate a testamentary disposition invalid under the testator's domicile law.
5. **Sham and capacity.** A trust that fails the Article 2 existence test (sham) is not protected — there is nothing for Article 9 to engage with. And while Article 9 governs capacity *for trust purposes* under Jersey law, the settlor's underlying right to dispose may still be impaired by personal-law capacity.
6. **Public policy.** The Royal Court retains a residual public-policy override (rarely invoked).

## Practical drafting points

- The deed must make an **express choice of Jersey law** as the proper law to engage the firewall.
- Avoid clauses that re-import foreign succession rules by the back door (e.g., a clause requiring distributions to follow the settlor's home-country succession law) — these can undercut Article 9.
- Where exposure to a forced-heirship jurisdiction is material, run **parallel substantive planning** in that jurisdiction (e.g., a French *pacte de famille*) rather than relying on the firewall alone.
