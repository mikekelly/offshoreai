## Short answer

**Almost certainly yes** — unless the rear extension is modest enough to fall fully within the **permitted-development** classes in the **Planning and Building (General Development) (Jersey) Order 2011** (the "GD Order 2011"), in which case planning permission is not required but **building approval under the Building Bye-Laws 2007 still is**.

## The rule

The **Planning and Building (Jersey) Law 2002** ("PBL 2002") requires planning permission for "development" of land, which includes building operations such as extensions. The GD Order 2011 grants **automatic planning permission** for specified categories of minor works, including **single-storey rear extensions** within prescribed limits on floor area, depth, height, eaves height, and distance from boundaries — and subject to conditions like materials matching the existing dwelling and not projecting in front of the main building line.

Key practical positions for a rear extension:

- **Small single-storey rear extension** (commonly cited as ~3–4m projection on a typical Jersey property): **may be permitted development** if it meets every GD Order condition.
- **Two-storey rear extension**: **always requires a full planning application** under current rules.
- **Anything beyond the GD Order thresholds** (floor area, depth, height): full application.
- **Listed building**: listed-building consent virtually always required, even for internal works.
- **Conservation area, Green Zone, or Coastal National Park**: most permitted-development cover is lost or narrowed — assume an application is needed.
- **Article 31 directions**: the Minister can remove permitted-development rights in specific locations.

Exceeding **any** condition (height, boundary distance, materials, position) takes the work outside permitted development entirely.

## What to do

1. Check whether your proposal fits a GD Order 2011 category and all its conditions — don't assume "small = permitted".
2. If borderline, apply for a **Certificate of Lawful Development** — modest fee, gives certainty and protects on sale.
3. Use the Department for the Environment's free **pre-application drop-in**.
4. Check your title for **restrictive covenants** — a private-law restriction can prohibit works even where planning would permit them.
5. Remember **building approval** under the Building Bye-Laws 2007 is required either way.
