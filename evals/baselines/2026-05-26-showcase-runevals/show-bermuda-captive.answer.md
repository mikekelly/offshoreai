## Why Bermuda

Bermuda is the **world's leading captive domicile** and the largest reinsurance market — the dominant choice for big multinational captives ([`knowledge/bermuda/insurance-classes.md`](knowledge/bermuda/insurance-classes.md); [`knowledge/CROSS-JURISDICTIONAL-MAP.md`](knowledge/CROSS-JURISDICTIONAL-MAP.md)). Three structural reasons:

1. **Scale and infrastructure.** The world's deepest pool of captive managers, brokers, fronting carriers, and BMA supervisors — captive setup and ongoing administration are routine rather than bespoke.
2. **Solvency II equivalence.** Bermuda is the only offshore jurisdiction with full EU Solvency II equivalence, so a Bermuda captive reinsuring EU group cedants faces **reduced collateral requirements** ([`knowledge/bermuda/insurance-classes.md`](knowledge/bermuda/insurance-classes.md)).
3. **Class structure built for scale.** The Class 1–4 ladder lets you pick the lightest-touch class consistent with your business; misclassification risks BMA enforcement, but the framework is well-defined.

The main trade-offs vs alternatives: **Cayman** is preferred for healthcare captives; **Guernsey** (the PCC inventor, 1997) is the European-parent choice and is preferred for cell-based captive structures where multiple captives share infrastructure ([`knowledge/CROSS-JURISDICTIONAL-MAP.md`](knowledge/CROSS-JURISDICTIONAL-MAP.md)).

## Which class

The class depends on **what your captive will write** (per [`knowledge/bermuda/insurance-classes.md`](knowledge/bermuda/insurance-classes.md)):

| Captive profile | Class |
|---|---|
| Pure single-parent — insures only the group's own risks | **Class 1** |
| Multi-owner / group / association captive — writes group risks plus limited unrelated risks | **Class 2** |
| Captive writing **significant unrelated** (third-party) business up to specified thresholds | **Class 3** |
| Multi-line / multi-programme structure within one licensed vehicle | Bermuda **SAC** (Segregated Accounts Company) — see [`knowledge/bermuda/segregated-accounts-company.md`](knowledge/bermuda/segregated-accounts-company.md) |

For a **typical multinational group captive insuring its own risks**, **Class 1** is the default. If you've got joint-venture risks, a trade association, or you might write some affinity / customer risk, **Class 2**. If the captive will write meaningful third-party business (e.g. selling extended warranties or affinity programmes to customers), **Class 3**.

If you're running multiple lines of business or distinct programmes (e.g. property/casualty + employee benefits + a customer-facing affinity programme), wrapping the captive in an **SAC** with separate cells per programme is the standard Bermuda pattern.

## Caveats

- The corpus file I'm citing is marked **`status: draft`** (last verified 2026-05-18). The class framework and Class 4 minimum (US$100m) are stable, but specific Class 3 / 3A / 3B premium thresholds and capital factors should be confirmed against the **Insurance Act 1978** and current BMA regulations before commitment.
- Bermuda's **CIT 2023** (15% corporate income tax effective 1 January 2025) applies to Pillar Two in-scope groups (EUR 750m+ revenue) — if your multinational hits that threshold, the captive's economics need to be modelled against that rate ([`knowledge/bermuda/tax.md`](knowledge/bermuda/tax.md) via [`knowledge/CROSS-JURISDICTIONAL-MAP.md`](knowledge/CROSS-JURISDICTIONAL-MAP.md)).
- **Substance**: Bermuda has an Economic Substance Act 2018 regime — a captive needs real Bermuda presence (board meetings, decisions, manager).

To narrow Class 1 vs 2 vs 3, I'd need to know: (a) is the captive insuring only group entities, or also third parties? (b) single corporate parent, or multi-owner / JV / association?
