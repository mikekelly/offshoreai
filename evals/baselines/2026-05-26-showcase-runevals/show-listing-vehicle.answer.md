I'll check the corpus files for each of the three vehicles to ground a comparison.## Recommendation: Cayman Exempted is the default for a NASDAQ listing

The corpus's cross-jurisdictional decision framework points to **Cayman Exempted** as the standard listing vehicle for US-listed holdcos — "the Alibaba pattern" [`knowledge/CROSS-JURISDICTIONAL-MAP.md`](knowledge/CROSS-JURISDICTIONAL-MAP.md). The substantive files confirm this and surface specific factors for each option:

### Cayman Exempted — the default choice
- Routinely accepted by NYSE and NASDAQ; "Many US-listed companies with operations in Asia / EMEA list as Cayman exempted companies (the Alibaba / TikTok holdco pattern). Cayman is one of three jurisdictions (alongside BVI and Delaware) routinely accepted by major exchanges as listing vehicles" [`knowledge/cayman/exempted-company.md`](knowledge/cayman/exempted-company.md).
- Highly flexible capital (par/no-par, multiple classes, redeemable shares, treasury shares, capital reduction).
- No pre-formation regulatory consent — 1–2 days to incorporate.
- No direct tax; 20-year (renewable to 30) tax undertaking available.
- MCIT 2025 (QDMTT) catches in-scope groups (≥ EUR 750m revenue).

### BVI BC — viable but with caveats
- Accepted on NYSE/NASDAQ but the comparison table flags listing acceptance as **"more limited"** than Cayman [`knowledge/cayman/exempted-company.md`](knowledge/cayman/exempted-company.md). The CROSS-JURISDICTIONAL-MAP echoes this: "**BVI BC** (some exchange-acceptance limits)" [`knowledge/CROSS-JURISDICTIONAL-MAP.md`](knowledge/CROSS-JURISDICTIONAL-MAP.md).
- Cheapest and fastest to incorporate, but the corpus positions BCs as the "volume / standard structures" vehicle — Hong Kong/Asia investor pattern — rather than the US-IPO workhorse [`knowledge/bvi/business-company.md`](knowledge/bvi/business-company.md).

### Bermuda Exempted — the right pick if you're an insurance/reinsurance business
- Routinely listed on NYSE/NASDAQ; "common for insurance and reinsurance groups" [`knowledge/bermuda/exempted-company.md`](knowledge/bermuda/exempted-company.md).
- Distinctive friction: **BMA pre-vetting** of incorporation. The corpus frames this as a feature — "Bermuda is harder to incorporate into for the wrong sort of business, but the filter delivers regulatory confidence to counterparties" — so insurance/reinsurance counterparty comfort is the payoff for the additional formation lift.
- **CIT 2023** imposes 15% corporate income tax on in-scope groups (≥ EUR 750m) from fiscal years beginning on or after 1 January 2025 — a material change in the historic Bermuda tax narrative [`knowledge/bermuda/exempted-company.md`](knowledge/bermuda/exempted-company.md).

### The rule of thumb from the corpus
> "**US-listed tech holdco** → **Cayman Exempted** (the Alibaba pattern)" 
> "**Insurance / reinsurance listing** → **Bermuda Exempted**"
> "**Lower-cost smaller listing** → **BVI BC** (some exchange-acceptance limits)"
> — [`knowledge/CROSS-JURISDICTIONAL-MAP.md`](knowledge/CROSS-JURISDICTIONAL-MAP.md)

Pick Cayman Exempted unless (a) you are an insurance/reinsurance business — go Bermuda; or (b) you are doing a smaller/lower-cost listing and have confirmed exchange and underwriter acceptance — BVI BC is possible. Note that all three of these files are status `draft` (last_verified 2026-05-17/18), so verify against your counsel before committing.

### Caveats worth flagging to your transaction team
- All three are subject to **Economic Substance Act 2018** (or equivalent); the holdco substance test is reduced but not zero.
- Pillar Two: Cayman MCIT, Bermuda CIT 2023, and BVI MTT 2025 all apply if the group is in scope (≥ EUR 750m consolidated revenue) — domicile arbitrage on Pillar Two is largely gone.
- Underwriter and SEC counsel preference matters in practice; the corpus rule is the starting point, not the final word.
