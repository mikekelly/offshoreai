## Naming caveat

In the Trusts (Jersey) Law 1984, **Article 47B itself is the *interpretation* provision** for the modern mistake regime — it sets out the wide statutory definition of "mistake". The procedural Articles are **47C–47H**, introduced by the Trusts (Amendment No. 6) (Jersey) Law 2013. For a *trustee* decision specifically, the operative Article is **47G** (set-aside of an exercise of a fiduciary power); for a settlor transfer *to* a trust, it is **47E**. Practitioners often say "Article 47B" loosely to mean the whole 47B–47H regime.

## What Article 47B defines as "mistake"

Article 47B captures a mistake as to:

- the **effect** of the transaction;
- its **consequences**, expressly including **tax consequences**;
- any **advantage** the person believed they would gain; or
- any **fact existing before or at the time** of the disposition / power-exercise.

Testamentary dispositions are excluded from the "transfer to a trust" limb.

## Procedure (Article 47G route for a trustee decision)

1. **Standing** — a trustee, protector, beneficiary or other appropriate person can apply to declare the exercise of the fiduciary power **voidable** on the ground that it was exercised under mistake.
2. **Vehicle** — a **Representation** to the **Royal Court, Samedi Division**, supported by **affidavit evidence** of the relevant decision-maker's state of mind at the time. Contemporaneous documentary evidence (file notes, advice, correspondence) is critical; reconstruction affidavits years later are weak.
3. **Three-limb test** the Royal Court applies (formulated in *In re S & T Trusts* [2015] JRC259):
   - there was a mistake within the wide Article 47B definition;
   - **but for** the mistake, the power would not have been exercised in that form; and
   - the mistake was **of so serious a character that it would be just** for the Court to grant relief.
4. **Effect of a declaration** — the power-exercise is unwound, typically *void ab initio*, restoring the prior position; bona fide third-party rights acquired for value before the declaration are generally protected. The Court has flexibility to fashion relief and will refuse anything that amounts to "re-writing history" rather than unwinding (cf. *In the Matter of the M Settlement*).

Limb 2 is the most common failure point in practice — applicants struggle to show they would not have entered *any* transaction, as opposed to a different one.

## How Jersey is wider than *Pitt v Holt*

The English Supreme Court in *Pitt v Holt; Futter v Futter* [2013] UKSC 26 draws a line between an **operative mistake** (which can ground rescission) and **mere causative ignorance / inadequate deliberation** (which generally cannot). The Royal Court, applying the **statutory** Jersey regime, has **rejected** that distinction:

> Under Jersey law … the jurisdiction to give statutory relief requires the Court to decide whether there was any mistake; in Jersey, the manner of the mistake is irrelevant and it is therefore inappropriate to make the distinctions applied under English law.

Practically:

- **Tax-trigger mistakes** (unforeseen tax charge, not merely a lost expected advantage) are routinely unwound under the Jersey regime, where English law would often refuse.
- **Mistakes resulting from inadequate advice** can qualify in Jersey without needing to clear the English "operative mistake" bar.
- The Royal Court has noted Walkers-style limits — a disappointed expectation of a *future* tax advantage is still unlikely to qualify, even in Jersey.

A separate cross-border step is always required afterwards: a Jersey declaration does not bind foreign revenue authorities. **HMRC** has historically accepted Jersey set-aside as restoring the *status quo ante* for IHT/CGT; **IRS** practice is more cautious and fact-dependent.

- Both corpus files cited below are **`draft`** status — the content is drafted but not yet through final editorial verification against the primary statute and judgments. Verify Article references and the *S & T Trusts* citation against the consolidated Law on jerseylaw.je before relying on it in formal work. - For a related but distinct door — mistake going to the **very intention to settle** (the settlor never validly intended to create a trust) — the route is **Article 11** (validity), not Articles 47C–H.
- For remedies that direct the trustee rather than rescind, an **Article 51** directions application may be the right vehicle, sometimes sought in parallel.
