## What changed on 15 April 2026

The **Residential Tenancy (Jersey) Amendment Law 2025** came into force on **15 April 2026** — the most significant Jersey residential-tenancy reform in over a decade. From that date the post-amendment regime applies to new tenancies, with transitional rules for existing ones. The headline changes are:

- **Fixed terms capped at 3 years.** The initial fixed term may not exceed 3 years (down from the prior 9-year ceiling), and **successive fixed terms are prohibited** — an expired fixed term either ends outright or converts to a periodic tenancy.
- **Tenant exit on one month's notice.** A tenant may end *any* residential tenancy on one month's written notice, regardless of any fixed term — fixed-term "lock-in" no longer binds tenants.
- **Landlord termination — justified, or 12 months' notice.** To end a periodic tenancy a landlord must either rely on a permitted ground (breach, sale, owner-occupation, redevelopment) or give 12 months' no-fault notice. This replaces the prior 3-month no-fault route; 3 months' notice now requires a justified ground.
- **Rent caps.** For periodic tenancies, rent may rise only once per year, by no more than the Jersey RPI, on at least 2 months' written notice — unless the rent is "significantly below market rent" (a narrow, challengeable carve-out).
- **A new independent Rent Tribunal** to hear rent-increase disputes (RPI breaches, the below-market carve-out, the once-yearly rule, notice compliance). It does *not* handle social housing rent, employment-linked accommodation, or eviction/breach disputes (those stay with the Petty Debts Court).
- **Strengthened written-form rules** — all tenant-chargeable fees must be clearly identified; hidden fees are unenforceable and may be an offence.

**Transitional treatment:** new tenancies fall fully under the new regime; pre-existing *periodic* tenancies convert immediately (rent caps, 12-month no-fault notice); pre-existing *fixed-term* tenancies continue under old law until expiry, then convert; successive-fixed-term clauses become unenforceable at the end of the current term.

**What did *not* change:** Petty Debts Court jurisdiction over eviction/breach, Viscount enforcement, the 2014 condition-report regime, the Tenants' Deposit Scheme, the lodging-house exclusion, and the 9-year contract-of-lease ceiling.
