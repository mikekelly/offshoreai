**Today (May 2026): an impairment-based regime, no numeric limits.** Driving while impaired by drugs is an offence under the Road Traffic (Jersey) Law 1956. Prosecution turns on **proving impairment** — police must show, on objective evidence (poor control, failed field sobriety tests, blood/urine showing drugs), that the driver was unfit to drive properly. Jersey currently has **no** statutory drug-concentration limits, **no** roadside drug-testing (swab) powers, and **no** per se "over the limit" offences. This makes drug-driving a harder evidential case than drink-driving's mechanical "over the limit" rule. (`knowledge/jersey/road-traffic/drug-driving.md`)

The offence is **substance-neutral** — impairment by controlled drugs, prescription medication, OTC medicines, or a drug/alcohol combination all count. "I have a prescription" is not a defence to genuine impairment. Sentencing tracks drink-driving: mandatory disqualification (typically 12+ months first offence), fine up to £2,000, up to 6 months' imprisonment, and licence endorsement. (`knowledge/jersey/road-traffic/drug-driving.md`)

**What's coming: a per se reform proposal (not yet enacted).** In **February 2026**, States representatives proposed legislation to introduce statutory drug limits for controlled and some prescription substances, give police roadside swab-testing powers (UK drug-wipe style), create per se offences, and impose mandatory disqualification on conviction. As of May 2026 this is **not yet enacted**; the States Assembly timetable suggests possible introduction during 2026 or 2027. Until then, the impairment-based regime continues to apply.
