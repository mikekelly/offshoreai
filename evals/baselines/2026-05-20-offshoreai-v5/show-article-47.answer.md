## A naming point up front

"Article 47B" is the **interpretation** Article — it supplies the *wide statutory definition of "mistake"*. The operative power to set aside a **trustee's decision** (an exercise of a fiduciary power) is **Article 47G**. The transfer-of-property-into-trust set-aside is Article 47E. The whole cluster (47B–47H) was inserted by the **Trusts (Amendment No. 6) (Jersey) Law 2013** to put the Royal Court's case-law mistake jurisdiction on a statutory footing (`article-47-set-aside.md`).

## The procedure to set aside a trustee decision (Article 47G + 47B)

1. **Standing / applicant.** A trustee, protector, beneficiary or other appropriate person applies for the Court to declare the exercise of a fiduciary power **voidable** for mistake. In 47G cases the applicant is typically the trustee itself.
2. **Forum and form.** Application by **Representation** to the Royal Court (Samedi Division), supported by **affidavit evidence of the relevant state of mind** at the time the power was exercised. Contemporaneous documentary evidence (file notes, advice, correspondence) is critical; a late reconstruction affidavit is weak.
3. **The three-limb test** the Royal Court applies (per *In re S & T Trusts* [2015] JRC259):
   - **(i)** there was a mistake within the wide Article 47B definition;
   - **(ii)** **but for** the mistake the power would not have been exercised in the form it was; and
   - **(iii)** the mistake was **so serious that it would be just** for the Court to grant relief.
   Failure on any limb defeats the application — limb (ii) is the usual failure point (showing you'd not have acted *at all*, not merely acted *differently*).
4. **Effect.** A declaration unwinds the power-exercise and restores the prior position (typically void *ab initio*, with judicial flexibility); good-faith-for-value third-party rights are generally protected.

The Article 47B definition of "mistake" is deliberately wide — covering mistakes as to a transaction's **effect**, its **consequences (including tax consequences)**, an expected **advantage**, or a **fact existing at the time** (`article-47-set-aside.md`).

## How Jersey is wider than *Pitt v Holt*

In *Pitt v Holt; Futter v Futter* [2013] UKSC 26, the English Supreme Court distinguished a true **"mistake"** (which can ground rescission) from **"mere causative ignorance" / inadequate deliberation** (which generally cannot). Under the Article 47 statutory regime the Royal Court has **rejected that distinction**: in Jersey "the manner of the mistake is irrelevant," so the Court need only decide *whether there was a mistake*, not police the English category boundary. That makes the Jersey jurisdiction materially **wider** — a future "we didn't think about that" position is more likely to be remediable in St Helier than in the English High Court (`article-47-set-aside.md`).

Two limits worth flagging: the Court will **not re-write history** (partial rescission that is substantively a re-write was refused in *In re the M Settlement*), and a disappointed expectation of a *future* tax advantage (as distinct from an unforeseen tax *charge*) is not readily a qualifying mistake (Walkers commentary, cited in the file).

## A note on what's adjacent
- A mistake going to the **very intention to settle** is an **Article 11** matter (trust invalid for mistake), not 47C–H.
- If the remedy sought is to *direct the trustee* rather than unwind a transaction, the **Article 51 directions** jurisdiction may be the right vehicle.
