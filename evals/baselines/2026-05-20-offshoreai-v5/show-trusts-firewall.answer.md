I'll read the firewall file and verify its freshness.## How the Article 9 firewall handles foreign forced heirship

**The core mechanism.** Article 9 of the Trusts (Jersey) Law 1984 instructs the Royal Court to determine the validity, interpretation, settlor capacity, administration, trustee powers, and beneficial-rights questions of a Jersey trust **solely under Jersey law**, and provides that "no rule of foreign law shall affect such question" (Article 9(1)). Crucially, Article 9(2) directs the court to reach that determination **without considering whether the trust avoids or defeats rights conferred by any foreign law** — which is the part that directly defuses forced heirship. So when civil-law *réserve héréditaire* / *légitime* rules (France, Italy) or clawback regimes (e.g. German *Pflichtteilsergänzung*) would otherwise treat a lifetime transfer into a Jersey trust as a fraudulent disposition returnable to the estate, the Royal Court refuses to give those rules effect. [`knowledge/jersey/trusts/firewall.md`]

**Strong-form protection against foreign judgments.** Article 9(4) goes further: a foreign judgment inconsistent with Article 9 is **unenforceable in Jersey**. So even if a forced-heir obtains a home-forum ruling that the transfer is invalid, that judgment cannot be enforced against the Jersey trust. [`knowledge/jersey/trusts/firewall.md`]

**What the firewall does NOT do (the limits that matter for forced heirship):**
- **Jersey-domiciled settlor.** Article 9(3) preserves Jersey's own *légitime* where the settlor is domiciled in Jersey — you cannot escape Jersey forced heirship simply by settling into a Jersey trust.
- **No ownership / invalid will.** Article 9(2A) means the firewall cannot validate a disposition where the settlor had no title or power to dispose, and cannot cure a testamentary disposition invalid under the testator's domicile.
- **In personam jurisdiction and asset location.** The firewall binds the Royal Court and Jersey-located assets; it does not bind a foreign court that has personal jurisdiction over a resident beneficiary, nor protect trust assets physically located in the foreign jurisdiction.
- **Sham.** A trust failing the Article 2 existence test is not protected — a sham is not a trust, so the firewall never engages. [`knowledge/jersey/trusts/firewall.md`]

**Practical drafting points.** Make an express choice of Jersey proper law (engaging Article 4) so the firewall is engaged; avoid clauses that re-import foreign succession rules through the back door; and where the family is exposed to a forced-heirship forum, consider parallel substantive lifetime planning in that jurisdiction rather than relying on the firewall alone. [`knowledge/jersey/trusts/firewall.md`]
