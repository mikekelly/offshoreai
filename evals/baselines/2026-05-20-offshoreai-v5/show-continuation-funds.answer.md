## Why continuation funds and GP-led secondaries are growing so fast

A **continuation fund** is a new vehicle into which a GP transfers one or more portfolio assets out of an existing fund, letting existing LPs either **roll** their interest into the new vehicle or **cash out** at a negotiated valuation; **GP-led secondaries** is the wider category (also tender offers, strip sales, preferred-equity deals). The corpus identifies these as the **single most active area of PE fund structuring in 2024–2026**, driven by four converging forces:

- **Tough M&A and IPO exit markets** through 2022–2025 — the traditional exit routes were largely shut;
- **LPs demanding DPI** (distributed-to-paid-in capital) so they can recycle into new commitments;
- **GPs unwilling to sell trophy assets** into depressed valuations;
- **Secondaries-market capital** ready to deploy into these structures.

The growth is broad-based and accelerating. Per the 2026 Global Private Equity Outlook survey (cited via Dechert, Nov 2025), **46% of respondents** were using GP-led secondaries or continuation vehicles to expedite distributions — roughly **double the prior year** — with North American (51%), APAC (55%) and EMEA (43%, up from just 14% in 2024) respondents all planning to increase activity (`knowledge/frontier/continuation-funds-and-gp-led-secondaries.md`).

## Why Guernsey is winning the business

The corpus is direct: Guernsey is the **leading European jurisdiction for continuation funds**, and the reasons are specific and durable (`knowledge/guernsey/continuation-funds.md`, stable, last verified 2026-05-19):

1. **Not-a-CIS treatment for single-asset CFs** — the structurally decisive feature. A single-asset continuation fund is **not treated as a collective investment scheme** under the Protection of Investors (Bailiwick of Guernsey) Law 2020, because a single-asset CF is characterised as a single co-investment vehicle (closer to a joint venture) rather than pooled, collectively-managed capital. It can therefore be established **without CIS authorisation** — a significant simplification of the regulatory pathway.
2. **GFSC 3-business-day approval** for single-asset CFs that do require review — the fastest in the European market, with next-day approval available for Private Investment Funds (PIFs). Combined with a mature admin/counsel ecosystem, a sponsor can go term-sheet-to-closing in **2–3 weeks** versus 4–8 weeks elsewhere.
3. **Explicit strategic positioning** — the GFSC has positioned Guernsey as a continuation-funds jurisdiction through guidance and case-handling, and **Guernsey Finance has marketed the product line aggressively** to UK and European sponsors. The corpus notes Guernsey's marketing is "the most aggressive" of the Crown Dependencies.
4. **Mature practitioner base** — the major Channel Islands firms (Mourant, Ogier, Carey Olsen, Walkers, Appleby) and admin houses (Aztec, IQ-EQ, JTC, Apex) have handled real transaction volume through 2024–2026.
5. **Tax-neutral** (0% standard corporate tax) and **substance-light** for the new vehicle where only the asset changes hands.

The workhorse vehicles are the **Class 1 PIF** or, for not-a-CIS single-asset structures, a plain Guernsey company under the Companies (Guernsey) Law 2008. Multi-asset CFs are generally treated as CIS and use the authorised/PIF Class 3 routes.

**For context — Jersey competes but trails here.** Jersey offers the JPF with **48-hour JFSC authorisation** and the DSP model, which is well-suited to continuation vehicles, and Jersey practitioners have launched multiple continuation funds in 2025–2026 to match (`knowledge/jersey/funds/continuation-funds.md`, stable, 2026-05-19). But Jersey lacks the single-asset not-a-CIS carve-out that gives Guernsey its decisive regulatory-pathway advantage. For institutional, US-LP-heavy deals, **Cayman ELPs** remain dominant.
