## What voisinage is

**Voisinage** (literally "neighbourhood") is a **Norman customary-law doctrine** of *good neighbourhood* preserved in Jersey law. It requires each landowner to use their property in a way that does not cause **unreasonable harm** to neighbouring owners. It derives from Norman customary law (Le Geyt, Pothier), developed by the Royal Court, and it is **distinct from English nuisance** even though the two often reach the same result (`knowledge/jersey/property/voisinage.md`).

The practically important differences from English nuisance:

- It can apply on an **objective basis without fault** — you don't necessarily have to show the neighbour was careless;
- There's **no closed list** of "smell, smoke, noise" categories — a broader range of activity can be caught;
- It rests on a **specific Jersey customary-law foundation**, not the English tort (`knowledge/jersey/property/voisinage.md`).

## Does it apply to your neighbour problem?

It *may*. The corpus sets out a threshold test — voisinage liability requires:

1. **Harm** that is objectively identifiable;
2. **Caused by** the neighbour's use of their property;
3. **Exceeding** the ordinary inconveniences of neighbourhood living; and
4. **Without** adequate justification (`knowledge/jersey/property/voisinage.md`).

Whether your situation qualifies depends entirely on the facts. The doctrine has been applied (per the corpus) to excessive **noise, smells (livestock, kitchens), smoke/fumes, vibrations, surface water/drainage, tree roots/overhanging branches, and light obstruction**, across rural, construction, commercial-premises, and apartment/inter-unit contexts. So if your problem falls into one of those buckets and crosses the "more than ordinary inconvenience" line, voisinage is a plausible route (`knowledge/jersey/property/voisinage.md`).

Two points that frequently trip people up:

- **Planning permission does not give the neighbour a defence** — lawful planning consent does not exclude a voisinage claim (though regulatory compliance can be *evidence* of reasonable use);
- A valid **servitude** (Jersey easement) may permit the activity, but *excessive* use of a servitude can still breach voisinage (`knowledge/jersey/property/voisinage.md`).

Available **defences** the neighbour might raise include reasonable use, your consent/acquiescence, statutory authority, and (limited) prescription. **Remedies** the Royal Court can grant are an injunction restraining the activity, damages, and orders for preventative measures (`knowledge/jersey/property/voisinage.md`).

## What to do next

The corpus recommends, in order: **document the interference** (logs, photos, recordings), **engage relevant experts early** (e.g. acoustic or environmental), keep a **correspondence trail** with the neighbour, **try mediation first**, and **instruct a Jersey advocate familiar with voisinage** before litigating by Order of Justice in the Royal Court — Royal Court costs are easy to underestimate (`knowledge/jersey/property/voisinage.md`).

I can't tell you whether *your* specific problem clears the threshold — that's a fact-specific judgement for a Jersey advocate. But the doctrine is real, it's broader than English nuisance, and the categories above suggest it's worth exploring.
