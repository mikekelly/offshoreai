## What voisinage is

**Voisinage** is Jersey's customary doctrine of *good neighbourhood* — a Norman customary-law rule that obliges every landowner to use their property in a way that doesn't cause **unreasonable harm** to their neighbours. It's been preserved in Jersey long after most other systems dropped it, sits alongside (but is distinct from) English nuisance, and is applied by the Royal Court (`jersey/property/voisinage.md`).

The duty is breached when there is:

- **harm** that is objectively identifiable,
- **caused by** the defendant's use of their property,
- **exceeding** the ordinary inconveniences of neighbourhood living,
- **without** adequate justification.

Unlike English nuisance, voisinage can apply **without fault** (it's objective) and isn't confined to the classic "smell, smoke, noise" categories — it can capture a broader range of activities (`jersey/property/voisinage.md`).

## Does it apply to your neighbour problem?

The corpus says voisinage *may* apply to, among other things: excessive noise, smells from livestock or activities, smoke/fumes, vibrations from machinery or construction, surface-water/drainage issues, tree roots and overhanging branches, and light obstruction. It also calls out specific contexts: rural/agricultural, construction (where temporary inconvenience usually isn't enough but excessive/prolonged disturbance can breach it), restaurants and commercial premises adjoining residential, and inter-unit issues in apartments (`jersey/property/voisinage.md`).

Important caveats from the corpus:

- **Planning permission does not exclude voisinage** — a neighbour with consent can still be liable. Regulatory compliance is only evidence of reasonable use, not a shield.
- **Servitudes (easements) aren't overridden** by voisinage, but excessive use of a servitude can itself breach voisinage.
- **Defences** include reasonable use, pre-existing state (limited), consent/acquiescence, statutory authority, and prescription (limited).
- **Don't assume English nuisance applies wholesale** — that's listed as a top pitfall.
- If the issue is really about *where the boundary runs* (encroachment, hedges, overhanging trees, party walls), boundary-disputes doctrine sits alongside voisinage and may be the better frame (`jersey/property/boundary-disputes.md`).

**Bottom line:** I can't tell you whether voisinage applies to *your* specific problem without knowing what your neighbour is actually doing. If it's a recurring interference — noise, smell, smoke, vibration, water, roots, light — voisinage is the right Jersey doctrine to test it against, and the threshold is "harm exceeding ordinary neighbourhood inconvenience, without justification." The corpus recommends documenting the interference (logs, photos, recordings), engaging experts (acoustic/environmental) early, building a correspondence trail, attempting mediation first, and instructing a Jersey advocate familiar with voisinage before going to the Royal Court (`jersey/property/voisinage.md`).
