The "Friday afternoon" property passing in Jersey is a Norman-customary-law procedural inheritance, not a statutory rule. Jersey real-property contracts — sales, gifts, hypothecs, and long leases — do not transfer privately by signed deed as in English conveyancing; instead they **pass before the Royal Court**, where the parties (or, more usually, their advocates acting under powers of attorney) appear, the contract is read or referenced, the Greffier records it, and the transfer takes legal effect at that moment in open court. By long-standing convention this passing happens on a Friday afternoon, with two Jurats sitting to witness, after which the contract is recorded in the Public Registry.

The procedure sits in the **Samedi Division** of the Royal Court — itself named after the historical Saturday sittings — which is the civil/administrative day-to-day forum of the Royal Court.

The reasons the system takes this shape are customary rather than functional:

- **Norman customary law inheritance** — real-property transfer in Jersey has always been a public Court act, not a private deed. The Royal Court is the repository of property records.
- **Publicity of transfer** — that a contract has passed is public (Acts of Court + Public Registry), giving certainty about title transitions, though the specific terms (e.g. price) remain private. This is materially different from English conveyancing, where the transaction is private until completion and only the buyer's identity and limited terms appear on the Land Registry afterwards.
- **Statutory anchor** — the modern framework around hypothecs and real-property transfer sits in the **Loi (1880) sur la propriété foncière**, which codifies aspects of the customary system but did not displace the Court-passing requirement.

In practice, **Jersey advocates act as attorneys** for absent buyers and sellers under powers of attorney drafted for the purpose, which is what makes the system workable for non-resident parties. Possession typically passes the same day, with post-completion steps (Public Registry recording, hypothec registration if a lender is involved) following.
