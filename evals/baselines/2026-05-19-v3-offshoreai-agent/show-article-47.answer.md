## Naming — Article 47B is the *interpretation* article

A small but important clarification before the procedure: in the Trusts (Jersey) Law 1984, **Article 47B is the *definition* provision** for the mistake regime — it tells you what counts as a "mistake" — not the operative set-aside provision. The set-aside powers themselves sit at:

- **Article 47E** — set-aside of a *transfer or disposition of property to a trust* made under mistake (settlor-side cases).
- **Article 47G** — set-aside of an *exercise of a fiduciary power* made under mistake (trustee-side cases — the one your question is really about).

Articles 47C, 47D, 47F and 47H handle standing, consequences, limitations and third-party effects. The whole **47B–47H** cluster was inserted by the **Trusts (Amendment No. 6) (Jersey) Law 2013**. ([`jersey/trusts/article-47-set-aside.md`](jersey/trusts/article-47-set-aside.md))

## The Article 47B definition of "mistake"

Article 47B is doing the heavy lifting because it defines "mistake" deliberately widely. The defined ground covers a mistake as to:

- the **effect** of the transaction;
- its **consequences** (including, expressly, its **tax consequences**);
- any **advantage** the person believed they would obtain; or
- any **fact existing before or at the time of** the disposition or power-exercise.

Testamentary dispositions are excluded from the "transfer to a trust" limb. ([`jersey/trusts/article-47-set-aside.md`](jersey/trusts/article-47-set-aside.md))

## Procedure to set aside a trustee decision (Article 47G)

1. **Identify the right gateway.** A trustee decision is an exercise of a fiduciary power → Article 47G. (If the question is really about unwinding the original settlement, the right gateway is 47E.)
2. **Confirm standing.** A trustee, protector, beneficiary or other appropriate person may apply.
3. **Issue a Representation in the Royal Court (Samedi Division)** supported by **affidavit evidence** going to the state of mind at the time of the decision.
4. **Satisfy the Royal Court's three-limb test** (per *In re S & T Trusts* [2015] JRC259):
   - (a) there was a mistake within the wide Article 47B definition;
   - (b) but for the mistake, the power-exercise would not have been made in the form it was; and
   - (c) the mistake was of so serious a character that it would be **just** for the Court to make a declaration.
5. **Marshal contemporaneous documentary evidence** — file notes, tax advice received, e-mail correspondence. A reconstruction affidavit years later is weak.
6. **Plead foreign tax consequences carefully.** A Jersey declaration is necessary but not sufficient — UK HMRC has historically accepted Jersey set-aside as restoring the *status quo ante* for IHT/CGT; the US IRS position is more fact-dependent.
7. **Effect of a declaration.** The power-exercise is declared **voidable**; the Court typically unwinds it *ab initio*, subject to protection for bona fide third parties for value. ([`jersey/trusts/article-47-set-aside.md`](jersey/trusts/article-47-set-aside.md))

The most common failure point in practice is limb 2 — the applicant can usually show they would have done *something different*, but not that they would have done *nothing*.

## How Jersey is wider than *Pitt v Holt*

The English Supreme Court in ***Pitt v Holt; Futter v Futter* [2013] UKSC 26** drew a line between:

- a **mistake** (an erroneous belief about a relevant fact or law), which can ground rescission; and
- **mere causative ignorance** or **inadequate deliberation**, which generally cannot.

The Royal Court has held that, under the **statutory** Jersey regime, that distinction does not apply: when the Court is asked under Article 47E (or 47G) whether there was "any mistake", **the *manner* of the mistake is irrelevant**, and it is inappropriate to import the English categorisations. The result is a materially **wider gateway** than *Pitt v Holt*:

- **Tax-consequence mistakes** are expressly within Article 47B and are routinely unwound — even where the underlying purpose of the transaction was tax-driven (a feature the English courts have treated as a disqualifying factor in some cases).
- **Causative ignorance / inadequate deliberation** can qualify in Jersey if the three-limb test is met; it generally cannot in England post-*Pitt v Holt*.
- The third limb ("just" to grant relief) gives the Royal Court an **equitable balancing discretion** that operates more flexibly than the English "unconscionability" gate.

There are limits — see ***In the Matter of the M Settlement*** (Royal Court declined a partial rescission that amounted to re-writing history) and the Walkers commentary noting reluctance to treat a disappointed expectation of a *future* tax advantage (as opposed to an unforeseen *charge*) as a qualifying mistake. ([`jersey/trusts/article-47-set-aside.md`](jersey/trusts/article-47-set-aside.md))
