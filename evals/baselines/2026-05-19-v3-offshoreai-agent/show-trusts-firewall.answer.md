## How the Article 9 firewall handles foreign forced-heirship

**The headline rule.** Article 9 of the Trusts (Jersey) Law 1984 directs the Royal Court to apply **Jersey law — and only Jersey law** — to questions about the validity of a Jersey trust, the capacity of the settlor, the trust's interpretation and administration, the powers of the trustees, and the beneficial rights under it. No rule of foreign law may affect those questions (Article 9(1)). (`jersey/trusts/firewall.md`)

**Express disregard of forced heirship.** Article 9(2) is the operative anti-forced-heirship lever: the Jersey-law determination proceeds *without consideration of whether the trust avoids or defeats rights, claims, or interests conferred by any foreign law*, and without consideration of whether the foreign forum recognises the concept of a trust at all. So a French *réserve héréditaire* claim, an Italian *legittima* claim, or a German *Pflichtteilsergänzung* clawback cannot, before the Royal Court, be used to attack the settlor's lifetime disposition into the Jersey trust. (`jersey/trusts/firewall.md`)

**Foreign judgments.** Article 9(4) closes the back door: a foreign judgment that is inconsistent with Article 9 — e.g., a civilian-court judgment ordering restoration of the *réserve* out of trust assets — is unenforceable in Jersey. (`jersey/trusts/firewall.md`)

**Renvoi blocked.** Article 9(3A) disapplies Jersey's other conflict-of-laws rules to Article 9 questions, so a forced-heirship claimant cannot argue that Jersey conflicts rules send the question back to the deceased's domiciliary law. (`jersey/trusts/firewall.md`)

**Important carve-outs.**
- **Jersey-domiciled settlors stay caught.** Article 9(3) preserves Jersey's own *légitime* against a Jersey-domiciled settlor — the firewall is for **foreign** forced heirship only, not Jersey customary forced heirship. (`jersey/trusts/firewall.md`)
- **Article 9(2A) savings.** The firewall does not cure (i) a settlor who had no ownership/no power to dispose, (ii) local-law formalities for transferring property (notably real estate located abroad), or (iii) testamentary dispositions invalid under the testator's domiciliary law. So it protects lifetime trust dispositions, not invalid wills. (`jersey/trusts/firewall.md`)

**Where the firewall stops biting in the real world.**
- It binds the **Royal Court** and Jersey-located assets — not the foreign forum. If the heir gets *in personam* jurisdiction over a beneficiary or trustee abroad, or if trust assets sit in the forced-heirship jurisdiction, the foreign court can still act there. Practical planning typically pairs the Jersey trust with substantive lifetime planning in the heir's jurisdiction (e.g., a French *pacte de famille*) rather than relying on the firewall alone. (`jersey/trusts/firewall.md`)
- The firewall does not save a **sham**: a trust that fails the Article 2 existence test isn't a trust, so Article 9 never engages. (`jersey/trusts/firewall.md`)
- A residual **Jersey public-policy** override remains in principle, though rarely invoked. (`jersey/trusts/firewall.md`)
