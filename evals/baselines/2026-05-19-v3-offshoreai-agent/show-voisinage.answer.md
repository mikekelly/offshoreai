# Voisinage and your neighbour problem

## What voisinage is

**Voisinage** is a Jersey **Norman customary-law** doctrine governing the relationship between neighbouring landowners. It requires each owner to use their property in a way that does not cause **unreasonable harm** to neighbours, and it sits alongside (but is distinct from) the English tort of nuisance. The Royal Court still applies it in modern cases (`jersey/property/voisinage.md`).

## Does it apply to your problem?

I can't tell from the question alone — I don't know what your neighbour is doing. Voisinage is potentially engaged whenever a neighbour's use of their land causes you harm that **exceeds the ordinary inconveniences of neighbourhood** and lacks adequate justification. The corpus lists these as typical claim categories (`jersey/property/voisinage.md`):

- Excessive noise
- Smells (livestock, kitchens, refuse)
- Smoke or fumes
- Vibrations from machinery or construction
- Surface water / drainage problems
- Tree roots and overhanging branches
- Light obstruction
- Commercial-residential interface issues (restaurants, late-night activity)
- Inter-unit noise/smells in apartments

If your problem fits one of those buckets and the interference is more than a trivial annoyance, voisinage is at least plausibly in play. If it is something else — a boundary dispute, an encroachment, breach of a servitude, a planning breach — a different doctrine probably fits better, and the file cross-references `boundary-disputes.md`, `servitudes.md`, and `../contract/delit-tort.md`.

## Threshold you'd need to meet

To make out a voisinage claim, the corpus says you would need (`jersey/property/voisinage.md`):

1. **Harm** that is objectively identifiable;
2. **Caused by** the neighbour's use of their property;
3. **Exceeding** the ordinary inconveniences of neighbourhood; and
4. **Without** adequate justification.

Two things worth knowing because they often surprise people:

- **Fault is not required.** Voisinage can be objective — unlike some formulations of English nuisance, you do not necessarily have to show the neighbour was at fault, only that the use is unreasonable in its effect (`jersey/property/voisinage.md`).
- **Planning permission is not a defence by itself.** Even if your neighbour has a planning consent for the activity, that does not exclude a voisinage claim, though regulatory compliance can be evidence the use is reasonable (`jersey/property/voisinage.md`).

## Defences you should expect

The neighbour will likely run one or more of (`jersey/property/voisinage.md`):

- **Reasonable use** of their property;
- **Consent or acquiescence** (e.g., you knew and didn't object for years);
- **Statutory authority** for the activity;
- **Prescription** (limited);
- **Pre-existing** state (limited — "I was here first" is weak on its own).

## Remedies

The Royal Court can grant an **injunction** restraining the activity, **damages**, and orders for **specific preventative measures** (`jersey/property/voisinage.md`).

## Practical next steps

The corpus's own practical guidance is (`jersey/property/voisinage.md`):

1. **Document** the interference — logs, photos, audio, video, dates and times.
2. **Build a correspondence trail** with the neighbour so the court can see attempts at resolution.
3. **Get expert evidence early** if relevant — acoustic, environmental, structural.
4. **Try mediation first.** Voisinage litigation runs through the Royal Court, which is expensive, and mediation is commonly used as the first step.
5. **Instruct a Jersey advocate** familiar with voisinage — the doctrine has customary-law roots that an English-trained lawyer applying English nuisance reasoning will misread. The corpus explicitly flags "assuming English nuisance test applies wholesale" as a top pitfall.
