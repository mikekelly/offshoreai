On **15 April 2026**, the **Residential Tenancy (Jersey) Amendment Law 2025** came into force, introducing the most significant overhaul of Jersey residential-tenancy law in over a decade.

## Headline changes

- **Fixed terms capped at 3 years.** The initial fixed term of a residential tenancy may not exceed 3 years, and successive fixed terms are prohibited — expired fixed-term tenancies either end outright or convert automatically to periodic tenancies (down from the prior 9-year ceiling).
- **Tenant exit on 1 month's notice.** A tenant may terminate any residential tenancy on one month's written notice, regardless of any fixed term.
- **Landlord termination — justified or 12 months.** A landlord ending a periodic tenancy must either rely on a permitted ground (breach, sale, owner-occupation, redevelopment — 3 months' notice) or give 12 months' no-fault notice. The pre-amendment 3-month no-fault route is gone.
- **Rent caps and the once-yearly rule.** Rent may be increased only once per year, by no more than Jersey RPI, with at least 2 months' written notice — unless rent is "significantly below market rent" (a narrow, challengeable carve-out).
- **New Rent Tribunal.** An independent Rent Tribunal hears disputes over RPI breaches, the below-market carve-out, the once-yearly rule, and notice compliance. It does **not** cover social housing rent, employment-linked accommodation, or eviction/breach (those stay with the Petty Debts Court).
- **Strengthened written-form rules.** Tenancy agreements must clearly identify all chargeable fees; hidden fees are unenforceable and may constitute an offence.

## Transitional treatment (15 April 2026)

- New tenancies: full post-amendment regime.
- Pre-existing **periodic** tenancies: convert immediately to the new regime.
- Pre-existing **fixed-term** tenancies: continue under pre-amendment law until expiry, then post-amendment rules apply.
- Successive-fixed-term clauses: unenforceable at expiry of the current term.

## What did *not* change

The Petty Debts Court's exclusive jurisdiction over eviction/breach, Viscount enforcement of eviction orders, the 2014 condition-report regime, the Tenants' Deposit Scheme (substantively), the lodging-house exclusion, and the 9-year ceiling above which a lease becomes a Royal-Court contract of lease.
