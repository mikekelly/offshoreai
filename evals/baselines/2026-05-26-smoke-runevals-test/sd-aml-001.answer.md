## Article 19 vs Article 21 — Terrorism (Jersey) Law 2002 SAR duties

The two offences cover the same conduct — failing to disclose suspected terrorism-financing offences under Articles 15, 16, or 16A to the FIU as soon as reasonably practicable — but apply different fault tests to different populations.

**Article 19 — general duty.** Applies to anyone who acquires the information "in the course of a trade, profession, business or employment". The fault standard is **subjective**: the person must actually **believe or suspect** that another has committed an Article 15/16/16A offence. A reasonable-excuse defence is available.
- Citation: `knowledge/jersey/aml-cft/terrorism-article-19.md`

**Article 21 — financial-institution failure-to-disclose.** Applies to persons carrying on **regulated business** as defined in Schedule 2 to POCL 1999. The fault standard adds an **objective limb**: the person commits the offence if they knew, suspected, **or had reasonable grounds for knowing or suspecting**. This catches **negligent** failure to spot suspicion that Article 19 (subjective only) would not reach. Reasonable excuse, including legal-professional-privilege carve-out, is available.
- Citation: `knowledge/jersey/aml-cft/terrorism-article-21.md`

**The practical difference.** Article 19 is the CFT analogue of the POCL Article 34A general SAR duty; Article 21 is the CFT analogue of the regulated-sector "should have known" enhancement that drives the JFSC AML/CFT Handbook's CDD, transaction-monitoring, and SAR disciplines. For a regulated MLRO, Article 21 is the operative trigger because it converts process failures into offences without requiring proof of subjective suspicion.
