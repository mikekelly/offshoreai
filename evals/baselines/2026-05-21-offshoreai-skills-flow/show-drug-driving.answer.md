**Today (May 2026): an impairment-based regime.** Driving while impaired by drugs is an offence under the Road Traffic (Jersey) Law 1956. Prosecution turns on proving the driver was actually *unfit to drive* because of drug use, on objective evidence (poor control, failed field sobriety tests, plus drugs in a blood/urine sample). Critically, Jersey has **no statutory drug-concentration limits, no per se "over the limit" offence, and no roadside drug-swab powers** — so the evidential test is harder than drink-driving's mechanical BAC rule (`knowledge/jersey/road-traffic/drug-driving.md`).

**Scope and penalties.** The impairment offence is substance-neutral — controlled drugs, prescription medication, OTC medicines, and drug/alcohol combinations all count, and "I have a prescription" is no defence to genuine impairment. Sentencing tracks drink-driving: mandatory disqualification (typically 12+ months first offence), a fine up to £2,000, up to 6 months' imprisonment, and licence endorsement (`knowledge/jersey/road-traffic/drug-driving.md`).

**What's coming.** In **February 2026**, States representatives proposed legislation to introduce statutory drug limits (controlled and some prescription substances), roadside swab-testing powers (UK-style drug-wipes), per se offences, and mandatory disqualification on conviction. This is **not yet enacted** — the States Assembly timetable suggests possible introduction during 2026 or 2027, and until then the impairment regime continues to apply (`knowledge/jersey/road-traffic/drug-driving.md`).
