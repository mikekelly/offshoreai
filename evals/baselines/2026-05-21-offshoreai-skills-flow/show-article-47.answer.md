## What "Article 47B" actually is

A small but important correction to the framing. **Article 47B is the *interpretation* provision** — it supplies the wide statutory definition of "mistake" for the whole regime. The Article that actually sets aside a *trustee's decision* (an exercise of a fiduciary power) is **Article 47G**. The cluster, all introduced by the **Trusts (Amendment No. 6) (Jersey) Law 2013**, runs:

- **47B** — interpretation, including the wide definition of "mistake"
- **47E** — set-aside of a *transfer of property to* a trust (the settlor-facing route)
- **47G** — set-aside of an *exercise of a fiduciary power* (the trustee-decision route)
- **47C, 47D, 47F, 47H** — standing, consequences, limits on relief, third-party position

(`knowledge/jersey/trusts/article-47-set-aside.md`)

## The Article 47B definition of "mistake"

Deliberately broad. It covers a mistake as to the transaction's **effect**, its **consequences** (expressly including tax consequences), any **advantage** the person believed they would gain, and any **fact existing before or at the time** of the disposition or power-exercise. Testamentary dispositions are excluded. (`knowledge/jersey/trusts/article-47-set-aside.md`, Art. 47B)

## The procedure to set aside a trustee decision (Art. 47G)

- **Vehicle:** application by **Representation** in the Royal Court, Samedi Division, supported by affidavit evidence of the relevant state of mind at the time of the decision.
- **Applicant:** typically the **trustee** (a protector, beneficiary, or other person with standing can also apply).
- **Test:** the Royal Court applies a **three-limb test** (formulated in *In re S & T Trusts* [2015] JRC259) before granting relief under 47E or 47G:
  1. there was a mistake within the wide Art. 47B definition;
  2. **but for** the mistake, the power would not have been exercised in the form it was;
  3. the mistake was **of so serious a character that it would be just** for the Court to make a declaration.
  Failure on any limb defeats the application — limb 2 is the most common point of failure.
- **Effect:** the Court declares the exercise **voidable**; relief unwinds the power-exercise and restores the prior position (ordinarily void *ab initio*, though the Court has flexibility), with good-faith-for-value third-party rights generally protected.
- **Evidence point:** contemporaneous documentary evidence (file notes, advice, correspondence) is critical; a late reconstruction affidavit is weak.

(`knowledge/jersey/trusts/article-47-set-aside.md`, Arts. 47G, 47B; procedure cross-refers `knowledge/jersey/legal-system/royal-court.md`)

## How Jersey is wider than *Pitt v Holt*

In *Pitt v Holt; Futter v Futter* [2013] UKSC 26 the English Supreme Court distinguished a true **"mistake"** (which can ground rescission) from **"mere causative ignorance" or "inadequate deliberation"** (which generally cannot). Under the statutory regime the Royal Court of Jersey has **rejected that distinction** — in Jersey "the manner of the mistake is irrelevant," so the English categories don't gate relief. The practical consequence: a future "we didn't think about that" position is more likely to be remediable in the Royal Court than in the English High Court. (`knowledge/jersey/trusts/article-47-set-aside.md`, citing *S & T Trusts* and Royal Court commentary)

Two boundaries the corpus flags: a mistake going to the *very intention to settle* is dealt with under **Article 11**, not 47C–H; and where the remedy sought is *directing* the trustee rather than unwinding a transaction, an **Article 51 directions** application may be the right vehicle. The Court has also declined relief amounting to a "re-writing of history" (*In the Matter of the M Settlement*) and is reluctant to treat a disappointed expectation of a *future* tax advantage as a qualifying mistake.

A caveat in the file worth surfacing: the underlying authority here is largely **secondary commentary** (Mondaq, Carey Olsen, Viberts, Walkers, Ogier) rather than the primary judgments or statute text. Per the source hierarchy, verify the three-limb test and the *Pitt v Holt* divergence against *In re S & T Trusts* [2015] JRC259 and the text of Articles 47B–47H on jerseylaw.je before relying on this.
