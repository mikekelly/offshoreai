Bermuda is the world's leading captive-insurance domicile, and the corpus identifies three reasons it tends to win for a *multinational group* specifically:

1. **Scale and depth** — it is the world's third-largest reinsurance market and the largest catastrophe-reinsurance market, so the underwriting, actuarial, and broker ecosystem to service a large multi-line programme is deepest there.
2. **Solvency II equivalence** — Bermuda's commercial solvency regime (BSCR) is recognised by the EU as equivalent. For a group with EU-cedant exposure this reduces collateral requirements, which Guernsey and Cayman cannot offer (per the cross-jurisdictional map, Bermuda and Isle of Man are the only offshore jurisdictions with Solvency II equivalence).
3. **Segregated structures for multi-line programmes** — a Bermuda Segregated Accounts Company (SAC) lets a group run multiple lines/programmes through one licensed entity with statutory ring-fencing between accounts.

The corpus does flag the trade-offs: higher BMA supervisory scrutiny and real substance requirements (Bermuda offices/staff). Cayman is positioned for *healthcare* captives, and Guernsey (the PCC inventor) for *European-parent* captives wanting an EU-near structure. ## Which Class

For a captive insuring your own group's risks, the relevant general-business (P&C) classes are:

| Class | Fits if… |
|---|---|
| **Class 1** | **Pure single-parent captive** — writes only the risks of the parent / group. This is the typical starting point for a large corporate self-insuring its own risks. |
| **Class 2** | Multi-owner / group or association captive, *or* a single-parent captive that also writes a limited amount of unrelated risk. |
| **Class 3** | Captive writing *significant unrelated* (third-party / commercial) business up to specified thresholds. |

The corpus's guiding rule: **pick the lowest class consistent with your business**, because minimum capital, solvency, reporting, audit, and BMA engagement all rise sharply through the scale, and misclassification risks regulatory action.

So for a standard multinational self-insuring its own group risks, **Class 1** is the natural fit. You move to **Class 2** if the captive is multi-parent (e.g. a JV or association) or takes on some unrelated risk, and to **Class 3** only if it writes meaningful third-party business. If you intend to run several distinct programmes through one vehicle, layer a **SAC** on top. (For long-term/life risks the parallel ladder is Class A–E; Class A is the long-term single-parent captive.) (`knowledge/bermuda/insurance-classes.md`)
