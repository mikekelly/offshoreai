## What voisinage is

**Voisinage** is Jersey's Norman customary-law doctrine of "good neighbourhood." It requires every landowner to use their property in a way that does not cause **unreasonable harm** to neighbours — harm that exceeds the ordinary inconveniences of neighbourhood living, without adequate justification. It's preserved in Jersey (and aligned with broader civil-law neighbour-responsibility doctrines via Pothier) even though most modern systems abandoned it, and it's applied by the Royal Court (`knowledge/jersey/property/voisinage.md`).

Critically, it is **not** English nuisance, even though it often produces similar results. Two differences matter for your situation:
- **Voisinage can apply without fault** — it's objective, so you don't necessarily have to prove the neighbour was careless.
- There's **no fixed list** of "smell, smoke, noise" categories — a broader range of activities can be caught (`knowledge/jersey/property/voisinage.md`).

## Does it apply to your neighbour problem?

I can't tell you without knowing what the problem actually is — and the corpus doesn't let me advise on your specific facts. But the doctrine **typically covers**: excessive noise, smells (livestock, kitchens), smoke/fumes, vibrations from machinery or construction, surface-water/drainage issues, tree roots/overhanging branches, and light obstruction (`knowledge/jersey/property/voisinage.md`). If your complaint resembles one of those, voisinage is a plausible route.

The test the Royal Court applies is whether there is (1) objectively identifiable **harm**, (2) **caused by** the neighbour's use of their property, (3) **exceeding** ordinary neighbourhood inconvenience, (4) **without** adequate justification (`knowledge/jersey/property/voisinage.md`).

A few things worth knowing before you act:
- **Planning permission is not a defence** — the fact your neighbour has consent for what they're doing does not exclude a voisinage claim (though regulatory compliance can be *evidence* of reasonable use). Statutory authority, the claimant's consent/acquiescence, and reasonable use are the recognised defences (`knowledge/jersey/property/voisinage.md`).
- A valid **servitude (easement)** may permit the activity and won't be overridden by voisinage — but *excessive* use of a servitude can still breach it (`knowledge/jersey/property/voisinage.md`).
- **Remedies** the Royal Court can grant: an injunction to stop the activity, damages, or an order for preventative measures (`knowledge/jersey/property/voisinage.md`).

## Practical first steps

The corpus recommends: document the interference (logs, photos, recordings); build a correspondence trail with the neighbour; **mediate as a first step** where possible; engage experts early (acoustic, environmental) if relevant; and instruct a Jersey advocate familiar with voisinage before litigating via an Order of Justice — Royal Court costs are easy to underestimate (`knowledge/jersey/property/voisinage.md`).
