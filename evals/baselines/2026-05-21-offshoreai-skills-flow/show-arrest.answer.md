## Your rights if arrested in Jersey

Your rights are governed by the **Police Procedures and Criminal Evidence (Jersey) Law 2003 (PPCE 2003)** and its Codes of Practice. The headline rights:

**1. Free legal advice — the most important one.** You have an absolute right to consult a solicitor or advocate, free of charge, 24/7 through the duty-solicitor scheme, in private (privileged), and *before any interview*. This can only be delayed in very serious cases (e.g. evidence-preservation in the most serious offences) on specific statutory grounds authorised at a senior level — routine delay is not permitted. If you decline initially and change your mind mid-interview, questioning must stop. **Practical advice from the corpus: ask for a solicitor immediately and don't answer substantive questions until you've spoken to one.**

**2. Right to inform someone.** One person (family, friend, or other) can be told of your arrest and location, with the police making the call at public expense, as soon as practicable. Delayable only on specific grounds in serious cases.

**3. Consular notification** if you are a foreign national.

**4. Right to silence.** The caution is: *"You are not obliged to say anything unless you wish to do so, but what you say may be put into writing and given in evidence."* You may say nothing, answer selectively, or give a prepared statement. Notably, adverse inferences from silence at police-station interview are narrower in Jersey than in post-2003 England.

**5. Custody record** — a written notice of your rights on arrival, and a copy of the full custody log on release. Keep it; it is critical evidence for any later challenge.

**6. Other safeguards** — an interpreter if needed (at public expense); an appropriate adult if you are a juvenile under 18 or vulnerable; medical attention; and adequate physical conditions including at least 8 continuous hours' rest in any 24-hour period.

**Detention limits:**

| Stage | Limit | Authorised by |
|---|---|---|
| Initial | 24 hours | Custody officer |
| Extension (serious offences) | up to 36 hours | Police inspector |
| Warrant of further detention | up to 96 hours total | Royal Court (Magistrate) |
| Beyond 96 hours | charge or release required |

Detention reviews occur at prescribed intervals (typically every 6 hours initially), and detention beyond these limits is unlawful.

**Interviews** must be audio-recorded (Code E), preceded by the caution each session, free of oppression, with your solicitor present if requested.

**If a right is breached:** note the details, tell your solicitor at the first opportunity, complain through formal channels after release, and seek exclusion of improperly obtained evidence at trial (a civil claim is possible for serious breaches).
