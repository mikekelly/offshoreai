## Do you need planning permission for a rear extension?

**Probably yes — unless the extension is modest enough to fall fully within "permitted development" under the Planning and Building (General Development) (Jersey) Order 2011 ("GD Order 2011").**

**The decision sequence:**

1. **Check the GD Order 2011 limits.** A *single-storey rear extension* is one of the categories the Order can grant automatic permission for, but only subject to specific numeric conditions — maximum floor area added, maximum projection from the original rear wall, maximum height and eaves height, distance from boundaries, materials matching the existing dwelling, and no projection in front of the main building line. A small single-storey rear extension on a typical Jersey dwelling is often within these limits. (`knowledge/jersey/planning/permitted-development.md`)

2. **A two-storey rear extension of any size, or a single-storey one exceeding the depth/area/height thresholds, needs a full planning application** under the Planning and Building (Jersey) Law 2002. The most common mistake is assuming a "modest" extension is automatically permitted when the area/height/position actually exceeds the Order's limits. (`knowledge/jersey/use-cases/jersey-resident/extension-planning-permission.md`)

3. **Building approval is always separate.** Even if planning permission isn't required, you still need building approval under the Building Bye-Laws 2007 (structure, insulation, fire, drainage). Permitted-development cover only relieves you of the planning application, not the building consent. (`knowledge/jersey/planning/permitted-development.md`)

**Things that change the analysis substantially** — and usually remove permitted-development cover, so even a small extension needs an application:
- **Listed building** — almost everything needs consent, including internal works;
- **Conservation area** — exemptions are much narrower;
- **Green Zone / Coastal National Park** — restricted to protect landscape;
- A **Minister's Article 31 direction** can remove permitted-development rights in a specific location.

(`knowledge/jersey/planning/permitted-development.md`; `knowledge/jersey/use-cases/jersey-resident/extension-planning-permission.md`)

**Two practical safeguards:**
- **If borderline, apply for a Certificate of Lawful Development** — a lower-fee route that confirms your proposal falls within permitted development and protects against future enforcement and sale/refinancing problems.
- **Check your title for restrictive covenants** — private-law restrictions can prohibit works even where planning would permit them (`knowledge/jersey/property/restrictive-covenants.md`).

**If you build without the necessary permission**, unauthorised development is a criminal offence under the 2002 Law: enforcement notices requiring removal at your cost, fines, and knock-on mortgage/insurance/sale problems.
