## The short version

These three regimes solve **three different problems**. They are not really competitors so much as tools for different jobs.

| Pick… | When the central problem is… |
|---|---|
| **Jersey trust** | Holding/transmitting family wealth with **maximum defence against foreign forced-heirship, matrimonial, and creditor attack** — plus settlor who wants to retain control |
| **Cayman STAR** | You need a trust with **no beneficiaries** — a pure-purpose vehicle (orphan SPV, PTC holding, family-governance council) |
| **BVI VISTA** | You're settling shares of an **operating company** into trust and want the **founder's chosen directors to run it without trustee interference** |

## Jersey trust — the forced-heirship and reserved-powers default

Use Jersey when the goal is a conventional (usually discretionary) family wealth wrapper and the dominant risk is a cross-border attack on the structure. The **Article 9 firewall** instructs the Royal Court to apply only Jersey law to validity, capacity, administration and beneficial-rights questions, and to refuse effect to foreign forced-heirship, matrimonial-regime and clawback rules — Article 9(4) also makes inconsistent foreign judgments unenforceable in Jersey (`knowledge/jersey/trusts/firewall.md`). It's the gold-standard firewall in the corpus's comparison (`knowledge/CROSS-JURISDICTIONAL-MAP.md`). Pair it with **Article 9A reserved powers** when a first-generation settlor wants to retain revocation, investment-direction or distribution powers without invalidating the trust or being deemed a trustee — the key enabler for US-grantor-trust and patriarch-controlled family-office structures (`knowledge/jersey/trusts/reserved-powers.md`).

What Jersey does *not* do well: it cannot, on its own, disengage the trustee from a held operating business — the trustee retains *Bartlett*-style monitoring duties unless negated by deed, which is contractual rather than statutory (`knowledge/bvi/vista-trusts.md`); and it has no no-beneficiary purpose-trust analogue to STAR for arbitrary purposes (Jersey's Article 12 purpose trusts are narrower in use).

## Cayman STAR — when there are no beneficiaries

Use STAR (Special Trusts — Alternative Regime, Special Trusts (Alternative Regime) Act 1997) when you need a **non-charitable purpose trust**: no beneficiaries required, enforcement by a mandatory **enforcer**, wide permitted purposes, unlimited duration (`knowledge/cayman/star-trusts.md`). The classic uses are **orphan/bankruptcy-remote SPVs** in structured finance, **private-trust-company (PTC) holding** structures, and **multi-generational family-governance councils** where you don't want identifiable beneficiaries. STAR removes the *beneficiary* requirement — that is its distinct job.

## BVI VISTA — when the trust holds an operating business

Use VISTA (Virgin Islands Special Trusts Act 2003) when settling shares of a **designated BVI company** into trust and the founder wants chosen directors (often family) to run the business **without the trustee second-guessing them**. VISTA statutorily disengages the trustee from the *Bartlett* duty to monitor and intervene in the company's affairs; control is governed by mandatory **office of director rules (ODRs)** in the trust instrument (`knowledge/bvi/vista-trusts.md`). It's purpose-built for **family-business succession** and **single-asset/illiquid private-company holdings**, and it gives professional trustees a statutory basis to accept operating-company shares they'd otherwise decline.

Watch-outs from the corpus: VISTA is **less robust than Jersey/Cayman on cross-border forced-heirship protection**; ODR drafting errors are the most common source of disputes; and re-domiciling the designated company out of BVI can forfeit VISTA protection (`knowledge/bvi/vista-trusts.md`).

## They can combine

These aren't mutually exclusive. A Cayman STAR purpose trust can sit at the top of a structure holding a PTC, while VISTA solves the operating-company-shares layer, and a Jersey trust acts as the central wealth wrapper for the family's diversified assets — the corpus's "classic mix" for international wealth uses several domiciles at once (`knowledge/CROSS-JURISDICTIONAL-MAP.md`).
