In Jersey, **real-property transfers are not private completions — they are passed before the Royal Court**. The buyer and seller (or, far more commonly, their lawyers acting as attorneys under powers of attorney) appear before the **Samedi Division** of the Royal Court; the contract is read or referenced, the Greffier signs and records it, and title passes by that Court act at that moment (`knowledge/jersey/property/contract-passing.md`). This is a **Norman customary-law inheritance** — the Royal Court has historically been the public repository of property records, and passing a contract in open court gives certainty and public visibility to the change of title (the fact and parties are public via the Acts of Court and Public Registry, though the price is typically kept private). This is structurally different from English conveyancing, which is largely private (`knowledge/jersey/property/contract-passing.md`).

## The "Friday afternoon" part — a caveat

The traditional rhythm of Jersey conveyancing — that property contracts are passed on **Friday afternoons** — is referenced in the corpus's cross-jurisdictional map as a distinctive Jersey feature (`knowledge/CROSS-JURISDICTIONAL-MAP.md`, "Royal Court Friday-afternoon property passings"). However, the dedicated contract-passing file states only that the Samedi Division sits "most weekdays" and routinely passes property contracts; **it does not itself document the specific Friday-afternoon convention** (`knowledge/jersey/property/contract-passing.md`). So the corpus confirms the *court-passing* mechanism and the *Friday* tradition exists, but the precise scheduling reason is not spelled out in the doctrinal file — you may want to verify the current sitting practice directly.
