## What a Parish Hall Enquiry is

A **Parish Hall Enquiry** is a distinctively Jersey **pre-court forum**, presided over by a **Centenier** — an elected, unpaid honorary police officer of the parish where the alleged offence took place. It is the route by which **most routine motoring offences are resolved without ever reaching the Magistrate's Court**. There is no direct equivalent in the UK or other common-law systems; it blends prosecutorial and limited adjudicative roles in one parish official (`knowledge/jersey/road-traffic/parish-hall-enquiries.md`).

At the enquiry the Centenier reviews the evidence, decides whether there is a case and whether the public interest requires court, and can then **caution**, issue a **written fine of up to £200 per offence**, or **refer the matter to the Magistrate's Court**. Crucially, the Centenier can only fine or caution if you **admit the offence and accept the proposed penalty** — if you dispute it, the case must go to court (same file).

## When your case goes there

Routine, lower-level motoring matters are typical Parish Hall fare — for example:

- minor speeding (under 15 mph over the limit)
- mobile phone offences
- failing to display an insurance disc
- some traffic-light and minor careless-driving cases
- parking offences

The route is **excluded** (referred straight to Magistrate's Court) for the more serious matters, including:

- speeding **15 mph or more** over the limit
- drink-driving or drug-driving (any reading)
- dangerous driving (Article 23 RTJL 1956)
- causing death or serious injury by driving
- driving while disqualified
- **any offence carrying mandatory disqualification**, or where the Centenier judges the £200 ceiling inadequate (same file).

## Two things worth knowing

You have a **right to opt out**: you can decline to admit the offence and **demand the case be heard at the Magistrate's Court** instead — before or after the Parish Hall outcome — and you can bring a friend, family member, or advocate. But note a Parish Hall outcome is still recorded and **can lead to licence endorsement**, even though it does not formally count as a criminal conviction for most purposes (same file).
