## Jersey trust vs Cayman STAR vs BVI VISTA — picking the right tool

These three vehicles do not compete on the same axis. Choose by **the structural problem you're trying to solve**, not by jurisdiction preference.

### Jersey trust — the cross-border wealth-planning default

A Jersey trust under the **Trusts (Jersey) Law 1984** is the go-to where the planning problem is **forced-heirship defence + settlor control + jurisprudential certainty**:

- **Article 9 firewall**: the Royal Court applies Jersey law (and only Jersey law) to validity, capacity, administration and beneficial-rights questions, and refuses to enforce foreign judgments inconsistent with that — the strongest statutory firewall in the offshore world (`knowledge/jersey/trusts/firewall.md`).
- **Article 9A reserved powers**: a settlor can reserve revocation, directive, appointment/removal, investment, and change-of-proper-law powers without invalidating the trust, and a trustee acting on a reserved-power instruction does not breach trust (`knowledge/jersey/trusts/reserved-powers.md`).

Use Jersey when the settlor is from a civil-law / forced-heirship jurisdiction, when US-grantor-trust treatment is desired, when there is litigation risk on attack-the-trust grounds, or when the structure needs the deepest body of trust case law (`knowledge/CROSS-JURISDICTIONAL-MAP.md` — "Maximum forced-heirship defence: Jersey (Article 9(4))").

### Cayman STAR — the non-charitable purpose trust

A **STAR trust** under the **Special Trusts (Alternative Regime) Act 1997** is a **purpose trust with no beneficiaries**, enforced by a mandatory **enforcer**, capable of holding any lawful purpose (not just charitable) for unlimited duration (`knowledge/cayman/star-trusts.md`).

Use STAR when the problem is that **classical trust law needs beneficiaries** and you don't want any:

- **Orphan SPVs** in securitisation / structured-finance transactions (the classic use — bankruptcy-remote, no consolidation);
- **PTC (Private Trust Company) share ownership** — STAR trust holds the PTC's shares so the PTC has no settlor / no beneficiaries;
- **Family-governance councils** with explicit purposes (education, philanthropy, property maintenance) rather than beneficiary entitlements;
- **Charitable-like purposes** that fail strict charitable status but still need a purpose vehicle.

(`knowledge/CROSS-JURISDICTIONAL-MAP.md` — "Orphan SPV / no-beneficiary purpose trust: Cayman STAR".)

### BVI VISTA — the operating-business share-ownership trust

A **VISTA trust** under the **Virgin Islands Special Trusts Act 2003** is a **share-ownership trust that disengages the trustee from the *Bartlett v Barclays Bank* duty** to monitor and intervene in the underlying company's affairs (`knowledge/bvi/vista-trusts.md`). Mandatory **office of director rules** (ODRs) specify who appoints / removes directors; the trustee holds legal title but does not second-guess management.

Use VISTA when the problem is **family-business succession with disengaged trustee**:

- Founder wants to settle shares of an **operating company** into trust;
- Chosen directors (often family members) must continue to run the business without trustee interference;
- The classical-trust-law duty to monitor management performance would be commercially destructive.

(`knowledge/CROSS-JURISDICTIONAL-MAP.md` — "Family-business succession with disengaged trustee: BVI VISTA".)

### The decision in one sentence each

- **Forced-heirship / wealth-planning wrapper with deep case law and reserved-powers flexibility?** → Jersey.
- **No-beneficiary purpose vehicle (orphan SPV, PTC parent, family-governance council)?** → Cayman STAR.
- **Operating-business shares with founder-chosen management you don't want the trustee second-guessing?** → BVI VISTA.

The three are **commonly combined** in the same family structure (e.g., a Jersey trust as the central wealth wrapper, with a BVI VISTA holding the family operating company and a Cayman STAR holding the PTC) — see the cross-jurisdictional planning pattern at `knowledge/CROSS-JURISDICTIONAL-MAP.md` ("Where should I plan my international wealth?").
