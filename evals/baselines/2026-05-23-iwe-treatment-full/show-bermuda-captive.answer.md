## Why Bermuda

Bermuda is the **world's leading captive-insurance domicile** and the largest offshore market by vehicle count, with a deep professional infrastructure (insurance managers, actuaries, lawyers, BMA supervision), **Solvency II equivalence**, and **NAIC qualified-jurisdiction** status — which matters for an insurer dealing with EU or US-domiciled parents or counterparties (`knowledge/bermuda/insurance.md`; `knowledge/CROSS-JURISDICTIONAL-MAP.md`). On the cross-jurisdictional matrix it is positioned as the **world's leading (re)insurance market** with the Insurance Act 1978 class system specifically designed to give pure captives a light-touch tier and commercial reinsurers a Solvency II-equivalent tier in the same statute (`knowledge/CROSS-JURISDICTIONAL-MAP.md`).

## Which Class

For a **multinational group's pure single-parent captive** writing only the group's own risks, the corpus's decision framework points to **Class 1**:

> "Pure single-parent captive → **Bermuda Class 1** or Guernsey PCC cell" (`knowledge/CROSS-JURISDICTIONAL-MAP.md`)

The class definition in the substantive file confirms: **Class 1 = pure single-parent captive, only writes risks of parent / group** — the lowest regulatory tier with the lightest minimum capital, solvency, reporting and BMA-supervisory burden (`knowledge/bermuda/insurance-classes.md`). The corpus's guidance is that sponsors pick "the lowest class consistent with their business to minimise regulatory overhead" — so Class 1 is correct unless the business intent goes beyond pure related-party risks.

Two variants to be aware of:

- **Class 2** — if the captive will also write some **limited unrelated / third-party** business, or you bring in other group-affiliated owners (a group / association captive) (`knowledge/bermuda/insurance-classes.md`).
- **Class 3** — if **significant unrelated business** is contemplated (up to specified thresholds) (`knowledge/bermuda/insurance-classes.md`).

If the multinational has **multiple distinct subsidiary risk pools** that you want statutorily ringfenced from each other inside a single licensed entity, the standard Bermuda answer is a **Segregated Accounts Company (SAC)** under the Segregated Accounts Companies Act 2000, with one Class 1 (or 2/3) licence at the SAC level and each programme in its own segregated account — Bermuda SACs are heavily used precisely for multi-programme captive structures (`knowledge/bermuda/segregated-accounts-company.md`).

## Caveats

- Both `knowledge/bermuda/insurance-classes.md` and `knowledge/bermuda/insurance.md` are **`status: draft`** in the corpus — drafted but not fully verified against primary sources. Treat the class definitions and minimum-capital figures as orientation; verify against the **Insurance Act 1978** (as amended) and current BMA rules before filing.
- The corpus's Bermuda coverage is explicitly framed as **adjacent-jurisdiction high-level**: "For full Bermuda practice, engage Bermuda counsel" (`knowledge/bermuda/index.md`).
- The corpus does not give a step-by-step formation sequence for a Class 1 captive or current minimum-capital figures — those need to come from BMA primary sources.
