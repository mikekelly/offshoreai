## JFSC civil financial penalties under FSL 1998 — there is no statutory cap

The corpus's answer is that the JFSC's civil-penalty power for FSL breaches (including unauthorised trust company business) has **no statutory upper limit**. The enabling provision is **Article 24 of the Financial Services (Jersey) Law 1998**, with the operational regime set out in the **Civil Financial Penalties (Financial Services Business) (Jersey) Order 2015** made under it.

### Three-band structure (typical ranges, not caps)

- **Band 1** — lesser / procedural breaches: typically £10k–£100k
- **Band 2** — material compliance failures: typically £100k–£500k
- **Band 3** — most serious breaches: typically £500k+, **with no statutory upper limit**

Source: `jersey/financial-regulation/fsl-article-24.md` (Article 24 — Civil Penalties).

### Two important distinctions to keep in mind

1. **Civil vs criminal.** The Article 24 civil regime is what the *JFSC itself* imposes (after a Decision Notice, with appeal to the Royal Court). Unauthorised financial service business is *also* a **criminal offence** under **Article 7**, prosecuted in the courts — substantial fines plus imprisonment up to **7 years** for individuals (`jersey/financial-regulation/fsl-article-7.md`). The criminal route is not a "JFSC-imposed" penalty.
2. **No TCB-specific cap.** The Article 24 regime is class-agnostic — there is no separate ceiling for trust company business as opposed to investment business, FSB, MSB or GIMB.

### Caveats

- Both cited files are **`draft`** status in the corpus (last verified 2026-05-17, two days old). The drafting framework and figures look right against the 2015 Order but have not been editorially elevated to `review`/`stable`. Verify the precise wording of Article 24 and the current 2015 Order on jerseylaw.je before relying on this.
- The "typical" Band 1/2/3 figures are JFSC practice ranges, not statutory bands — the statute and the 2015 Order do not themselves set numerical bands.
