## The wrapper: a Jersey discretionary trust

Use a **discretionary trust under the Trusts (Jersey) Law 1984**, settled by the family with a Jersey-licensed Trust Company Business as trustee. The discretionary form is doing real work here: because no beneficiary holds a fixed entitlement, there is no defined interest an Italian forced heir can attach. It is also well understood by foreign tax authorities and is the form in which the Article 9 firewall is most fully developed (`knowledge/jersey/use-cases/family-office-adviser/wormhole-international-family-wealth.md`; `knowledge/jersey/use-cases/family-office-adviser/worked-example-international-family-wealth.md`, Steps 1–2).

## The forced-heirship defence: Article 9 firewall

The core protection is the **Article 9 firewall**. It directs the Royal Court to apply **Jersey law and only Jersey law** to questions of the trust's validity, the settlor's capacity, administration, and beneficial rights, and to **disregard foreign forced-heirship rules** — including the Italian *legittima* (the indefeasible shares reserved for spouse and children). Critically, **Article 9(4)** makes a foreign judgment inconsistent with Article 9 — e.g. an Italian clawback order against lifetime gifts into the trust — **unenforceable in Jersey** (`knowledge/jersey/trusts/firewall.md`, Article 9(1), (2), (4)).

**Know the limits**, and advise on them explicitly:
- The firewall binds the Jersey court and Jersey-situs assets; it does not control a foreign court acting over foreign-situs assets.
- **Article 9(2A)** means the firewall cannot validate a disposition where the settlor had no power to dispose, cannot override local-law formalities for transferring real estate (relevant to the Caribbean villa and EU shares), and cannot cure an invalid will.
- It does **not** protect against *in personam* orders against Italian-resident beneficiaries personally — only the trust itself (`knowledge/jersey/trusts/firewall.md`, Article 9(2A); worked example "Pitfalls").

In practice, if Italian heirs make claims, the trustee can seek **Article 51 directions** from the Royal Court for an authoritative confirmation of the firewall position.

## Settlor comfort: Article 9A reserved powers

The settlor can **reserve powers** — investment direction, power to add/remove beneficiaries, power to remove and replace trustees, a veto over distributions — **without invalidating the trust**. Jersey also disapplies the customary *donner et retenir ne vaut* rule within the trust context, which historically threatened reserved-powers planning. Pair this with a non-binding **letter of wishes** (`knowledge/jersey/trusts/reserved-powers.md`; `knowledge/jersey/trusts/firewall.md`, Article 9(6); worked example Step 2).

## The asset layer: underlying companies and a US blocker

Hold assets through one or more **underlying holding companies** beneath the trust rather than directly, segregating asset classes and jurisdictions. For the **US public equities**, interpose a **non-US blocker company** (commonly Cayman or BVI): a non-US-domiciled individual holding US-situs assets directly faces US estate tax above only a ~$60,000 threshold, so the trust owns the blocker and the blocker owns the US assets. The Caribbean villa is typically held by a BVI company directly (worked example Steps 3, 7).

## Beneficiary-specific tax: the UK-resident child

One child being UK-resident pulls in UK anti-avoidance: the settlements code, transfer-of-assets-abroad rules, and the IHT relevant-property regime (10-year and exit charges). Excluded-property treatment may assist where a non-UK-domiciled settlor settles non-UK assets, but this turns on current UK rules — **specialist UK tax advice is essential** and sits outside the Jersey corpus (worked example Step 6).

## Reporting — transparency is the operating assumption

The structure is fully reportable, not secret: **CRS** automatic exchange to controlling persons' home jurisdictions (Italy, UK, possibly US); **FATCA** for any US-reportable person; **DAC6** where an EU intermediary triggers a hallmark; and **UK TRS** registration where there is a UK nexus. Italian declarations of the trust by the settlor are also required (`knowledge/jersey/international/crs.md`; `knowledge/jersey/international/fatca.md`; worked example Step 8).

## Optional: Private Trust Company

As governance matures, a **Jersey Private Trust Company** (owned by a purpose trust, with family/advisers on the board, and JFSC exemption/Article 4A status under FSL 1998) can replace the professional trustee to give the family more control while retaining the firewall (`knowledge/jersey/financial-regulation/private-trust-company.md`; worked example Step 4).
