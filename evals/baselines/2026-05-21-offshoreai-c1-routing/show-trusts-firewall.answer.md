## The mechanism

Article 9 of the Trusts (Jersey) Law 1984 instructs the Royal Court to determine questions of a Jersey trust's **validity, interpretation, settlor capacity, administration, trustees' powers, and beneficial rights** under **Jersey law alone** — "no rule of foreign law shall affect such question" (Art. 9(1)). The decisive provision against forced heirship is **Article 9(2)**: the determination is made *without consideration of whether the trust avoids or defeats rights, claims or interests conferred by any foreign law*. So even where a civil-law jurisdiction treats a lifetime transfer into a Jersey trust as a fraud on the *réserve héréditaire* / *légitime*, the Royal Court will not give effect to that foreign view. ## How a forced-heirship attack typically runs — and where Article 9 stops it

The standard pattern: a settlor with heirs in (e.g.) France or Italy transfers assets into a Jersey trust during life; after death the reserved heirs attack the disposition as one that should be notionally clawed back into the estate to compute their fixed share (the file gives German *Pflichtteilsergänzung* as a clawback example). Article 9 refuses to apply those forced-heirship and clawback rules in Jersey. **Article 9(4)** adds the strong-form protection: a **foreign judgment inconsistent with Article 9 is unenforceable in Jersey** — so even a foreign court ruling that the transfer was invalid cannot be enforced here against the trust. (`knowledge/jersey/trusts/firewall.md`)

## The carve-outs that survive the firewall

Article 9 is not unlimited, and the forced-heirship-relevant boundaries matter:

- **Article 9(2A) savings** — the firewall cannot validate a disposition where the settlor had no ownership or power to dispose, cannot override foreign-law formalities for transferring property, and cannot validate a will invalid under the testator's domicile law. It turns *fraus legis* attacks aside, but it does not turn theft into title or cure a defective will.
- **Article 9(3) — Jersey domicile** — Jersey's *own* légitime (customary forced heirship for spouse/children of the movable estate) still applies if the **settlor is Jersey-domiciled**. A Jersey-domiciled settlor cannot escape Jersey forced-heirship merely by settling into a Jersey trust.
- **Article 9(3A)** — Jersey conflict-of-laws rules (other than Article 9 itself) are excluded, closing off a *renvoi* argument back to the foreign law.

## Practical limits — what the firewall does not reach

The firewall binds the Royal Court and Jersey-located assets; it does not bind a foreign court. So it does not stop: a foreign forum exercising **in personam jurisdiction over a resident beneficiary**; a foreign court reaching **trust assets physically located in that jurisdiction**; or enforcement of a foreign judgment **abroad** against foreign assets. The corpus also flags that a **sham** trust (failing the Article 2 existence test) gets no Article 9 protection at all, and Jersey public policy is a residual override. Drafting points: make an **express choice of Jersey proper law** (engaging Article 4) and avoid clauses that re-import foreign succession rules by the back door; where exposure is real, pair the trust with substantive lifetime planning in the heirship jurisdiction rather than relying on the firewall alone. (`knowledge/jersey/trusts/firewall.md`)
