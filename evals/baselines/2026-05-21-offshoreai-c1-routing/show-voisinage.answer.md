## What voisinage is

**Voisinage** is a Jersey **Norman customary-law doctrine of "good neighbourhood."** It requires each landowner to use their property in a way that does not cause **unreasonable harm** to neighbours. Jersey preserved the doctrine where most modern legal systems abandoned it, and the Royal Court still applies it today. It is *distinct from* English nuisance — overlapping in result, but with its own customary-law foundation. (`knowledge/jersey/property/voisinage.md`)

The key practical difference from English nuisance: voisinage can apply **without fault** (it's an objective test) and is **not limited to enumerated categories** like smell/smoke/noise — a broader range of activity can be captured. (`knowledge/jersey/property/voisinage.md`)

## Does it apply to your problem?

I can't tell you that without knowing what your problem actually is — and even then, this is information, not advice. But here is the test the corpus sets out. Voisinage liability requires **all** of:

1. **Harm** that is objectively identifiable;
2. **caused by** the neighbour's use of their property;
3. **exceeding** the ordinary inconveniences of neighbourhood living; and
4. **without** adequate justification.

(`knowledge/jersey/property/voisinage.md`)

Typical claims the doctrine is invoked for include excessive noise, smells (livestock, kitchens), smoke/fumes, vibrations from machinery or construction, surface-water/drainage, tree roots and overhanging branches, and light obstruction. So if your "neighbour problem" is one of those, voisinage is at least plausibly in scope. (`knowledge/jersey/property/voisinage.md`)

A few things worth knowing because they often catch people out:

- **Planning permission does not exclude voisinage** — a neighbour can be acting with full planning consent and still breach it (though regulatory compliance can be *evidence* of reasonable use).
- **Defences** include reasonable use, the claimant's consent/acquiescence, statutory authority, and (limited) prescription or pre-existing state.
- **Servitudes** (easements) may license a specific activity, but *excessive* use of a servitude can still breach voisinage.

(`knowledge/jersey/property/voisinage.md`)

## Practical next steps the corpus suggests

Document the interference (logs, photos, recordings), build a correspondence trail with your neighbour, engage relevant experts early (e.g. acoustic/environmental), **mediate as a first step**, and instruct a Jersey advocate familiar with voisinage. If it goes to litigation it proceeds by Order of Justice through the Royal Court, often with a site visit and expert evidence — and Royal Court costs are not trivial.
