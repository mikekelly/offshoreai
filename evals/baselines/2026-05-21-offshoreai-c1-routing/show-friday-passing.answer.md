In Jersey, **real-property contracts are "passed" before the Royal Court** rather than completed privately between solicitors as in England. The buyer and seller (usually their lawyers acting as attorneys under powers of attorney) appear before the **Samedi Division** of the Royal Court, the contract is read or referenced, the Greffier records and signs it, and **the transfer of title takes effect by that Court act** — the property changes hands at that moment, then the transaction is recorded in the Public Registry (`knowledge/jersey/property/contract-passing.md`; `knowledge/jersey/property/real-property-overview.md`).

This is a **Norman customary-law inheritance**: real-property transfer is a *public* act, with the Royal Court as the repository of property records, giving certainty about title transitions and public visibility that a sale has occurred (though the price/terms stay private) (`knowledge/jersey/property/contract-passing.md`).

## The "Friday afternoon" part

The Samedi Division is the Court's civil day-to-day forum — its name derives from its **historical Saturday sittings** ("samedi" = Saturday), and conveyancing contracts are passed there (`knowledge/jersey/legal-system/royal-court.md`). The corpus records the **Friday-afternoon property-passing convention** as a distinctive Jersey feature in the cross-jurisdictional synthesis layer (`knowledge/CROSS-JURISDICTIONAL-MAP.md`, "Distinctive other" row).

**One honesty caveat:** the doctrinal property files describe contracts being passed on "most weekdays" and do not themselves single out Friday afternoon, nor do they explain *why* Friday specifically became the customary slot. The Friday-afternoon characterisation appears only in the synthesis map. So the corpus supports the *what* (a recurring Royal Court conveyancing session, by long-standing Samedi convention) but does **not** give the historical reason Friday afternoon in particular became the fixed slot — verify that detail with a Jersey conveyancing advocate.
