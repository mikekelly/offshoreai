## The procedure

A drafting clarification first: "Article 47B" is the **interpretation** provision of the regime. The set-aside *power* itself sits in **Articles 47E–47G**; 47B supplies the wide definition of "mistake" they run on. The whole 47B–47H regime was inserted by the **Trusts (Amendment No. 6) (Jersey) Law 2013** to put the Royal Court's case-law mistake jurisdiction on a statutory footing (`knowledge/jersey/trusts/article-47-set-aside.md`).

**What Article 47B counts as a "mistake."** A mistake as to: the *effect* of a transaction; its *consequences* (expressly including tax consequences); any *advantage* the person believed they'd gain; or any *fact existing before or at the time* of the disposition or power-exercise. Testamentary dispositions are excluded.

**Which door you use:**
- **Article 47E** — set aside a transfer of property *to* a trust (the settlor's common "I settled assets then discovered an unforeseen tax charge" case). The Court declares the transfer voidable; property returns to the transferor.
- **Article 47G** — set aside an *exercise of a fiduciary power* (e.g. a trustee's capital appointment, a protector's consent, a settlor's reserved-power exercise). The exercise is unwound and the prior position restored.

**The three-limb test** the Royal Court applies before granting relief (formulated in *In re S & T Trusts* [2015] JRC259):
1. there was a mistake within the wide 47B definition;
2. **but for** the mistake, the transfer/power-exercise would not have been made in that form;
3. the mistake was **so serious a character that it would be just** for the Court to grant the declaration.

Failing any limb defeats the application — limb 2 is the usual stumbling block (showing you'd have done *nothing*, not merely something different).

**Mechanics:** application by **Representation** in the Royal Court (Samedi Division), supported by affidavit evidence of the relevant state of mind, ideally backed by contemporaneous documents (file notes, advice, correspondence) rather than a years-later reconstruction. A successful declaration renders the transaction void *ab initio* in the usual case, with good-faith-for-value third parties typically protected (`knowledge/jersey/trusts/article-47-set-aside.md`).

## How Jersey is wider than *Pitt v Holt*

In *Pitt v Holt; Futter v Futter* [2013] UKSC 26 the English Supreme Court distinguished a true **mistake** (which can ground rescission) from **"mere causative ignorance" or "inadequate deliberation"** (which generally cannot). Under the statutory regime the Royal Court has **rejected that distinction**: in Jersey the *manner* of the mistake is irrelevant — the Court asks only whether there was a mistake at all, not what kind. That makes the Jersey jurisdiction materially wider than the post-*Pitt-v-Holt* English position, so a "we didn't think about that" outcome is more likely to be remediable in St Helier than in the English High Court (`knowledge/jersey/trusts/article-47-set-aside.md`).

Two boundaries worth noting: the Court will not use the jurisdiction to *re-write history* / permit partial rescission dressed up as an unwinding (*In the Matter of the M Settlement*), and a disappointed expectation of a *future* tax advantage (as opposed to an unforeseen tax *charge*) tends not to qualify.

**Adjacent doors:** a mistake going to the very intention to settle is dealt with under **Article 11** (trust invalid for mistake), not 47C–H; and where the remedy sought is a direction to the trustee rather than unwinding a transaction, the **Article 51** directions jurisdiction may be the right vehicle (`knowledge/jersey/trusts/article-51-directions.md`).
