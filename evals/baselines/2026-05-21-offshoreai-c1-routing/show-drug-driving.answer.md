## Drug-driving in Jersey today

The current regime is **impairment-based**, prosecuted under the **Road Traffic (Jersey) Law 1956**. To convict, police must prove the driver was *actually unfit to drive* because of drug use — on objective evidence (poor vehicle control, slurred speech, failed field sobriety tests, plus drugs found in a blood/urine sample). Critically, Jersey as of May 2026 **does not** have:

- statutory drug-concentration limits (no equivalent of the English specified-limit regime),
- police roadside drug-testing powers (no drug-wipe swabs), or
- *per se* offences (being over a numeric limit regardless of impairment).

That makes drug-driving harder to prove than drink-driving, which has a mechanical "over the limit" test. The offence is **substance-neutral** — controlled drugs, prescription medication, OTC medicines, and drug/alcohol combinations all count, and "I had a prescription" is no defence if impairment is real. Sentencing tracks drink-driving: mandatory disqualification (typically 12+ months for a first offence), a fine up to £2,000, up to 6 months' imprisonment, and licence endorsement. ## What's coming

In **February 2026**, States representatives proposed legislation to bring Jersey closer to the UK model:

- introduce **statutory drug limits** for controlled and some prescription substances,
- give police **roadside testing powers** (UK-style drug-wipes),
- create **per se offences** (over the limit is itself the offence), and
- impose **mandatory disqualification** on conviction.

As of May 2026 this is **not yet enacted**; the States Assembly timetable suggests possible introduction during 2026 or 2027. Until then, the impairment-based regime continues to apply. (`knowledge/jersey/road-traffic/drug-driving.md`)
