## Article 47B and setting aside a trustee decision for mistake

A naming point first: in Jersey trust law the **"Article 47B procedure"** is really the regime at **Articles 47C–47H** of the **Trusts (Jersey) Law 1984** (inserted by the Trusts (Amendment No. 6) (Jersey) Law 2013). **Article 47B itself is the interpretation section** — it carries the wide statutory definition of "mistake". A trustee's exercise of a fiduciary power is set aside under **Article 47G**; a transfer *into* a trust is set aside under **Article 47E**. Citing "Article 47" alone is ambiguous (Article 47 simpliciter is the older variation-of-trusts jurisdiction; 47A handles failed purpose trusts), so a Jersey brief should always cite the sub-Article (jersey/trusts/article-47-set-aside.md).

### Substantive ground — what counts as a "mistake" (Article 47B)

Article 47B defines mistake broadly. A qualifying mistake can be as to:

- the **effect** of the transaction;
- its **consequences**, *including its tax consequences*;
- any **advantage** the person believed they would gain; or
- any **fact existing before or at the time of** the disposition or power-exercise.

Testamentary dispositions are expressly excluded from the "transfer into a trust" limb (jersey/trusts/article-47-set-aside.md).

### Operative articles

- **Article 47E** — set-aside of a *transfer or disposition of property to a trust*. Standing: transferor or person acting on their behalf. Effect: Court declares the transfer voidable; property returns to the transferor on any conditions the Court imposes.
- **Article 47G** — set-aside of an *exercise of a fiduciary power* (the trustee-decision route). Standing: trustee, protector, beneficiary or other appropriate person. Effect: the power-exercise is declared voidable and unwound, restoring the position immediately prior. This is the article that catches, e.g., a trustee's appointment of capital made without appreciating a tax charge, a protector's consent given under a factual misapprehension, or a settlor's exercise of a reserved power producing unintended results.
- **Articles 47C, 47D, 47F, 47H** — standing rules, consequences of a declaration, limitations on relief, and protection of third parties (jersey/trusts/article-47-set-aside.md).

### The three-limb test (S & T Trusts [2015] JRC259 and following)

The Royal Court will grant relief under 47E or 47G only if the applicant shows:

1. **There was a mistake** within the wide Article 47B definition.
2. **But for the mistake, the disposition or power-exercise would not have been made** in the form it was.
3. **The mistake was of so serious a character that it would be just** for the Court to make a declaration.

Failure on any one limb defeats the application; limb 2 is the most common point of failure (the applicant can usually show they would have done *something different*, not that they would have done *nothing*) (jersey/trusts/article-47-set-aside.md, jersey/use-cases/trust-officer/article-47-set-aside-for-mistake.md).

### Procedure

- **Vehicle**: a **Representation** in the Royal Court (Samedi Division), supported by affidavit evidence of the relevant state of mind at the relevant time.
- **Respondents**: the trustee that took the impugned decision, the beneficiaries, and any third party that received property under the impugned transaction.
- **Evidence**: contemporaneous documentary evidence is critical — file notes, board minutes, adviser correspondence, the actual advice given. A reconstruction affidavit years after the fact is weak.
- **Costs**: the application is typically a "friendly" proceeding; trustee costs usually come out of the trust fund, while a settlor-applicant in a personal-mistake case generally bears their own costs (jersey/use-cases/trust-officer/article-47-set-aside-for-mistake.md).
- **Effect of a declaration**: typically void *ab initio*, with the Court retaining flexibility to fashion relief; good-faith-for-value third-party rights are usually protected; foreign tax authorities recognise the unwind to varying degrees (HMRC has historically accepted Jersey rescissions for IHT/CGT purposes; the IRS is more cautious) (jersey/trusts/article-47-set-aside.md).

### How Jersey is **wider than Pitt v Holt**

In ***Pitt v Holt; Futter v Futter* [2013] UKSC 26** the UK Supreme Court drew a line between a true **"mistake"** (an erroneous belief about a relevant fact or law), which can ground rescission, and **"mere causative ignorance"** or **"inadequate deliberation"**, which generally cannot. English law is therefore narrower: a trustee who simply failed to think about a consequence (rather than positively believing something false about it) is largely out of court.

The Royal Court, applying the statutory regime, has **expressly rejected that distinction**. As paraphrased in the corpus:

> Under Jersey law … the jurisdiction to give statutory relief requires the Court to decide whether there was *any* mistake; in Jersey, the **manner of the mistake is irrelevant** and it is therefore inappropriate to make the distinctions applied under English law.

So a Jersey 47E/47G applicant whose case rests on plain ignorance, oversight or "we didn't think about that" can still get over limb 1 of the test, where the equivalent English Pitt-v-Holt applicant would not. The Court still polices the test through limbs 2 and 3 (and through its reluctance to permit *partial* rescissions that amount to re-writing history — see *In re M Settlement* — or to treat the loss of a hoped-for *future* tax advantage as a qualifying mistake), but the **threshold for what kind of mental state counts** is materially wider than in England. That width is part of why an English adviser may prefer a Jersey-law trust when the principal risk being hedged is the future application of a tax rule the adviser cannot yet see clearly (jersey/trusts/article-47-set-aside.md, jersey/use-cases/trust-officer/article-47-set-aside-for-mistake.md).
