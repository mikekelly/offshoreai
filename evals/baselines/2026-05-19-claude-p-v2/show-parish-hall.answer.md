## What a Parish Hall Enquiry is

A **Parish Hall Enquiry** is a pre-court forum, presided over by a **Centenier** — an elected, unpaid honorary police officer of one of Jersey's 12 parishes — held in the parish where the alleged offence took place. It is the route by which the majority of routine Jersey motoring offences are resolved without ever reaching the Magistrate's Court. There is **no direct equivalent** in the UK or other common-law jurisdictions: it isn't a fixed-penalty notice and isn't a court, although it borrows features of each (`jersey/road-traffic/parish-hall-enquiries.md`).

The Centenier is **not a judge** in the conventional sense — they combine prosecutorial and limited adjudicative roles. At the Parish Hall they review the police evidence, decide whether there is enough to justify a charge, decide whether the public interest needs court prosecution, engage with the alleged offender, and then either **caution**, **issue a written fine of up to £200 per offence**, or **refer to Magistrate's Court** (`jersey/road-traffic/parish-hall-enquiries.md`).

Critically, the Centenier may only impose a caution or fine if the offender **admits** the offence **and accepts** the proposed penalty. A defendant who disputes the allegation — or who simply wants formal court treatment — must be referred to Magistrate's Court, and that "right to opt out" can be exercised at any point before or after the Parish Hall outcome (`jersey/road-traffic/parish-hall-enquiries.md`).

## When your case goes there

The route is typically used for routine motoring matters such as:

- minor speeding (under 15 mph over the limit)
- mobile phone offences
- failing to display an insurance disc
- some traffic-light offences
- some minor careless-driving offences
- parking offences

(`jersey/road-traffic/parish-hall-enquiries.md`)

## When it does NOT go there

The Parish Hall route is excluded — and the matter goes directly to Magistrate's Court by summons — for:

- speeding 15 mph or more over the limit
- drink-driving (any reading)
- drug-driving
- dangerous driving (Article 23 of the Road Traffic Law)
- causing death or serious injury by driving
- driving while disqualified
- failing to stop / report after an accident (in serious cases)
- any offence carrying mandatory disqualification
- any offence where the Centenier judges the £200 ceiling inadequate

(`jersey/road-traffic/parish-hall-enquiries.md`)

The schedule of which offences are treatable at Parish Hall is maintained by the Law Officers' Department and reviewed periodically (`jersey/road-traffic/parish-hall-enquiries.md`).

## How the day itself works

The honorary or States Police report the alleged offence to the parish; you're summoned to attend on a set date; the Centenier meets you, reviews the evidence, and invites you to admit or deny. If you admit, the Centenier proposes a penalty which you can accept or refuse (refusing sends it to court); if you deny, it is referred to Magistrate's Court. The whole thing is informal — no robes, no transcripts, typically 15–30 minutes. You have the right to be told the alleged offence in advance, to bring a friend, family member, or advocate, to decline to admit, to demand a Magistrate's Court hearing instead, to appeal any outcome by requesting a Magistrate's Court re-hearing, and to receive a copy of the Centenier's record (`jersey/road-traffic/parish-hall-enquiries.md`).

## Caveats worth flagging

A Parish Hall outcome is recorded and **does not formally count as a criminal conviction** for most purposes, but it **can endorse your driving licence** for relevant offences and may be reported to other authorities (e.g. for licensing implications). Centenier fines are not always lower than court fines for the same conduct, so admitting at Parish Hall isn't automatically the cheaper option (`jersey/road-traffic/parish-hall-enquiries.md`).
