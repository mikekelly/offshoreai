## The Article 47B–H procedure

A small but important framing point first: **Article 47B itself is the interpretation/definitions provision** for the mistake regime. The operative set-aside power for a **trustee decision** (i.e. an exercise of a fiduciary power) sits at **Article 47G**; **Article 47E** is the parallel provision for a transfer of property *into* a trust. Together, Articles 47B–47H are the modern statutory mistake regime introduced by the **Trusts (Amendment No. 6) (Jersey) Law 2013** (`jersey/trusts/article-47-set-aside.md`).

**The procedure**

1. **Identify the right Article.** Mistake invalidating the trust itself = Article 11. Mistake about a transfer into a trust = 47E. Mistake about a trustee's (or protector's, or settlor's) **exercise of a fiduciary power** = 47G. Pleading the wrong one is the most common pitfall.
2. **Frame the mistake against Article 47B's four limbs** — a mistake as to the *effect* of the transaction, its *consequences* (including tax consequences), any *advantage* the actor believed they would gain, or any *fact existing before or at the time of* the disposition or power-exercise. Testamentary dispositions are expressly excluded.
3. **Apply the three-limb Royal Court test** (per *In re S & T Trusts* [2015] JRC259):
   - (i) there was a mistake within the wide Article 47B definition;
   - (ii) **but for** the mistake, the power-exercise would not have been made in the form it was; and
   - (iii) the mistake is of so serious a character that it would be **just** for the Court to grant a declaration.
4. **Issue by Representation** in the Royal Court, Samedi Division, supported by affidavit evidence of the decision-maker's state of mind at the relevant time. Contemporaneous documentary evidence (file notes, board minutes, adviser correspondence) is critical — reconstruction affidavits years later are weak.
5. **Join all affected parties** as respondents: the trustee that made the impugned decision, beneficiaries, any third-party transferees.
6. **Relief.** If granted, the declaration renders the power-exercise **voidable** (typically *ab initio*), unwinding it and restoring the *status quo ante*. Bona fide third-party rights for value are normally protected. Foreign tax consequences only fall away if the foreign revenue authority recognises the Jersey rescission (HMRC historically does; the IRS is more cautious).

## How Jersey is wider than *Pitt v Holt*

The English Supreme Court in ***Pitt v Holt; Futter v Futter* [2013] UKSC 26** distinguished between a true **mistake** (which can ground rescission) and **"mere causative ignorance" or "inadequate deliberation"** (which generally cannot).

Under the statutory regime, **the Royal Court has rejected that distinction**. The Court's stated position is that, on an Article 47E/G application, the only question is whether there was *any* mistake within the wide Article 47B definition — **"the manner of the mistake is irrelevant"** — and the English distinction does not apply (`jersey/trusts/article-47-set-aside.md`). The practical effect: facts that would fail in the English High Court as mere inadequate deliberation can still ground a successful set-aside in Jersey, which is the policy reason English advisers sometimes prefer a Jersey-law trust where the principal risk is a future tax-or-other-rule change the adviser cannot yet see clearly.

The Court is, however, **not unlimited**: lost *future* tax advantages (as distinct from unforeseen *charges*) and applications that are substantively partial rewrites rather than unwinds have been refused (e.g. *In re M Settlement*).
