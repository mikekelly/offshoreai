On 15 April 2026 the **Residential Tenancy (Jersey) Amendment Law 2025** came into force, marking the most significant Jersey residential-tenancy reform in over a decade. The headline changes:

**Fixed-term tenancies capped at 3 years.** The initial fixed term may not exceed 3 years (down from the prior 9-year ceiling above which a lease becomes a Royal-Court-passed contract of lease), and successive fixed terms are prohibited — a tenancy expiring at the end of its fixed term either ends outright or converts automatically to a periodic tenancy.

**Tenant exit on one month's notice.** A tenant may terminate any residential tenancy on one month's written notice, regardless of any fixed term — fixed-term "lock-in" is gone.

**Landlord termination — justified ground or 12 months.** To end a periodic tenancy, a landlord must either justify on a permitted ground (breach, sale, owner-occupation, redevelopment — 3 months' notice) or give 12 months' notice without justification. This replaces the prior 3-month no-fault notice.

**Rent caps with a once-yearly rule.** For periodic tenancies, rent may be increased only once per year, capped at the Jersey RPI for the relevant period, on at least 2 months' written notice — with a narrow "significantly below market rent" carve-out.

**New Rent Tribunal.** An independent Rent Tribunal hears disputes over rent increases (RPI compliance, the carve-out, the once-yearly rule, and the 2-month notice). It does **not** cover social housing, employment-linked accommodation, or eviction/breach — those remain with the Petty Debts Court.

**Strengthened written-form rules.** Tenancy agreements must clearly identify all fees chargeable to the tenant; hidden fees are unenforceable and may constitute an offence.

**Transitional treatment** at 15 April 2026: pre-existing **periodic** tenancies convert immediately to the new regime (rent caps, 12-month no-fault notice, etc.); pre-existing **fixed-term** tenancies continue under pre-amendment law until expiry, then flip to the new rules; any successive-fixed-term clause becomes unenforceable at expiry of the current fixed term.

**What did not change:** the Petty Debts Court retains exclusive jurisdiction over eviction and breach; the Viscount continues to enforce eviction orders; the condition-report regime (2014 Order), Tenants' Deposit Scheme, lodging-house exclusion, and the 9-year contract-of-lease ceiling are all substantively unchanged.
