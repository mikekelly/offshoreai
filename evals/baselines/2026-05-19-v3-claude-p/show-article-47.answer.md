## Article 47B and the Jersey set-aside-for-mistake regime

### What "Article 47B" actually is

A naming nuance the corpus flags up front: **Article 47B itself is the *interpretation* provision**, not the operative set-aside power. It supplies the wide statutory definition of "mistake" that the rest of the regime (Articles 47C–47H) hangs off. The operative set-aside powers sit at **47E** (transfers/dispositions of property *to* a trust) and **47G** (exercise of a fiduciary power in relation to a trust). When practitioners say "Article 47" colloquially they almost always mean the 47B–47H cluster — citing the sub-Article matters. (`jersey/trusts/article-47-set-aside.md`, "Naming conventions and a warning"; `jersey/use-cases/trust-officer/article-47-set-aside-for-mistake.md`, "Pitfalls")

The regime was inserted by the **Trusts (Amendment No. 6) (Jersey) Law 2013**. (`jersey/trusts/article-47-set-aside.md`)

### The Article 47B definition of "mistake"

A "mistake" qualifies if it is a mistake as to:

- the **effect** of a transaction;
- its **consequences**, expressly including its **tax consequences**;
- any **advantage** the person believed they would gain; or
- any **fact existing before or at the time of** the disposition or power-exercise.

Testamentary dispositions are excluded from the "transfer to a trust" branch. (`jersey/trusts/article-47-set-aside.md`, "Article 47B — what is a mistake")

### The procedure — what an applicant actually does

Drawing on the use-case checklist (`jersey/use-cases/trust-officer/article-47-set-aside-for-mistake.md`, "What to do — checklist") and the practical points in the topic file:

1. **Identify the transaction and pick the right door.** Article 11 (the trust itself never validly created), Article 47E (a transfer/disposition *into* a valid trust), or Article 47G (exercise of a fiduciary power — trustee appointment of capital, protector consent, settlor reserved-power exercise, etc.). The three doors are distinct; pleading the wrong one is the most common pitfall.
2. **Frame the mistake** against Article 47B's four limbs (effect / consequences / advantage / fact existing at the time).
3. **Gather contemporaneous evidence** of the relevant state of mind — file notes, board minutes, original advice, e-mail. A reconstruction affidavit produced years later is weak.
4. **Test the three-limb judicial test** internally (see below).
5. **Cross-border tax analysis** — a Jersey declaration is necessary but not sufficient; the foreign revenue authority must recognise it. HMRC has historically given effect to a Jersey rescission for IHT/CGT; US IRS practice is more cautious.
6. **Consider Article 51 instead/as well.** If what's wanted is a remedial direction rather than rescission, the directions jurisdiction may be the right vehicle, and trustees often plead both in a single Representation. (`jersey/trusts/article-47-set-aside.md`, "Relationship with the Article 51 directions jurisdiction")
7. **Issue a Representation in the Royal Court, Samedi Division**, joining the trustee, beneficiaries and any third-party recipients as respondents, supported by affidavit evidence of state of mind at the relevant time.
8. **Costs** — typically a friendly application with trustee costs from the fund; a settlor-applicant in a personal-mistake case bears their own costs.

The Royal Court then applies a **three-limb test** (per *In re S & T Trusts* [2015] JRC259 and the cases that have followed):

1. There was a mistake within the Article 47B definition.
2. **But for** the mistake, the transaction or power-exercise would not have been made as it was.
3. The mistake was so serious that it would be **just** for the Court to make a declaration.

Failure on any one limb defeats the application; limb 2 (but-for) is the most common point of failure. (`jersey/trusts/article-47-set-aside.md`, "The three-limb test")

If granted, the typical effect is that the transaction is **void *ab initio***, property returns to the transferor (47E) or the power-exercise is reversed (47G). The trust itself is not voided unless an Article 11 application is also brought. Third-party bona fide purchasers for value are generally protected. (`jersey/trusts/article-47-set-aside.md`, "Effect of a declaration"; use-case file, "Effect of an Article 47 set-aside")

### How Jersey is wider than *Pitt v Holt*

The English Supreme Court in ***Pitt v Holt; Futter v Futter*** [2013] UKSC 26 drew a line between:

- **a "mistake"** — an erroneous belief about a relevant fact or law — which can ground rescission, and
- **"mere causative ignorance"** / **"inadequate deliberation"** — which generally cannot.

The Royal Court has *rejected* that distinction when applying the statutory regime. As the corpus quotes:

> Under Jersey law, when considering applications under Article 47E, the jurisdiction to give statutory relief requires the Court to decide whether there was any mistake; in Jersey, the manner of the mistake is irrelevant and it is therefore inappropriate to make the distinctions applied under English law.

So the *manner* of the mistake — whether the applicant was actively mistaken, merely ignorant, or simply failed to deliberate — does not matter in Jersey. If a mistake exists within the wide Article 47B definition, the gate is open; the case then turns on but-for and seriousness, not on categorising the mistake. (`jersey/trusts/article-47-set-aside.md`, "Divergence from English law"; `jersey/use-cases/trust-officer/article-47-set-aside-for-mistake.md`, "What counts as a mistake")

The practical implication called out in the corpus: where the principal risk to be hedged is a tax (or other) rule that the adviser cannot yet foresee clearly, a Jersey-law trust is more remediable later than an English one, because a future "we didn't think about that" position is more likely to clear the lower Jersey threshold.

### Limits on Jersey's width

The width is real but not unlimited (`jersey/trusts/article-47-set-aside.md`, "Cases — successful and unsuccessful"; use-case "When the Court will / will not grant relief"):

- The Court has declined to grant **partial rescission** where what was sought was substantively a re-writing of history rather than an unwinding (*In re M Settlement*).
- **Disappointed expectation of a future tax advantage** (as distinct from suffering an unforeseen tax charge) has been treated reluctantly.
- A **commercial misjudgement** — as opposed to a mistake about effect, consequences, advantage or existing fact — is not within 47B.
