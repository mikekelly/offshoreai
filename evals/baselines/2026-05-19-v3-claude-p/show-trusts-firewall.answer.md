# How the Article 9 trust firewall works when foreign forced-heirship is in play

**Article 9 of the Trusts (Jersey) Law 1984** is the "firewall": a self-contained choice-of-law rule that tells the Royal Court to apply Jersey law — and only Jersey law — to the validity, interpretation, settlor capacity, administration, trustee powers, and beneficial-rights aspects of a Jersey trust, and to refuse to give effect to foreign rules (notably forced-heirship and matrimonial-regime rules) that would constrain the settlor's freedom to settle (`jersey/trusts/firewall.md`). Its modern form derives from the **Trusts (Amendment No. 4) (Jersey) Law 2006**, which strengthened it by extending the rule to foreign judgments and clarifying capacity (`jersey/trusts/firewall.md`).

The mechanics, paragraph by paragraph (`jersey/trusts/firewall.md`):

- **Paragraph (1) — the headline rule.** Questions of validity (of the trust and of dispositions into it), interpretation, settlor capacity, administration, trustee powers, foreign-court variation, and beneficial rights "shall be determined in accordance with the law of Jersey and no rule of foreign law shall affect such question."
- **Paragraph (2) — disregard of foreign non-trust regimes.** The Article 9 determination proceeds **without regard** to whether a foreign law (a) recognises the trust concept at all, or (b) treats the disposition into the Jersey trust as defeating rights conferred by foreign law. This is the heart of the firewall — a civil-law jurisdiction's view that the disposition is a fraud on *réserve héréditaire* or similar forced-heirship rules is simply not given effect in Jersey.
- **Paragraph (2A) — savings.** The firewall cannot turn theft into title: it does **not** validate a disposition where the settlor had no ownership or power to dispose, does not override foreign ownership/formalities rules for property transfer, and does not validate a testamentary disposition that is invalid under the testator's domicile law.
- **Paragraph (3) — domestic carve-out for Jersey-domiciled settlors.** Jersey's own *légitime* (Jersey customary forced-heirship) **does** apply where the settlor is Jersey-domiciled — a Jersey-domiciled settlor cannot use a Jersey trust to escape Jersey forced-heirship.
- **Paragraph (3A) — renvoi excluded.** Jersey conflict-of-laws rules other than Article 9 itself do not apply to the questions captured by Article 9, blocking any argument that Jersey *renvoi* sends the question back to the foreign law.
- **Paragraphs (4)–(7) — foreign judgments and customary rules.** Foreign judgments **inconsistent with Article 9 are unenforceable in Jersey** (the strong-form firewall against, e.g., a French or Italian heir's home-court order). The Jersey customary rule *donner et retenir ne vaut* (you cannot validly give while retaining control) is disapplied in the trust context, complementing reserved-powers planning under Article 9A. Definitions of "foreign", "heirship rights", "*légitime*", and "personal relationship" are supplied.

**Typical foreign forced-heirship attacks Article 9 is designed to defeat** (`jersey/trusts/firewall.md`):

1. **Civil-law *réserve héréditaire*** (e.g., France, Italy) — heirs after the settlor's death attack a lifetime transfer into the Jersey trust as a fraudulent disposition that should notionally return to the estate for reserve calculation. Article 9 refuses to give effect.
2. **Community-property matrimonial regimes** — non-settlor spouse's co-ownership claim under local law is similarly disregarded.
3. **Foreign divorce orders against trust assets** — unenforceable in Jersey under Article 9(4) to the extent inconsistent with Article 9.
4. **Foreign forced-share clawback** (e.g., German *Pflichtteilsergänzung*) — same outcome.

**Limits when forced-heirship is in play** (`jersey/trusts/firewall.md`):

- **In-personam reach of the foreign court.** Article 9 binds the Royal Court and Jersey-located assets, not the foreign forum. A foreign court with personal jurisdiction over a beneficiary resident there can order that beneficiary to take steps in relation to trust property.
- **Asset location.** Trust assets sitting in the foreign jurisdiction can be reached directly there; the firewall protects what is in Jersey (or held through Jersey-located intermediaries).
- **Settlor domicile in Jersey.** Article 9(3) preserves Jersey *légitime*.
- **Personal-law capacity.** Article 9 governs capacity *for the purposes of the trust*, but cannot cure a settlor's underlying lack of capacity to dispose under their personal law.
- **Sham / Article 2 failure.** If the settlor in substance retained beneficial ownership and the arrangement fails the Article 2 existence test, the firewall never engages — a sham is not a trust at all.
- **Public policy.** Jersey public policy is the residual safety valve (e.g., a trust for criminal purposes), rarely invoked but available.

**Practical drafting points the corpus highlights** (`jersey/trusts/firewall.md`):

- Make an **express choice of Jersey law** as the proper law (engaging Article 4), so Article 9 is engaged.
- Avoid back-door re-importation of foreign rules (e.g., a clause requiring distributions "in accordance with the laws of succession of the settlor's home country") — that can undercut the firewall.
- Where exposure to a forced-heirship jurisdiction is significant, consider **parallel substantive lifetime planning** in that jurisdiction (e.g., a notarial *pacte de famille*) rather than relying on Article 9 alone.
- Article 9 also works in tandem with **Article 9A** on reserved powers: without 9A, *donner et retenir ne vaut* / substance-over-form arguments would gain traction against reserved-powers trusts; together, the package is structurally robust (`jersey/trusts/firewall.md`, cross-references to `jersey/trusts/reserved-powers.md`).
