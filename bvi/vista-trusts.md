# BVI VISTA Trusts

## What VISTA is

**VISTA** stands for the **Virgin Islands Special Trusts
Act 2003**. It creates a distinct **share-ownership trust
regime** designed to solve a specific commercial problem:
how to settle shares of an operating company into trust
without imposing on the trustee the usual common-law duty
to monitor and intervene in the company's affairs.

Under classical trust law (the Bartlett v Barclays Bank
[1980] standard) a trustee holding shares must keep itself
informed of, and where necessary intervene in, the
company's operations. That duty is appropriate for a
trustee holding shares as an investment, but is **deeply
problematic** when:

- The settlor wants the company to be **run by chosen
  directors** (often family members) without trustee
  second-guessing;
- The trust assets are **operating-business shares**
  rather than a portfolio;
- **Succession planning** for a family business is the
  goal, not investment management.

VISTA's signature feature is the **disengagement of the
trustee from the underlying company's management**. The
trustee holds the shares as legal owner but is **not
required** to intervene in the company's affairs, and is
**not liable** for the consequences of not intervening.

## Eligibility — designated companies

A VISTA trust must hold shares in a **designated
company** — a company incorporated under the BVI Business
Companies Act 2004 (or a permitted equivalent) whose
shares are held under the trust.

The trust instrument **designates** the underlying company
as subject to VISTA. The designation can be made on
settlement or later.

## The "office of director rules"

Each VISTA trust must include **office of director rules**
(ODRs) specifying:

- **Who** can appoint and remove directors of the
  designated company;
- **How** appointments and removals are made;
- **Restrictions** on the trustee's power to interfere;
- **Specific** mechanics by which the settlor's chosen
  control structure is preserved.

The ODRs are the heart of the VISTA arrangement —
typically drafted to retain settlor / family control over
the company's management while the legal title to shares
sits in trust.

## What the trustee is and isn't required to do

Under a VISTA trust:

- The trustee **holds legal title** to the designated
  company's shares;
- The trustee **does not** have the *Bartlett* duty of
  active intervention in the company's affairs;
- The trustee **need not** monitor management
  performance, vote shares on commercial matters, or
  remove directors for poor performance;
- The trustee **must** comply with the ODRs and the
  trust instrument;
- The trustee **retains** core trustee duties (no fraud,
  proper administration of the trust itself,
  beneficiary information rights subject to specific
  provisions).

The trustee is essentially a **custodian of legal title**
for the shares, with management of the underlying
business left to those nominated by the settlor under the
ODRs.

## Permitted intervention

VISTA does not entirely prohibit trustee intervention.
The trust instrument may permit specified intervention,
typically:

- **In extremis** (fraud, gross misconduct, regulatory
  breach by directors);
- **At the request of permitted persons** identified in
  the ODRs;
- **Where beneficiaries make a formal complaint** under
  defined procedures;
- **Specific** events triggering intervention by the
  trustee.

The default is **no intervention**; the trust instrument
draws the boundaries of when the default flips.

## The "interest of beneficiaries" exception

A trustee may intervene where doing so is necessary to
**protect the interests of beneficiaries** — but the
standard is narrower than under classical trust law.
Beneficiaries cannot insist on trustee intervention
simply because they disagree with how the directors are
running the company.

## Why VISTA matters

### Family business succession

The principal use case. A founder wants to:

- Pass legal title to operating-company shares into
  trust;
- Retain trusted family / appointed directors running
  the business;
- Avoid trustee intervention in business decisions;
- Plan for tax-efficient succession.

Classical trust law would force the trustee to second-
guess the family directors. VISTA disables that
obligation, letting the business run as the settlor
intends.

### Single-asset / illiquid holdings

A trust holding shares of a single private company often
cannot diversify (the whole point is to hold those
shares). The Bartlett duty doesn't fit. VISTA solves the
mismatch.

### Reluctance of professional trustees

Without VISTA, many professional trustees decline to take
on operating-business shares because of the management-
oversight liability. VISTA gives professional trustees a
statutory basis to accept the role without that exposure.

## Comparison to Jersey and Cayman regimes

### Jersey classical trust + Article 9A reserved powers

A Jersey trust with Article 9A reserved powers can
achieve part of what VISTA does — the settlor reserves
investment power, removing the trustee's investment
discretion. But the **trustee still has Bartlett-style
monitoring duties** unless specifically negated.

Jersey solutions for family-business holdings often
involve **non-reduction clauses** in the trust deed
plus Article 9A reserved powers, but these are
contractual rather than statutory.

### Cayman STAR trusts

A Cayman STAR trust is a **purpose trust** — it can have
no beneficiaries, only stated purposes. STAR has been
used for orphan structures and family-governance
vehicles. It's a different solution to a different
problem: STAR removes the beneficiary requirement; VISTA
removes the management-oversight duty.

A trust can be **both** STAR and VISTA-equivalent in some
structures — but the statutes operate on different
mechanics. STAR is for purpose-trust structuring;
VISTA is for share-holding-with-disengaged-trustee.

### When to pick which

| Feature | Jersey trust | Cayman STAR | BVI VISTA |
|---|---|---|---|
| Cross-border forced-heirship protection | ✅ (Article 9) | Yes (different mechanism) | Less robust than Jersey/Cayman |
| Settlor-reserved powers | ✅ (Article 9A) | Yes | Yes |
| Trustee disengagement from business | Limited | N/A | ✅ (the point of VISTA) |
| No-beneficiary purpose trust | No | ✅ | No |
| Family-business succession | Works with reserved powers | Possible but indirect | Designed for this |
| Orphan SPV structures | Possible | ✅ (typical use) | Possible |
| Unlimited duration | Yes | ✅ | Yes |

## Drafting and practical considerations

### Designated-company requirements

- BVI BC (most common);
- Specific eligibility under the VISTA Act;
- **Cannot** typically be a non-BVI company without
  specific arrangement.

### ODRs drafting

ODRs are the most critical part of the document. Common
elements:

- **Initial directors** named;
- **Appointing person** (settlor, protector, family
  committee);
- **Removing person**;
- **Quorum** and **voting** in board decisions;
- **Reserved board matters** (specific decisions
  needing wider consent);
- **Succession** of appointing / removing roles.

Poorly drafted ODRs are the most common source of VISTA
disputes.

### Bilateral arrangements

Sometimes a VISTA trust holds an intermediate company
whose subsidiaries are non-BVI operating companies. The
trustee's disengagement applies to the BVI designated
company; the **subsidiary chain** is governed by ordinary
company-law rules of the relevant jurisdictions.

### Migration / re-domiciliation

BVI companies can be re-domiciled to other jurisdictions
under the BC Act. A VISTA trust holding shares in a
company that re-domiciles out of BVI may lose VISTA
protection — careful planning required.

### Beneficiary information rights

VISTA specifically modifies trustee disclosure duties.
Beneficiaries don't have the same right to information
about company affairs as they would under classical
trust law. The trustee can be a "black box" to
beneficiaries with respect to the underlying business.

This can be a feature (privacy) or a bug (beneficiary
mistrust) depending on the family.

## Pitfalls

- **ODRs that don't reflect** family reality — leads to
  disputes;
- **Designating a company** that subsequently changes
  in ways inconsistent with VISTA (e.g. re-domiciling);
- **Mixed regimes** — combining VISTA with non-VISTA
  trust property without clear segregation;
- **Foreign-court orders** against family members in
  forced-heirship or matrimonial proceedings — VISTA's
  trustee-disengagement doesn't protect beneficiaries
  personally;
- **Over-reliance on the disengagement** — VISTA does
  **not** absolve the trustee of fundamental honesty
  and proper administration; it only removes the
  Bartlett-style monitoring duty.

## Recent developments

The VISTA Act has been amended multiple times since 2003
to refine the regime — extending the kinds of company
that can be designated, refining ODR mechanics, and
addressing edge cases. Practitioners should check the
current consolidated Act before drafting.
