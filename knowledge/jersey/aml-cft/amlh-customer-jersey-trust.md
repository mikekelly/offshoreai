# AML/CFT Handbook — CDD on a Jersey Trust Customer

A Jersey trust customer presents distinctive CDD challenges
because **the customer (the trust) does not have its own
legal personality** — it is a contractual arrangement
between the settlor, the trustees, and the beneficiaries.
The Handbook's trust-specific CDD section addresses these
challenges.

This file applies to **Jersey-law trusts**. For foreign-law
trusts as customers of a Jersey regulated business, broadly
similar principles apply with adjustments for the foreign-
law context — see
[`./amlh-customer-types.md`](./amlh-customer-types.md).

## What's distinctive about trust CDD

A Jersey trust customer differs from a typical corporate
customer in three ways:

1. **No legal personality** — the trust itself is not a
   legal person; the trustees hold property and contract on
   the trust's behalf;
2. **Multiple roles** to identify — settlor, trustees,
   protector, enforcer, beneficiaries, default beneficiaries,
   class of objects;
3. **Beneficial-ownership question is complex** — who is
   the beneficial owner of a discretionary trust where no
   specific beneficiary has a vested interest?

The Handbook addresses each.

## Who must be identified — the trust CDD population

The full population for identification on a Jersey trust:

### Settlor

The person who established the trust by transferring
property to the trustees. Identification covers:

- The settlor as a natural person (or, where the settlor is
  itself an entity, the beneficial owners behind the
  entity);
- The settlor's **source of wealth** (often the primary SoW
  question for the entire trust — see
  [`./amlh-edd-source-of-wealth.md`](./amlh-edd-source-of-wealth.md));
- The **circumstances of settlement** — when, where, what
  property, what was the settlor's intent.

For a long-established trust with deceased settlor, the
settlor identification covers historic evidence available;
SoW evidence may rely on contemporaneous documents.

### Contributors (other than the settlor)

Where the trust has received property from contributors
other than the original settlor (later contributions,
appointments-in from other trusts, inheritance additions),
the contributors are also identified and SoW evidence
gathered for each.

### Trustees

The current trustees — typically Jersey-licensed TCB firms.
Identification covers:

- The trustee firm's regulated status (JFSC registration);
- The **individual officers** of the trustee firm
  responsible for the trust;
- The trustee firm's own CDD discipline (the regulated TCB
  conducts CDD on its clients including the trust;
  identification interlocks with the TCB's own controls).

### Protector (if appointed)

The protector exercises specific powers — typically over
trustee appointment / removal, beneficiary changes, or
distribution oversight. Identification:

- The protector as a natural person (or beneficial owners
  if the protector is an entity);
- The **scope of the protector's powers** (per the trust
  deed) — protectors with broad reserved powers may
  themselves be treated as beneficial owners under the
  Handbook's "control by other means" test;
- The protector's relationship to the settlor / beneficiaries
  (family member / professional adviser / independent).

### Enforcer (for non-charitable purpose trusts)

For [purpose trusts](../trusts/purpose-trusts.md) under
the Trusts Law, identification of the enforcer mirrors the
protector identification.

### Beneficiaries — named

Beneficiaries individually named in the trust deed are
identified using natural-person CDD discipline (or, where
the named beneficiary is an entity, the beneficial-owners-
of-the-entity CDD).

### Beneficiaries — class of objects (discretionary)

For discretionary trusts where beneficiaries are defined as
a **class** ("descendants of X", "children of Y", etc.),
the Handbook's expectation is:

- Identify the **class definition** in the trust deed;
- Identify any **currently-known members of the class**
  (typically named in the letter of wishes or addressed in
  the trustees' distribution history);
- Apply CDD to **beneficiaries actually receiving
  distributions** at the time of distribution.

Beneficiaries who are members of the class but have never
received and are not currently expected to receive
distributions may not require full CDD at the trust-CDD
stage — the regulated business applies CDD at the point of
actual benefit.

### Default beneficiaries

Where the trust deed names default beneficiaries (the
ultimate recipients if the discretionary powers are not
exercised), they are identified.

## Beneficial ownership of a Jersey trust

The Handbook's beneficial-ownership framework for trusts:

The natural persons treated as beneficial owners of a trust
typically include:

1. **The settlor**;
2. **Each trustee** (or controllers of corporate trustees);
3. **The protector** (where appointed);
4. **Any natural person** identified in the trust deed
   as a beneficiary entitled to **25% or more** of the trust
   property (fixed-interest trusts);
5. **The class of beneficiaries** (where discretionary,
   identified at class level);
6. **Any natural person** with **control over the trust by
   other means**.

The breadth of the population — settlor + trustees +
protector + beneficiaries + class — is **wider than the
beneficial-owner population for a typical company**. This
is by design: the trust's economic position is distributed
across multiple roles.

## Interaction with the FSDI Law 2020 beneficial-ownership register

The
[FSDI Law 2020](../registries/fs-dpi-law-2020.md)
beneficial-ownership register obligations interact with but
are not identical to the AML/CFT Handbook's CDD framework.
The FSDI register applies to specific entity types; the
Handbook's CDD framework is the operational discipline the
regulated business applies. Both regimes use the **25%
threshold + control-by-other-means** standard, but the
**precise populations identified differ**.

The regulated TCB administering a Jersey trust maintains:

- **The CDD file** under the Handbook;
- **The trust's information** for the FSDI register where
  applicable;
- Reconciliation between the two.

## Purpose and intended nature of the trust

Beyond identification, the Handbook expects the regulated
business to understand:

- **The trust's purpose** — wealth-preservation, succession,
  forced-heirship defence, family-business holding, asset
  protection, charitable, etc.;
- **The intended nature of the relationship** — what
  services the trustee will provide, what transactions are
  contemplated;
- **The settlor's stated intent** — typically captured in
  the letter of wishes;
- **The trust's documented structure** — settlement deed,
  any subsequent variation, addition of property,
  appointment / removal of trustees / protector.

This goes beyond identification — it goes to **whether the
trust's purpose is legitimate and consistent with the
client's documented circumstances**. A trust whose purpose
is unclear, or whose stated purpose does not match the
actual structure, is a red flag.

## EDD on a Jersey trust customer

Trust customers frequently trigger EDD. Common triggers:

- **PEP exposure** at any of the trust roles (settlor,
  trustee, protector, beneficiary);
- **High-risk-country nexus** through any role;
- **Complex structure** where the trust is part of layered
  arrangements;
- **Significant cross-border profile**.

EDD measures particular to trust customers:

- **Source-of-wealth on the settlor** to enhanced standard;
- **Senior-management approval** of the trust relationship;
- **Enhanced ongoing monitoring** of trust distributions
  and additions;
- **Periodic refresh** at higher cadence covering all
  roles (not just the settlor).

## Wealth-management practical reality

For a Jersey wealth-management business with a substantial
trust book — typically a TCB integrated with an investment-
business arm — trust CDD is the **daily operational
discipline** of onboarding teams, relationship managers, and
compliance teams. The discipline of doing it thoroughly is
what distinguishes a competent operation from one that will
fail JFSC thematic-review scrutiny.

## Common trust-CDD failure modes

- **Settlor identification stopping at the immediate
  contributor** rather than tracing to the natural-person
  source of wealth;
- **Class-of-objects beneficiary identification skipped** in
  favour of "no identifiable beneficiary" answer;
- **Protector role under-examined** where the protector
  exercises substantive powers;
- **Letter of wishes not reviewed** when interpreting the
  trust's purpose;
- **Trust deed amendments not tracked** — initial deed
  reviewed, subsequent variations missed;
- **Foreign-law trust patterns** applied to Jersey-law
  trusts without recognising the Jersey-specific
  beneficial-ownership framework.
