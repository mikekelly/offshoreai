# POCL 1999 Part 2 — Confiscation Orders

Part 2 governs **post-conviction confiscation orders** — the
Royal Court's power to require a convicted defendant to pay an
amount equivalent to their benefit from criminal conduct.

## Article 3A — When a confiscation order may be made

The Court may make a confiscation order if:

- The defendant has been **convicted** of an offence to which
  Part 2 applies (broadly: indictable offences);
- The prosecutor or the Court considers a confiscation order
  appropriate;
- The Court is satisfied that the defendant has **benefited**
  from criminal conduct.

## Articles 4-7 — Assessment of benefit

The Court must determine:

- **The benefit** the defendant has obtained from the
  particular offence and from any **course of criminal
  conduct**;
- **The recoverable amount** — typically the benefit, but
  capped at the defendant's **available amount** (assets that
  can practicably be realised);
- **Assumptions** that the Court may make against a defendant
  with a **criminal lifestyle** (six-year lookback on
  unexplained property, expenditure, transfers).

## Articles 8-12 — Confiscation order

The Court orders the defendant to pay the recoverable amount:

- Within a specified **period** (typically 6 months,
  extendable);
- With **interest** on default;
- Backed by **default sentences** of imprisonment if unpaid
  (the original sentence is served first; the default sentence
  is additional).

## Articles 13-16 — Enforcement, variation, reconsideration

- **Enforcement** — typically through the Viscount, with
  realisation of assets;
- **Variation** — the order can be increased if previously
  hidden assets emerge, decreased if assets unrealisable;
- **Reconsideration** — on application by the prosecutor or
  defendant in changed circumstances;
- **Compensation** to victims where appropriate, before
  confiscation;
- **Civil debts** of the defendant can be paid first in some
  cases.

## Confiscation vs civil recovery

| Feature | Confiscation (Part 2) | Civil recovery |
|---|---|---|
| Trigger | Criminal conviction | None — civil claim |
| Standard | Criminal (beyond reasonable doubt) for conviction; civil standard for benefit | Balance of probabilities |
| Defendant | Convicted person | Holder of suspect property |
| Reach | Benefit + criminal lifestyle assumptions | Property identifiable as proceeds |
| Court | Royal Court (Criminal jurisdiction) | Royal Court (Civil) |

Jersey law has civil-recovery mechanisms but they are
**narrower** than the post-Proceeds-of-Crime-Act 2002 UK
regime; in practice, most asset recovery is post-conviction
confiscation under Part 2.

## Practical issues

- **International cooperation** — Jersey-Court confiscation
  orders are enforceable abroad via mutual legal assistance
  treaties and the **Crime (International Co-operation)
  (Jersey) Law 2001**;
- **Realisation** of assets internationally is co-ordinated
  through the Attorney General and the FIU;
- **Trust assets** are reachable where the defendant has an
  equitable interest or where the trust is sham / used to
  conceal criminal property.
