# AML/CFT Handbook — Risk Assessment

The Handbook's risk-assessment chapter is the **foundation** for
every other AML/CFT control. The Money Laundering (Jersey) Order
2008 Article 3 obligation is to **identify and assess** money-
laundering and terrorist-financing risk; the Handbook
operationalises that obligation into two interlocking
assessments — at the **business level** and at the **customer
level** — which drive everything from CDD scope to ongoing
monitoring cadence.

This file is the **section index**. Sub-section content
referenced below is stub pending a primary-source pass against
the current published Handbook.

## Two assessments, one risk-based approach

### Business-wide risk assessment (BWRA)

A documented assessment of the money-laundering and terrorist-
financing risk the **business as a whole** faces. The Handbook
expects:

- A **product / service** risk dimension — which offerings
  carry which risks (e.g. trust administration carries different
  risk than fund services).
- A **customer** risk dimension — the customer base's profile,
  including PEP exposure and high-risk-jurisdiction exposure.
- A **geographic** risk dimension — where customers, assets,
  and counterparties are located.
- A **delivery channel** risk dimension — face-to-face vs
  remote onboarding, intermediary reliance.
- A **transaction** risk dimension — types, sizes, frequencies.

The BWRA is **the document the JFSC asks for first** in any
supervisory visit. Its quality is the principal indicator of
whether the business is operating risk-based.

### Customer risk assessment

For each customer (or class of customer) the business
documents a **risk rating** — typically **low / medium / high /
unacceptable** — driven by the BWRA factors. The customer risk
rating determines:

- The **depth of CDD** at onboarding (standard, simplified,
  enhanced — see [`./amlh-cdd.md`](./amlh-cdd.md) and
  [`./amlh-edd.md`](./amlh-edd.md));
- The **ongoing-monitoring intensity** (see
  [`./amlh-ongoing-monitoring.md`](./amlh-ongoing-monitoring.md));
- Whether **senior-management approval** is required at
  onboarding or refresh.

## Risk factors the Handbook expects to be considered

The factors below are typical Handbook content, applied through
the BWRA and customer risk rating:

### Customer-related

- **PEP status** (foreign or domestic) — see
  [`./amlh-peps.md`](./amlh-peps.md).
- **Adverse media or reputation issues**.
- **Complex ownership structures** that obscure beneficial
  ownership — opaque trusts, layered company structures,
  nominee arrangements.
- **Cash-intensive businesses** as underlying source of funds.
- **Customer types** with elevated inherent risk — see
  [`./amlh-customer-types.md`](./amlh-customer-types.md).

### Geographic

- **High-risk jurisdictions** — FATF Black List, FATF Grey
  List, JFSC-listed high-risk countries, EU high-risk-third-
  country list. See
  [`./amlh-high-risk-countries.md`](./amlh-high-risk-countries.md).
- **Jurisdictions with prevalent corruption, sanctions
  regimes, or terrorism financing**.
- **Customer-country / asset-country / counterparty-country**
  considered separately and in combination.

### Product / service

- **Private banking** — relatively higher risk.
- **Trust and corporate-service-provider services** — wide
  variation.
- **Cross-border payments / correspondent relationships** —
  elevated.
- **Virtual assets / VASP business** — high-risk-by-default
  under FATF and JFSC framing.

### Delivery channel

- **Remote (non-face-to-face) onboarding** — elevated risk
  factor that typically triggers additional verification
  steps.
- **Reliance on third-party introducers** — both a risk and an
  operational efficiency lever; the Handbook conditions the
  reliance carefully.

### Transaction

- **Large or unusual transactions** for the customer profile.
- **Structuring patterns** (transactions sized to avoid
  reporting thresholds).
- **Pass-through patterns** with no economic substance.

## The risk-based approach in supervisory practice

The JFSC expects the business to be able to demonstrate, on
inspection:

1. A current, documented **BWRA** updated at least annually;
2. A **customer risk methodology** that maps BWRA factors to
   ratings;
3. **Evidence of application** — sampled customer files
   showing the rating reasoning, the CDD applied, and any
   senior-management approvals;
4. **Continuous improvement** — feedback loops from SAR
   patterns, regulator engagement, and the Handbook's
   periodic updates.

A business that has a CDD process but cannot connect it back
to the BWRA is doing **rules-based** AML, not **risk-based**
AML — and is liable to enforcement criticism.

## Interaction with the Codes of Practice

The regulated business's
[Code of Practice](../financial-regulation/codes-of-practice.md)
also imposes risk-management obligations at the business level
(Principle 4 in the FSL-class Codes — see
[`../financial-regulation/cop-tcb-principles.md`](../financial-regulation/cop-tcb-principles.md)).
The BWRA satisfies the AML-specific part of that Principle;
together they form one integrated risk-management programme.
