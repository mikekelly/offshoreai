# AML/CFT Handbook — CDD on Individual Customers

Natural-person customers — high-net-worth individuals, family
office principals, expatriate clients, founders, professionals
— are the **most common customer type** for many Jersey
wealth-management businesses. The CDD discipline for
individuals is correspondingly the **most-practised
discipline** but also the one where casual habits develop and
JFSC enforcement findings recur.

## Identification

For a natural-person customer:

- **Full name** including any prior names, maiden names,
  aliases;
- **Date and place of birth**;
- **Residential address** (current; with history for higher-
  risk relationships);
- **Nationality and citizenship** (multiple where applicable);
- **Identity number** — passport for international clients,
  national ID where applicable, additional documents for
  higher-risk profiles;
- **Tax-residence jurisdiction(s)** — relevant for CRS /
  FATCA reporting;
- **Marital status** and family-relationship information
  where relevant to the relationship purpose.

## Verification

The Handbook expects verification on **reliable and
independent evidence**:

### Identity

- **Passport or government-issued photo ID** — original or
  certified copy with up-to-date validity;
- For higher-risk relationships, **multiple identity
  documents** corroborating each other.

### Address

- **Utility bill** within prescribed age (typically 3
  months);
- **Bank statement** within prescribed age;
- **Government correspondence** (tax, electoral roll, etc.);
- For higher-risk relationships, **multiple address
  verifications**.

### Tax-residence

- **Self-certification** is the standard CRS / FATCA
  mechanism;
- **Corroborating evidence** — tax-residence certificate,
  tax-returns evidence, residence-visa evidence — for
  higher-risk relationships.

### Certified copies

For non-original documents, the Handbook expects
certification by a **trusted intermediary** — lawyer,
notary, regulated trust company, accountant — with the
certification dated, signed, and specifying the certifying
person's capacity. Self-certified documents are
insufficient.

### Electronic verification

The Handbook permits electronic verification (database
checks, electronic-ID systems) subject to:

- The verifier being reliable;
- The data being current;
- The overall reliability standard being met for the risk
  level.

For higher-risk individual customers, electronic verification
is typically supplemented with documentary verification.

## Understanding purpose and intended nature

Beyond identification, the Handbook requires understanding:

- **Why the customer is opening the relationship** —
  wealth-preservation, succession planning, investment
  platform, business-related, etc.;
- **What services the customer will use** — discretionary
  investment management, trust administration, banking,
  custody;
- **Expected transaction profile** — frequency, types,
  sizes, counterparties;
- **Time horizon** — short-term, long-term, multi-
  generational.

This is **purpose discovery**, not box-ticking. A
relationship the firm cannot articulate the purpose of
should not proceed.

## Source of funds (initial transactions)

For the funds expected to be invested or held:

- **Origin** — employment income, business proceeds,
  inheritance, investment realisations, etc.;
- **Path** — what accounts / institutions the funds flowed
  through before reaching the firm;
- **Evidence** — bank statements, sale contracts,
  employment records, inheritance documents.

The standard for SoF is **proportionate to the relationship
size and risk**. A modest-relationship individual provides a
lighter SoF evidence package; a HNW relationship provides
robust SoF documentation.

## Source of wealth (background)

For higher-risk individual customers — and for most HNW
clients in practice — the Handbook expects **source-of-wealth
(SoW) evidence** distinct from SoF. See
[`./amlh-edd-source-of-wealth.md`](./amlh-edd-source-of-wealth.md).

The discipline of SoW evidence is the **single most
consequential CDD element** for HNW individual
relationships.

## PEP and sanctions screening

Standard at onboarding and on ongoing-monitoring cadence:

- **PEP screening** (see [`./amlh-peps-screening.md`](./amlh-peps-screening.md));
- **Sanctions screening** (Jersey, UN, UK OFSI, EU, US OFAC
  as applicable);
- **Adverse-media screening**;
- **Match-disposition** and escalation per the firm's
  procedures.

## Risk rating

The individual customer's risk rating drives every subsequent
CDD intensity decision. Factors:

- **PEP status** (self or family / associates);
- **Country-risk** through residence / nationality / asset
  location / counterparty;
- **Source-of-wealth complexity** — straightforward
  employment / business income (low risk for SoW) vs
  multi-jurisdictional patchwork (higher risk);
- **Product / service** profile;
- **Customer behaviour** during onboarding (transparent and
  cooperative vs evasive);
- **Adverse-media exposure**.

## Particular individual-customer scenarios

### HNW founder / entrepreneur

- SoW heavily focused on business-formation / sale evidence;
- Often UHNW pre-IPO / post-IPO with complex equity
  positions;
- Family-member screening across multiple jurisdictions.

### Expatriate professional

- Multi-jurisdictional tax-residence history;
- Employment income source typically well-documented;
- Forced-heirship considerations for some home jurisdictions.

### Family member of an existing customer

- Often onboarded on the back of an existing relationship
  (settlor's spouse, founder's child);
- The firm's prior CDD on the related customer does not
  substitute for fresh CDD on the new individual;
- Family-link verification an additional element.

### High-profile public figure (sport / media / political)

- Adverse-media exposure typically substantial;
- Reputational and operational considerations elevated;
- PEP status considerations for political figures.

### Older / retired client with legacy wealth

- SoW evidence may rely on historic documentation;
- Family-succession planning often within the engagement
  scope from the outset;
- Capacity considerations as the relationship matures (see
  the [Capacity and Self-Determination Law 2016](../health-and-capacity/index.md)
  in some matters).

## Common individual-CDD failure points

JFSC supervisory themes:

- **Verification staleness** — initial verification at
  onboarding never refreshed;
- **Tax-residence assertions** unsupported by corroborating
  evidence;
- **SoW gaps for HNW clients** treated as acceptable when
  the relationship size warranted deeper evidence;
- **Family-link verification weak** — relationship to
  existing customers asserted but not verified;
- **PEP screening missed family-member triggers**;
- **Adverse-media handled lightly** — match generated, file
  closed without substantive review;
- **Updated identity / address documents not refreshed**
  on changes in customer circumstances.
