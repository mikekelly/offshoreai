# AML/CFT Handbook — CDD on Regulated-Institution Customers (Reliance)

Where the customer is itself a **regulated financial
institution** in an **equivalent jurisdiction**, the
Handbook permits a calibrated approach — the regulated-
institution customer is already subject to its own AML/CFT
regime, which may permit the Jersey firm to rely on its
counterparty's CDD discipline in defined ways.

This file covers two related topics:

1. **Identification standards** for regulated-institution
   customers;
2. **The reliance regime** — when the Jersey firm can rely
   on a counterparty's CDD on the underlying customers
   served through the relationship.

## What counts as a regulated-institution customer

The Handbook's definition typically includes:

- **Banks** licensed in the customer's jurisdiction;
- **Investment firms** regulated for MiFID-equivalent
  activities;
- **Fund managers** under AIFMD-equivalent regulation;
- **Insurance and reinsurance companies** subject to
  Solvency II equivalent supervision;
- **Trust companies and corporate-services providers**
  licensed for fiduciary activity;
- **Designated non-financial businesses** (DNFBPs) where
  subject to AML supervision (lawyers, accountants under
  prescribed conditions).

The qualifier is **regulated for AML/CFT purposes** — not
merely licensed for some commercial activity.

## The equivalent-jurisdiction test

For the Handbook's simplified-CDD and reliance treatment to
apply, the customer's jurisdiction must be considered to
have AML/CFT standards **equivalent** to Jersey's. The
Handbook typically references:

- **FATF membership** of the jurisdiction;
- **MoneyVal evaluations** (for European jurisdictions);
- **JFSC-published equivalent-jurisdiction list** where
  available;
- **Adverse considerations** — FATF Black / Grey listing,
  significant AML deficiencies in regulator reports.

A customer that is regulated in a **non-equivalent**
jurisdiction is treated to **full CDD discipline** with
likely EDD application — the regulated status alone is
insufficient.

## Identification

For a regulated-institution customer:

- **Legal name and structure** verified through public
  registry;
- **Regulatory licence(s)** verified through the relevant
  regulator's public register (FCA, BaFin, SFC, etc.);
- **Senior officers and beneficial owners** identified at
  the level appropriate to risk (often light for listed-
  group institutions, fuller for private institutions);
- **Recent regulatory standing** — public enforcement
  history, sanctions, any restrictions.

## Simplified CDD where permissible

Under the Handbook's simplified-due-diligence (SDD)
provisions, certain regulated-institution customers may
qualify for reduced verification:

- **Listed financial institutions** on recognised regulated
  markets — beneficial-owner identification typically not
  required given the public listing;
- **Wholly-owned subsidiaries** of regulated entities —
  reliance on the parent's regulated status;
- **Government-owned financial institutions** in
  equivalent jurisdictions.

SDD permits **reduced verification**, not **zero
verification** — the obligation to identify and to monitor
remains.

## The reliance regime

The most distinctive operational feature: where the customer
is a regulated institution, the Jersey firm may **rely on the
customer's own CDD** in respect of underlying clients
served **through** the relationship.

### When reliance applies

Reliance is permitted when:

- The relied-upon institution is **regulated for AML/CFT** in
  an **equivalent jurisdiction**;
- There is a **written arrangement** between the Jersey firm
  and the relied-upon institution;
- The Jersey firm is **confident** that the relied-upon
  institution applies AML/CFT standards equivalent to those
  it would itself apply;
- The Jersey firm can **obtain CDD records on demand** from
  the relied-upon institution.

### What can be relied upon

Typically:

- **Identification** of the underlying customer;
- **Verification** evidence held by the relied-upon
  institution;
- **Beneficial-owner identification**;
- **Initial PEP and sanctions screening**.

### What cannot be relied upon

Even where reliance applies:

- **The Jersey firm remains accountable** for the underlying
  CDD — reliance does not transfer regulatory
  responsibility;
- **Ongoing monitoring** of the relationship by the Jersey
  firm continues at its own discipline;
- **The Jersey firm must obtain CDD records** when
  requested by the JFSC or law enforcement;
- **In-life events** affecting the underlying customers
  (PEP status change, sanctions designation) must be picked
  up by the Jersey firm's own monitoring, not solely
  by the relied-upon institution.

### Documenting the reliance arrangement

The reliance is typically documented in a **written
agreement** covering:

- **Scope of reliance** — what specifically is relied upon;
- **Record-access** — the Jersey firm's right to CDD records
  on demand;
- **Notification** of in-life events affecting underlying
  customers;
- **Termination** of the reliance arrangement;
- **Indemnities** and liability allocation where
  appropriate.

## Common reliance scenarios

### Jersey custodian for an EU fund manager

The customer is an EU-regulated AIFM marketing into Jersey.
The Jersey custodian relies on the AIFM's CDD on the fund's
investors, subject to written reliance arrangement and
record-access rights.

### Jersey administrator for a Cayman fund

The customer is a Cayman-regulated fund. The Jersey
administrator's CDD reliance scope depends on Cayman's
equivalent-jurisdiction status and the specific Cayman
regulator's AML expectations.

### Jersey investment business with introducer relationship

An equivalent-jurisdiction regulated introducer refers
underlying clients to the Jersey firm. Reliance on the
introducer's CDD subject to the written arrangement and
the introducer's regulated standing.

## Common reliance failure modes

- **Reliance asserted without written arrangement** —
  practical CDD reliance occurs but is not documented;
- **Equivalent-jurisdiction assumptions wrong** — relying
  on a non-equivalent jurisdiction;
- **Record-access untested** until the moment a JFSC
  request arrives;
- **In-life event monitoring** assumed to flow
  automatically from the relied-upon institution when it
  does not;
- **Regulated-status verification stale** — initial
  verification good, subsequent regulator-status changes
  not picked up.

## Why this matters for wealth management

For Jersey wealth-management businesses with substantial
**intermediated client flow** — institutional investors,
fund-of-funds, multi-family-office referrals — the reliance
regime is operationally significant. Done well, it
streamlines onboarding for regulated counterparties; done
badly, it generates JFSC criticism.
