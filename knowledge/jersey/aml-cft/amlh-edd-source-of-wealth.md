# AML/CFT Handbook — Source of Wealth (EDD)

**Source of wealth (SoW)** is the single most common
enhanced-due-diligence failure point in JFSC enforcement
records, and the single most consequential evidence
discipline for an EDD relationship. This file is the
operational deep dive for the SoW pillar of EDD.

## SoW vs SoF — the distinction

| | Source of Funds (SoF) | Source of Wealth (SoW) |
|---|---|---|
| **Question** | Where did *these* funds come from? | Where did the customer's *cumulative wealth* originate? |
| **Time horizon** | Specific transaction or initial deposit | Customer's economic history |
| **Evidence standard for EDD** | Primary documentation (bank statements, sale contracts) | Primary documentation of wealth-origin events (employment history, business sale, inheritance, asset disposal) |
| **Failure mode** | Funds appear from unverified source | Wealth narrative does not explain the assets observed |

For a high-net-worth client investing £5m via a Jersey
structure:

- **SoF** might be evidenced by a £5m incoming wire from a
  named bank account in a named jurisdiction.
- **SoW** asks: how did this customer accumulate £50m+ over
  their career? Employment / business / inheritance /
  investment records must reconcile the wealth narrative.

## What SoW evidence the Handbook expects

The expectation is **documented, primary-source evidence** —
not customer declarations alone. By category:

### Employment / professional services income

- Employment history with corroborating evidence (HR records,
  pay slips, employment contracts, professional-body
  membership);
- For executive-level wealth: shareholding details, option /
  RSU grant records, vesting timetables;
- Cumulative remuneration narrative matching the wealth
  profile.

### Business ownership / sale

- Founding / shareholding history (shareholder registers,
  formation documents, capitalisation tables);
- Operating-business financials (audited accounts for the
  years relevant to wealth accumulation);
- Sale-of-business evidence (SPA, completion accounts,
  consideration mechanics, escrow arrangements);
- Post-sale residual interests (earn-out, deferred
  consideration, retained equity).

### Investment / portfolio appreciation

- Portfolio statements covering the period of accumulation;
- Custodian or broker confirmations;
- Tax returns showing investment income / gains
  (jurisdiction-permitting);
- Pre-tax-residency wealth evidence if the wealth predates
  the client's current tax-residence jurisdiction.

### Inheritance / gift

- Death certificate and probate / grant of representation
  for inheritance;
- Trust deeds and distribution records for trust-derived
  wealth;
- Deed of gift / contemporaneous evidence for inter-vivos
  gifts;
- Family-tree corroboration where relevant.

### Real-estate sale

- Title documents pre-sale;
- Sale contract / completion statement;
- Conveyancing records;
- Capital-gain narrative consistent with the broader wealth
  profile.

### Crypto / digital assets

- The most difficult SoW category given crypto's history.
  See [`amlh-sector-specific.md`](./amlh-sector-specific.md)
  and the
  [crypto sector file](../sectors/crypto-digital-assets.md);
- Evidence required: acquisition history (early-stage
  purchase or mining records), wallet provenance, on-chain
  analysis where available, exchange records for
  realisations into fiat;
- Tax-reporting evidence where the client's home
  jurisdiction has applied crypto-tax treatment.

## When the SoW evidence is incomplete

The Handbook acknowledges that **perfect SoW evidence is
sometimes impossible** — for very-large legacy family
wealth, for example, the original source may predate
modern record-keeping. In those cases:

1. **Document the gap explicitly** — what evidence is
   available, what is not, and why;
2. **Apply compensating measures** — heightened ongoing
   monitoring, lower transaction-monitoring alert thresholds,
   periodic refresh at the highest cadence;
3. **Senior-management approval** of the relationship despite
   incomplete SoW;
4. **Board awareness** for material exposures;
5. **Independent verification where possible** — third-party
   corroborations (legal-profession declarations,
   accountancy-firm statements of historic wealth).

The JFSC's expectation is **not** zero-uncertainty SoW
verification; it is a **risk-based, documented programme
of evidence and compensating mitigation**.

## Common SoW failure modes (JFSC enforcement themes)

- **Declaration-only SoW** — customer questionnaire
  responses unsupported by documentary evidence;
- **SoW based on SoF** — confusing the two (the customer's
  bank statement showing they received a payment is SoF, not
  SoW for the wealth that funded the payment);
- **Generic SoW narratives** — "client is a successful
  businessman" without the specific business / role /
  timeline / evidence package;
- **No reconciliation between SoW narrative and observed
  wealth** — narrative explains £10m but the relationship is
  managing £100m;
- **No SoW refresh** — initial verification at onboarding,
  no subsequent refresh as the relationship grows or as new
  wealth-events occur;
- **Inadequate handling of inherited / family wealth** — the
  multi-generational source is not traced or documented.

A SoW programme that addresses each of these in its design
will pass thematic-review scrutiny.

## SoW for specific client categories

### PEPs

For PEPs, SoW evidentiary standard is **at its highest**.
The relationship between the PEP's public position and the
SoW narrative must be **explainable on the SoW's own terms**
— not "the client is a senior official so they have
significant wealth" but "this specific wealth came from
this specific source, evidenced by these specific
documents". See [`amlh-peps.md`](./amlh-peps.md).

### Family-wealth multi-generational

For very-large legacy family wealth (the GCC, India, parts of
Europe), SoW often spans several generations. The Handbook
accepts that:

- **Documentary evidence will be patchy** for older
  generations;
- **Family-business history** can be the primary SoW
  evidence;
- **Independent third-party corroboration** (legal /
  accounting firms with long-standing family relationships)
  is acceptable where direct documentation is unavailable;
- **Senior-management sign-off** is required.

### Recently-realised wealth (founders, sale of business)

For a founder client who has just realised significant
wealth through a sale or IPO, SoW evidence is typically
**robust and recent** — capitalisation tables, founding
documents, sale contracts, completion accounts. This is the
easiest SoW category to document.

### Crypto / digital-asset wealth

The most difficult category. See above and the
[crypto sector file](../sectors/crypto-digital-assets.md).

## Wealth-management practical reality

For an international wealth-management business serving
high-net-worth clients, SoW is the **daily operational
discipline** the relationship managers and onboarding teams
spend most time on. The compliance function's role is to:

- Maintain a **documented SoW methodology** by client
  category;
- Provide **training** to RMs on SoW evidence gathering;
- Provide **escalation procedures** for difficult SoW
  cases;
- **Sample and review** SoW files periodically;
- Track **SoW-related enforcement themes** at the JFSC and
  internationally.
