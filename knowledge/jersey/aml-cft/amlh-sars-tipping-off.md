# AML/CFT Handbook — Tipping-Off

**Tipping-off** is the criminal offence of disclosing
information likely to prejudice an investigation following
(or in contemplation of) a suspicious-activity report. The
offence is set out in
[POCL Article 35](./pocl-article-35.md); the Handbook
operationalises the discipline by which staff and the firm
avoid committing it.

This is **load-bearing operationally** — the practical
challenge of managing customer relationships while a SAR is
under investigation, without communicating anything that
would alert the customer to the suspicion, is among the
**hardest day-to-day demands** on a regulated business.

## The offence

A person commits tipping-off if:

- Knowing or suspecting that a SAR has been made (or is
  contemplated);
- Disclosing to **another person** information likely to
  prejudice the investigation that follows.

The "another person" can be:

- **The customer** themselves;
- **Related parties** (family, business associates,
  advisers) who would communicate to the customer;
- **Other firm staff** outside the SAR-handling pipeline
  (where the staff would discuss with the customer or
  related parties);
- **Third parties** (counterparties, external advisers,
  introducers) where the disclosure could flow back.

The offence carries criminal penalties including
imprisonment. It applies independently of any other AML/CFT
offence.

## The protected period

The tipping-off discipline applies from the moment a SAR is
contemplated (i.e. when the staff member identifies
suspicion) through:

- The internal investigation period;
- After external SAR filing;
- Through any law-enforcement investigation that follows;
- Until the investigation concludes or the prohibition is
  formally lifted.

In practice, the discipline applies **for the indefinite
future** for the relationships involved — the firm
generally cannot disclose the historical SAR even after
investigations close, and certainly cannot disclose to the
customer that a SAR was made.

## What's prohibited

Specific prohibited disclosures:

### To the customer

- That a SAR has been filed about them;
- That a SAR is being contemplated;
- That the firm has concerns about their conduct or
  transactions;
- That law enforcement has made enquiries about them;
- That the firm has changed any aspect of the relationship
  because of suspicion.

### To related parties

- Same as above where the related party would communicate
  to the customer;
- Even where the related party would not communicate,
  disclosure typically requires careful internal
  authorisation given the risk of indirect tipping-off.

### Within the firm

- Discussion of the SAR with staff outside the prescribed
  pipeline;
- Email / chat references to the SAR in unsecured systems;
- Inclusion of SAR-related information in routine reporting
  that goes beyond the prescribed audience.

## What's permitted

The discipline does **not** prohibit:

- **Continued normal communication** with the customer on
  the unrelated aspects of the relationship — answering
  routine queries, processing routine instructions,
  providing periodic statements;
- **Internal SAR-pipeline communication** — between the
  staff who filed the internal SAR, the MLRO, the Deputy
  MLRO, the compliance team within the SAR pipeline, and
  (where necessary) senior management and the board;
- **Communication with regulated counterparts** under
  prescribed information-sharing arrangements (group-level
  AML/CFT, regulated counterparties under MLO 2008 sharing
  permissions);
- **Communication with law enforcement** — direct sharing
  with the FIU is the whole point of the SAR regime;
- **Communication with the firm's external counsel** for
  the purpose of the firm's own legal protection.

## The practical challenge

The hardest practical situations:

### Customer asks a direct question

The customer asks "is there a problem with my account?"
or "have you reported anything about me?" The firm cannot
confirm or deny without tipping-off. The Handbook expects
**neutral handling** — neither confirming nor denying,
neither suggesting concern nor providing false reassurance.
The discipline is **maintained politeness without
substantive content**.

### Customer asks for a service that would tip-off if delayed

Continuing the service may be a SAR-pipeline issue (the SAR
investigation is examining the legitimacy of the
transaction); refusing to continue tips off. The MLRO may
apply for **DAML consent** from the FIU to continue the
transaction while the investigation proceeds, or may
escalate to the FIU for guidance.

### Relationship exit

Where the firm decides to exit the relationship as a result
of the SAR investigation, the exit must be **timed and
communicated** carefully:

- **Reasons given** that do not reveal the SAR (often
  generic "commercial review" or "strategic decision");
- **Exit timeline** that does not coincide suspiciously
  with any specific transaction or event;
- **Asset return** processed in line with normal exit
  arrangements.

This is one of the most operationally sensitive moments in
AML/CFT practice.

### Cross-firm communications

For a wealth-management group with multiple regulated
entities — see the
[group-MLRO architecture](./amlh-controls-mlro.md) — SAR
information may flow between entities within prescribed
permissions. But communication with **external firms** (a
correspondent bank, an external custodian, an introducer)
is tightly constrained.

## Operational mitigations

The discipline of avoiding tipping-off is operationalised
through:

- **Training** — staff trained specifically on tipping-off
  scenarios with realistic examples;
- **Written procedures** for SAR-pipeline communications;
- **Customer-communication protocols** for the
  investigation period;
- **System markers** — customer files flagged for SAR-
  sensitive handling without the flag itself being
  disclosed in routine reports;
- **Exit procedures** with documented templates for
  reasoning;
- **Senior-management oversight** of exit decisions in
  SAR cases.

## Common tipping-off failure modes

JFSC and law-enforcement enforcement themes:

- **Inadvertent disclosure** by staff who did not know a
  SAR was open on the customer;
- **Indirect tipping-off** through changed customer
  communications patterns;
- **Exit-reason transparency** — firm explains the exit in
  terms that reveal the underlying concern;
- **System leakage** — SAR flags visible in customer-
  facing portals or correspondence;
- **External-party disclosure** to counterparties or
  introducers without authority;
- **Documentary evidence** of SAR in places customer or
  their advisers could access (subpoenaed in later
  litigation).

## Why this matters for wealth management

For wealth-management businesses with high-touch customer
relationships — RMs in regular contact, family-office
relationships spanning years, in-person meetings — the
tipping-off discipline is operationally taxing. The RM may
have personal relationships with the customer and yet must
operate under prohibition that the customer must never
know about.

Training, written procedures, and culture are the controls
that make the discipline workable. Failures here can be
career-ending for individuals and licence-threatening for
firms.
