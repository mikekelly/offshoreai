# Proceeds of Crime (Supervisory Bodies) (Jersey) Law 2008

The **Proceeds of Crime (Supervisory Bodies) (Jersey) Law
2008** establishes the **statutory supervisory framework** for
AML/CFT supervision in Jersey — designating the
[JFSC](../financial-regulation/jfsc.md) and other bodies as
**supervisory authorities** for the Schedule 2 businesses
within their respective remits, and giving them statutory
powers to supervise AML/CFT compliance.

## Why this Law sits separately from POCL 1999

The [Proceeds of Crime (Jersey) Law 1999](./proceeds-of-crime-law-1999.md)
creates the **substantive ML offences** and the regulated-
business gateway (Schedule 2). The
[Money Laundering Order 2008](./money-laundering-order-2008.md)
creates the **operative compliance obligations**.

This 2008 Law fills the third gap: **who supervises whom, with
what powers**. It is the **supervisory framework** that gives
the JFSC and other bodies the legal authority to:

- Inspect supervised businesses for AML/CFT compliance;
- Issue directions and conditions;
- Take enforcement action (in addition to powers under the
  substantive regulatory Laws);
- Cooperate with other supervisory authorities.

## Supervisory bodies designated

The Law designates supervisory authorities by sector. The
principal ones include:

- **[JFSC](../financial-regulation/jfsc.md)** — for financial-
  services Schedule 2 businesses (TCB, FSB, IB, banking,
  insurance, MSB, GIMB, certified funds, AIFs).
- **Jersey Gambling Commission** — for gambling businesses
  within Schedule 2.
- **Law Society of Jersey** — for legal practitioners.
- **Designated Professional Bodies** — for accountants and
  certain other DNFBPs.

For most Jersey-supervised businesses in the financial-
services sector, **the JFSC is the supervisory body** under
this Law and the
[Financial Services Commission (Jersey) Law 1998](../financial-regulation/jfsc-law-1998.md)
simultaneously. The two statutes work together to give the
JFSC its full supervisory toolkit.

## Practitioner interaction

For a JFSC-supervised business, this Law is rarely cited
directly in operational compliance work — it is the
infrastructure on which the JFSC's supervisory practice rests.
It surfaces explicitly when:

- The JFSC asserts its supervisory authority in an
  enforcement action;
- A supervisory-body interaction issue arises in a
  cross-sector business (e.g. a firm regulated by both the
  JFSC and a Designated Professional Body for different
  parts of its activities);
- The business needs to confirm the relevant supervisory
  authority for a specific Schedule 2 activity.
