# AML/CFT Handbook — Suspicious Activity Reporting (SARs)

The SAR regime is the **outward-facing** end of the AML/CFT
programme. When the business **knows or suspects** money
laundering or terrorist financing, the
[Proceeds of Crime (Jersey) Law 1999](./pocl-article-34.md)
imposes a **reporting obligation** to the Financial
Intelligence Unit (FIU), and a **tipping-off offence** for
disclosing the report to the subject.

The Handbook operationalises both — the internal SAR process
(staff → MLRO) and the external SAR process (MLRO → FIU).

## The two-stage SAR pipeline

### Internal SAR

Any staff member who **knows or suspects** money laundering or
terrorist financing — or who has reasonable grounds for
knowing or suspecting — files an **internal SAR** with the
[MLRO](./amlh-internal-controls.md).

The internal SAR captures the staff member's observations,
the customer / transaction context, and any relevant evidence.
The MLRO investigates: reviews CDD, transaction records,
related-party information, open-source media, and the broader
customer profile.

### External SAR

If the MLRO concludes after investigation that there is
**knowledge or suspicion** of money laundering or terrorist
financing, the MLRO files an **external SAR** with the FIU
(the Jersey Financial Crimes Unit, hosted by the States of
Jersey Police).

The MLRO documents the investigation and the SAR-or-no-SAR
decision in either case. **A decision not to file is itself
documented** with reasoning.

## Defence-against-money-laundering (DAML) consent

The MLRO may, where the business intends to continue with a
transaction that may constitute a prohibited act, seek
**DAML consent** from the FIU before proceeding. Without DAML
consent the transaction may not be undertaken; with consent,
the relevant
[POCL offences](./pocl-article-30.md) are not committed.

The Handbook expects the DAML process to be documented and
the response handling to be defined operationally — including
escalation if the FIU does not respond within the prescribed
timeframe.

## Tipping-off offence

Disclosing to the subject of a SAR (or to any third party
likely to prejudice an investigation) that a SAR has been
made or is contemplated is a **criminal offence** under
[POCL Article 35](./pocl-article-35.md). The Handbook expects:

- **Staff training** specifically on the tipping-off
  prohibition;
- **Customer communications protocols** that protect against
  inadvertent tipping;
- **Documented exit procedures** where the business needs to
  exit a relationship in a manner that does not constitute
  tipping-off.

The interaction with **ordinary customer communications**
during the SAR investigation period is operationally tricky
and a frequent practitioner question.

## Records

Every internal SAR (whether or not it became an external SAR)
is retained for the prescribed period — see
[`./amlh-record-keeping.md`](./amlh-record-keeping.md).

## What the SAR programme demonstrates to the JFSC

The JFSC's view is that **a regulated business with a
plausible customer book that files no SARs is operating
deficient AML/CFT** — either the monitoring isn't detecting
suspicion, or the MLRO is not escalating, or staff are not
filing internal SARs. The **SAR pipeline output** is a key
inspection focus.

Conversely, a high SAR volume is not in itself protective —
the JFSC looks for **quality and proportionality**, not
volume per se.
