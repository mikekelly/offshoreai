# Schedule 2 Supervised Businesses — Jersey

**Schedule 2** of the **Proceeds of Crime (Jersey) Law 1999**
sets out the categories of business subject to Jersey **AML
supervision**.

## What Schedule 2 covers

Schedule 2 lists categories of activity captured as "supervised
business" — i.e. subject to the Money Laundering (Jersey) Order
2008 compliance obligations and JFSC supervision (or, for some
categories, supervision by other bodies).

Categories include:

- **Financial services activities** captured under FS(J)L 1998,
  Banking Business Law 1991, CIF Law 1988, Insurance Business
  Law 1996 — banks, investment firms, fund administrators,
  insurers, etc.;
- **Trust company business** and **fund services business**;
- **Lawyers and accountants** when engaged in specified
  activities (e.g. creating companies / trusts, holding client
  money, conducting transactions on behalf of clients);
- **Estate agents** and **property professionals**;
- **High-value dealers** (sellers of precious metals, antiques,
  art);
- **Casinos and gaming** operators;
- **Virtual Asset Service Providers (VASPs)**;
- **Trust and company service providers** more broadly;
- Other categories as the regime expands.

This corresponds to FATF's category of **Designated Non-Financial
Businesses and Professions (DNFBPs)** plus the financial-services
sectors.

## What Schedule 2 inclusion means

A business in Schedule 2 must:

- **Comply** with the Money Laundering (Jersey) Order 2008
  (CDD, record-keeping, internal controls, reporting, training);
- **Submit** to AML supervision by the JFSC (or in some cases
  a profession-specific supervisor);
- **Pay** any prescribed AML supervision fees;
- **Receive** site visits, document requests, and other
  supervisory engagement.

## Expansion of scope

The Schedule has been **expanded over time** as FATF and
Moneyval expectations have broadened:

- Originally focused on financial-services activities;
- Extended to lawyers and accountants under FATF DNFBP
  recommendations;
- Extended to real-estate, high-value dealers, gaming;
- Most recently extended to VASPs reflecting the crypto sector's
  AML risks.
