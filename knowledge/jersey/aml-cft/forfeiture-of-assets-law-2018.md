# Forfeiture of Assets (Civil Proceedings) (Jersey) Law 2018

The **Forfeiture of Assets (Civil Proceedings) (Jersey) Law
2018** establishes a **civil forfeiture regime** for property
suspected to be the proceeds of, or intended for use in,
unlawful conduct — distinct from and supplementing the
**criminal confiscation** regime under the
[Proceeds of Crime (Jersey) Law 1999](./proceeds-of-crime-law-1999.md).

## What the Law does

The Law:

1. Creates a **civil-proceedings forfeiture regime** — assets
   can be forfeited on the **civil standard of proof** (balance
   of probabilities), without a prior criminal conviction.
2. Empowers the **Attorney General** to bring proceedings before
   the **Royal Court** seeking forfeiture of property.
3. Provides for **freezing orders** during proceedings to
   preserve the property pending the Court's determination.
4. Addresses **innocent owners and third-party rights** —
   protections for persons who can demonstrate property was
   acquired in good faith for value.

## Why this Law exists alongside POCL 1999

POCL 1999 confiscation requires a **criminal conviction**
(Part 2 of POCL 1999 — see
[`./pocl-part-2-confiscation.md`](./pocl-part-2-confiscation.md)).
The Forfeiture Law 2018 fills the gap where:

- The criminal-standard threshold cannot be reached;
- The defendant is abroad and cannot be brought to trial;
- The criminal case has not been brought (e.g. statutory
  limitation, evidential difficulty);
- A civil action is more proportionate to the property at
  stake.

It is a **last-resort** civil enforcement tool used in
specific high-value or international cases.

## Practitioner interaction

For a JFSC-supervised business, the Law most often appears
when:

- The business has held property that becomes subject to a
  freezing order (e.g. trust assets, custody accounts);
- The business needs to **respond to a freezing order**
  promptly without violating
  [tipping-off](./pocl-article-35.md) restrictions;
- Customer relationships intersect with **international
  enforcement cooperation** under the
  [Criminal Justice (International Co-operation) Law 2001](./cicl-articles-index.md).

The Handbook treats this as part of the broader sanctions /
freezing / enforcement landscape — see
[`./amlh-sanctions.md`](./amlh-sanctions.md) for the
operational framing.
