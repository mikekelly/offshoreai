# AML/CFT Handbook — CDD on a Jersey Foundation Customer

A Jersey **foundation** is a civil-law-flavoured body
corporate established under the
[Foundations (Jersey) Law 2009](../foundations/foundations-law-2009.md).
Unlike a trust, a foundation is a **legal person** in its own
right — but the AML/CFT discipline applied to it shares much
of the structure of the
[Jersey-trust customer CDD](./amlh-customer-jersey-trust.md)
because the underlying economic position has similar shape.

## Identification population

For a Jersey foundation, identification covers:

### Founder

The person who established the foundation by transferring
property at formation. Identification covers:

- Identity verification on the same standards as a trust
  settlor;
- **Source of wealth** evidence at appropriate standard;
- **Circumstances of formation** — when, where, what
  property, what was the founder's purpose.

### Council members

The **governing body** of the foundation. Identification:

- Each individual council member's identity (or
  beneficial-owners-of-corporate-council-members);
- The mandatory **qualified member** — a Jersey-licensed
  TCB principal — verified as the regulated party;
- The council's resolutions and decision-making documented.

### Guardian (if appointed)

For foundations with a guardian (analogous to a trust
protector), identification:

- The guardian's identity;
- The **scope of guardian powers** per the foundation
  charter and regulations — guardians with broad powers
  may themselves be treated as beneficial owners under the
  "control by other means" test.

### Beneficiaries

For beneficiary foundations:

- **Named beneficiaries** identified individually;
- **Class of objects** (where the foundation has a class
  rather than named beneficiaries) identified at class
  level.

For **purpose foundations** (where the foundation exists for
a defined purpose rather than beneficiaries), identification
of the **purpose** and any persons with rights of
enforcement of the purpose.

### Persons in control by other means

Per the standard
[CDD identification framework](./amlh-cdd-identification.md),
any natural person exercising control by other means.

## Distinctive points vs trust customer CDD

### Legal personality

The foundation is itself a legal person, so:

- **The customer relationship is with the foundation**
  rather than with the council on behalf of the foundation
  (cf. trust: relationship is with trustees on behalf of
  the trust);
- The foundation **holds property in its own name**;
- The foundation **contracts in its own name** through its
  council.

### Charter and regulations

The foundation's **charter** (public document) and
**regulations** (typically private) are the constitutional
documents. CDD requires:

- Review of the charter for the foundation's purpose,
  beneficiary class, and council composition;
- Review of the regulations for the operational arrangements,
  guardian role, and any reserved powers.

The regulations may contain **reserved-powers analogues** to
trust Article 9A — the founder may retain specific powers
under the regulations that should be reviewed for control
implications.

### Mandatory qualified member

The qualified member is a **regulated party** — a Jersey-
licensed TCB. The CDD on the foundation interlocks with the
qualified member's own regulatory standing:

- The qualified member's TCB licence is part of the diligence
  on the foundation;
- Where the regulated business itself is the qualified
  member (common for foundations administered by Jersey
  TCBs), the firm's own controls apply to the foundation
  alongside CDD on the founder and other parties.

## Beneficial ownership of a foundation

The Handbook's beneficial-ownership framework for
foundations typically includes:

1. **The founder**;
2. **Council members** (and controllers of corporate
   council members);
3. **The guardian** (where appointed);
4. **Named beneficiaries** with fixed entitlements of 25%+
   (for fixed-entitlement foundations);
5. **The class of beneficiaries** (where discretionary);
6. **Any natural person** with **control over the
   foundation by other means** (founder reserved powers
   under regulations being the most common example).

This is **broadly parallel** to the trust framework but
adapted to foundation-specific role names.

## Purpose and intended nature

The foundation's purpose is documented in the **charter**
and elaborated in the **regulations**. CDD covers:

- The stated purpose (wealth-preservation, succession,
  philanthropic, asset-protection, family-governance);
- The intended operations (passive holding vs active
  decision-making);
- The transaction profile expected.

Purposes for which Jersey foundations are typically used:

- **Family-governance** vehicles for civil-law-flavoured
  families (Latin American, European, Middle Eastern);
- **Philanthropic** endowment foundations;
- **Image-rights** holding structures (where the
  foundation owns the image-rights IP — though
  [Guernsey](../../guernsey/image-rights-register.md) is
  the unique image-rights-registry jurisdiction);
- **Orphan-structure** purpose foundations holding shares
  in PTCs or SPVs.

## EDD application

Common EDD triggers for foundation customers:

- **PEP exposure** at any role (founder, council member,
  guardian, beneficiary);
- **High-risk-country nexus** through any role;
- **Complex structure** where the foundation is part of
  layered arrangements;
- **Civil-law-jurisdiction founder** with significant
  international footprint.

## Common foundation-CDD failure modes

- **Founder identification stopping at first-level** —
  the founder's own beneficial-ownership not traced where
  the founder is itself a vehicle;
- **Regulations under-reviewed** — charter examined but
  regulations not, missing reserved-power patterns;
- **Council-member identification light** for non-active
  council members;
- **Class-of-objects beneficiaries handled lightly** —
  similar issue to discretionary-trust beneficiary CDD;
- **Qualified-member-firm reliance** — assuming the
  qualified member's diligence substitutes for own diligence
  when the qualified member is a related party.
