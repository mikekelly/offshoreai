# AML/CFT Handbook — Internal Controls, Governance, MLRO

The Handbook expects every supervised business to operate an
**AML/CFT control framework** proportionate to the size,
nature and risk of the business. The framework comprises
**governance**, **policies and procedures**, and **defined
roles** — chief among them the **Money Laundering Reporting
Officer (MLRO)** and where applicable the **Money Laundering
Compliance Officer (MLCO)**.

This file is the section index.

## Three lines of defence

The Handbook articulates the standard **three-lines-of-defence**
governance framing:

1. **First line** — operational management with primary
   responsibility for risk identification and CDD execution
   (RMs, account managers, front-office staff).
2. **Second line** — independent risk and compliance functions
   (MLRO, MLCO, compliance officer, risk officer).
3. **Third line** — internal audit (or equivalent external
   assurance) providing independent assurance over the first
   two lines.

For smaller businesses, the lines may be combined provided
**conflicts of interest are managed**; a single individual
cannot effectively perform first-line and second-line roles in
parallel without documented mitigation.

## The MLRO role

The MLRO is the **statutory officer** designated under the
[Money Laundering Order 2008 Article 16](./mlo-article-16.md)
as the focal point for SAR receipt, investigation, and external
reporting to the Financial Intelligence Unit (FIU).

Handbook expectations for the MLRO:

- **Sufficient seniority and authority** — typically board
  level or reporting directly to the board.
- **Independence** from front-office revenue generation.
- **Sufficient resources** (time, staff, systems) to discharge
  the function effectively.
- **Direct access to the board**.
- **Adequate qualifications and experience** consistent with
  the business size and complexity.
- **JFSC-recognised principal-person status** — the MLRO is a
  notified position to the JFSC under the
  [licensing](../financial-regulation/licensing.md) regime.

The MLRO cannot delegate the **statutory SAR responsibility**
but can be supported by a Deputy MLRO and broader compliance
team.

## The MLCO role (where applicable)

For larger or more complex businesses, the Handbook expects a
separate **Money Laundering Compliance Officer (MLCO)** — a
person responsible for the **operational AML/CFT compliance
programme** (policies, procedures, training, monitoring),
distinct from the MLRO's SAR-focused statutory role. In smaller
businesses, MLRO and MLCO can be the same person.

## Board responsibility

The Handbook places **ultimate accountability** with the
**board of directors** (or equivalent governing body). The
board must:

- Approve the AML/CFT policy framework;
- Receive regular reporting from the MLRO and MLCO;
- Approve the BWRA and material updates to it;
- Approve PEP and other higher-risk relationships at
  onboarding (and refresh) where the relationship requires
  board-level approval under
  [`./amlh-edd.md`](./amlh-edd.md);
- Ensure adequate resourcing of the AML/CFT function;
- Set the risk appetite and culture.

Board minutes evidencing engagement with AML/CFT matters are
expected JFSC inspection material.

## Policies and procedures

The Handbook expects a documented **policy framework**
covering at minimum:

- The risk-based approach;
- CDD / EDD / PEPs / customer-type / country-risk processes;
- Ongoing monitoring;
- Record-keeping;
- SAR process (internal and external);
- Sanctions screening and reporting;
- Training;
- Outsourcing controls;
- Sanctions for non-compliance internally.

The policy framework is **reviewed at least annually** and
material changes are board-approved.
