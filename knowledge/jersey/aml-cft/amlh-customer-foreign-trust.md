# AML/CFT Handbook — CDD on a Foreign Trust Customer

A Jersey supervised business may have customers that are
**foreign-law trusts** — trusts governed by the laws of a
non-Jersey jurisdiction. The CDD discipline is similar to
that for [Jersey trusts](./amlh-customer-jersey-trust.md) but
with **adjustments for the foreign-law context** — different
trust-role nomenclature, different beneficiary-information
norms, different supervisory treatment of foreign trustees.

## When a foreign-trust scenario arises

Common scenarios:

- **Cayman / BVI / Bermuda trust** as a customer of a Jersey
  fund-services business, with Jersey-administered fund
  vehicles held below the foreign trust;
- **English-law trust** with international beneficiaries
  using Jersey banking / investment services;
- **US trust** (often a grantor trust under US tax) with
  Jersey-administered offshore holdings;
- **Civil-law foundation-equivalent** (Liechtenstein, Panama,
  etc.) treated within the trust category for AML/CFT
  purposes.

## Identification — the full population

For a foreign trust, identification covers:

### Settlor

- Identity verified on the same standards as a Jersey-trust
  settlor;
- **Source of wealth** evidence to enhanced standard
  (foreign-trust customers frequently trigger EDD);
- **Circumstances of settlement** documented.

### Foreign trustee(s)

The trustee identification is where foreign-trust CDD
diverges most from Jersey-trust CDD:

- **Regulated vs unregulated foreign trustee**;
- If **regulated** (e.g. a Cayman / BVI / Bermuda licensed
  trust company), the foreign-trustee's licensing and
  AML/CFT framework is itself part of the diligence;
- If **unregulated** (e.g. a private individual or a non-
  regulated company acting as trustee), the trustee's
  identity, capability, and SoW must be evidenced
  directly;
- **Controllers of corporate trustees** identified.

### Protector / enforcer (if appointed)

Same population and standards as Jersey trust.

### Beneficiaries

Same approach as Jersey trust:

- **Named beneficiaries** identified;
- **Class of objects** (for discretionary trusts)
  identified at class level with currently-known members;
- **Default beneficiaries** identified.

### Settlor's family / linked persons

Often more relevant for foreign trusts because foreign-trust
arrangements may have less formal documentation of
beneficiary classes — the family map is part of the CDD.

## Trust-law-specific differences

### Jurisdiction of governing law

The trust's governing law determines:

- **Beneficial-ownership concepts** — some jurisdictions
  recognise trusts (common-law tradition), others do not
  (civil-law tradition treating equivalent arrangements as
  foundations);
- **Beneficiary rights** — some jurisdictions have stronger
  beneficiary information rights than others;
- **Forced-heirship interaction** — the trust's protection
  against home-country forced-heirship varies by trust-law
  jurisdiction;
- **Trustee duty standards** — convergent but not identical
  across common-law jurisdictions.

The regulated business notes the governing law and
calibrates the diligence accordingly.

### Settlement formality

- Common-law trusts typically have a clear settlement
  document (the trust deed) with identifiable
  beneficiaries;
- Some foreign arrangements are **less documented** —
  family arrangements styled as trusts in jurisdictions
  with informal documentation conventions;
- The Handbook's expectation is that the CDD captures the
  **substantive arrangement**, not just the documentary
  form.

### Reporting and registry transparency

- Some foreign trust jurisdictions have **public or
  semi-public registries** for trusts (some EU member
  states);
- Others maintain **private registries** with regulator-
  access only (Jersey-equivalent);
- Some have **no registry** at all (historic position in
  some Caribbean jurisdictions, evolving);
- The information available varies; the CDD discipline
  works with what is available.

## EDD application

Foreign trusts frequently trigger EDD:

- **Multi-jurisdictional risk profile** — trust in one
  jurisdiction, settlor in another, beneficiaries in
  others;
- **Foreign-trustee regulatory standing** — particularly
  for trustees from less-rigorously-supervised
  jurisdictions;
- **Complex-structure trigger** where the trust is part of
  layered international arrangements;
- **Country-risk trigger** through any nexus.

EDD measures particular to foreign-trust customers:

- **Enhanced SoW on the settlor** with extra attention to
  pre-settlement wealth history;
- **Foreign-trustee diligence** to enhanced standard;
- **Documentary corroboration** of the trust's
  establishment circumstances (independent legal opinion
  from the trust's home jurisdiction may be sought for
  higher-risk trusts);
- **Periodic refresh** at higher cadence;
- **Enhanced monitoring** of trust transactions.

## Particular foreign-trust scenarios

### US grantor trust with non-US settlor

Common for US-person clients (see
[`../use-cases/client-cohorts/us-person-abroad.md`](../use-cases/client-cohorts/us-person-abroad.md)):

- US settlor's grantor-trust position is part of the
  background;
- Jersey supervised business may be administering offshore
  holdings below the US trust;
- US tax-reporting (Form 3520-A) flows alongside Jersey
  AML/CFT.

### Cayman / BVI trust with international beneficiaries

Common for international wealth-management structures:

- Cayman / BVI trustee typically licensed; the trustee's
  regulatory standing informs the Jersey CDD;
- Beneficiaries often spread across multiple jurisdictions
  with diverse risk profiles;
- Jersey supervised business may be administering specific
  underlying companies or fund interests held by the trust.

### Civil-law foundation treated as trust-equivalent

Liechtenstein, Panama, and similar civil-law foundations
share characteristics with trusts but operate under
**different legal frameworks**. CDD treats them as
trust-equivalent for AML/CFT purposes with adjustments for
the foundation-specific role names (founder vs settlor,
council vs trustees, guardian vs protector).

## Common foreign-trust CDD failure modes

JFSC and international AML themes:

- **Reliance on foreign trustee's CDD** without
  verification of the foreign trustee's actual standards;
- **Settlor SoW from foreign-trust onboarding light** —
  reliance on the foreign trustee's prior CDD without
  fresh evidence;
- **Beneficiary class under-mapped** because the foreign
  trust's documentation is less specific than typical
  Jersey trust documentation;
- **Governing-law assumptions** — applying Jersey-trust-law
  thinking to non-Jersey trusts without recognising the
  framework differences;
- **Periodic refresh skipped** for "stable" foreign-trust
  customers where the perceived stability concealed
  changes in underlying circumstances.
