# AML/CFT Handbook — Internal SAR Process

The internal SAR process is the **upstream half** of the SAR
pipeline — the discipline by which staff escalate suspicions
to the MLRO, and the MLRO investigates and decides whether
to file externally. Effective internal SAR process is the
mechanism that surfaces suspicion the customer-facing staff
encounter; weak internal process means external SARs are
either too few (suspicions never escalated) or too many
(escalation noise without judgement).

## The legal foundation

The
[Proceeds of Crime (Jersey) Law 1999 Article 34](./pocl-article-34.md)
creates a **statutory duty to report** where the person knows
or suspects (or has reasonable grounds for knowing or
suspecting) that another person is engaged in money
laundering. The Money Laundering Order 2008 elaborates the
operational obligations for supervised businesses; the
Handbook gives the operational detail.

The internal SAR is the **mechanism by which staff discharge
their own statutory duty** through the regulated business's
internal pipeline rather than reporting directly to law
enforcement.

## The internal SAR workflow

A typical Handbook-compliant internal SAR workflow:

### Step 1 — Staff member suspicion

A staff member (relationship manager, onboarding officer,
operations staff, fund administrator, etc.) encounters
information that gives rise to suspicion. Common triggers:

- **Customer behaviour** — unusual transaction patterns,
  evasiveness on CDD questions, structure changes without
  clear reason;
- **Counterparty information** — sanctions / adverse-media
  match on a counterparty;
- **Source-of-funds inconsistencies** — funds appearing
  from a source inconsistent with the documented SoF;
- **External information** — news of the customer's
  involvement in an investigation, family-member
  designation, regulatory action;
- **Pattern across customers** — similar suspicious
  patterns across multiple relationships.

The Handbook expects **suspicion at a low threshold** — staff
should escalate when in doubt, not after they have resolved
the doubt themselves.

### Step 2 — Internal SAR filing

The staff member files an internal SAR using the firm's
documented process:

- **Internal SAR form** capturing identity of the customer,
  nature of the suspicion, facts giving rise to it,
  supporting evidence references;
- **Submission to the MLRO** (and Deputy MLRO where
  applicable);
- **No discussion with the customer** about the SAR — the
  [tipping-off offence](./pocl-article-35.md) applies from
  this point;
- **No discussion with other staff** beyond the prescribed
  pipeline — confidentiality is part of the discipline.

The firm's process must make filing internal SARs **easy and
psychologically safe** — a process that is bureaucratically
burdensome or socially uncomfortable produces under-
reporting.

### Step 3 — MLRO investigation

The MLRO investigates:

- **Reviews the customer file** — CDD, EDD, transaction
  history, prior internal SARs, correspondence;
- **Reviews related-party files** — other customers
  potentially connected to the suspicion;
- **External information** — open-source media, court
  records, regulatory publications, commercial information
  databases;
- **Discusses with the staff member** who filed the SAR
  for context not captured in the written submission;
- **Considers compensating mitigation** — would additional
  evidence dispel the suspicion?

The MLRO's investigation file is the documentary record of
the decision-making.

### Step 4 — MLRO determination

Three possible determinations:

#### A — Determine that no SAR is warranted

The MLRO concludes after investigation that the suspicion is
not borne out. The determination is **documented with
reasoning**. The internal SAR is closed; the customer
relationship continues normally.

A determination of "no SAR" is itself documented and
defended — the JFSC may inspect the no-SAR decision file as
part of any thematic review.

#### B — Determine that an external SAR is warranted

The MLRO concludes that knowledge / suspicion / reasonable
grounds for suspicion exists. The MLRO files an external
SAR with the FIU per
[`./amlh-sars.md`](./amlh-sars.md).

#### C — Determine that further information is needed

The MLRO concludes investigation is incomplete and requires
further information. The investigation continues, with
documented intermediate findings. The MLRO may seek further
information from the customer (subject to tipping-off
constraints) or from third parties.

### Step 5 — Follow-through

After the MLRO's determination:

- **Customer-relationship handling** continues per the
  determination (continue / exit / continue with enhanced
  monitoring);
- **Records retained** per
  [record-keeping requirements](./amlh-record-keeping.md);
- **Periodic re-examination** if new information emerges.

## The threshold for suspicion

The Handbook's view of "suspicion":

- Suspicion is **less than knowledge** but **more than
  speculation**;
- It is an **honestly-held doubt** based on **identifiable
  facts**;
- It does **not require proof** — the SAR is the mechanism
  by which proof is potentially pursued;
- It is **calibrated to the staff member's reasonable
  experience** — a staff member should be expected to
  recognise patterns they have been trained on.

The threshold is **intentionally low**. The legal and
regulatory framework prefers over-reporting to
under-reporting — false-positive SARs are processed and
closed; missed-suspicion under-reporting is the failure
mode the regime is designed to prevent.

## Tipping-off — the critical constraint

From the moment a staff member identifies suspicion (and
particularly from the moment an internal SAR is filed):

- **No discussion with the customer** about the suspicion
  or about the SAR;
- **No discussion with anyone** outside the SAR-handling
  pipeline;
- **Customer communications** continue normally where
  possible (avoiding indicators that something is happening)
  but **without disclosing** the SAR;
- **Exit decisions** (where the firm decides to terminate
  the relationship) are handled to avoid tipping-off —
  often requiring careful timing and documented exit
  procedures.

Tipping-off is a **criminal offence** under
[POCL Article 35](./pocl-article-35.md). The Handbook
expects training, written procedures, and managerial
oversight to prevent inadvertent tipping-off.

## Common internal SAR failure modes

JFSC thematic-review and enforcement themes:

- **Under-reporting** — staff did not escalate suspicions
  because the process was burdensome, the culture
  discouraged escalation, or training was inadequate;
- **Over-reporting** — staff escalated every minor anomaly
  generating noise that drowned out genuine suspicions;
- **MLRO investigation files thin** — formal investigation
  but limited substance;
- **No-SAR decisions un-documented** — closure of internal
  SARs without recorded reasoning;
- **Tipping-off lapses** — inadvertent disclosure to the
  customer during the investigation window;
- **Customer-communications not coordinated** with the SAR
  pipeline — staff continued normal communications without
  awareness of the tipping-off risk;
- **Relationship-exit handling** badly timed (closure
  within a window where tipping-off can be inferred).

## Why this matters for wealth management

For wealth-management businesses with international HNW
clientele, the internal SAR process is the **single most
sensitive operational discipline**:

- **PEP relationships** generate elevated SAR scrutiny;
- **Adverse-media events** (a client's name appears in
  international news) trigger SAR consideration;
- **Family-disputes** can surface SAR-relevant facts;
- **Source-of-wealth challenges** can generate SAR
  escalations.

Operating the internal SAR pipeline well **without
tipping-off lapses** is the discipline that distinguishes
mature wealth-management operations.
