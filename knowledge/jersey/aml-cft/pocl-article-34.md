# POCL 1999 Article 34 — Failure to Disclose (Regulated Sector)

Article 34 creates the **failure-to-disclose offence** for
persons in the **regulated sector** (Schedule 2 supervised
businesses) who know or suspect money laundering but do not
report.

## The provision

A person commits an offence if:

- They are working in the **regulated sector**;
- They **know or suspect**, or have **reasonable grounds for
  knowing or suspecting**, that another person is engaged in
  money laundering;
- The information came to them in the **course of business**
  in the regulated sector;
- They do **not** make a disclosure as soon as practicable;
- They are not protected by **legal professional privilege**
  or another statutory exception.

## "Reasonable grounds" — objective threshold

A key feature of Article 34 (distinguishing it from the
substantive offences in Articles 30-32):

- The **threshold** is "knows or suspects, or has reasonable
  grounds for knowing or suspecting";
- "Reasonable grounds" is **objective** — even a person who
  did not actually suspect can be guilty if a reasonable
  person in their position would have suspected;
- This is **stricter** than the subjective "suspects" standard
  in Articles 30-32.

The objective threshold reflects the regulated sector's
**professional duty** to be alert to ML indicators.

## Who is in the "regulated sector"

The regulated sector comprises **Schedule 2 businesses**:

- **Banks** and other deposit-takers;
- **Trust company businesses** (TCBs);
- **Fund services businesses** (FSBs);
- **Investment businesses**;
- **General insurance mediation businesses**;
- **Money service businesses**;
- **Lawyers** acting in specified financial activities;
- **Accountants** acting in specified financial activities;
- **Estate agents**;
- **High-value dealers**;
- **VASPs** (virtual asset service providers);
- **Others** as added to Schedule 2.

## To whom is disclosure made

Disclosure under Article 34 is made:

- **Internally** to the firm's **nominated officer** (MLRO);
- The nominated officer then has obligations under **Article
  34A** to make external disclosure to the FIU.

For a **sole-practitioner** in the regulated sector with no
internal nominated officer, disclosure is made **directly to
the FIU**.

## Defences

Article 34 carries the following defences:

- **Reasonable excuse** for not disclosing;
- **Legal professional privilege** for legal advisers (limited
  to legal-advice privilege — does not extend to material
  giving rise to the suspicion itself in most cases);
- **Disclosure made by a colleague** of the same firm —
  duplicate disclosures are not required;
- **Privilege from self-incrimination** in some circumstances.

## Penalty

- **Fine** (unlimited on indictment);
- **Imprisonment** up to **5 years** on indictment.

## Practical scenarios

- **TCB officer** identifies suspicious transaction pattern
  but does not report to MLRO — guilty of Article 34;
- **Bank teller** processes large unexplained cash deposits
  without escalating — potential Article 34 (depending on
  training and procedures);
- **Lawyer** advising on transaction with apparent ML
  features — may be Article 34 (subject to LPP);
- **Accountant** preparing tax returns identifies undisclosed
  income — Article 34 (subject to LPP for legal-advice
  content).
