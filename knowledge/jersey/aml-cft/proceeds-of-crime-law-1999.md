# Proceeds of Crime (Jersey) Law 1999

The **Proceeds of Crime (Jersey) Law 1999** (POCL) is the
**foundation statute** for Jersey's AML / CFT regime. It creates
the **substantive money-laundering offences** and is the basis on
which "supervised business" is brought within scope of
compliance obligations.

## What POCL does

The Law:

- Defines the **money-laundering offences** (concealing, arranging,
  acquisition / use of, criminal property);
- Defines **criminal conduct** broadly (all-crimes regime — predicate
  offences include tax evasion, fraud, drug offences, etc.);
- Creates the **failure-to-disclose offence** for persons in the
  regulated sector who suspect but don't report;
- Creates the **tipping-off offence** for warning suspected
  launderers;
- Provides **confiscation** and **restraint** powers for the
  Royal Court;
- Provides the framework for **"supervised business"** brought
  into the scope of AML compliance (Schedule 2).

## The money-laundering offences

The substantive offences mirror those in UK POCA 2002:

- **Concealing** — concealing, disguising, converting, transferring
  or removing criminal property;
- **Arranging** — entering into or becoming concerned in an
  arrangement which a person knows or suspects will facilitate
  the use of criminal property;
- **Acquisition, use and possession** — acquiring, using or
  possessing criminal property.

All are punishable by significant terms of imprisonment.

## The defence — authorised disclosure / SAR

A person who **suspects** money laundering can avoid liability by
making an **authorised disclosure** (Suspicious Activity Report,
SAR) to the **Financial Intelligence Unit (FIU)** within Jersey
Customs and Immigration.

The SAR triggers:

- **Consent** procedures — the FIU may consent to the transaction
  proceeding or refuse;
- **Reporting** continues as the relationship continues;
- **Information sharing** within prescribed channels.

## Confiscation

The Law provides for **confiscation orders** following criminal
conviction — orders requiring the convicted person to pay an
amount equivalent to their benefit from criminal conduct.

**Civil recovery** mechanisms are also available where conviction
is not possible but assets can be linked to crime.

## Schedule 2

**Schedule 2 of POCL** specifies the categories of business
subject to **AML supervision**:

- Financial-services businesses (banking, investment, fund, TCB,
  etc.);
- Lawyers, accountants, tax advisers acting in specified
  activities;
- Estate agents;
- Real estate developers;
- High-value dealers;
- Casinos and other gaming operators;
- Virtual asset service providers (VASPs);
- Others as the regime evolves.

The list has been **expanded** over time as international AML
expectations have broadened (FATF, Moneyval).
