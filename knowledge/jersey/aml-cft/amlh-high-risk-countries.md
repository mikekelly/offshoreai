# AML/CFT Handbook — High-Risk Countries

Country risk is one of the load-bearing dimensions of the
[risk assessment](./amlh-risk-assessment.md) and the trigger
for mandatory [EDD](./amlh-edd.md) under both the Handbook and
the [MLO 2008](./mlo-article-15.md).

## The three principal lists

### FATF Black List ("Call for Action")

Jurisdictions assessed by FATF as having significant strategic
AML/CFT deficiencies and being subject to a **call for
counter-measures**. The most serious category. Business with
any nexus to these jurisdictions requires the highest
intensity of EDD.

The current published list (a small group, updated 3x a year
by FATF) is at
[fatf-gafi.org](https://www.fatf-gafi.org/en/publications/High-risk-and-other-monitored-jurisdictions.html).

### FATF Grey List ("Increased Monitoring")

Jurisdictions with strategic AML/CFT deficiencies under
**increased monitoring**. Less severe than Black List but
still requiring enhanced scrutiny. Larger list, updated 3x a
year.

### JFSC-listed high-risk jurisdictions

The JFSC maintains its own list of high-risk countries — which
typically tracks the FATF lists but may include additional
country-specific risk designations. The Handbook is the
authoritative source for the current JFSC list.

## Other relevant sources

### EU high-risk third countries

The EU Commission's high-risk-third-country list (under
Article 9 of the EU Anti-Money Laundering Directive). For
Jersey businesses with EU customer flow or EU counterparty
exposure, the EU list is a relevant overlay.

### UK High-risk Third Countries (HRTC)

The UK's onshored equivalent of the EU list, maintained by
HM Treasury. Relevant where the Jersey business has UK
customer flow.

### Country-specific risk factors

Beyond formal lists, the Handbook expects consideration of:

- **Corruption indices** (Transparency International CPI);
- **Sanctions exposure** of the country (UN, UK, EU, US
  designations against the country or its officials);
- **Terrorism financing risk indicators**;
- **Lack of beneficial-ownership transparency**;
- **Banking secrecy laws** that impede AML/CFT cooperation.

A jurisdiction not on any formal list may still be **high-
risk in the circumstances** of a specific customer or
transaction.

## What the Handbook requires for high-risk countries

A relationship or transaction connected to a high-risk country
triggers **mandatory EDD** under
[`./amlh-edd.md`](./amlh-edd.md), with country-specific
adaptations:

- **Black List** — typically the highest possible EDD plus
  consideration of whether to do business at all.
- **Grey List** — full EDD; senior-management approval;
  enhanced ongoing monitoring; documented rationale for the
  relationship.
- **JFSC-listed** — EDD calibrated to the specific risk the
  JFSC has flagged.
- **EU / UK HRTC** — EDD where the Jersey business has
  EU / UK relevance; otherwise treated as a risk factor in
  the customer risk rating.

## The country-of-residence vs country-of-assets vs country-of-counterparty distinction

The Handbook expects the country-risk analysis to consider:

1. The **customer's country of residence and nationality**;
2. The **country in which the customer's assets are located**;
3. The **country of counterparties** in the customer's
   transactions;
4. The **country of underlying business activity**.

A customer who is resident in a low-risk jurisdiction but has
substantial assets and counterparties in a high-risk
jurisdiction is **high-risk** despite the residence profile.

## Sanctions interface

Country risk and sanctions risk are related but distinct. The
**sanctions regime** ([`./sanctions.md`](./sanctions.md)) is
about specific designated persons and entities; **country
risk** is about jurisdictional context. A non-sanctioned
person in a sanctioned country may be a legitimate customer
under the sanctions regime but still high-risk under the
Handbook's country-risk lens.

## Wealth-management practical reality

For an international wealth-management business serving
clients across the Middle East, Africa, Asia, and Latin
America, **multiple-jurisdiction high-risk-country exposure
is the norm**, not the exception. The operating consequence:

- A robust **country-risk methodology** is core operating
  infrastructure;
- **EDD is the default**, not the exception, for material
  parts of the customer book;
- **Periodic re-screening** captures FATF / JFSC / EU list
  changes;
- The business expects the JFSC to focus on country-risk
  controls in any thematic review or supervisory visit.
