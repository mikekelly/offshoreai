# AML/CFT Handbook — Transaction Monitoring

Transaction monitoring is the **continuous discipline** of
reviewing customer transactions for inconsistency with the
customer's documented profile. Where CDD captures the
customer's expected behaviour, transaction monitoring
surfaces **departures from that expectation** that may
indicate money laundering, terrorism financing, or other
financial crime.

## What transaction monitoring catches

The discipline targets:

### Profile-inconsistency

Transactions that do not fit the customer's documented
purpose, expected volume, expected counterparty profile,
or expected frequency:

- A customer onboarded as a long-term investment-platform
  client suddenly executing large cross-border payments
  to non-investment counterparties;
- A customer with documented modest income receiving very
  large deposits;
- A customer with primarily Western-European counterparties
  suddenly transacting with parties in high-risk
  jurisdictions.

### Structuring patterns

Transactions sized to avoid reporting thresholds:

- Multiple deposits each below the cash-reporting
  threshold;
- Wire transfers structured to fall just below large-value
  reporting triggers;
- Transactions broken across multiple accounts of related
  parties.

### Pass-through patterns

Transactions with no apparent economic substance:

- Funds in / funds out same value, same date, no business
  purpose;
- Repeating round-trip patterns;
- Apparent corporate purpose without underlying activity
  evidence.

### Counterparty risk

Transactions with counterparties that themselves trigger
concern:

- Sanctions-designated parties (interdicted at the
  sanctions-screening tier);
- Adverse-media counterparties;
- High-risk-country counterparties (calibrated to country
  risk);
- Shell-company counterparties without commercial
  substance.

### Velocity anomalies

Sudden changes in transaction velocity:

- Dormant accounts becoming active;
- Active accounts becoming dormant;
- Sudden volume spikes around specific events.

## How monitoring is operated

### Automated screening

For businesses with material transaction volumes, automated
transaction-monitoring systems are the norm. Typical
components:

- **Rules-based screening** — defined thresholds and
  patterns that generate alerts;
- **Statistical / behavioural screening** — customer-
  specific behaviour baselines with deviation alerts;
- **Network analysis** — transaction graphs flagging
  unusual connectivity patterns;
- **Sanctions interdiction** at the payment level;
- **Real-time and post-trade** screening combined.

### Human review

Alerts generated are reviewed by:

- **First-line operations** for initial disposition;
- **Compliance team / Tier 2** for escalations;
- **MLRO** for SAR-decision-level reviews.

### Customer-specific monitoring

For higher-risk customers (PEP, high-risk-country, EDD),
the monitoring is **enhanced**:

- Lower alert thresholds;
- Tighter pattern definitions;
- More frequent customer-specific reviews;
- Direct RM attention rather than purely automated
  screening.

## Calibration

The monitoring programme is **calibrated to the business**:

- **Customer-profile mix** — different customer profiles
  warrant different monitoring rules;
- **Product / service mix** — fund subscriptions, trust
  distributions, payment services each have different
  typology profiles;
- **Geographic profile** — country-risk-weighted
  monitoring;
- **Business-wide risk assessment** — the BWRA findings
  inform the monitoring rule-set.

Calibration is **reviewed periodically** — at least
annually — to keep monitoring effective as the business
and the typology landscape evolve.

## Alert disposition

Every alert generated is dispositioned:

| Disposition | Meaning |
|---|---|
| **False positive** | Alert generated by rule match but transaction is genuinely consistent with profile; closed with documented reasoning |
| **Investigation** | Alert warrants further investigation; held open while investigation proceeds |
| **Customer engagement** | Alert prompts contact with the customer (without tipping-off where SAR-relevant) for clarification |
| **SAR escalation** | Alert investigation surfaces suspicion warranting internal SAR per [`./amlh-sars-internal-process.md`](./amlh-sars-internal-process.md) |
| **Rule recalibration** | Pattern of false-positives indicates the rule itself is mis-calibrated and warrants adjustment |

Disposition is **documented per alert**. The JFSC will
sample alert-disposition files in any thematic review.

## Wealth-management-specific patterns

For wealth-management businesses, typical transaction-
monitoring triggers:

- **Large subscriptions** to private investments
  inconsistent with documented liquidity profile;
- **Cross-currency conversions** at unusual volumes for
  the customer;
- **Cash payments** out (rare in wealth management; the
  pattern is itself worth attention);
- **Beneficiary changes / distributions** from trusts that
  do not fit the documented family pattern;
- **Inter-family transfers** indicating undocumented
  family relationships;
- **Sudden international moves** of large funds (e.g.
  property purchase via international transfer);
- **PEP-family-member transactions** routed through
  apparently-unrelated parties.

## Common monitoring failure modes

JFSC enforcement and thematic-review themes:

- **Rule-set stale** — initial rules well-calibrated but
  not refreshed as business evolves;
- **Threshold over-tuning to reduce false positives** —
  thresholds raised so high that genuine alerts are
  missed;
- **Alert-disposition lacking substance** — formally
  dispositioned but the investigation file is thin;
- **Customer-specific calibration absent** — same rules
  applied uniformly when risk profile varies materially;
- **RM-channel insights not flowing** into monitoring —
  RMs observe inconsistencies but the channel to
  compliance is informal and unreliable;
- **Post-incident learning loops** absent — past failures
  not feeding back into rule calibration.

## Integration with the rest of the AML/CFT framework

Transaction monitoring is **the input** to the SAR pipeline
(see [`./amlh-sars-internal-process.md`](./amlh-sars-internal-process.md)).
A robust monitoring programme produces:

- **Genuine alerts** at appropriate rate;
- **Dispositioned** with documented reasoning;
- **Patterns that feed customer risk re-rating**;
- **SAR escalations** at the threshold appropriate to
  suspicion identification.

Monitoring without effective downstream SAR pipeline, or
SAR pipeline without effective upstream monitoring, are
each insufficient. The two must work together.
