# AML/CFT Handbook — Customer Due Diligence (CDD)

The Handbook's CDD chapter operationalises the
[MLO 2008 Part 3 CDD obligations](./mlo-part-3-cdd.md) — the
core compliance lift for any Jersey-supervised business. CDD is
the discipline of **identifying** the customer and beneficial
owner, **verifying** that identification on reliable evidence,
**understanding** the purpose and intended nature of the
business relationship, and applying a depth of scrutiny
**proportionate to risk** (driven by the
[risk assessment](./amlh-risk-assessment.md)).

This file is the section index. Sub-section files declare
scope and remain stub pending an editorial pass against the
current Handbook.

## The three CDD intensities

The Handbook recognises three levels of CDD intensity, applied
based on the customer risk rating:

### Standard CDD

Default. Applied to customers rated low to medium risk under
the [risk-rating methodology](./amlh-risk-assessment.md).
Requires:

- **Identification** of the customer and the beneficial owner;
- **Verification** of identity from reliable and independent
  sources;
- **Understanding** the nature and intended purpose of the
  business relationship;
- **Understanding** the source of funds for the transactions
  expected.

### Simplified CDD (SDD)

Available in narrowly-defined low-risk situations — typically
where the customer is itself a regulated financial institution
in an equivalent jurisdiction, a listed company on a recognised
market, or a government body. SDD permits **reduced
verification**, **not** zero verification — the obligation to
identify and to monitor remains.

The Handbook conditions SDD eligibility tightly. A business
relying on SDD must document the basis.

### Enhanced due diligence (EDD)

Required for higher-risk customers. The triggers, additional
evidence requirements, and approval gates are covered in detail
in [`./amlh-edd.md`](./amlh-edd.md). EDD is mandatory for
PEPs ([`./amlh-peps.md`](./amlh-peps.md)) and for relationships
linked to high-risk jurisdictions
([`./amlh-high-risk-countries.md`](./amlh-high-risk-countries.md)).

## The four CDD obligations

The MLO 2008 CDD obligations the Handbook elaborates:

1. **Identify the customer and verify identity** on reliable
   evidence — typically passport, government ID, and proof of
   address for individuals; corporate registry extracts and
   constitutional documents for entities.
2. **Identify the beneficial owner** — the natural person(s)
   ultimately owning or controlling 25% or more of an entity,
   or who otherwise exercise control. The Handbook expects
   verification through registry extracts, structure charts,
   shareholder registers, and declarations supported by
   evidence.
3. **Understand purpose and intended nature** of the
   relationship — what services, what volumes, what frequency,
   what counterparties.
4. **Understand source of funds** for the transactions expected
   (with **source of wealth** assessed separately for higher-
   risk customers — see [`./amlh-edd.md`](./amlh-edd.md)).

## Customer-type variation

The CDD obligation applies differently to different customer
types — trusts, foundations, companies, partnerships, charities,
governmental bodies, listed-company customers, and regulated-
institution customers (CDD reliance) all have their own
treatment. See [`./amlh-customer-types.md`](./amlh-customer-types.md).

## Timing — when CDD must be complete

The Handbook expects CDD to be **completed before the business
relationship is established** (i.e. before services are
provided), with **narrow exceptions** for permissible delayed
completion (e.g. trust formation where beneficiaries are
unborn). Delayed completion requires risk mitigation and
documentation.

A relationship cannot be opened on the basis of incomplete CDD
on the expectation of completion later — the JFSC's enforcement
record includes substantive sanctions where this happens.

## Reliance and outsourcing

The Handbook permits **reliance** on CDD performed by another
regulated party (typically an introducer or another supervised
business in an equivalent jurisdiction), subject to:

- A written arrangement;
- Confidence that the relied-upon party is subject to
  equivalent CDD standards;
- The ability to **obtain CDD records on demand**;
- **Continuing accountability** — reliance does not transfer
  the obligation.

CDD execution can be **outsourced** to a service provider, but
**not the obligation itself**. Outsourcing is governed by both
the Handbook and the relevant
[Code of Practice's outsourcing section](../financial-regulation/codes-of-practice.md).
