# AML/CFT Handbook — The MLRO Role

The **Money Laundering Reporting Officer (MLRO)** is the
**statutory officer** designated under
[Article 16 of the Money Laundering Order 2008](./mlo-article-16.md)
as the focal point for suspicious-activity reporting,
internal investigation, and external reporting to the
Financial Intelligence Unit (FIU).

The role is **load-bearing** — a Jersey supervised business
that does not have an effective MLRO has failed at the
foundational level of AML/CFT compliance.

## What the MLRO does

The MLRO's statutory responsibilities:

1. **Receive internal SARs** from staff who suspect money
   laundering or terrorism financing — see
   [`./amlh-sars.md`](./amlh-sars.md);
2. **Investigate** each internal SAR by reviewing the
   underlying CDD, transaction records, related-party
   information, open-source media, and the broader
   customer context;
3. **Determine** whether to file an **external SAR** with
   the FIU;
4. **File external SARs** on the prescribed form and within
   prescribed timing;
5. **Apply for DAML consent** where the business intends to
   continue a transaction that may otherwise constitute a
   prohibited act;
6. **Maintain records** of every internal SAR, investigation,
   and external SAR;
7. **Report to senior management and the board** on SAR
   patterns, AML/CFT control performance, and emerging
   risks;
8. **Engage with the JFSC** on AML/CFT supervisory matters.

## What the MLRO must be

The Handbook's expectations for MLRO eligibility:

### Seniority and authority

- **Sufficiently senior** within the business to investigate
  effectively and to escalate findings;
- **Direct access to the board** — the MLRO's reporting
  line cannot run only through the business's commercial
  leadership;
- **Authority to act** on SAR conclusions without requiring
  commercial sign-off (the MLRO's decision to file an
  external SAR is the MLRO's decision, not the firm's).

### Independence

- **Independent from front-office revenue generation** —
  the MLRO is in the second line of defence
  ([`./amlh-internal-controls.md`](./amlh-internal-controls.md));
- **No commercial-incentive conflicts** affecting the
  MLRO's SAR judgement;
- **Protected from retaliation** for SAR-related decisions
  unfavourable to commercial outcomes.

### Resources

- **Sufficient time** to discharge MLRO duties — for
  larger businesses this is a full-time role; for smaller
  businesses it may be combined with other compliance
  duties but the MLRO function must be effective;
- **Sufficient staff** support where needed — Deputy MLROs,
  compliance team support;
- **Sufficient systems** access — the MLRO can pull CDD
  files, transaction records, communications records as
  needed for investigation.

### Qualifications and experience

- **Adequate knowledge** of Jersey AML/CFT framework,
  applicable international standards, and the firm's
  specific risk profile;
- **Relevant professional qualifications** (typically
  compliance / financial-crime / legal certifications);
- **Experience** consistent with the firm's size and
  complexity.

### JFSC-recognised principal-person status

- The MLRO is a **notified principal person** to the JFSC
  under the firm's
  [registration regime](../financial-regulation/licensing.md);
- The JFSC may **decline** to recognise an MLRO it
  considers unsuitable, or may require their replacement;
- Change of MLRO is a **notifiable event** to the JFSC.

## What the MLRO cannot do

The Handbook is clear that **the statutory SAR
responsibility cannot be delegated** outside the MLRO
function. The MLRO can be supported by:

- **Deputy MLRO** — a designated alternate who can act in
  the MLRO's absence;
- **Compliance team** — operational support on investigation
  workflow, screening, monitoring;
- **External counsel / consultants** — advisory input on
  difficult cases.

But the **filing decision and the responsibility for it
remain with the MLRO**.

## MLRO vs MLCO

For larger or more complex businesses, the Handbook
recognises a separate **Money Laundering Compliance Officer
(MLCO)** function:

- **MLRO** — focused on the **statutory SAR pipeline**
  (internal SAR receipt, investigation, external filing,
  DAML, tipping-off compliance);
- **MLCO** — focused on the **operational AML/CFT compliance
  programme** (policies, procedures, training, monitoring,
  business-wide risk assessment, sector-specific
  application).

In smaller businesses, MLRO and MLCO can be the same person
— but the **distinct functions** still operate. A robust
larger business typically separates the two for capacity
and conflict reasons.

## The MLRO's role in business decisions

The MLRO is not a commercial decision-maker but does
materially influence the business:

- **Onboarding decisions** — high-risk customer onboardings
  often require MLRO sign-off (alongside senior-management
  approval);
- **Relationship-exit decisions** — where a SAR investigation
  leads to a decision to exit a relationship, the MLRO is
  central to the exit handling (managing tipping-off risk,
  documenting reasoning);
- **Group escalations** — for groups with UK FCA-authorised
  affiliates, the MLRO's findings often need to flow into
  the UK firm's own SM&CR-allocated AML accountability;
- **Material breach handling** — when an AML/CFT control
  failure surfaces, the MLRO leads the internal
  investigation.

## Common MLRO-role failure points

JFSC enforcement and supervisory themes:

- **Insufficient MLRO authority** — the MLRO is junior or
  subordinated to commercial pressure;
- **Insufficient resources** — the MLRO has the title but
  not the time / staff to discharge the role effectively;
- **Reporting-line compromises** — the MLRO reports only to
  the CEO, not also to the board;
- **MLRO turnover without notification** — interim periods
  where the role is vacant or temporarily covered;
- **SAR investigation files thin** — formally compliant but
  not substantively investigative;
- **Tipping-off risk** management inconsistent.

A robust MLRO appointment and role-operation withstands
both JFSC inspection and (in the worst case) regulatory
enforcement scrutiny.

## Wealth-management practical reality

For a Jersey wealth-management group with multiple
regulated subsidiaries (TCB, FSB, IB), the **group-level
MLRO architecture** is operationally complex:

- **Per-subsidiary MLROs** — each regulated entity has its
  own MLRO;
- **Group MLRO** coordination — a senior individual
  coordinates across subsidiaries (sometimes the MLRO of
  the principal entity, sometimes a designated group
  compliance leader);
- **Information sharing** between MLROs subject to
  tipping-off and confidentiality constraints;
- **Group SAR coherence** — SAR filings from related
  customers across subsidiaries flagged for group view.

Getting the group-MLRO architecture right is a load-bearing
piece of the group's AML/CFT operating model.
