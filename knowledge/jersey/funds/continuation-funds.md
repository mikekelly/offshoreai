# Continuation Funds and GP-Led Secondaries — Jersey Treatment

## What this is

A **continuation fund** is a fund vehicle into which a
sponsor moves one or more portfolio assets from an
existing (typically older-vintage) fund in order to
**extend the hold period**, **provide liquidity to
existing LPs who want exit**, and **continue running the
asset for incoming LPs and the sponsor**.

A **GP-led secondary** is the broader category of
transactions where the **sponsor (not LPs) initiates** a
secondary-market transaction — the continuation fund is
the dominant form, but tender offers, strip sales, and
preferred-equity recapitalisations are all part of the
GP-led secondaries market.

For the market context, current adoption statistics, and
strategic narrative, see the frontier file at
[`../../frontier/continuation-funds-and-gp-led-secondaries.md`](../../frontier/continuation-funds-and-gp-led-secondaries.md).
This doctrinal file covers the **Jersey-law treatment**:
which Jersey fund regime applies, the authorisation path,
the substantive regulatory issues, and the structural
pattern.

## Why a doctrinal file (separate from frontier)

The frontier file is decay-managed (6-month refresh). The
**legal architecture** — which Jersey fund regime
applies, how to register, what disclosure is required, how
LP voting and conflicts work — is **structurally stable**
and belongs in the doctrinal corpus.

## Choice of Jersey fund regime

Continuation funds in Jersey are typically set up under
the [Jersey Private Fund (JPF) regime](./jersey-private-fund.md),
which is the workhorse private-capital vehicle: ≤50
investors, 48-hour authorisation, Designated Service
Provider (DSP) delegated-compliance model.

The natural fit is because:

- **Investor count**: continuation funds typically have
  a limited LP base (the rolling LPs + new continuation
  LPs);
- **Speed**: JPF 48-hour authorisation matches the speed
  required for secondary transactions where pricing and
  fairness opinions have time-sensitive validity;
- **DSP model**: the DSP carries the regulatory
  obligations, reducing the cost of standing up a new
  vehicle for a one-asset or limited-asset continuation;
- **Cost**: lower establishment and ongoing cost than
  Expert Fund or Eligible Investor Fund routes.

For **larger** continuation transactions or where the LP
base exceeds 50, sponsors may use the
[Expert Fund regime](./expert-fund.md) or the
[Eligible Investor Fund regime](./eligible-investor-fund.md).
The choice is investor-count-led and offering-doc-
intensity-led, not structurally different.

## Single-asset vs multi-asset continuation funds

| Feature | Single-asset CF | Multi-asset CF |
|---|---|---|
| Number of underlying assets | 1 | 2+ |
| Typical use case | Highest-conviction asset in an aging fund | Portfolio re-sleeving; smaller assets bundled |
| Fairness opinion intensity | Single-asset valuation; cleaner | Portfolio valuation; can be harder |
| LP perception | Cleaner sponsor narrative | Sometimes seen as bundling weaker assets |
| Conflict-of-interest pressure | Acute (sponsor seller + buyer) | Same conflict structure |
| Regulatory treatment in Jersey | Same — both can use JPF | Same |

The **single-asset CF** has been the dominant market
form through 2024-2026 because:

- Cleaner narrative ("we have unique conviction on this
  one asset and want more time to realise value");
- Cleaner fairness opinion;
- Better LP outcomes;
- Lower hostile-LP-litigation risk.

## Transaction structure

A typical Jersey single-asset continuation fund structure
involves:

### Parties

- **Old Fund** — the existing fund holding the asset (the
  "seller");
- **Continuation Fund (Jersey JPF)** — the new vehicle
  acquiring the asset (the "buyer");
- **Sponsor / GP** — initiates and runs both;
- **Existing LPs** — given a choice to **roll** (move
  into the Continuation Fund) or **cash out** (receive
  proceeds from the sale);
- **New LPs** — typically secondary investors funding
  the cash-out option and additional capital;
- **Lead investor / secondary syndicate** — anchors the
  continuation transaction and sets price.

### Steps

1. **Sponsor initiates** the continuation transaction
   based on identified value-creation thesis for further
   hold;
2. **Independent valuation** of the asset by a third-
   party valuer;
3. **Fairness opinion** from a qualified financial
   adviser (typically a top-tier investment bank);
4. **LPAC (Limited Partner Advisory Committee) consent**
   under the Old Fund's LPA — often the LPAC is asked
   to bless the conflict and the process even where
   strict consent isn't required;
5. **Notice to LPs** with the roll/cash election
   mechanics and timeline;
6. **LP elections** processed (typical 30-45-day
   election window);
7. **JPF authorisation** for the Continuation Fund (the
   48-hour JFSC route);
8. **Closing** — Old Fund sells the asset to the
   Continuation Fund at the fairness-opinion-supported
   price; cashing-out LPs receive proceeds; rolling LPs
   receive equivalent interests in the Continuation
   Fund;
9. **Carry crystallisation** for the Old Fund based on
   the realised value;
10. **New carry waterfall** starts in the Continuation
    Fund (typically with reset hurdle and a fresh carry
    arrangement).

## Substantive regulatory and structural issues

### Conflicts of interest

The dominant issue. The sponsor sits on **both sides** of
the transaction (as seller via the Old Fund and as buyer
via the Continuation Fund). This is the most serious
conflict in private-equity practice. Mitigants include:

- **Independent valuation and fairness opinion**;
- **LPAC consent process** (even where not strictly
  required);
- **Notice to all LPs** of the conflict and the LP-
  election mechanics;
- **Independent advisor** representing LPs (sometimes);
- **JFSC engagement** if there are unusual features.

### LP election fairness

The roll/cash election must be **structurally fair**:

- The cash price should equal the agreed transaction
  price (no haircut for cashing-out LPs);
- Rolling LPs should not be advantaged or disadvantaged
  vs cashing-out LPs;
- The timeline should be long enough for considered
  decisions.

### Carry crystallisation

The sponsor's carry in the Old Fund is **crystallised** at
the transaction price. This is one of the most economically
significant features of continuation funds — sponsors
realise carry value they would otherwise have had to wait
years to realise. LPs should understand:

- The carry-crystallisation is a real economic transfer
  from LPs (in the form of carry paid earlier than would
  otherwise have occurred);
- The new carry arrangement in the Continuation Fund
  applies on the going-forward economics.

### Substance and Substance Law 2019

The Continuation Fund as a Jersey-resident entity engaged
in "fund management" or "holding company" activity falls
within the [Economic Substance Law 2019](../tax/economic-substance.md).
Adequate Jersey substance must be maintained.

### AIFMD and marketing

If the Continuation Fund is marketed to EU investors
(rolling LPs in the EU, new continuation LPs in the EU),
the [AIFMD NPPR route](./aifmd-nppr.md) applies. The
sponsor's AIFM appointment, Annex IV reporting, and
pre-marketing rules need to be considered. AIFMD II
adjustments apply for transactions post-16 April 2026 —
see [`../../frontier/aifmd-ii-april-2026.md`](../../frontier/aifmd-ii-april-2026.md).

### Tax — Pillar Two / MCIT 2025

For in-scope MNE-group sponsors, the
[MCIT 2025](../international/pillar-two-mcit.md) regime
applies to the Continuation Fund as it would to any
in-scope Jersey entity. For most sponsors the Continuation
Fund is below the EUR 750m revenue threshold and the QDMTT
does not bite.

## Jersey vs Guernsey for continuation funds

Both Channel Islands handle continuation funds well, but
Guernsey has been the **more aggressive marketer** of the
product line. For the Guernsey treatment — including the
3-business-day GFSC approval for single-asset CFs and the
not-a-CIS treatment — see [`../../guernsey/continuation-funds.md`](../../guernsey/continuation-funds.md).

The choice between Jersey and Guernsey for a specific
continuation fund typically turns on:

- **Existing infrastructure** — is the sponsor already
  in one jurisdiction?
- **Speed urgency** — Guernsey's 3-business-day approval
  vs Jersey's 48-hour JPF authorisation;
- **Investor preferences** — UK-LP-led transactions may
  favour Jersey; EU-LP-led transactions may favour
  Guernsey;
- **Fund counsel preferences** — both jurisdictions have
  sophisticated practitioner bases.

## Pitfalls

- **Underestimating the conflict-of-interest pressure**
  — LPACs and litigious LPs scrutinise these transactions;
- **Sloppy fairness-opinion drafting** — the fairness
  opinion is the legal-defence foundation of the
  transaction;
- **Roll/cash election mechanics done badly** — the most
  common source of LP grievance;
- **Carry-crystallisation not fully disclosed** — some
  LPs object retrospectively if they didn't understand
  the economics;
- **Ignoring AIFMD II disclosure uplift** — post-April
  2026, the disclosure obligations on continuation
  vehicles marketed into the EU are materially heavier;
- **Substance gap** — a Jersey continuation fund still
  needs Jersey substance.
