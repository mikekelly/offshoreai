# CIFL 1988 Article 2 — Meaning of "Collective Investment Fund"

Article 2 is the **gateway** to the CIFL regime — what
constitutes a "collective investment fund" requiring JFSC
authorisation.

## The provision

A **collective investment fund** is any arrangement where:

- **Property** (the "scheme property") is held for the
  purpose of enabling investors to **participate in or
  receive profits or income** arising from the acquisition,
  holding, management, or disposal of the property;
- The participants **do not have day-to-day control** over
  the management of the property;
- **Either** the scheme property is **pooled** by the
  arrangement, **or** the scheme property is managed as a
  whole by or on behalf of the operator.

## Key elements

### Investor participation in profits

The arrangement must give investors:

- A **return** linked to the performance of the scheme
  property;
- Whether by **income**, **capital gain**, or both;
- Through **units, shares, or other participations**.

### Absence of day-to-day control

Investors **do not** direct the day-to-day management:

- Investors **may** have voting rights at general meetings;
- Investors **may** have rights to redeem;
- Investors **may** have rights of information;
- But investment decisions are **delegated** to the manager.

### Pooling or central management

- **Pooled** — investor contributions combined and invested
  collectively;
- **Centrally managed** — even if not strictly pooled, the
  arrangement is managed as a whole.

## What is in scope

### Mutual funds / unit trusts

- The classic open-ended fund;
- Investors hold units, redeemable at NAV;
- Manager makes investment decisions.

### Closed-ended funds

- Fixed term and capital;
- Investors hold shares (typically); redemption limited;
- Common for private equity, real estate, hedge.

### REITs

- Real estate investment trusts;
- Property-focused collective investments;
- Open- or closed-ended.

### Multi-class / umbrella funds

- One fund with multiple sub-funds or classes;
- Each within CIFL scope.

## What is out of scope

### Single-asset holdings

- A company owning a single property;
- An individual's investment portfolio;
- Pure trust arrangements (the trust as such — though see
  unit trusts which use trust structure for collective
  investment).

### Insurance products

- Investment-linked life policies are not collective
  investment funds for CIFL purposes (different regulatory
  regime under IBL).

### Pension schemes

- Occupational pension schemes are not CIFL funds (separate
  regulation);
- But pension fund **assets** may be invested in CIFL funds.

### Direct securities ownership

- Holding shares in operating companies directly;
- Not a collective investment.

## Public vs non-public

A CIFL fund can be:

- **Public** — marketed to the public; subject to **CIFL
  certificate** under Article 4;
- **Non-public** — marketed only to defined categories
  (expert, eligible investor, private fund); governed by
  specific Orders.
