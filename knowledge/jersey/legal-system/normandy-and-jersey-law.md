# Norman Origins of Jersey Law

## Historical foundation

Jersey law has its **roots in Norman customary law**. The
Channel Islands were part of the **Duchy of Normandy** from
the **10th century** until **1204**, when **King John of
England** lost continental Normandy but **retained the
islands**. The historical connection has fundamentally shaped
Jersey law.

## Why this matters

Modern Jersey law is **not English law**:

- **Customary-law** roots in Norman law tradition;
- **Civil-law** influences (Roman law via Norman customary law);
- **Common-law** influences (English law as persuasive
  authority);
- A **mixed legal system** — distinctive blend.

For practitioners, **English authorities are persuasive but
not binding** — Jersey courts have their own corpus of law.

## Key Norman legal sources

### Grand Coutumier de Normandie

The **Grand Coutumier** (13th century) is the **classical
statement** of Norman customary law:

- **Compilation** of Norman customs and judicial decisions;
- **Cited** as authority by Jersey courts;
- **Available** in old French (Anglo-Norman) — translations
  exist;
- **Foundational** text for Norman-law principles.

### Coutume reformée

The **Coutume reformée** (1583) is the **modernised** version
of the Norman customary law:

- **Formal text** promulgated by Henri III;
- **Continued** to apply in Jersey;
- **Cited** in Jersey case law;
- **Still occasionally referred to** in modern Jersey
  proceedings.

### Pothier

**Robert-Joseph Pothier** (1699-1772) was a **French jurist**
whose treatises on contract, obligations, sale, and other
areas remain **highly influential** in Jersey law:

- **Traité des obligations** — Jersey contract law foundation;
- **Treatises** widely cited in Jersey decisions;
- **Le Geyt** (Jersey jurist) drew on Pothier;
- **English Lord Mansfield** also used Pothier's work, which
  is why English contract law and Jersey contract law share
  significant common ground.

## Continued Norman influence

Areas where Norman customary law is most evident in modern
Jersey:

### Property law

- **Real property** ownership and transfer;
- **Hypothec** (a form of security interest distinct from
  English mortgage);
- **Specific** customary inheritance rules;
- **Specific** servitude rules.

### Succession

- **Légitime** (forced heirship) for movable estate;
- **Customary rules** for real-property succession;
- **Specific** customary doctrines.

### Contract / obligations

- **Civil-law approach** to formation, performance, breach;
- **Specific** Norman-law concepts (e.g., **cause** of
  contract — though debated);
- **Pothier-derived** principles.

### Procedural

- **Specific** Jersey procedural terminology (representation,
  Doyenneship);
- **Doleance** (an older form of complaint);
- **Specific** customary procedural rules.

## Modern Jersey law's development

Modern Jersey law is the product of:

- **Customary law** (Norman, partially codified);
- **Jersey statute** (extensive modern legislation);
- **Jersey case law** (Royal Court, Court of Appeal, Privy
  Council);
- **English law** (highly persuasive, especially commercial /
  trust law);
- **International** influences (FATF, OECD, ECHR).

## When Norman customary law applies

Modern Jersey courts apply Norman customary law where:

- **No directly applicable statute**;
- **Subject matter** has customary-law roots (property,
  succession, certain civil-law concepts);
- **Customary law** has not been displaced by statute.

For **most commercial law** (companies, trusts, banking,
funds), **statutory law** applies and the Norman foundation
is less directly invoked. For **real property**, **succession**,
and **specific civil-law concepts**, customary law remains
significant.
