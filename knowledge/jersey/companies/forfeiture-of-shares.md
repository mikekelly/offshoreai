# Article 60 — Forfeiture of Shares

Article 60 of the **[Companies (Jersey) Law 1991][cjl]** permits a
Jersey company to **forfeit shares** held by a member who fails to
pay a call or installment due on the shares.

## What Article 60 says

A company may **forfeit shares** when a member **fails to pay
calls or installments** as required by:

- the **articles**; or
- a **notice served on the member**.

(See the consolidated text at [jerseylaw.je][cjl].)

## When forfeiture applies

Forfeiture is a remedy for **partly-paid shares** under
[Article 37](./different-amounts-on-shares.md). The mechanism:

1. The company issues shares with **part of the subscription
   price** unpaid;
2. The company has the right to **call for the unpaid balance**
   from the member at a stated date or trigger;
3. If the member **fails to pay** the call, the company can
   **serve a forfeiture notice**;
4. After the notice period, the **shares are forfeited** — the
   member loses them.

## The forfeiture mechanism

The detailed mechanics depend on the **articles of association**.
Typical articles will include:

- The **notice required** before forfeiture (typically 14 or 28
  days);
- The **form of notice** — must specify the unpaid amount and the
  consequence of non-payment;
- The **effect of forfeiture** — the shares revert to the company
  (typically as treasury shares or for cancellation);
- The **member's continuing liability** — typically the member
  remains liable for the unpaid amount as a debt even after
  forfeiture.

## Modern practice

Forfeiture is **uncommon in modern Jersey practice** because:

- Most Jersey shares are issued **fully paid**;
- Partly-paid structures are unusual outside fund-vehicle and
  employee-share contexts;
- Where partly-paid shares are used in funds, the **call
  mechanism** typically follows a structured drawdown that pre-
  empts forfeiture risk.

Forfeiture remains in the Law as the **remedy of last resort**
for partly-paid shares where the member defaults.

## Pitfalls

- **Procedural defects** in the forfeiture notice — typically
  invalidate the forfeiture.
- **Articles silent** on forfeiture procedure — the company may
  need to rely on common-law default or court-supervised
  procedure.
