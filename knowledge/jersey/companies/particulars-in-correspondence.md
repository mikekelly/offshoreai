# Article 70 — Particulars in Correspondence

Article 70 of the **[Companies (Jersey) Law 1991][cjl]** requires a
Jersey company's business documents to include additional
**particulars** beyond just the name (which is covered by Article
69).

## What Article 70 says

Business documents must include:

- the **company's registered number**;
- the **registered-office address**;
- the **jurisdiction of incorporation** (where applicable, e.g.,
  in overseas correspondence indicating the company is Jersey-
  incorporated).

(See the consolidated text at [jerseylaw.je][cjl].)

## Why these particulars

Counterparties and third parties dealing with the company need
to know:

- The **registered number** — to identify the company on the
  Registry (especially useful where names are similar);
- The **registered office address** — for service of documents;
- The **jurisdiction** — to know which legal system governs the
  company (and what foreign-law rules might apply).

## What counts as a "business document"

The Article applies to:

- **Letters** and physical correspondence;
- **Invoices** and credit notes;
- **Receipts**;
- **Order forms**;
- Other formal **business communications**.

It typically does **not** apply to informal communications, but
the boundary can be context-specific.

## Standard letterhead practice

Modern Jersey companies typically include on letterhead:

- **Company name** (with "Limited" / "Ltd" suffix);
- **Registered number**;
- **Registered office address**;
- **"Incorporated in Jersey"** or similar jurisdiction
  identifier;
- **Directors** (sometimes — depending on internal policy);
- **VAT/GST registration** numbers if applicable.

This standardised format satisfies both Articles 69 and 70
disclosure requirements.

## Pitfalls

- **Inconsistent letterhead** — different templates with
  different particulars. Audit and standardise.
- **Missing the registered number** — most-frequent omission.
  The Registry number is the canonical company identifier.
- **Inadequate "incorporated in Jersey"** notice on overseas
  correspondence — confuses foreign counterparties about the
  governing law.
