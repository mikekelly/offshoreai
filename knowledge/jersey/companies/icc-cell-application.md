# Article 127YA — Application by Cell Company for Creation of Cells

Article 127YA of the **[Companies (Jersey) Law 1991][cjl]** is
the **application mechanism** by which an **incorporated cell
company (ICC)** creates a new **cell** (an "incorporated cell").

## What Article 127YA covers

An ICC may apply to the registrar for the **creation of one or
more cells** by delivering:

- An **application** in prescribed form;
- The proposed **memorandum and articles** of the new cell
  (Article 127YB);
- A statement of the **proposed name** of the cell (subject to
  Article 127YL);
- A statement of the **proposed registered office**, **secretary**,
  **directors** and other prescribed particulars;
- **Prescribed fees**.

(See the consolidated text at [jerseylaw.je][cjl].)

## Why this matters

The **ICC structure** is a Jersey-specific corporate vehicle
that comprises:

- A **head company** (the ICC itself), and
- One or more **incorporated cells** — each a **separately
  incorporated body corporate** with its own legal personality.

This contrasts with the **protected cell company (PCC)**
structure (Article 127YO onwards), where the cells are **not
separately incorporated** — they are **statutory compartments**
within a single company.

The ICC structure is heavily used for:

- **Investment fund umbrellas** with multiple sub-funds, each
  needing legal segregation;
- **Insurance** structures with multiple cells for separate
  risks;
- **Securitisation** with cells for separate transactions.

Article 127YA is the **start of the cell-creation lifecycle** —
how a new cell comes into being.
