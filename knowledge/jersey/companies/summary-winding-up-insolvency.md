# Article 151 — Effect of Insolvency (Summary Winding Up)

Article 151 of the **[Companies (Jersey) Law 1991][cjl]** sets
out what happens when a **summary winding up turns out to be
insolvent** — i.e. the directors' solvency statement under
Article 146 proves wrong.

## What Article 151 covers

If the liquidator forms the opinion that the company **cannot pay
its debts in full** within 12 months of commencement of summary
winding up:

- The liquidator **must** convert the procedure to a **creditors'
  winding up** (or désastre under Article 154A);
- A **meeting of creditors** must be called within a prescribed
  period;
- The procedure thereafter follows Chapter 4 (creditors' winding
  up) or the désastre regime.

The original directors who made the solvency statement may face
**personal liability** and **offence proceedings** if the
solvency opinion lacked reasonable grounds.

(See the consolidated text at [jerseylaw.je][cjl].)

## Why this matters

The summary winding-up procedure assumes solvency. If the
assumption proves wrong:

- **Creditors need protection** — the simple summary procedure
  doesn't give creditors the voice they need in an insolvent
  liquidation;
- **The director attestation regime** must be enforced — the
  solvency statement was a sworn document, and its falsity has
  consequences;
- **The liquidator** must switch tracks — the powers and
  procedures of creditors' winding up are needed.

Article 151 is the **graceful failure mode** that protects the
system when initial assumptions turn out wrong.
