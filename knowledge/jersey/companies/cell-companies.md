# Article 3I — Cell Companies (PCC and ICC)

Article 3I of the **[Companies (Jersey) Law 1991][cjl]** is the
**gateway** to Jersey's **cell-company** regimes. Two flavours are
recognised:

- **Protected Cell Companies (PCC)** — a single legal entity divided
  into cells whose assets are statutorily ringfenced from each
  other and from the core; the cells themselves do **not** have
  separate legal personality.
- **Incorporated Cell Companies (ICC)** — a parent legal entity
  with **incorporated cells**, each of which **does** have separate
  legal personality.

The detailed regime is in **Part 18D** (Articles 127YA–127YW) of
the Law.

## What Article 3I says

A cell company is one whose memorandum states it is an
**incorporated cell company** or a **protected cell company**, as
specified in the memorandum.

(See the consolidated text at [jerseylaw.je][cjl].)

## PCC — Protected Cell Company

A PCC is **a single legal person**. It is divided internally into:

- a **core** — the non-cellular part of the company;
- one or more **cells** — each with segregated cellular assets and
  cellular liabilities.

Key features:

- the cells are **not separate legal persons** — only the PCC
  itself can contract and litigate;
- **cellular assets** of one cell are **statutorily ringfenced**
  from the cellular liabilities of other cells and (subject to
  specific rules) of the core;
- **non-cellular** (core) assets are available to meet liabilities
  of the core and (in defined circumstances) of cells.

Typical PCC use cases:

- **insurance** — segregating underwriting books, particularly for
  captive insurance ([`../insurance/captive-insurance.md`](../insurance/captive-insurance.md)
  — *planned*);
- **structured finance** — ringfencing distinct transaction
  underlyings.

## ICC — Incorporated Cell Company

An ICC is a **two-tier structure**:

- the **ICC parent** is a legal person;
- each **incorporated cell** is **itself a separate legal person**,
  registered separately in the Companies Registry.

Key features:

- cells have their own legal personality, can contract in their
  own name, sue and be sued;
- each cell has its own **memorandum**, **articles**, **directors**
  (typically the same as the parent), and **registered office**;
- cellular asset ringfencing is structural (not just statutory)
  because each cell is its own legal entity.

Typical ICC use cases:

- **funds** — umbrella fund structures with each sub-fund as an
  incorporated cell;
- **insurance** where contracting in the cell's own name (rather
  than the PCC's name "acting for and on behalf of" the cell) is
  commercially required;
- **multi-jurisdiction structures** where the cell's separate
  legal personality is needed for foreign-law recognition.

## Choice between PCC and ICC

| Factor | PCC | ICC |
|---|---|---|
| Number of legal persons | One (the PCC itself) | The parent plus each cell |
| Cell separately contracts | No — PCC contracts on behalf | Yes — cell contracts in own name |
| Ringfencing | Statutory (Article 127YU) | Structural (separate entities) |
| Registry filings | One PCC + cell creation applications | Parent + separate cell incorporations |
| Foreign-law recognition | Sometimes problematic | Generally cleaner |
| Operational complexity | Lower | Higher |
| Cost | Lower | Higher |

## Setting up a cell company

The detailed mechanics are in **Part 18D** (Articles 127YA–127YW).
The high-level steps:

1. **Form the parent** (PCC or ICC) as a Jersey company with the
   cell-company designation in its memorandum.
2. **Apply for creation of cells** under Article 127YA (ICC) or
   the equivalent PCC mechanism. Each cell creation requires
   appropriate registrar / JFSC action.
3. **Cell-specific memorandum and articles** (where required —
   ICCs need their own; PCCs may have governing terms in the
   parent's articles or in cell-specific creation documents).
4. **Operational segregation** — bank accounts, books and records,
   asset registers, all kept distinct per cell.

## Pitfalls

- **Foreign-law respect for PCC ringfencing.** A PCC's
  statutory ringfencing depends on foreign courts recognising
  the Jersey statute. Some jurisdictions are more accommodating
  than others. For cross-border structures, ICC may give cleaner
  protection.
- **Operational segregation discipline.** A PCC that fails to
  maintain real segregation between cells (commingling of
  accounts, mixed-up minutes) risks the ringfencing being
  challenged in litigation.
- **Directors' duties to each cell.** Directors of a cell company
  owe duties to each cell — see
  [`pcc-director-duties.md`](./pcc-director-duties.md) (Article
  127YR).
