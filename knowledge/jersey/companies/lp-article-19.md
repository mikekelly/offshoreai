# LP Law 1994 Article 19 — Limited partner's liability to creditors

**The single most important Article in the LP Law.** Defines
the limit of limited-partner liability and — critically —
the **management safe harbours** that allow limited partners
to engage with the LP without losing their limited
liability.

## The general rule

A limited partner is **not liable** to LP creditors beyond
the capital contributed, **except where** the limited
partner has **participated in the management** of the LP
with creditor knowledge of that participation.

The "participation in management" trigger is what distinguishes
limited partners (passive investors) from general partners
(active managers).

## The safe-harbour list

Article 19 includes an **extensive list of activities** that
do **not** constitute "participation in management" — and
therefore do not lose limited liability. These typically
include:

- **Acting as a director, officer or shareholder** of a
  corporate general partner;
- **Consulting with or advising** the GP about the LP's
  business;
- **Approving or disapproving** specific matters where the
  partnership agreement requires LP consent (admission of
  new partners, fundamental changes, etc.);
- **Voting on prescribed matters** including dissolution,
  merger, sale of substantial assets;
- **Bringing derivative claims** under
  [Article 28](./lp-article-28.md);
- **Serving on a committee** of limited partners (e.g. an
  LP advisory committee or LPAC — typical in PE funds);
- **Investigating or reviewing** the LP's affairs;
- **Receiving distributions** and information;
- Other prescribed activities.

The safe harbours **enable the typical PE / VC fund
operational reality** — limited partners serve on LPACs,
approve specific decisions, attend annual meetings, receive
detailed reporting — all without losing limited liability.

## Operational significance

For a Jersey-LP fund vehicle, the safe-harbour list is the
**load-bearing structural feature**. The partnership
agreement is drafted to keep limited-partner activities
within the safe-harbour catalogue.

A limited partner whose activities go beyond the safe
harbours and constitutes substantive "management" of the LP
becomes potentially liable to creditors — converting from
passive investor to active partner.
