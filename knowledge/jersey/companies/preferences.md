# Article 176A — Giving of Preferences

Article 176A of the **[Companies (Jersey) Law 1991][cjl]** is the
companion claw-back provision to Article 176: a **preference**
given to a creditor before liquidation can be unwound.

## What Article 176A covers

A preference is given where the company:

- Did or suffered anything that has the effect of putting a
  creditor (or surety / guarantor) in a **better position** in
  the event of the company's insolvency than they would have
  been in otherwise;
- The company was **influenced by a desire** to produce that
  effect (the "desire" element);
- The company was **insolvent** at the time or became insolvent
  in consequence;
- The preference was given within the **relevant period** before
  winding up (**12 months** for unconnected creditors, **5
  years** for connected persons).

The Royal Court may make orders to **restore** the position,
typically by:

- **Recovery** of the preferential payment;
- **Reversal** of the preferential transaction;
- **Equivalent compensation** from the preferred creditor.

(See the consolidated text at [jerseylaw.je][cjl].)

## Why this matters

Preferences arise commonly:

- **Pay-down of director loans** when insolvency looms;
- **Granting of security** to a previously unsecured creditor
  (often a related party);
- **Targeted payment** of a friendly creditor while leaving
  others unpaid;
- **Bank facility paid down** at the expense of trade creditors.

Article 176A enforces the principle of **pari passu**
distribution: in insolvency, similarly ranked creditors should
share rateably. Pre-insolvency favouritism violates this and
gets unwound.

## The "desire" element

Unlike transactions at undervalue, preferences require **desire
to prefer** — making them harder to prove than undervalue claims.
For connected-person preferences, **desire is presumed**.
