# Article 24 — Official Seal for Securities

Article 24 of the **[Companies (Jersey) Law 1991][cjl]** permits
Jersey companies to maintain a **dedicated securities seal** — a
specialised seal for sealing security-related documents (share
certificates, bond certificates, etc.).

## What Article 24 says

Companies **may create an official seal marked "Securities"** for
sealing security-related documents.

(See the consolidated text at [jerseylaw.je][cjl].)

## When this is used

The securities seal is operationally useful where a company:

- Issues **share certificates** that require sealing for
  authentication;
- Issues **bond or debenture certificates** that require sealing;
- Operates as a fund vehicle issuing **participating-share
  certificates** to investors.

The securities seal can be held by the **company secretary** or
**registrar** and applied to security certificates in volume,
without needing access to the company's common seal for each
certificate.

## Format

The seal must be marked **"Securities"**, distinguishing it from
the common seal and from any Article 23 branch seal.

## Effect

Documents sealed with the securities seal have the **same effect**
as if the common seal had been applied — the seal authenticates
the document as a corporate act of the company.

For **share certificates** specifically, the seal historically
served the function of authenticating the certificate as evidence
of title (see [Article 51](certificate-evidence-of-title.md)
*(planned)*). Modern Jersey practice often relies on signature
authentication rather than sealing, but where sealing is used,
the Article 24 securities seal is the appropriate vehicle.

## Modern relevance

In a modern Jersey company:

- **Uncertificated securities** (see
  [Article 51A](uncertificated-securities.md) *(planned)*) don't
  require sealing at all;
- **Certificated shares** typically use signature authentication
  rather than sealing;
- **Bond issues** under English-law indentures may use the seal
  or signatures depending on the indenture's terms.

So Article 24's relevance is operational rather than
foundational — it's a tool the company can use when sealing is
the chosen authentication mechanism.

## Pitfalls

- **Confusing seals** — the company should have a clear protocol
  for which seal applies to which document.
- **Inappropriate use** of the securities seal on non-security
  documents — would be operationally irregular even if not
  invalid.
