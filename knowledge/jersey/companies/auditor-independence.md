# Article 113E — Independence Requirement

Article 113E of the **[Companies (Jersey) Law 1991][cjl]** is the
**independence** standard for auditors — the principle that
auditors must be independent from the company and its
management.

## What Article 113E covers

Auditors must be **independent** from the company and its
management.

**Independence is compromised** by:

- **financial interests** in the company;
- **close family relationships** with key personnel;
- **provision of non-audit services** that create conflicts
  affecting audit objectivity.

(See the consolidated text at [jerseylaw.je][cjl].)

## Why independence matters

An audit's value depends on **objectivity**. A non-independent
auditor:

- May be **financially motivated** to give a clean opinion;
- May be **socially compromised** by relationships with management;
- May have **professional self-interest** in not raising issues
  that affect their other work for the client.

The audit profession's whole credibility rests on independence.

## Threats to independence

The Article catches three primary threats:

### Financial interests

The auditor (or its partners) holding **shares** in the company,
**loans** to the company, or other financial stakes that would
benefit from a particular audit outcome.

### Close family relationships

The auditor's **close family members** working in **key
positions** at the company — director, finance officer, etc.

### Non-audit services

The auditor providing **non-audit services** that:

- Create **economic dependence** on the client;
- Involve **management-style decisions** that the auditor would
  later need to audit;
- Create **conflicts** in audit judgements.

Common problematic services: tax structuring of audited
transactions, valuation work that affects accounts, IT-systems
design for accounting systems being audited.

## Independence in practice

Modern audit firms manage independence through:

- **Independence questionnaires** for partners and staff;
- **Pre-clearance** of non-audit services;
- **Rotation** of audit partners on long-running clients;
- **Independence-monitoring** committees;
- **Reporting** to professional bodies on independence issues.

## Pitfalls

- **Drift into non-audit services** over time — the auditor
  picks up consulting work that ultimately compromises audit
  independence.
- **Family-relationship surprises** — staff may not realise the
  significance of family ties to client personnel.
