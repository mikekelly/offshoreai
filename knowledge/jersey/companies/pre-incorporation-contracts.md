# Article 21 — Pre-Incorporation Contracts

Article 21 of the **[Companies (Jersey) Law 1991][cjl]** addresses
the awkward situation where someone purports to enter a contract
"for" or "on behalf of" a company that **does not yet exist**.

## What Article 21 says

When a transaction purports to involve a **company not yet formed**:

- the **person acting** becomes **personally bound** by the
  transaction; and
- is **personally entitled** to its benefits;

— **unless otherwise agreed**.

The company, **once incorporated**, may **adopt** the transaction
within a **specified or reasonable timeframe**, displacing the
personal liability of the original signatory.

(See the consolidated text at [jerseylaw.je][cjl].)

## The pre-incorporation problem

A Jersey company exists only from the date of its certificate of
incorporation under [Article 9](effect-of-registration.md). Before
that:

- it has **no legal personality**;
- it cannot **contract**, **own property**, or **sue**;
- a purported contract "on behalf of" the not-yet-existent company
  is not binding on the company itself.

The default common-law position would be that such a contract is
**void** — neither the future company nor the signing party is
bound.

Article 21 changes this: the signing party becomes **personally
bound and entitled** by default, and the company can **adopt** the
contract after incorporation.

## Default position

Without specific agreement to the contrary, the signing party
**becomes a contracting party in their own right**:

- They can **enforce** the contract against the counterparty;
- They are **liable** under the contract to the counterparty;
- The contract is a personal contract between the signing party
  and the counterparty.

## Contracting out

The Article allows the parties to **agree otherwise**. Typical
contracting-out provisions:

- "The signing party acts only as agent for a company to be
  formed, and is not personally bound";
- "The contract is conditional on the company being incorporated
  and adopting it";
- "Personal liability of the signing party ceases on the
  company's adoption of the contract".

These provisions are common in **transactional drafting** where a
new SPV is being formed and counterparties want efficient handover
of the contract to the SPV once it exists.

## Adoption by the company

The company, once incorporated, may **adopt** the pre-incorporation
contract within the **specified or a reasonable** timeframe.
Effects of adoption:

- The contract becomes **binding on the company** as if it had
  contracted directly;
- The **signing party** is typically released from personal
  liability (subject to the contract's terms);
- The counterparty's rights transfer to enforceability against
  the company.

Adoption is typically by:

- **Board resolution** of the new company adopting the contract;
- A **deed of adoption** signed by the company;
- **Conduct** indicating adoption (where the contract permits).

## Practical drafting

A typical pre-incorporation contract in a Jersey M&A or financing
context includes:

- Clear statement of **whose obligation it is** pending
  incorporation (typically a sponsor or founding shareholder);
- A **conditional clause** making the deal effective on
  incorporation and adoption;
- A **timeframe** within which adoption must occur, failing which
  the deal terminates;
- **Release language** on adoption.

## Pitfalls

- **Forgetting to adopt** — the signing party remains personally
  liable indefinitely.
- **Ambiguous "for and on behalf of"** drafting — if the contract
  doesn't clearly say the signing party is acting only as agent,
  Article 21 will impose personal liability by default.
- **Adoption deadline** — if the contract requires adoption
  within a stated period and the company doesn't meet it, the
  contract may terminate or revert to personal liability of the
  signatory.
