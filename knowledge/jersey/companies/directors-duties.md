# Article 74 — Duties of Directors

**Article 74** of the **[Companies (Jersey) Law 1991][cjl]** is the
**directors' duties** provision — the single most-cited Article in
the Companies Law in director-liability disputes. It codifies the
statutory duties a Jersey director owes to the company, sitting on
top of preserved common-law and equitable duties.

## What Article 74 says

Directors must:

- Exercise powers **for proper purposes** and **avoid conflicts
  of interest**.

The key duties:

- Act **honestly** and **in good faith** with a view to
  **advancing the company's interests**;
- **Avoid situations** where **personal interests conflict with
  fiduciary duties**;
- **Not profit** from the directorial position except as
  **authorised**;
- Exercise **care, diligence, and skill** comparable to a
  **reasonably prudent person** in comparable circumstances;
- **Not enter contracts** with the company without **disclosure**
  (subject to the Article 74A exception for sole member-
  directors);
- **Not compete** with the company's activities without
  authorisation.

(See the consolidated text at [jerseylaw.je][cjl].)

## The duties unpacked

### 1. Honesty and good faith

The trustee-style duty of **good faith** in the best interests
of the company. This is the **subjective** test — the director's
state of mind matters.

A director acting in **subjective good faith** has discharged
this duty even if the decision turns out to be commercially
poor. A director acting in bad faith — for ulterior motives, to
benefit a third party at the company's expense — is in breach.

### 2. Proper purpose

A director must exercise their powers **for the purpose for
which the power was conferred**. Exercising a power for a
**collateral purpose** is a breach, even where the director
believes (in good faith) the exercise is in the company's
interests.

Classic example: the **share-issue power** is typically conferred
to raise capital. Exercising the share-issue power **to dilute a
hostile takeover bidder** uses the power for a collateral
purpose — even if the directors believe the dilution serves
the company.

### 3. Avoiding conflicts

A director must **avoid situations** where personal interests
conflict with the company's interests. Where a conflict exists,
the director must:

- **Disclose** it (under Article 75);
- **Recuse** from decisions where conflicted;
- Where the conflict is **structural** (e.g., the director
  represents a different group company with competing interests),
  consider whether continued directorship is appropriate.

### 4. No profit without authorisation

The classic equitable **no-profit rule**: a director cannot
profit from their position except as the deed, members, or the
court authorise.

Profits caught include:

- **Salary or fees** above what is authorised;
- **Commissions** from counterparties to the company;
- **Property dealings** with the company at preferential prices;
- **Information-based profits** from confidential company
  information.

### 5. Care, diligence, and skill

The **objective** standard — what a **reasonably prudent person**
would do in comparable circumstances. The standard is uplifted
for directors with **particular skills** or **professional
qualifications**:

- A director who is also a **chartered accountant** is held to a
  higher standard on financial matters;
- A director who is a **lawyer** is held to a higher standard
  on legal matters;
- A **professional director** (a director who serves on multiple
  boards as a livelihood) is held to a higher standard generally.

This is the same "objective floor, professional uplift" pattern
as Trusts Law Article 21 — see
[`../trusts/trustee-duties.md`](../trusts/trustee-duties.md).

### 6. Self-dealing without disclosure

A director must **not enter contracts with the company** without
disclosing the interest. The Article 75 disclosure regime puts
detail on the duty.

The **Article 74A exception** for **sole-member-directors**
(where the company has only one member who is also the sole
director) is the practical relaxation — but the disclosure
practice is to **record the transaction** in the company's
records.

### 7. No competition

A director must not **compete with the company's business**
without authorisation. This captures:

- A director who **starts a competing business**;
- A director who **takes a directorship** of a competitor;
- A director who **diverts a corporate opportunity** to a
  separate vehicle.

The duty is **prophylactic** — it bars the competition rather
than requiring proof of actual harm to the company.

## Shadow directors

The [Article 1 definition of "director"](companies-interpretation.md)
captures **shadow directors** — persons whose directions or
instructions the formally-appointed board is accustomed to
follow. **Article 74 duties apply to shadow directors** equally:

- A **dominant shareholder** who directs the board behind the
  scenes may be a shadow director with Article 74 duties;
- A **parent company** instructing its subsidiary's board may be
  a shadow director of the subsidiary;
- A **professional adviser** who steps beyond advice into
  direction may become a shadow director.

The shadow-director risk is substantial in offshore structures
where governance lines are not always clean.

## Common-law and equitable duties preserved

Article 74 codifies the statutory floor. **Common-law and
equitable duties** continue to apply alongside, including:

- The **fiduciary duties** of loyalty and care;
- The **rule against profit** from a fiduciary position;
- The **rule against conflicts** in equity;
- **Trustee-style** duties where the directors hold property
  effectively in trust for the company.

Article 74 does not displace these — the directors are bound by
**both** the statutory duties and the equitable duties.

## Relief from liability

A director who breaches Article 74 may seek relief under:

- [`relief-from-breach.md`](relief-from-breach.md) — Article 67A:
  for breach in unavoidable circumstances;
- **Court discretion** to relieve where the director acted
  honestly and reasonably (parallel to Trusts Law Article 45);
- **Indemnity** from the company under
  [`officer-indemnity.md`](officer-indemnity.md) — Article 77:
  within the limits the Article permits.

## Pitfalls

- **Conflating subjective good faith with proper purpose** — both
  are required. A director who acts in good faith for a
  collateral purpose is still in breach.
- **Cross-border directorships** — Jersey-law directors of Jersey
  companies must observe Article 74 regardless of where they
  live. Foreign-resident directors should ensure they have
  Jersey-law-aware governance support.
- **Professional uplift** — directors with specialist
  qualifications cannot defend low standards by reference to lay
  directors. The standard is what their skill set required.
- **Shadow-director risk** — advisers and parent-company
  representatives can drift into shadow directorship and pick up
  Article 74 duties without intending to.
