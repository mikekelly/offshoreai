# Article 30 — Compensation for Misleading Statements in Prospectus

Article 30 of the **[Companies (Jersey) Law 1991][cjl]** is the
**civil-liability** mechanism for prospectus misstatements. A
person who suffers loss because of a misleading statement or
omission in a prospectus can claim compensation from defined
categories of liable persons.

## What Article 30 says

### Who can be liable

A person who suffers a loss because of a **misstatement or
omission** in a prospectus may seek compensation from:

- the **body corporate issuing the securities** and **each
  director** of it at the time;
- the **person making the offer** and that person's relevant
  directors;
- those who have **accepted responsibility** for the prospectus
  (or any part);
- those who **authorised the contents** of the prospectus.

### Professional advisers

Professional advisers giving guidance **do not become liable
under this Article alone** by reason only of giving their advice.
Other legal remedies (negligence, breach of contract) remain
unaffected.

### Existing remedies preserved

The Article doesn't displace other legal remedies — common-law
negligence, breach-of-contract claims, misrepresentation
remedies all continue to be available.

(See the consolidated text at [jerseylaw.je][cjl].)

## Who is "responsible" for a prospectus

The Article casts a wide net:

- The **issuer** — the company itself.
- The **directors** at the time the prospectus was issued (they
  are jointly and severally liable with the company).
- The **offeror** (which may be different from the issuer — e.g.,
  in a secondary offering where existing shareholders are
  selling).
- **Any person** who has formally accepted responsibility — often
  reflected in the prospectus's "Responsibility Statement" or
  equivalent;
- **Any person** who has **authorised the contents** — typically
  read broadly to capture those with the power to approve or
  reject content.

## Professional adviser carve-out

The carve-out for professional advisers is significant. A
solicitor or accountant who **gives advice to the issuer** about
the prospectus is not, by that alone, liable under Article 30.

The carve-out does **not** protect an adviser who:

- **Accepts responsibility** for the prospectus or part of it
  (e.g., a reporting accountant who signs off on financial
  statements with a public statement of responsibility);
- **Authorises** the prospectus contents in a manner going
  beyond ordinary advice;
- **Is named** in the prospectus as having approved a specific
  part (e.g., expert statements);
- Is **otherwise involved** in a way that makes them
  substantively responsible.

For sensitive prospectuses, professional advisers typically
include **explicit disclaimers** in their engagement letters and
in any letters appended to the prospectus, distinguishing
"advice given" from "responsibility accepted".

## Strict liability vs negligence

The Article 30 claim is **not strict liability**. The defendant
has access to the [Article 31](./prospectus-exemption.md) exemption
defences — most importantly, the **reasonable-investigation /
reasonable-belief** defence. A defendant who took reasonable
steps to verify the prospectus contents has a defence.

## Claimants

Claimants are typically:

- **Investors who subscribed** for the securities on the
  prospectus;
- **Investors who acquired** the securities on the secondary
  market within a period that the misstatement affected the
  price;

— who can show **loss** caused by the misstatement.

[Article 32](./prospectus-recovery.md) addresses procedural aspects
of recovery, including that the compensation is treated as a debt
rather than a member-contribution issue.

## Pitfalls

- **Director liability is personal** and joint-and-several with
  the issuer. Directors of issuing companies should ensure they
  have **prospectus-liability insurance** and that the
  prospectus has been **diligence-verified** before sign-off.
- **Adviser-responsibility-statement creep** — advisers
  sometimes get drawn into responsibility for parts of a
  prospectus (e.g., legal opinions). Be deliberate about what
  responsibility is being accepted.
- **Forgetting cross-border claims** — Article 30 governs
  Jersey-law claims; the foreign jurisdiction may have its own
  prospectus-liability regime that applies in parallel.
