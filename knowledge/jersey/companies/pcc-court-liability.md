# Article 127YW — Court May Determine Liability of Protected Cell Companies

Article 127YW of the **[Companies (Jersey) Law 1991][cjl]** gives
the **Royal Court** power to determine **disputes about cellular
attribution** in a PCC.

## What Article 127YW covers

On the application of the PCC, a creditor, a member, a cell
shareholder, or any other person with sufficient interest, the
Royal Court may make orders:

- **Determining** whether a particular asset is cellular (and
  if so, to which cell) or non-cellular;
- **Determining** whether a particular liability is cellular (and
  if so, of which cell) or non-cellular;
- Determining **whether recourse** is available from one pool to
  another;
- Resolving **competing claims** between cells, between cellular
  and non-cellular creditors;
- Other relief consistent with Part 18D.

(See the consolidated text at [jerseylaw.je][cjl].)

## Why this matters

The segregation rules in Articles 127YT and 127YU are
**substantive law**, but their application to specific factual
scenarios can be **contested**:

- A creditor alleges that **the PCC** (not a cell) was the
  contracting party, hence has access to non-cellular assets;
- A cell shareholder alleges that **directors misallocated** an
  asset to the wrong pool;
- Two cells dispute **shared overhead allocation**.

Article 127YW gives the Royal Court a **clean statutory basis**
for resolving these disputes — rather than relying on general
declaratory jurisdiction or hostile insolvency-court
proceedings.

The Court's order is **binding on all interested persons** and
provides authoritative clarity for future dealings.
