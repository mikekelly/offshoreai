# Article 16 — Change of Status of Public Company (Public → Private)

Article 16 of the **[Companies (Jersey) Law 1991][cjl]** governs
the **conversion of a public company to private status**.

## What Article 16 says

A public company **with 30 or fewer members** may become private
by **altering its memorandum**.

A public company **exceeding 30 members** requires **JFSC
approval** if its activities represent "**domestic concern of
members**".

The JFSC may **impose conditions** on the approval and may
**withdraw approval** if conditions are breached or circumstances
change.

The company must **deliver notice** of any conversion (with the
altered memorandum copy) to the **Registrar within 14 days**.

**Members may appeal** any directions of the JFSC as
"**unreasonable**" within **28 days**.

(See the consolidated text at [jerseylaw.je][cjl].)

## When public-to-private conversion is used

Common scenarios:

- **De-listing**: a publicly-listed Jersey TopCo that has been
  acquired or taken private converts to private status to reduce
  ongoing public-company obligations (audit, prospectus regime,
  AGM formality).
- **Restructuring**: a Jersey holding company in a group whose
  parent has been acquired or is going through restructuring may
  shift to private form.
- **Mature small public companies** that never needed public form
  but were incorporated as public for historic reasons.

## The 30-member threshold

The 30-member threshold reflects historical Jersey policy:

- Up to 30 members, the conversion is **routine** — just a
  memorandum amendment.
- Above 30 members, JFSC scrutiny applies because the conversion
  is approaching the territory where the company may resemble a
  public market participant.

Note that the **30-member limit** historically applied to private
companies under Article 17 (private companies couldn't have more
than 30 members), but that limit has been **abolished by Order**
under Article 17D. So a private company can have any number of
members; but the 30-member threshold in Article 16 for *converting
to private* still bites at conversion.

## JFSC approval and conditions

Where JFSC approval is required, the Commission may:

- **approve** unconditionally;
- **approve subject to conditions** (typical: a continuing
  disclosure regime, a member-information policy);
- **decline** approval;
- **withdraw** approval later if conditions are breached.

The "**domestic concern of members**" test is the Commission's
discretionary judgment — typically focused on whether the
company's affairs are sufficiently contained that the additional
public-company protections are not needed.

## Appeal as "unreasonable"

The 28-day appeal window allows members or the company to
challenge a JFSC direction on grounds of unreasonableness. The
Royal Court's review applies standard public-law principles:
*Wednesbury*-style unreasonableness, procedural fairness, and
material-consideration tests.

## Procedure

1. **Board proposal** to convert.
2. **JFSC pre-clearance** if more than 30 members.
3. **Special resolution** of members altering the memorandum.
4. **Filing** with the Registrar within 14 days.
5. **Updated certificate** of incorporation reflecting new
   status.

The effective-date mechanics are in
[Article 17B](./status-change-effective-date.md).

## Pitfalls

- **Failing to obtain JFSC pre-clearance** for >30-member
  companies — invalidates the conversion.
- **Missing the 14-day filing window** — exposes the company to
  late-filing penalties and can complicate the effective-date
  question.
- **Continuing public-company practices** post-conversion (e.g.,
  publishing public financials) — administratively wasteful
  and may invite confusion about the company's status.
