# Article 11 — Alteration of Memorandum and Articles

Article 11 of the **[Companies (Jersey) Law 1991][cjl]** sets out
how the **memorandum and articles** of a Jersey company can be
altered after incorporation, and the limits on what alterations
may do.

## What Article 11 says

The memorandum and articles may be altered:

- by **special resolution** of the members;
- including provisions for **winding-up timelines** (limited-
  duration company terms);

**but** alterations cannot:

- require a member to **take additional shares** without that
  member's **written consent**;
- **increase the liability** of a member without that member's
  **written consent**.

For **existing companies** (those carried over from the pre-1991
regime), additional restrictions apply:

- alterations cannot **shorten** an existing duration period;
- alterations cannot **alter class rights** unless approved by
  **all members** or by **the Court**.

(See the consolidated text at [jerseylaw.je][cjl].)

## The special-resolution gate

A **special resolution** under
[Article 90](special-resolution.md) *(planned)* of the Companies
Law is the threshold: typically requires:

- the **majority specified in the deed** (the Companies Law
  default is a two-thirds majority of votes cast, but the deed
  may impose higher thresholds);
- **notice** as required by the deed and Part 15 of the Law;
- the **resolution text** as proposed (subject to amendments
  within the deed's procedure).

Most alterations to memoranda and articles in modern Jersey
practice are routine special resolutions — director changes,
share-class adjustments, fee-clause updates, governance
modernisation.

## The two member-protection rules

### No new share obligations without written consent

A member who has subscribed for, say, 100 shares cannot be
compelled by majority resolution to take more. The taking of
shares is a **personal commitment** that the majority cannot
impose on the minority.

### No increased liability without written consent

A member's **liability is bounded** by what the member agreed to
on becoming a member. The majority cannot expand it. This
protects, for example:

- a member of a **limited company** from being switched into an
  **unlimited** form;
- a **guarantee** member from having the guarantee amount raised;
- a **shareholder** from having further calls imposed beyond what
  the original deed permits.

If the company wants to make such a change, **the affected
member's written consent** is required.

## Class rights and pre-1991 companies

For pre-1991 carry-over companies, alteration of **class rights**
needs:

- **unanimous member consent**; or
- **Court approval**.

For post-1991 companies, alteration of class rights is governed by
[Article 52 (variation of class rights)](variation-of-class-rights.md)
*(planned)* — which has its own mechanism (typically a class
meeting and class-special-resolution).

## When the Court is required

The Royal Court is the fallback authority where:

- the deed's own amendment mechanism cannot be invoked;
- the proposed amendment requires consents that cannot be
  obtained;
- there is a class-rights variation that needs Court approval.

Court applications under the Companies Law are typically by
Representation in the Royal Court (Samedi Division).

## Practical drafting

A typical alteration of articles:

1. **Board proposes** the alteration.
2. **Notice** of general meeting (or written resolution
   circulation) per the deed.
3. **Special resolution** passed.
4. **Filed copy** of the resolution delivered to the Registrar
   under [Article 100](filing-of-resolutions.md) *(planned)*.
5. **Updated articles** become the company's constitution from
   the resolution date (or such later date as the resolution
   provides).

## Pitfalls

- **Increasing liability without consent**. A resolution that
  effectively increases member liability is **void** as to any
  member who didn't consent in writing. Document consents
  individually.
- **Class-rights changes via ordinary articles amendment**. A
  variation of class rights is governed by Article 52, not
  Article 11. Confusing the two creates risk of invalidity.
- **Pre-1991 companies and shortened duration**. Don't try to
  cut the duration of a carry-over company by general
  amendment — special protections apply.
