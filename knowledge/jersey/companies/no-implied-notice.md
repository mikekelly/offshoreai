# Article 19 — No Implied Notice of Public Records

Article 19 of the **[Companies (Jersey) Law 1991][cjl]** abolishes
the doctrine of **constructive notice** of company records held at
the Registry.

## What Article 19 says

A person **cannot be deemed to have notice** of the contents of
the Registrar's records or of a company's filed records **by
reason only** that they are made available for inspection.

(See the consolidated text at [jerseylaw.je][cjl].)

## What this changes

At common law (historically), counterparties dealing with a
company were deemed to have **constructive notice** of the
company's publicly-filed memorandum, articles, and other records.
A counterparty who didn't actually read the filed documents was
treated **as if** they had read them — and so was bound by any
restrictions in the documents.

Article 19 abolishes this for Jersey companies. The
consequences:

- **Counterparties need not investigate the Registry** before
  contracting with a Jersey company.
- A restriction in the deed — e.g., a limit on the directors'
  authority to sign without member approval, an unusual
  requirement of two-director signing — **does not bind** a
  counterparty who didn't have **actual** notice of it.
- This is the **mirror image** of [Article 18](./corporate-capacity.md)
  (capacity abolished) and works together with
  [Article 20](./form-of-contracts.md) (contract authority).

## Actual notice still binds

Article 19 only abolishes **constructive** notice. **Actual
notice** still binds:

- A counterparty who **has been told** of a restriction is
  bound by it;
- A counterparty who **has reviewed** the deed and seen the
  restriction is bound by it;
- A counterparty whose **agents** (e.g., its own lawyer)
  reviewed the deed and saw the restriction may have imputed
  notice through the agent.

For routine commercial transactions, the typical counterparty
relies on Article 19 (no constructive notice) and Article 20 (a
person with express or implied authority can bind the company)
without doing deed-review.

## Comparison with the firewall in trust law

Article 19's "no implied notice" rule for company records has a
loose parallel in [Article 31 of the Trusts Law](../trusts/trustee-multiple-trusts.md)
(no cross-trust notice for trustees acting in multiple trusts).
Both are pragmatic responses to the practical impossibility of
holding parties to constructive knowledge of everything they
could in principle look up.

## Counterparty due diligence

Notwithstanding Article 19, sophisticated counterparties
typically still:

- Obtain a **certificate of incumbency / good standing** from
  the registered agent;
- Obtain a **certified copy of the memorandum and articles**
  for the file;
- Verify the **directors and authorised signatories**;
- Obtain **board resolutions** authorising specific large
  transactions.

Article 19 means this is **optional protection** rather than
required investigation — but for material transactions it's
prudent.

## Pitfalls

- **Treating actual notice as constructive notice**. If the
  counterparty has been put on notice of a restriction (e.g., a
  letter referring to it), Article 19 doesn't help.
- **Imputed knowledge through agents**. The counterparty's
  lawyer's knowledge may be imputed to the counterparty in some
  scenarios.
