# Companies (Jersey) Law 1991

The **Companies (Jersey) Law 1991** is Jersey's principal company
statute. Its long title is:

> A LAW to provide for the incorporation, regulation and winding up of
> companies, and for connected purposes.

It is consolidated on [jerseylaw.je][cjl] and has been amended
repeatedly since enactment. The Law has been the model for the modern
Jersey financial-services industry's corporate vehicles, including
**cell companies**, **continuance** (a.k.a. corporate migration), and
**no-par-value** share capital.

This page summarises the **structure** of the Law — its Parts and the
principal Articles — and provides cross-links to topic-specific files
that go deeper.

The Law is divided into 24 Parts (some lettered to preserve numbering
after later insertions):

| 8 | Share Capital |

| 10 | Class Rights |
| 11 | Redemption and Purchase of Shares |
| 12 | Reduction of Capital |
| 13 | Administration |
| 14 | Directors and Secretary |

| 16 | Accounts and Audits |
| 17 | Distributions |
| 18 | Takeovers |

(Confirmed against the consolidated text on jerseylaw.je. ([cjl][cjl]))

The lettered Parts (18A through 20A) reflect later additions: the
mergers regime (Part 18B), the continuance regime (Part 18C), the cell
company regime (Part 18D, Articles 127YA–127YW), and the economic-
substance test (Part 20A).

## Types of company — Article 3 series

Jersey companies are classified by the **member-liability axis** and
by the **share-capital axis**, both set out in the Article 3 cluster.

- **Limited / Unlimited.** A Jersey company may be **limited by
  shares**, **limited by guarantee**, or have **no limit on the
  liability of members** ("unlimited"). The election is made on
  incorporation. Articles 3C and 3D formally define limited and
  unlimited companies; Article 3(7) provides the menu of liability
  options.
- **Par-value / No-par-value.** Article 3E defines a **par-value
  company** (shares with a nominal value); Article 3F defines a
  **no-par-value company** (shares without a nominal value, all
  paid-up). No-par-value is the more common modern choice for
  Jersey finance-industry companies because it removes the
  share-premium-account technicalities and simplifies distributions.
- **Guarantee companies.** Article 3G defines a **guarantee company**
  as one whose members are guarantors (typically used for not-for-
  profit and certain charity-style vehicles).
- **Cell companies.** Article 3I defines **incorporated** and
  **protected** cell companies, with the detailed regime in Part 18D
  (Articles 127YA–127YW). See [cell-companies.md](cell-companies.md).

The orthogonal **public / private** classification sits in Part 4 of
the Law — see [types-of-company.md](types-of-company.md).

## Directors' duties — Article 74

Article 74 of the Companies Law sets out the **statutory duty** of a
director:

- to act **honestly** and **in good faith** with a view to the **best
  interests** of the company, and
- to exercise the **care, diligence and skill** that a reasonably
  prudent person would exercise in comparable circumstances.

The statutory duty does not displace common-law and equitable duties
preserved alongside it (fiduciary duties to the company, the duty to
avoid conflicts, the no-profit rule); the Article 74 wording is the
floor, not the ceiling.

Treatment of practical issues — cross-border directorships,
shadow-director liability, board minutes as evidence of management
and control for tax and substance purposes — is in
[directors-duties.md](directors-duties.md).

## Distributions and the solvency test — Part 17 and Article 61A

Jersey's distribution regime is different from the UK's. Instead of a
*profit-based* test for lawful dividends, Jersey uses a **cash-flow
solvency test**: a company may make a distribution if its directors
have made a **solvency statement** under **Article 61A** of the
Companies Law confirming that, immediately after the distribution,
the company will be able to discharge its liabilities as they fall
due over the relevant 12-month period, and that the value of the
company's assets will not be less than the amount of its liabilities.

Failure to support the distribution with a solvency statement renders
it unlawful and exposes the directors personally to claims by the
company and its creditors. See
[distributions-and-solvency-test.md](distributions-and-solvency-test.md).

## Cell companies — Part 18D

Part 18D of the Companies Law (Articles 127YA–127YW) governs cell
companies:

- A **Protected Cell Company (PCC)** is a single legal entity with
  multiple cells; each cell's assets are protected by statute from
  the liabilities of the other cells and of the company's "core",
  but the cells do not have separate legal personality.
- An **Incorporated Cell Company (ICC)** is similar but each cell is
  a **separate legal person** (an "incorporated cell"), giving
  cleaner ring-fencing at the cost of having to maintain separate
  cells with their own registration files.

Cell companies are used heavily in **insurance** (segregating
underwriting books) and in **funds** (segregating sub-funds within a
single umbrella). See [cell-companies.md](cell-companies.md).

## Mergers, demergers, continuance — Parts 18A, 18B, 18BA, 18C

The Companies Law allows Jersey companies to:

- enter **compromises and arrangements** under Part 18A (Court-
  sanctioned schemes analogous to the English scheme of arrangement);
- **merge** with another company under Part 18B (a statutory merger
  that allows one entity to assume the rights and liabilities of
  another without separate transfers);
- **demerge** under Part 18BA;
- **continue** into Jersey from another jurisdiction (immigration of
  a foreign company), or out of Jersey into another jurisdiction
  (emigration), under Part 18C — i.e., corporate **redomiciliation**.

See [mergers-and-continuance.md](mergers-and-continuance.md).

## Unfair prejudice and just-and-equitable winding-up — Part 20

A member of a Jersey company may apply to the Royal Court under
Part 20 for relief on the basis that the company's affairs are
being or have been conducted in a manner **unfairly prejudicial** to
the member's interests. The Court has a wide range of remedies,
including a share-purchase order at a court-determined valuation.
See [unfair-prejudice.md](unfair-prejudice.md).

## Economic substance test — Part 20A

**Part 20A** of the Companies Law was inserted to give the **economic-
substance** test for Jersey-tax-resident companies a foothold in the
companies regime. The substantive substance rules live in the
**Taxation (Companies — Economic Substance) (Jersey) Law 2019** —
covered in [tax/economic-substance.md](../tax/economic-substance.md).

## Winding-up — Part 21

Jersey has two principal corporate insolvency / wind-up routes:

- **Solvent members' winding-up** under Part 21 of the Companies Law
  — a board-and-shareholder-driven process for an orderly close-out.
- **Désastre** — a customary-law procedure on application of the
  company itself or a creditor to the Royal Court, with the Viscount
  of Jersey taking control of assets.

A **creditors' winding-up** route also sits within Part 21 and can be
initiated where a company cannot continue solvent. See
[winding-up.md](winding-up.md).

## Relationship with other Laws

The Companies Law sits alongside several adjacent statutes:

- **[Control of Borrowing (Jersey) Law 1947][cbl]** — the consent
  regime for raising capital, including the consent-to-incorporate
  paperwork.
- **[Financial Services (Jersey) Law 1998][fsjl]** — the regulatory
  regime that captures Jersey companies operating in defined
  regulated activities (trust company business, fund services
  business, investment business, etc.).
- **[Income Tax (Jersey) Law 1961][itl]** — the tax statute that
  determines how a Jersey company is taxed: see
  [tax/zero-ten.md](../tax/zero-ten.md).
- **[Companies (Takeovers and Mergers Panel) (Jersey) Law 2009][cmp]**
  — the panel-administered takeover regime for Jersey public
  companies.
