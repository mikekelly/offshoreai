# Article 61A — Solvency Statement

**Article 61A** of the **[Companies (Jersey) Law 1991][cjl]** is
the **centrepiece** of Jersey corporate capital management. The
**solvency statement** is the directors' written confirmation that
the company will remain solvent after a proposed capital reduction
(or distribution under Article 115). Get the solvency statement
right and the company can reduce its capital fast and cheaply;
get it wrong and the directors face personal liability.

## What Article 61A says

The directors must make a **solvency statement** declaring that,
having regard to the company's circumstances after the proposed
reduction, **the company will be able to pay its debts as they
fall due**.

The solvency statement must:

- **Identify the company**;
- **State the date** it was made;
- Be **signed by each director**;
- Be **accompanied by a statutory declaration**.

Directors making a **false solvency statement** face potential
liability for the company's debts.

(See the consolidated text at [jerseylaw.je][cjl].)

## What the directors must assess

The directors must form the view that the company will be **able
to pay its debts as they fall due**. This is a **cash-flow
solvency test**, not a balance-sheet test:

- The question is **future cash flow** — can the company actually
  meet its obligations as they come due?
- Not "are the company's assets greater than its liabilities at
  this moment"?

The directors must consider:

- **Existing debts** and their maturity profile;
- **Contingent and prospective debts** (including reasonably
  foreseeable future obligations);
- **Operating cash flow** projections;
- **Available financing** — committed facilities, parent
  guarantees, etc.;
- The **effect of the reduction itself** — does the reduction
  leave the company with adequate liquidity?

A **forward-looking horizon** — typically 12 months minimum — is
appropriate.

## The "all directors must sign" requirement

**Every director** must sign the solvency statement. This:

- Creates **joint and several** exposure for inaccuracy;
- Ensures **all directors have considered** the question;
- Prevents the company from using one director as a "fall guy"
  for an aggressive statement.

A director who **disagrees** with the proposed solvency view
should **not sign**. If a director cannot sign, the solvency-
statement route is **unavailable** and the company must use the
court route.

## Statutory declaration

The Article requires the solvency statement to be **accompanied
by a statutory declaration**. The declaration is sworn before a
**Commissioner for Oaths** in Jersey (or equivalent abroad) and
gives the document additional formal weight.

## Personal liability for false statements

A director who makes a **false solvency statement** without
reasonable grounds:

- May be **personally liable** for the company's debts (typically
  to the extent of the reduction or the creditor losses);
- Commits an **offence** under the Companies Law;
- Faces possible **disqualification** under
  [Article 78](disqualification-orders.md) *(planned)*.

The liability is **substantial** — it can be the full amount of
the reduction in some cases. This is the **price** of the
solvency-statement route's speed and simplicity.

## The cash-flow vs profits-available distinction

Jersey's reliance on cash-flow solvency rather than the English
**"profits available for distribution"** test is a **defining
feature** of the Jersey corporate regime:

- **Jersey**: future cash-flow ability to pay debts;
- **English**: accumulated realised profits less realised losses
  on a balance-sheet basis.

The Jersey test is **easier to satisfy** for early-stage or
loss-making companies that have liquidity but not accumulated
profits — they can still reduce capital or pay distributions if
they are cash-flow solvent. The English test rules them out.

## Article 115 distributions

The same solvency-statement mechanism is used for **distributions
under Part 17** — see
[Article 115](distribution-restrictions.md) *(planned)*. The
directors confirm solvency before authorising a distribution. The
solvency test is the **unified Jersey-law standard** for capital-
out-of-company transactions.

## Pitfalls

- **Inadequate forward-looking analysis** — relying on a
  balance-sheet snapshot rather than cash-flow projections.
  Inadequate, and risky if the company subsequently encounters
  liquidity stress.
- **Reluctant-director signing** — every director must
  genuinely assess and sign. A director pressured into signing
  without conviction is personally exposed.
- **Ignoring contingent liabilities** — pending litigation,
  guarantee exposures, tax assessments. These can crystallise
  and undermine the solvency view.
- **Cross-border insolvency** — a Jersey-solvent company in a
  group where foreign subsidiaries are stressed should consider
  the group context.
- **Documentation gaps** — the statement should be supported by
  a contemporaneous **directors' memorandum** explaining the
  basis. A bare signed statement is weak evidence in subsequent
  challenge.
