# Article 155 — Power for Court to Wind Up (Just and Equitable)

Article 155 of the **[Companies (Jersey) Law 1991][cjl]** gives
the **Royal Court** power to wind up a Jersey company on the
**"just and equitable"** ground — even where the company is
solvent.

## What Article 155 covers

The Royal Court may wind up a Jersey company on application by
a **member**, the **Minister**, or the **JFSC**, where the Court
considers it **just and equitable** to do so.

Recognised grounds for "just and equitable" winding up include:

- **Loss of substratum** — the company's commercial purpose has
  become impossible or has fundamentally changed;
- **Deadlock** — the members or directors are deadlocked and the
  company cannot function;
- **Quasi-partnership breakdown** — in small companies with the
  characteristics of partnership, the breakdown of mutual trust
  and confidence;
- **Justifiable lack of confidence** in the management;
- **Fraud or oppression** of a kind not adequately addressed by
  unfair-prejudice proceedings.

(See the consolidated text at [jerseylaw.je][cjl].)

## Why this matters

The **just-and-equitable jurisdiction** is one of the oldest in
company law — it goes back to the original Companies Acts and is
recognised across the Commonwealth.

It is **complementary** to:

- **Unfair-prejudice** (Article 141) — focused on remedying
  specific wrongful conduct, often by buyout;
- **Solvent liquidation** (summary winding up) — voluntary
  member-driven;
- **Creditors' winding up** — insolvency-driven.

A member who has lost faith in the company and **cannot get a fair
exit** may apply under Article 155 to force the company into
liquidation, with distribution of the surplus.

## Relationship with unfair-prejudice

Article 141 (unfair prejudice) is often the **first port of call**
because the buyout remedy is cleaner than full liquidation.
Article 155 is invoked where:

- A buyout cannot work (no one to buy);
- The company is genuinely unviable;
- The applicant prefers cash from liquidation to share value;
- The fact pattern is more **deadlock** than **prejudice**.
