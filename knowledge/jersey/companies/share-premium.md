# Article 39 — Share Premium Accounts (Par-Value Companies)

Article 39 of the **[Companies (Jersey) Law 1991][cjl]** requires
a **par-value** Jersey company to credit any **premium received on
share issuance** to a separate **share-premium account**. The
account is subject to specific usage restrictions.

## What Article 39 says

Any **premium received on issue** of par-value shares **shall be
credited to a share-premium account**.

The share-premium account is **subject to restrictions on
distribution** and must be **maintained separately** from the
nominal capital account.

(See the consolidated text at [jerseylaw.je][cjl].)

## How the share-premium mechanism works

For a par-value company:

- Each share has a **nominal value** (the par);
- A share **issued for more than par** generates a **premium** —
  the difference between issue price and par value;
- The premium goes to the **share-premium account**, which is a
  reserved capital account distinct from the nominal capital;
- The **paid-up capital** of the company is the **sum of**
  nominal capital and share-premium balances.

Example:

- Par value: £1 per share
- Issue price: £10 per share
- Per share: £1 to nominal capital, £9 to share-premium account

The share-premium account is a kind of **secondary capital
reserve** — it's part of the company's capital base but is
recorded separately for legal and accounting purposes.

## Distribution restrictions

The Article restricts distribution from the share-premium
account. The traditional reasoning:

- **Creditors and other shareholders** rely on the company's
  capital base (nominal + premium) being preserved;
- **Distributing share premium** as a dividend would undermine
  that base.

Permitted uses of the share-premium account historically
included:

- **Bonus issues** of fully-paid shares (capitalising the premium);
- Write-off of **preliminary expenses** of incorporation;
- Write-off of **expenses of share issue or share-buyback
  premiums**.

But **direct distribution** to shareholders generally requires
following the **Part 12 capital-reduction procedure** with the
Article 61A solvency-statement.

## Relief from transfers — Article 39B

[Article 39B](./relief-from-transfers.md) provides for the **States**
to make Regulations relieving companies from the requirement to
maintain the share-premium account in specified circumstances.
This is the legislative gateway for streamlining the
share-premium regime where appropriate.

## No-par-value alternative

The complications of the share-premium account are one reason
modern Jersey practice favours **no-par-value** share capital —
under [Article 39A](./stated-capital.md), no-par-value companies
use a **stated-capital account** instead, which has fewer
historical use restrictions.

## Conversion from par-value to no-par-value

A par-value company can **convert to no-par-value** under
[Article 40A](./conversion-par-value.md). On conversion, the
share-premium account typically merges into the stated-capital
account.

## Pitfalls

- **Distributing share premium as a dividend** without following
  Part 12 procedure — exposes the directors to breach claims and
  the recipient shareholders to clawback under
  [Article 115A](./unlawful-distribution-consequences.md) *(planned)*.
- **Confusing share premium with profits available for
  distribution** — they're different concepts; profits are
  available for ordinary dividends, share premium typically is
  not.
