# Article 29 — Prospectuses

Article 29 of the **[Companies (Jersey) Law 1991][cjl]** is the
**prospectus gateway** — it defines what counts as a prospectus,
authorises the Minister to make Orders restricting their
circulation, and frames the public-offering regime for Jersey
companies.

## What Article 29 says

### Ministerial Order power

The **Minister may make Orders** restricting:

- the **circulation in Jersey** of prospectuses; or
- the circulation **by Jersey companies** outside Jersey;

**except** under specified circumstances and conditions. The
Orders may require **fee payments**.

Non-compliance is an **offence** for individuals and corporate
officers alike.

### Definition of "prospectus"

The Law defines "prospectus" **narrowly to exclude**:

- **qualified-investor** offerings;
- **small-scale distributions**;
- offerings with **minimum-investment thresholds**;
- offerings under **specific regulatory schemes**.

An **invitation to acquire** unpaid or recently-allotted
securities **constitutes a prospectus** unless proven otherwise.

(See the consolidated text at [jerseylaw.je][cjl].)

## What the regime catches

The Article 29 regime captures **broad-market offerings of
securities** by or for Jersey companies. The detailed
prospectus content and process requirements are set by the
**Companies (General Provisions) (Jersey) Order 2002** and
related orders made under Article 29.

In practice the Jersey prospectus regime is **less restrictive**
than the EU prospectus regime (PR3 / its successor) because
Jersey is outside the EU. Jersey-based issuers offering to EU or
UK markets typically need to comply with the **foreign-market
prospectus rules** as well as (or instead of) Jersey's.

## The qualified-investor / small-scale carve-outs

A Jersey company offering securities to:

- **qualified investors** (typically defined by reference to net
  worth or sophistication);
- a **small number of offerees** (the threshold is specified in
  the Order);
- with a **high minimum investment** per offeree;

is **not making a prospectus offer** for Article 29 purposes.
These offerings are operationally lighter — they can be
documented in **information memoranda** or **offering circulars**
without the full prospectus framework.

This is the regime under which most Jersey **private placements**
and **fund offerings to professional investors** operate.

## Interaction with private-to-public auto-conversion

[Article 17](./private-to-public.md) **automatically converts a
private company to public** when it **circulates a prospectus**.
Article 29 defines what circulation counts.

A private company that issues an **information memorandum to
qualified investors** is **not** circulating a prospectus under
Article 29 and therefore does **not** auto-convert under Article
17. A private company that issues a **public-facing prospectus**
does.

## Modern practice

Modern Jersey prospectus offerings:

- **Public listings** on the LSE, Euronext, NASDAQ, etc. follow
  the **listing-market's prospectus rules** with Jersey-law
  compliance as a complementary requirement;
- **Private placements** rely on the qualified-investor or
  minimum-investment carve-outs;
- **Fund offerings** typically use **fund-specific exemptions**
  under the Collective Investment Funds (Jersey) Law 1988 or the
  Alternative Investment Funds (Jersey) Regulations 2012, plus
  the Article 29 carve-outs;
- **Bond issues** follow the indenture-level prospectus rules of
  the listing or governing law.

## Pitfalls

- **Inadvertent prospectus** — a private company circulating
  an investment invitation that exceeds the carve-out limits
  triggers Article 17 auto-conversion to public status.
- **Cross-border offerings** — Jersey carve-outs don't cover
  the foreign jurisdiction; comply with the foreign rules also.
- **Misclassifying recipients** — relying on the
  qualified-investor carve-out requires diligence on each
  recipient's status.
