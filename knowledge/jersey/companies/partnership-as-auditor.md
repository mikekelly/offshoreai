# Article 113G — Effect of Appointment of a Partnership

Article 113G of the **[Companies (Jersey) Law 1991][cjl]**
addresses the position when an **audit partnership** (typically
a firm of accountants) is appointed as auditor of a Jersey
company.

## What Article 113G covers

When a **partnership** is appointed as auditor:

- **all partners** must meet independence and qualification
  requirements;
- **removal** of the auditor partnership requires consideration
  of **individual partner circumstances** affecting their
  collective ability to conduct the audit objectively.

(See the consolidated text at [jerseylaw.je][cjl].)

## How audit firms work

Most Jersey company audits are conducted by **partnerships** —
firms of accountants. The partnership is appointed as
**auditor**, and the firm assigns specific partners and staff
to conduct the work.

Article 113G clarifies the position:

- The **whole partnership** is the auditor (not just the lead
  partner);
- **All partners** must satisfy independence and qualification
  requirements;
- A **single partner's** independence problem can compromise the
  whole firm.

## Practical implications

Audit firms maintain **firm-wide independence systems**:

- Partner-level **independence questionnaires** (no individual
  partner can hold shares in audit clients);
- **Client-acceptance protocols** vetting independence at
  engagement;
- **Ongoing monitoring** of independence throughout the
  engagement period;
- **Insurance and indemnity** structures protecting the
  partnership.

## Mid-engagement partner changes

A change in audit partners during an engagement:

- Doesn't typically affect the **firm's** appointment;
- Requires the **new partner** to be independent;
- May require **disclosure** to the audit committee or company
  governance.

## Pitfalls

- **Off-side partner** with a financial interest — can
  compromise the firm's audit appointment.
- **Inadequate firm-wide independence** systems — a small firm
  without robust systems is at higher risk.
