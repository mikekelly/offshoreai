# Article 143 — Powers of Court (Unfair Prejudice)

Article 143 of the **[Companies (Jersey) Law 1991][cjl]** sets
out the **Royal Court's remedial powers** on a successful unfair-
prejudice petition.

## What Article 143 covers

If satisfied that the petition is made out, the Court may make
**such order as it thinks fit** for giving relief, including:

- **Regulating the conduct** of the company's affairs in the
  future;
- **Requiring** the company to do or refrain from doing
  specified acts;
- **Authorising civil proceedings** to be brought in the name of
  the company by such person as the Court appoints;
- **Restraining changes** to the memorandum or articles without
  Court leave;
- **Ordering the purchase** of shares of any members by other
  members or by the company itself (a **buyout order**);
- **Ordering a reduction of capital** to effect a buyout;
- **Any other relief** the Court considers just.

(See the consolidated text at [jerseylaw.je][cjl].)

## Why this matters

The Court's powers are **deliberately broad** because unfair-
prejudice cases are **fact-sensitive** and remedies must fit the
specific wrong:

- **The buyout order** is by far the most common remedy — the
  petitioner exits at a court-determined fair value, with the
  conduct ceasing because the parties separate;
- **Regulation of future affairs** is used in continuing
  relationships where separation is impractical;
- **Authorisation of derivative proceedings** is used to recover
  losses caused by wrongdoers.

## Valuation in buyout orders

The most-litigated issue in unfair-prejudice cases is the
**valuation** of the petitioner's shares for a buyout order:

- **Date of valuation** — typically the date of the order, but
  the Court may select an earlier date if the conduct has
  depressed value (so the petitioner gets a fair price unaffected
  by the prejudicial conduct);
- **Discount for minority** — usually **not** applied in
  quasi-partnership cases (because the petitioner is treated as
  giving up a partnership interest);
- **Tax adjustments** — typically not made, as they introduce
  speculative elements.
