# Article 33 — Criminal Liability for Prospectuses

Article 33 of the **[Companies (Jersey) Law 1991][cjl]** is the
**criminal** counterpart to the
[Article 30](./prospectus-compensation.md) civil compensation
regime. Authorising circulation of a prospectus containing
material falsehoods or omissions is a criminal offence, subject
to a reasonable-belief defence.

## What Article 33 says

**Authorising circulation** of a prospectus that contains
**material falsehoods or omissions** constitutes an **offence** —
**unless** the person demonstrates **reasonable belief in
statement accuracy** when the prospectus was circulated.

(See the consolidated text at [jerseylaw.je][cjl].)

## What the offence captures

The offence requires:

- a **prospectus** within the meaning of
  [Article 29](./prospectuses.md);
- containing a **material falsehood** or **omission**;
- **circulated** in or from Jersey;
- with the defendant's **authorisation**.

"Authorisation" is broad — it captures the issuer, the directors
who approved circulation, the offeror, and those who took an
active role in the circulation. It does **not** capture
professionals giving advice that didn't extend to authorising
circulation.

## Reasonable belief defence

The defendant has a defence if they can show **reasonable belief
in accuracy** at the time of circulation. This mirrors the
[Article 31](./prospectus-exemption.md) civil defence — both turn
on the same essential due-diligence inquiry.

In practice:

- The same **verification exercise** that supports the Article 31
  civil defence supports the Article 33 criminal defence;
- The **contemporaneous documentation** of the verification is
  the key evidence in either case.

## Material falsehood / omission

The offence requires the misstatement to be **material** —
significant enough to affect a reasonable investor's decision.
Minor errors don't ground criminal liability (though may still
give rise to civil claims under Article 30 where loss results).

Both **affirmative misstatements** (saying something false) and
**omissions** (failing to disclose a material fact) can ground
the offence.

## Penalty

Convicted offenders face the penalty specified in **Schedule 1**
of the Companies Law — typically a fine, imprisonment, or both
(at the Royal Court's discretion). Directors and officers
convicted may also face **disqualification** under
[Article 78](./disqualification-orders.md) *(planned)*.

## Relationship with general fraud / Proceeds of Crime offences

A prospectus offence under Article 33 may also constitute:

- **Fraud** under general Jersey criminal law;
- A **predicate offence** for money-laundering purposes under the
  Proceeds of Crime (Jersey) Law 1999;
- **Insider-dealing or market-manipulation** offences where the
  prospectus is on a regulated market.

A serious prospectus misrepresentation may therefore engage
multiple criminal regimes in parallel.

## Prosecution

Prosecutions are brought by the **Attorney General** (the senior
Crown Officer) or by the Crown Advocates. The JFSC's role is
typically as **referrer** — the JFSC investigates and refers
matters to the AG for prosecution decision.

## Pitfalls

- **Treating Article 33 as purely civil-defence territory** —
  it has criminal teeth, including imprisonment in serious
  cases.
- **Director sign-off without verification** — directors who
  sign off on a prospectus without adequate due diligence are
  personally exposed under both Article 30 (civil) and Article
  33 (criminal).
- **Forgetting cross-border criminal exposure** — a prospectus
  circulated in foreign markets may engage foreign criminal
  prospectus regimes also.
