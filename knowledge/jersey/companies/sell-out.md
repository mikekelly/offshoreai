# Article 119 — Right of Minority Shareholder to be Bought Out by Offeror

Article 119 of the **[Companies (Jersey) Law 1991][cjl]** is the
**reciprocal of the squeeze-out** — the **sell-out right**. Where
a takeover offer has been very widely accepted, a minority
shareholder who **did not accept** can require the offeror to buy
their shares **on the offer terms**.

## What Article 119 covers

Where, by virtue of acceptances of a takeover offer, the offeror
has come to hold (alone or with associates) **at least 90%** in
value of all the shares of the relevant class (or carrying votes),
a holder of any shares to which the offer relates **may require**
the offeror to acquire those shares on the offer terms.

The shareholder exercises the right by:

- Serving a **written demand** on the offeror within a prescribed
  period (commonly 3 months from end of the offer period);
- The offeror is then **bound** to acquire the shares on the
  terms of the offer (or such other terms as may be agreed).

(See the consolidated text at [jerseylaw.je][cjl].)

## Why this matters

The sell-out right exists because, once a single bidder owns
**≥ 90%** of a company, the residual minority is in a fragile
position:

- The company is effectively under sole control;
- Liquidity in the residual shares disappears;
- The company is likely to be **delisted**;
- Future strategic decisions will be dictated by the controller.

Article 119 lets the minority **exit** at the offer price rather
than be trapped as a stranded minority shareholder.

## Mechanics

1. Takeover offer is made under Article 116;
2. Offer is accepted by enough shareholders that the offeror (with
   associates) holds **≥ 90%** of the relevant class;
3. **Notification** — the offeror must give notice to remaining
   shareholders of their **sell-out rights** within a prescribed
   time;
4. **Demand** — a remaining shareholder serves a written demand
   on the offeror;
5. **Acquisition** — see Article 120 for the effect of the demand
   (consideration on the same terms, transfer mechanics).

## The notification duty

The notification duty on the offeror is important: minority
shareholders cannot exercise the sell-out right if they don't
know about it. The notice tells them:

- That the threshold has been reached;
- Their right to require the offeror to buy them out;
- The deadline for serving a demand;
- The terms available (which must mirror the offer terms).
