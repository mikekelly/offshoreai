# Article 127YP — Status of Cells of Protected Cell Companies

Article 127YP of the **[Companies (Jersey) Law 1991][cjl]**
clarifies the **legal status of a cell within a PCC** — the
provision that distinguishes the PCC structure most clearly from
the ICC structure.

## What Article 127YP covers

A cell of a PCC:

- Is **not a separate legal person**;
- Has **no separate corporate existence** apart from the PCC;
- Is a **statutory compartment** of the PCC with its own
  segregated assets and liabilities;
- Can have **cell-specific shares** issued by the PCC (with the
  rights attributable solely to that cell's performance);
- Acts through the **PCC's directors** (the same directors govern
  all cells of the PCC).

The PCC as a whole is the **legal person** that contracts, holds
title, sues and is sued — but it does so **on behalf of** a
specific cell where the activity relates to that cell.

(See the consolidated text at [jerseylaw.je][cjl].)

## Why this matters

The PCC's **single-legal-personality** with **statutory
segregation** model has two main advantages over the ICC:

- **Lower cost** — only one company to administer, with shared
  service-provider, registered office, and overheads costing
  effectively across the cells;
- **Faster cell creation** — cells can be created and dissolved
  with internal documents, not registrar applications.

The corresponding cost:

- **Segregation is statutory, not structural** — it depends on
  the statutory effectiveness of Article 127YT and may not be
  recognised in jurisdictions that don't have PCC-equivalent
  laws;
- **Counterparty risk** — counterparties who don't appreciate
  the structure may not segregate properly in their documentation
  or accounting.
