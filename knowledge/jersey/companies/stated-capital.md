# Article 39A — Stated Capital Accounts (No-Par-Value Companies)

Article 39A of the **[Companies (Jersey) Law 1991][cjl]** is the
**no-par-value equivalent** of the par-value share-premium account.
A no-par-value Jersey company maintains a **stated-capital
account** for amounts received on share issuance.

## What Article 39A says

Amounts received on share issuance by a no-par-value company are
**credited to a stated-capital account** as **determined by the
company**.

This provides **greater flexibility** than the par-value share-
premium equivalent.

(See the consolidated text at [jerseylaw.je][cjl].)

## How the stated-capital mechanism works

For a no-par-value company:

- Shares have **no nominal value**;
- Issue proceeds go to the **stated-capital account** in full;
- The company **determines** how amounts are accounted for —
  there's no automatic par-vs-premium split.

Example:

- Issue price: £10 per share
- Whole £10 goes to stated capital

This is simpler than the par-value mechanism, which requires
splitting between nominal capital and share premium.

## Why stated capital is more flexible

The stated-capital account is **functionally similar** to the
share-premium account in that it represents the company's
capital base. But it has **fewer historical use restrictions**:

- The traditional limitations on **using share premium** (only
  for bonus issues, preliminary expenses, etc.) are loosened in
  the stated-capital context;
- The company has more **discretion in allocation** between
  classes;
- **Multi-currency** denominations are operationally cleaner —
  see [Article 38B](currency-conversion-rate.md).

The stated-capital account is still subject to the **Part 12
reduction-of-capital** rules with the Article 61A solvency-
statement mechanic — i.e., distributing stated capital still
requires following the capital-reduction procedure.

## Distribution constraints

The Article 39A stated-capital account, like the share-premium
account, represents **capital** rather than distributable
profits. Distributing stated capital to shareholders requires:

- Following **Part 12** procedure (reduction of capital);
- Making an **Article 61A solvency statement**;
- **Registration** of the solvency statement and reduction
  minute.

What can be distributed as **ordinary dividends** is profits
above and beyond the stated capital — the "distributable
profits" in accounting terms.

## Class-specific stated capital

A no-par-value company can have **multiple share classes** with
**different stated-capital balances per class**. The deed and
accounting practice typically:

- Track **stated capital per class** separately;
- Allocate **issue proceeds** to the correct class's account;
- Apply **class-rights variation** procedures under Articles
  52–54 to any change that affects only one class's stated
  capital.

## Relief from transfer requirements

[Article 39B](relief-from-transfers.md) lets the States provide
relief from the requirement to maintain separate stated-capital
accounts in specified circumstances — primarily for **specific
restructuring transactions**.

## Pitfalls

- **Treating stated capital as distributable** — it's capital,
  not profits. Distributing it requires Part 12 procedure.
- **Misallocation between classes** — issue proceeds should be
  allocated to the correct class's stated-capital account.
- **Multi-currency tracking** — multi-currency stated capital
  needs careful accounting to maintain class integrity.
