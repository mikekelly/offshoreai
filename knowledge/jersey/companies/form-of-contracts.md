# Article 20 — Form of Contracts

Article 20 of the **[Companies (Jersey) Law 1991][cjl]** is the
**authority-to-bind** provision: a person with express or implied
company authority can bind the company using normal contractual
mechanics, without ceremonial formalities.

## What Article 20 says

A person with **express or implied authority** to act on behalf of
the company **may**:

- **make, vary, or discharge** a contract on behalf of the
  company;
- **sign an instrument** on behalf of the company;

using **normal contractual procedures**.

**Court-approval requirements** under other Articles are
**unaffected** (e.g., where the Companies Law specifically
requires Court approval, that requirement still applies).

(See the consolidated text at [jerseylaw.je][cjl].)

## Authority — express or implied

A person can bind the company where they have:

- **Express authority** — a written power of attorney, board
  resolution, or specific delegation;
- **Implied authority** — authority that flows from their
  position (a managing director typically has wide implied
  authority; a junior employee typically does not).

The Article doesn't define "implied authority"; the case law
of agency and corporate-authority applies. The Royal Court
follows English common-law principles on the scope of implied
authority and apparent authority.

## Apparent authority

Although Article 20 mentions only express and implied authority,
the **apparent (ostensible) authority** doctrine survives. A
counterparty dealing with a person who **appears** to have
authority (by reason of the company's conduct or representations)
can hold the company to the contract even if the person did not
have actual authority.

Apparent authority is particularly important for:

- **Senior officers** (CEO, CFO) whose position implies wide
  authority to a counterparty;
- **Established signing patterns** — a company that has
  routinely accepted contracts signed by X is later estopped
  from saying X lacked authority on the next one;
- **Hold-out** of the person as authorised (giving them a title,
  business cards, etc.).

## No seal required for ordinary contracts

A Jersey company **can contract without applying its seal**.
Modern Jersey practice almost never uses the company seal for
ordinary commercial contracts — these are signed by authorised
officers under the Article 20 framework.

**Seal use is reserved** for:

- Documents the deed specifically requires to be sealed;
- Documents another jurisdiction's law requires to be sealed (some
  foreign-property transfer documents);
- Specific instruments under the Companies Law (e.g., share
  certificates in some cases).

For the seal mechanics, see
[`company-seals.md`](./company-seals.md) — Articles 22–24.

## Court-approval reservations

Where the Companies Law itself requires Court approval (e.g., for
reduction of capital under Part 12, for confirmation of certain
mergers, for variation orders under Article 47), Article 20 does
**not** override that requirement. The Court-approval gate
remains.

## Pitfalls

- **Mistaking apparent authority for actual authority**. The
  Article 20 doctrine is helpful for counterparties but doesn't
  cure a complete absence of authority — a person with no
  apparent or actual authority cannot bind the company.
- **Relying on seal-formality where Article 20 suffices**. Modern
  contracts typically don't need sealing; insisting on the seal
  can complicate transactions.
- **Over-reliance on Article 20 in high-value transactions**.
  For material transactions, counterparties typically also
  obtain a board resolution and an incumbency certificate.
