# Article 63 — Court Order Confirming Reduction

Article 63 of the **[Companies (Jersey) Law 1991][cjl]** sets the
**scope of the court order** confirming a capital reduction under
the Article 62 route.

## What Article 63 says

The Court may confirm the reduction:

- **on such terms and conditions as it thinks fit**;
- and may **modify members' liability** on the reduced shares.

(See the consolidated text at [jerseylaw.je][cjl].)

## What the court can include in the order

The Court's discretion is **wide** — the order can include:

- **Conditions for confirmation** — e.g., notice to creditors,
  staggered effective dates, holdback amounts;
- **Modifications** to the proposed reduction — e.g., a smaller
  reduction than originally proposed;
- **Members' liability adjustments** — Article 63 expressly
  contemplates modifying liability on the reduced shares,
  recognising that reduction can affect call obligations on
  partly-paid shares;
- **Creditor protection** — the court can order that specified
  creditors be paid, secured, or notified before the reduction
  takes effect.

## Member liability on reduced shares

A key Article 63 power: **modifying members' liability** on
reduced shares. This handles the case where:

- Shares are **partly paid**;
- The unpaid amount represents a continuing **call obligation**
  on the member;
- The **reduction** affects the amount that can lawfully be
  called.

The court can specify that the **reduced share** is treated as
either:

- **Fully paid** (the company waives the continuing call); or
- **Partly paid at a reduced amount** (the call obligation is
  reduced in proportion to the reduction).

This avoids the technical problem of partly-paid shares being
left in an ambiguous state post-reduction.

## Comparison with solvency-statement route

The solvency-statement route under
[Article 61A](./solvency-statement.md) doesn't involve a court
order — the reduction simply takes effect on registration of
the solvency statement and minute.

The court route under Articles 62–63 produces a **court order**
with potentially **bespoke terms** — useful for complex or
contested reductions but operationally heavier.

## Pitfalls

- **Court conditions overlooked** — the order may impose
  conditions that need to be satisfied before registration; miss
  one and the reduction is not validly effective.
- **Member-liability ambiguity** — if the court order doesn't
  expressly modify member liability and the deed is silent, the
  position on partly-paid reduced shares can be unclear.
