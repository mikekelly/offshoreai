# Article 3G — Guarantee Companies

Article 3G of the **[Companies (Jersey) Law 1991][cjl]** defines a
**guarantee company** — a company whose members consist **only of
guarantor members**. Guarantee companies have **no share capital**;
members agree to contribute a specified sum on wind-up.

## What Article 3G says

A guarantee company consists "**only of guarantor members**". Each
guarantor member's liability is to **contribute to the company's
assets** up to the amount of their guarantee on wind-up.

(See the consolidated text at [jerseylaw.je][cjl].)

## When guarantee companies are used

Guarantee companies are uncommon in commercial Jersey practice but
have specific roles:

### 1. Not-for-profit and charitable vehicles

A guarantee company has no share capital and no distributable
profits in the share-capital sense. This makes it well-suited to:

- **charitable companies** holding charity property;
- **not-for-profit associations** — clubs, societies, professional
  bodies;
- **mutual associations** where there is no profit motive.

### 2. Specific orphan structures

Some commercial structures use a guarantee company as an "orphan"
vehicle to hold shares of an operational SPV — the guarantee
company's lack of share capital makes it not "owned" in any
conventional sense, parallel to the function served by a
non-charitable purpose trust under
[Trusts Law Article 12](../trusts/purpose-trusts.md).

### 3. Hybrid arrangements

A company **limited by guarantee and having a share capital** was
historically a permitted hybrid form in some jurisdictions. The
Jersey position is that a company under Article 3G is **purely
guarantee** — no share capital. For a hybrid commercial-charitable
structure, a separate vehicle is typically used for each function.

## The "guarantor member" arrangement

Each member of a guarantee company:

- agrees in the memorandum to **contribute up to a specified
  amount** (the "guaranteed amount") on wind-up of the company;
- has no shares — their "membership" is the guarantee;
- has voting and other rights as specified in the memorandum and
  articles.

The **guaranteed amount per member** is usually nominal — often
£1, £10, or £100 per member. The function of the guarantee is to
provide a **wind-up contribution mechanism**, not to capitalise the
company. Operational funding comes from members' subscriptions or
external sources (donations, fees, grants).

## Memorandum and articles

A guarantee-company memorandum (under [Article 4B](./memorandum-guarantee.md)
— *planned*):

- states the company is **limited by guarantee**;
- specifies the **guarantee amount** per member;
- states the **objects** of the company (more important for
  guarantee companies than for share companies, because there is
  no shareholder-residual claim disciplining the directors);
- specifies any restrictions on distribution (typically prohibiting
  distribution of income or assets to members — the charitable /
  not-for-profit structure).

## Pitfalls

- **Confusing with limited-by-shares.** A guarantee company is a
  distinct structure; share-company concepts (issued capital,
  distributions, dividends) do not apply.
- **Charitable status.** Where the guarantee company is to be a
  charity, registration under the **Charities (Jersey) Law 2014**
  is a separate step from incorporation under the Companies Law.
- **Foreign tax characterisation.** Guarantee companies may be
  treated unusually in foreign jurisdictions — pre-model the tax
  position before forming.
