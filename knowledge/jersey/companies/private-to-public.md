# Article 17 — Change of Status of Private Company (Private → Public)

Article 17 of the **[Companies (Jersey) Law 1991][cjl]** governs
the **conversion of a private company to public status** — and
specifies when a private company is **automatically treated as
public** because of what it has done.

## What Article 17 says

### Voluntary conversion

A private company **with two or more members** may become public
by **altering its memorandum**.

### Automatic public treatment

A private company is **treated as public** if it:

- **exceeds 30 members**; or
- **circulates a prospectus**; or
- **becomes a "market traded company"** (i.e., has its shares
  admitted to trading on a recognised market).

### Notification

Notice to the Registrar within **14 days** of exceeding the
30-member threshold.

### Court relief

The **Court may grant relief** from the consequences of automatic
public treatment in appropriate cases.

### JFSC modification

The JFSC may **direct modified applicability** with appeal rights
(28 days) on "**unreasonableness**" grounds.

(See the consolidated text at [jerseylaw.je][cjl].)

## When private-to-public conversion is used

Common scenarios:

- **Pre-IPO restructuring** — a Jersey holding company being
  prepared for a public listing converts to public form ahead of
  the prospectus filing.
- **Public offering of bonds or equity** — even without listing,
  any public offer typically requires public-company status.
- **Group-structure reorganisation** — a private subsidiary in a
  publicly-held group is converted to public form to align with
  parent-company governance.

## The 30-member trigger

The most common Article 17 issue in practice is the **30-member
auto-conversion**. A private Jersey company that admits members
beyond 30 (typical in employee-share schemes, capital raises with
many small subscribers) becomes a **deemed public company**
without specific resolution to that effect.

The consequences are not trivial:

- **Audit becomes mandatory** under Part 16;
- **Prospectus rules** become applicable on issues to the public;
- **Annual general meeting** requirement under Part 15.

Note that the **Article 17D Order has abolished** the formal
30-member limit for private companies in much modern practice;
check the current Order before assuming the rule still bites in
its old form.

## The market-traded trigger

A private company that gets its shares **admitted to trading on
a recognised market** is treated as public automatically. This
applies to:

- Admission to a **recognised stock exchange** (LSE, NASDAQ,
  Euronext, etc.);
- Admission to a **Jersey-recognised foreign market** in
  appropriate cases;
- Listing of **debt securities** in some contexts.

The trigger applies on admission to trading; the company's
internal documents may not have caught up yet, but the deemed
public status applies from admission.

## The prospectus trigger

The third auto-conversion trigger: **circulation of a
prospectus** (in the Jersey Companies Law sense — see
[Article 29](prospectuses.md) *(planned)*). A private company
that issues a prospectus is deemed public from circulation.

## Court relief

Where auto-conversion would produce unfair or disproportionate
consequences, the **Royal Court may grant relief** under
Article 17 — typically by:

- excusing the company from specific public-company obligations
  for a transitional period;
- permitting the company to remain in private form despite the
  auto-trigger;
- imposing conditions less onerous than the full public-company
  regime.

## Pitfalls

- **Drifting past 30 members** without realising — most common
  failure. Audit obligations and AGM requirements kick in
  immediately; the company may be in technical breach for periods
  it didn't realise it was deemed public.
- **Failing to file the 14-day notice** when crossing the
  threshold.
- **Auto-conversion via inadvertent prospectus** — circulating
  what is in substance a prospectus (an invitation to subscribe
  to the public) can trigger automatic public status. Watch the
  definition.
- **Not applying for Court relief** in genuinely deserving
  cases — the relief is available but must be sought.
