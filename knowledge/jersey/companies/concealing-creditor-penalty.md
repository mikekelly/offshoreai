# Article 66 — Penalty for Concealing Name of Creditor

Article 66 of the **[Companies (Jersey) Law 1991][cjl]** is a
**criminal offence** for directors who **conceal creditor
information** during a capital-reduction procedure — protecting
the integrity of both the solvency-statement and court-
confirmation routes.

## What Article 66 says

**Directors concealing creditor information** during reduction
proceedings commit an **offence**.

The company may also be liable for **debts fraudulently
concealed**.

(See the consolidated text at [jerseylaw.je][cjl].)

## Why the offence exists

The capital-reduction regime depends on **accurate disclosure**
to:

- **Members** voting on the special resolution under Article 61;
- The **directors** signing the solvency statement under Article
  61A (so they can form a genuine view);
- The **Court** confirming under Article 62 (so it can assess
  creditor interests);
- **Creditors themselves** where notice procedures apply.

If directors **conceal creditors** to make the solvency picture
look better than it is, the reduction may proceed on a false
basis. Article 66 criminalises this concealment.

## What concealment looks like

- **Omitting** known creditors from the solvency analysis;
- **Disguising** contingent liabilities as remote;
- **Failing to notify** creditors who would otherwise object;
- **Misrepresenting** the size or nature of liabilities to the
  court.

## Personal director liability

Convicted directors face:

- **Penalty** specified in Schedule 1 of the Companies Law
  (typically fine, imprisonment, or both);
- **Disqualification** under
  [Article 78](./disqualification-orders.md) *(planned)*;
- **Personal liability** for the concealed debts (in some
  contexts).

## Company liability

The Article also provides that the **company may be liable** for
the **fraudulently concealed debts**. This means:

- The reduction does **not extinguish** the concealed debt;
- Creditors who were concealed can **continue to pursue** the
  company (and may pursue the directors personally in addition).

This protection means a fraudulent reduction does not actually
achieve its purpose — the concealed creditor's claim survives.

## Pitfalls

- **Inadequate creditor identification** in the solvency
  analysis — even without fraudulent intent, missing creditors
  can produce issues. Use systematic identification (account
  records, legal-claim review, contingent-liability schedules).
- **Concealing for "convenience"** — never; the criminal
  consequences are severe.
