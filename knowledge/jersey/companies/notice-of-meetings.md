# Article 91 — Notice of Meetings

Article 91 of the **[Companies (Jersey) Law 1991][cjl]** sets the
**notice** requirements for general meetings of a Jersey company.

## What Article 91 says

Notice of meetings must be:

- given to **members entitled to attend**;
- typically **21 days before** the meeting;
- stating the **general nature of business** to be transacted.

(See the consolidated text at [jerseylaw.je][cjl].)

## The 21-day default

The standard notice period is **21 days**. Specific deed
provisions or member consents may permit:

- **Shorter notice** with member consent (typically all members
  for AGMs, a stated majority for other meetings);
- **Longer notice** if the deed specifies.

For **special resolutions** the deed must also satisfy
[Article 90](./special-resolution.md)'s notice-specifying-intent
requirement.

## Who must be noticed

Notice must go to **members entitled to attend** the meeting.
This typically means:

- **All members** with voting rights;
- **Specified non-voting members** if the deed includes them;
- **Directors** for their information;
- **Auditors** (for audited companies);
- Other persons specified in the deed.

## Content of notice

A typical notice includes:

- **Date and time** of the meeting;
- **Location** (or details of electronic participation);
- **General nature of business** — broad enough to inform but
  not exhaustive;
- **Specific resolutions** to be proposed (especially special
  resolutions — see Article 90);
- **Proxy form** or proxy-appointment instructions;
- **Quorum and voting** procedures.

## Short notice consent

A meeting may be held on **less than 21 days' notice** if
members consent. The consent must come from:

- **All members** in the case of an AGM;
- A **stated majority** (typically the holders of 95% of voting
  rights) for other general meetings.

Short-notice meetings are common in close companies and
fund-vehicle contexts where all members can co-ordinate.

## Pitfalls

- **Inadequate notice period** — invalidates the meeting and any
  resolutions purportedly passed.
- **Failure to notice all entitled members** — invalidates the
  meeting as to omitted members.
- **Insufficient business specification** — a generic notice may
  be challenged where specific business required specific
  notice.
