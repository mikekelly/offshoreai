# Article 8 — Registration

Article 8 of the **[Companies (Jersey) Law 1991][cjl]** is the
**Registrar's authority to register** a new Jersey company. It
governs the moment a proposed company becomes a real, legally-
recognised body corporate.

## What Article 8 says

The Registrar **shall register** the memorandum and any articles
delivered under Article 5, provided:

- the **statutory requirements** for incorporation are satisfied;
- if the Registrar considers the formation may be **contrary to
  the public interest**, the matter may be **referred to the
  Court** for authorisation;
- if the Court authorises, the Registrar registers; if not, the
  Registrar refuses.

Key statutory text: "the registrar shall register the memorandum
and any articles of the company delivered to the registrar under
Article 5."

(See the consolidated text at [jerseylaw.je][cjl].)

## The Registrar's discretion

The Registrar's role is **mostly ministerial**: where the
statutory paperwork is in order, the Registrar registers. But the
Article 8 power to refer to the Court provides a **public-interest
safety valve**:

- The Registrar can decline a proposed incorporation where
  formation appears to be **contrary to the public interest**;
- That refusal is **subject to Court authorisation**, which can
  override the Registrar;
- The Court considers the public-interest question on the
  evidence presented.

In practice this power is rarely exercised — most concerns about
proposed Jersey companies are handled through the
**JFSC consent-to-incorporate** process under the
[Control of Borrowing (Jersey) Order 1958][cob], which sits
**upstream** of Article 8 registration. The COBO consent step
filters out proposed companies that the JFSC views as inappropriate
before the Registrar sees them.

## Practical sequence

A typical Jersey incorporation:

1. **Pre-clearance**: registered agent prepares paperwork;
   beneficial-ownership data captured.
2. **COBO consent**: application to the JFSC under the Control of
   Borrowing (Jersey) Order 1958 for consent to incorporate. The
   COBO consent step is the operational discretion gate.
3. **Delivery to the Registrar**: memorandum, articles, statement
   of particulars (Article 7), fee payment, electronic submission
   through the JFSC online portal.
4. **Article 8 registration**: Registrar registers the documents
   and issues the certificate of incorporation (the Article 9
   step).

Speed:

- **Standard service**: 24 hours from delivery (subject to
  paperwork being clean).
- **Fast-track**: 2 hours, on payment of premium fees.

## Public-interest concerns

What the Registrar might treat as "contrary to the public
interest" is not exhaustively defined but typically:

- proposed names or activities that are **misleading**, **illegal**
  in Jersey, or **inconsistent with public policy**;
- corporate structures **designed for purposes that contravene
  Jersey law** (proceeds of crime, sanctions breach, etc.);
- structures where the **proposed shareholders, directors, or
  beneficial owners** are inappropriate (fit-and-proper concerns —
  though most of these are caught upstream at COBO consent).

The Court is sparingly invoked under Article 8; most
public-interest concerns are resolved by the registered agent
withdrawing the application or modifying it in response to JFSC
queries.

## Pitfalls

- **Incomplete documentation** — missing director particulars,
  unsigned forms, fees not paid. Routine but causes delays.
- **Name clashes** — a proposed name too similar to an existing
  Jersey company name will be rejected (see
  [`name-requirements.md`](name-requirements.md) *(planned —
  Article 13)*).
- **Beneficial-ownership disclosure gaps** — the FSDI Law 2020
  data should be complete at incorporation.
