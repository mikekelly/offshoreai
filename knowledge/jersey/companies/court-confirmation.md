# Article 62 — Application to Court for Order of Confirmation

Article 62 of the **[Companies (Jersey) Law 1991][cjl]** is the
**court-confirmation route** for capital reduction — used where
the **solvency-statement route is unavailable** or inappropriate.

## What Article 62 says

Where **no solvency statement is made**, a company must **apply
to the Court** for confirmation of the reduction.

(See the consolidated text at [jerseylaw.je][cjl].)

## When the court route is used

The court route applies where:

- **Directors cannot make the solvency statement** — typically
  because the company is marginally solvent, has substantial
  contingent liabilities, or faces uncertainty;
- The **reduction is complex** — multi-class effects, creditor
  protections, or unusual capital-account adjustments;
- The **deed specifies** court confirmation;
- The **company prefers** court oversight for transactional
  certainty (e.g., as part of a broader restructuring).

The court route is **slower and more expensive** than the
solvency-statement route — typically taking weeks or months and
involving counsel costs — but provides **judicial confirmation**
that the reduction is proper.

## What the court considers

On a confirmation application, the Royal Court considers:

- **Solvency** of the company after the reduction — even without
  a solvency statement, the court will form its own view based
  on the evidence;
- **Creditor interests** — are creditors adequately protected?
  The court may direct **notice to creditors** and consider
  objections;
- **Class-rights** — is the reduction fair across classes? Have
  class-rights variation procedures been followed where needed?
- **Procedure** — has the company complied with the procedural
  steps (special resolution, member notice)?
- **Public interest** — exceptionally, the court may consider
  broader policy implications.

The court has **wide discretion** to confirm, modify, or refuse.

## Procedure

Standard procedure for a court application:

1. **Special resolution** of members under Article 61.
2. **Application by Representation** to the Royal Court (Samedi
   Division).
3. **Affidavit evidence** of the company's solvency position,
   the rationale for the reduction, and the procedural compliance.
4. **Service** on interested parties — typically all members and
   significant creditors.
5. **Hearing** with the Court considering objections (if any).
6. **Order** of confirmation, modification, or refusal — see
   [Article 63](./court-confirmation-order.md).
7. **Registration** of the court order and the minute of
   reduction — see [Article 64](./act-and-minute-registration.md).

## Effect of confirmation

A court-confirmed reduction takes effect on **registration** of
the court order and the minute (Article 64). The court order:

- **Confirms** the reduction as proper;
- May impose **conditions** (e.g., creditor protections,
  staggered effective dates);
- Modifies **members' liability** on reduced shares where
  appropriate;
- Has the **same effect** as a solvency-statement reduction once
  registered.

## Pitfalls

- **Using the court route unnecessarily** — slower and more
  expensive than the solvency-statement route. Use it only when
  needed.
- **Inadequate evidence** — court applications need solid
  supporting evidence on solvency and procedure.
- **Creditor neglect** — significant creditors who would object
  must typically be given notice; failure can derail the
  application.
