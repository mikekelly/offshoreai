# Article 18 — Capacity of Company (Ultra Vires Abolished)

Article 18 of the **[Companies (Jersey) Law 1991][cjl]** **abolishes
the doctrine of *ultra vires*** for Jersey companies. A Jersey
company has **unlimited corporate capacity** — its acts are not
invalidated because they fall outside the objects clause or any
other limit in the memorandum or articles.

## What Article 18 says

A company's capacity is **not limited** by:

- anything in its **memorandum** or **articles**; or
- any act of its **members**.

The abolition is **not retroactive** to acts done before the
Article came into force.

(See the consolidated text at [jerseylaw.je][cjl].)

## What this changes

At common law (historically), a company that acted outside the
purposes stated in its objects clause was acting ***ultra vires***
— "beyond the powers" — and the act was **void**. Counterparties
dealing with the company were at risk: if the company's act fell
outside its objects, the counterparty could not enforce the
resulting contract.

Article 18 abolishes the rule for Jersey companies. The
consequences:

- The company's acts are **valid as against third parties**
  regardless of the objects clause.
- Counterparties **need not verify the company's objects** before
  contracting.
- **Modern Jersey memoranda typically omit a substantive objects
  clause** altogether (or use a wide-purpose clause) because the
  Article 18 capacity is wide enough that specific objects add
  nothing.

## Internal vs external effect

Article 18 governs the **external** position — i.e., the position
between the company and third parties. It does not necessarily
release the **directors** from internal duties:

- A director who causes the company to act outside the deed's
  objects may still be in **breach of fiduciary duty** to the
  company (and so personally liable to it, under the Article 74
  duties);
- The company's **members** may complain via Part 20
  unfair-prejudice if the directors are running the company outside
  the agreed business purpose.

So Article 18 protects **third parties** but does not give
directors free rein to ignore the deed.

## Companion provisions

- **[Article 19](./no-implied-notice.md)** — no implied notice of
  public records: counterparties are not deemed to know what's in
  the company's filed documents merely because those are public.
- **[Article 20](./form-of-contracts.md)** — form of contracts: a
  person with express or implied authority to act for the company
  can bind it by normal contractual procedures.

Together, Articles 18–20 form a coherent **third-party-protection
package**: capacity is wide (Article 18), counterparties are not
caught by constructive notice of public records (Article 19), and
authority to bind the company is exercised through normal
contractual mechanics (Article 20).

## Pre-Article-18 acts

The abolition is **not retroactive**. Acts of a Jersey company
**before the Article came into force** are still tested under the
old ultra-vires doctrine. For a pre-1991 company that did pre-
commencement acts under a restrictive objects clause, the old
rules can still surface.

## Modern drafting

Modern Jersey memoranda typically:

- **Omit a substantive objects clause** — relying on the wide
  Article 18 capacity;
- Or include a **wide objects clause** ("to carry on any business
  whatsoever, and to do all such things as are incidental or
  conducive to the attainment of the company's objects") which
  has the same effect as omission;
- Or, for **specific-purpose vehicles** (SPVs in finance
  transactions), include a **narrow objects clause** as a
  **commercial constraint** between members rather than a
  capacity limit — knowing that the Article 18 third-party
  protection still applies.

## Pitfalls

- **Assuming a narrow objects clause** in a special-purpose
  vehicle invalidates acts outside the purpose. Article 18 says
  it doesn't — the vehicle can still bind itself to third
  parties on out-of-purpose acts. The members' protection is
  internal (against directors), not external.
- **Pre-commencement acts** of older companies — check the
  effective date if the act predates Article 18.
