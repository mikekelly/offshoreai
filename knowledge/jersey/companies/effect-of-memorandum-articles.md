# Article 10 — Effect of Memorandum and Articles

Article 10 of the **[Companies (Jersey) Law 1991][cjl]** establishes
the **statutory contract** between the company and its members.
Once registered, the memorandum and articles bind both as if signed
covenants existed between them.

## What Article 10 says

- The registered memorandum and articles **bind the company and
  its members** as though signed covenants existed between them;
- **Money payable** by a member to the company under the
  memorandum or articles **is a debt due** from the member to the
  company.

(See the consolidated text at [jerseylaw.je][cjl].)

## The statutory contract

The Article 10 mechanism is the **statutory contract** that runs
between:

- the **company** and **each member**;
- (impliedly) **each member** and **every other member**.

Each party can enforce the constitutional documents against the
others as if they were a multi-party contract executed under deed.

This is the foundation for member-vs-company and member-vs-member
remedies on breach of the constitutional documents — typically
exercised through:

- **declaratory or injunctive relief** in the Royal Court;
- **damages** for breach of the statutory contract;
- statutory **member remedies** under Part 20 (unfair prejudice)
  and Part 21 (just-and-equitable winding-up).

## Money obligations as debts

The "money payable...is a debt due" rule has practical
consequences:

- **Unpaid amounts on shares** — where shares are partly paid, the
  unpaid balance is a **debt** the company can recover from the
  member.
- **Guarantee amounts** — for guarantee companies (Article 3G),
  the guaranteed contribution becomes a **debt** on the trigger
  event (wind-up).
- **Member contributions** under specific deed provisions —
  capital calls in fund-vehicle structures, periodic membership
  contributions in not-for-profit structures.

The debt characterisation matters for **limitation** (debt-style
limitation periods rather than equitable doctrines) and for
**insolvency** (the company has a recoverable receivable on
wind-up).

## Limits of the contract analogy

The statutory contract has some peculiarities:

- It is **automatically applicable** to new members on becoming
  members — no need for re-execution.
- It can be **altered** by special resolution under
  [Article 11](alteration-of-memorandum-articles.md) *(planned)*,
  which is unusual for a contract (ordinary contracts cannot be
  amended unilaterally by majority).
- Certain **rights conferred on outside third parties** by the
  articles may not be enforceable by them — only by the members
  in their capacity as members.

## Outside-of-deed rights

A typical issue: a deed gives "the auditor" or "the company
secretary" specific rights or protections. Are these enforceable
by the auditor or secretary directly? The Royal Court's approach
follows the English line: **persons whose only connection to the
company is through a role-specific deed provision** typically
cannot enforce the deed directly — the right is enforceable by
members in their capacity as members, who may then require the
company to honour the provision.

## Pitfalls

- **Treating the deed as a normal contract**. Special resolution
  can amend it without the affected member's consent (subject to
  the Article 11 protections against increased liability). A
  member who relied on a current deed term may find it amended.
- **Drafting outside-of-deed rights** that purport to bind the
  company in favour of non-members. Such provisions may not be
  directly enforceable; use a separate contract with the
  third party.
