# Article 17D — Power to Abolish 30-Member Limit

Article 17D of the **[Companies (Jersey) Law 1991][cjl]** is the
**limit-abolition** provision. The States may by Order **eliminate**
the provisions limiting private-company membership numbers or the
thresholds that trigger public-company treatment.

## What Article 17D says

The **States may by Order**:

- **eliminate provisions** that limit the number of members a
  private company may have; and
- **eliminate thresholds** that trigger automatic public-company
  treatment.

(See the consolidated text at [jerseylaw.je][cjl].)

## Why this exists

In the original 1991 Law, private companies were capped at **30
members**. Above that number a company had to be public — with the
attendant audit, prospectus, AGM, and disclosure obligations.

Modern Jersey practice has moved away from a hard cap. Private
companies with employee-share schemes, fund vehicles, and
family-office structures can comfortably have more than 30 members
without resembling a public market participant. Article 17D is
the legislative gateway that lets the States lift the cap by Order
rather than requiring a full amendment to the Companies Law.

## Current status

The States has historically exercised the Article 17D power.
**Private companies in modern Jersey practice can have any number
of members** without an automatic public-company trigger purely
on member count.

Check the current Order made under Article 17D before relying on
this position for a specific company; the rules can be re-tightened
or re-loosened.

## What remains

Even with the 30-member cap abolished, several other features of
the public-vs-private distinction remain:

- **Public form requires** an opt-in (private companies aren't
  forced to public just by member count).
- **Prospectus circulation** still triggers automatic public
  treatment under Article 17.
- **Market trading** still triggers automatic public treatment.
- **The Article 17 voluntary route** to public status remains
  available where appropriate.

So abolition of the 30-member cap simplifies private-company
operation but does not remove all private/public distinctions.

## Pitfalls

- **Relying on the 30-member rule** as still in force without
  checking the current Order.
- **Assuming abolition is complete** — it only addresses the
  member-count trigger, not the other auto-conversion triggers.
