# Article 113F — Effect of Lack of Independence

Article 113F of the **[Companies (Jersey) Law 1991][cjl]** sets out
the **consequences** when an auditor's independence is
compromised during an audit.

## What Article 113F covers

If an auditor becomes **dependent on the company** during the
audit period, they must:

- **notify the Commission**;
- **notify the company**.

**Continued service** by a dependent auditor:

- **renders the audit report potentially ineffective**;
- may trigger **regulatory enforcement action**.

(See the consolidated text at [jerseylaw.je][cjl].)

## When independence is lost

Mid-audit loss of independence can happen through:

- The auditor's firm **acquiring** the audit client (or vice
  versa);
- A partner of the auditor **joining** the client's management;
- A **family member** of the audit team taking a key client
  position;
- The auditor providing **non-audit services** that subsequently
  compromise audit work.

## Required action

The auditor must:

- **Notify** the JFSC of the independence issue;
- **Notify** the company so it can plan a remedial response;
- **Cease** the audit if the issue cannot be resolved;
- **Decline** to sign an audit report where independence is
  compromised.

## Consequences for the report

An audit report signed by a non-independent auditor:

- May be **ineffective** for Companies Law purposes;
- Can be **challenged** by third parties relying on the report;
- May trigger **JFSC enforcement** against the auditor and
  potentially the company.

## Pitfalls

- **Hoping the issue resolves** — independence loss must be
  promptly disclosed; delay compounds the breach.
- **Continuing in compromised audit** to complete the engagement
  — the audit isn't valid.
