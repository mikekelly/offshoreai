# Article 50 — Share Certificates

Article 50 of the **[Companies (Jersey) Law 1991][cjl]** sets the
**minimum content** for share certificates and the **timing** for
their issue.

## What Article 50 says

Issued certificates must show:

- **Holder names**;
- **Shareholdings** (number and class);
- **Share numbers** (where applicable);
- **Limitations on liability** (where the shares are partly paid
  or otherwise carry a member liability).

Companies must **issue certificates within specified periods
after allotment or transfer**.

(See the consolidated text at [jerseylaw.je][cjl].)

## Required content

A typical Jersey share certificate shows:

- The **company's name** (and that it is a Jersey company);
- The **holder's name** (typically as on the register);
- The **share class** and **number of shares** represented;
- The **share numbers** for numbered share categories (per
  Article 34);
- Any **limitations** — e.g., partly-paid amount;
- **Date of issue** of the certificate.

Where shares are **unnumbered** (full-paid equal-rights — per
Article 34), the certificate shows aggregate holding rather than
specific share numbers.

## Timing

The Article requires certificates within **specified periods**
of allotment or transfer. In practice this is typically:

- **2 months** from allotment;
- **2 months** from delivery of a complete transfer.

Specific deeds may specify shorter periods.

## Modern alternative — uncertificated shares

Many modern Jersey companies maintain shares in
**uncertificated form** under
[Article 51A](./uncertificated-securities.md) — the share is
recorded electronically in the register and **no physical
certificate is issued**.

For uncertificated shares, Article 50 is **not engaged** —
there's no certificate to comply with the content requirements.

## Securities seal

Where the company has chosen to **seal certificates** (rather
than authenticate by signature), the
[Article 24 securities seal](./official-seal-securities.md) is
typically used.

## Pitfalls

- **Late issue** of certificates — exposes the company to
  offences and may affect the member's ability to deal with
  the shares.
- **Incorrect content** — e.g., wrong share count, missing
  liability indication — can lead to evidential problems on
  subsequent transfer or wind-up.
