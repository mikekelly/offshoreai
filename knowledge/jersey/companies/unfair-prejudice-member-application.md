# Article 141 — Power for Member to Apply to Court

Article 141 of the **[Companies (Jersey) Law 1991][cjl]** is the
**unfair prejudice remedy** — the central minority-shareholder
protection in Jersey company law.

## What Article 141 covers

A **member** of a Jersey company may apply to the **Royal Court**
for an order under Article 143 on the ground that:

- The **affairs of the company** have been, are being, or are
  proposed to be conducted in a manner **unfairly prejudicial**
  to the interests of the members generally or of some part of
  the members (including the applicant); or
- An **actual or proposed act or omission** of the company
  (including an act or omission on its behalf) is or would be so
  prejudicial.

The application can be made by a member regardless of voting
power — even a 1% shareholder has standing.

(See the consolidated text at [jerseylaw.je][cjl].)

## Why this matters

The unfair-prejudice action is the **principal remedy** for
minority shareholders who are being oppressed, frozen out, or
otherwise treated unfairly by the controllers of the company.
Typical scenarios:

- **Exclusion from management** — minority directors removed
  without cause in a quasi-partnership;
- **Diversion of profits** — controlling shareholders diverting
  business or profits to themselves through related entities;
- **Excessive director remuneration** — controllers paying
  themselves at the expense of the dividend stream;
- **Failure to declare dividends** — earnings retained
  indefinitely to suppress minority returns;
- **Dilution of minority** — share issues structured to reduce
  minority percentage interest at less than market value;
- **Information starvation** — minority kept in the dark about
  the company's affairs.

## The unfair-prejudice standard

"Unfairly prejudicial" is a **broad concept**:

- **Unfairness** is assessed by reference to the company's
  constitution and the **legitimate expectations** of members in
  the particular type of company (quasi-partnerships attract
  more equitable expectations than purely commercial entities);
- **Prejudice** must be to the member's interests **as a member**
  (not as employee, lender, or other capacity);
- **Conduct** of the company's affairs is the focus — not merely
  unfortunate commercial outcomes.

The Royal Court draws heavily on English **section 994** (CA 2006)
jurisprudence (and its predecessors), which is rich and well
developed.
