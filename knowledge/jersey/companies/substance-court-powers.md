# Article 143B — Powers of Court (Economic Substance)

Article 143B of the **[Companies (Jersey) Law 1991][cjl]** sets
out the **Royal Court's powers** on a successful Article 143A
application by the Minister.

## What Article 143B covers

On an Article 143A application, the Court may make such order as
it thinks fit, including:

- **Requiring** the company to comply with the economic
  substance requirements;
- **Ordering** specified persons (directors, controllers) to
  procure compliance;
- **Restricting** the company's activities pending compliance;
- **Striking the company off** the register where compliance is
  not achievable;
- **Winding up** the company;
- **Other relief** including injunctive and structural orders.

(See the consolidated text at [jerseylaw.je][cjl].)

## Why this matters

The Court's power to **strike off** or **wind up** a substance-
failing company is the **ultimate enforcement** tool. It signals
that Jersey is **serious** about the substance regime:

- Companies that **persistently fail** the test cannot remain on
  the register;
- Jersey's **international reputation** for substance
  compliance depends on this credible enforcement;
- The threat of judicial strike-off concentrates **directors'
  minds** on substance compliance in a way that pure tax
  penalties may not.

In practice, Article 143B orders are **rare**:

- Most companies comply once warned;
- Tax-level penalties usually suffice;
- The companies-law strike-off is reserved for **persistent**
  or **flagrant** non-compliance.

But the **availability** of the remedy is what makes the
substance regime credible.
