# Article 27 — Minimum Membership for Carrying on Business

Article 27 of the **[Companies (Jersey) Law 1991][cjl]** requires
**public companies** to maintain at least **two members**, with a
personal-liability consequence if a public company carries on
business with only one member for more than six months.

## What Article 27 says

**Public companies must maintain at least two members**.

If a public company **operates with a single member for more than
six consecutive months**, any member who **knows** of this
situation becomes **jointly and severally liable** for the
company's debts contracted during the extended period.

**Exception**: this liability does **not** apply to a public
company where **all issued shares** are held by, or on behalf of,
a **holding body**.

(See the consolidated text at [jerseylaw.je][cjl].)

## What this means in practice

### Two-member requirement for public companies

A public company is fundamentally a **collective** entity. The
two-member minimum reflects the requirement that public companies
have a member base broader than a single beneficial owner.

**Private companies have no minimum-of-two requirement** —
[Article 3](./formation-of-a-company.md) permits a single-member
private company.

### Personal liability for sole-member trading

If a public company drops to one member (e.g., through transfers,
deaths, or share buy-backs) and **continues to trade for more
than six consecutive months**, the **remaining member who knows**
becomes **personally liable** for debts incurred during the
extended sole-member period.

The liability:

- is **joint and several** with the company;
- applies to **debts contracted during the sole-member period**;
- requires the member to **know** of the position (a member
  unaware that they're the sole member is not caught).

### Wholly-owned subsidiary carve-out

The personal-liability rule does **not** apply where **all shares
are held by, or on behalf of, a holding body**. A wholly-owned
public-company subsidiary is the typical example.

This carve-out reflects the **commercial reality** that
wholly-owned public subsidiaries in group structures often have a
single member (the parent) and that the policy reason for the
two-member rule (collective membership) doesn't apply.

## When the rule bites

Common scenarios:

- A public company has its shares transferred so that one person
  ends up holding them all;
- A public company buys back shares from other members, leaving
  one;
- A public company's other members die or are wound up.

In each case, the remaining knowing member should either:

- **Bring in another member** within six months; or
- **Convert the company to private** under
  [Article 16](./public-to-private.md); or
- **Wind up** the company.

Continuing to trade past six months exposes the member personally.

## Pitfalls

- **Forgetting the six-month clock** — the rule runs in the
  background; if other members exit and only one remains, monitor
  the timeline.
- **Treating "knew" as a high bar** — a member with access to
  share-register information "knows" the situation; ignorance
  is not a complete defence.
- **Wholly-owned-subsidiary scenarios** — confirm the holding
  body's beneficial ownership is genuine; nominee or trustee
  arrangements may not qualify for the carve-out depending on
  the substance.
