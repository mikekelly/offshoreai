# Article 139 — Privileged Information

Article 139 of the **[Companies (Jersey) Law 1991][cjl]**
preserves **legal professional privilege** and certain other
privileges from the inspector's compulsion powers.

## What Article 139 covers

Despite the broad production duties in Article 130, a person is
**not required** to disclose to an inspector:

- **Communications** between a client and the client's legal
  adviser that are protected by **legal professional privilege**;
- **Confidential bank communications** beyond what is strictly
  needed (limited carve-outs);
- **Other privileged information** as recognised by Jersey law.

Privilege is asserted by:

- **Identifying** privileged material on a documented basis;
- **Segregating** it from production materials;
- **Submitting to the Royal Court** for determination if disputed.

(See the consolidated text at [jerseylaw.je][cjl].)

## Why this matters

Legal professional privilege is a **fundamental safeguard**:

- It ensures clients can speak frankly with their lawyers;
- It prevents the inspector's powers being used to **manufacture
  evidence** from privileged advice;
- It is a **constitutional-level** protection in common-law
  systems.

Article 139 preserves this safeguard while allowing the
inspector's powers to operate fully against **non-privileged**
materials.

## Pitfalls

- **Privilege exception for crime / fraud** — Jersey (like
  English law) recognises that **privilege does not extend** to
  communications made in furtherance of crime or fraud. An
  inspector may seek the court's determination that specific
  communications fall outside privilege on this ground.
- **Joint privilege** — where the company and its directors
  shared legal advice on a matter the inspector is investigating,
  the question of who holds the privilege can be complex.
