# Article 49 — Overseas Branch Registers

Article 49 of the **[Companies (Jersey) Law 1991][cjl]** permits a
Jersey company to maintain **overseas branch registers** for
members in foreign jurisdictions, supplementing (but not
replacing) the principal Jersey register.

## What Article 49 says

Companies may maintain **overseas branch registers** in other
jurisdictions.

These **supplement but do not replace** the principal Jersey
register.

(See the consolidated text at [jerseylaw.je][cjl].)

## When this is used

A Jersey company with significant foreign-resident shareholders
may maintain a branch register in:

- the **UK** — for UK-resident shareholders, particularly in
  listed-company contexts where same-day-settlement market
  infrastructure favours a UK register;
- the **US** — where shares are listed on a US exchange or
  marketed to US investors;
- **EU member states** — for institutional investors;
- other jurisdictions with significant shareholder presence.

The branch register typically records:

- Shareholders **resident in or notable to** that jurisdiction;
- Their **holdings**;
- Transactions affecting those holdings.

## Principal register remains

The **principal register** in Jersey is the **canonical
register** for legal purposes. The branch register:

- **Mirrors** the relevant entries on the principal register;
- Is **updated in coordination** with the principal register
  (typically synchronised electronically);
- Is **inspected** under the local jurisdiction's rules in
  addition to Article 45.

Where there is **conflict** between the principal register and
a branch register, the **principal register** prevails as the
legal record.

## Practical use in listed companies

For Jersey-incorporated public companies listed on the LSE
(common for many UK-business operating groups), an **overseas
branch register in the UK** is operationally critical:

- UK market settlement runs on UK-register-based systems;
- UK shareholders expect UK-side service of notices,
  dividends, and votes;
- The Jersey principal register remains the canonical record for
  Jersey legal purposes.

## Pitfalls

- **De-synchronisation** between principal and branch registers
  — operational problem that can affect voting and dividend
  calculations.
- **Treating the branch register as the legal record** — it's
  not; the principal Jersey register is canonical.
- **Foreign-jurisdiction inspection rules** that differ from
  Article 45 — both regimes apply to the relevant entries.
