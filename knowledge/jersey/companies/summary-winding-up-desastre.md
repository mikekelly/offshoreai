# Article 154A — Declaration under Désastre Law (Summary Winding Up)

Article 154A of the **[Companies (Jersey) Law 1991][cjl]** is the
**bridge** from summary winding up to the **désastre regime**
under the Bankruptcy (Désastre) (Jersey) Law 1990.

## What Article 154A covers

Where a summary winding up turns out to be insolvent, the
liquidator (or other interested party) may apply to the Royal
Court for a **declaration of désastre** under the 1990 Law.

The désastre declaration:

- Transfers control to the **Viscount** (the principal Crown
  officer who administers désastre);
- Triggers the **désastre procedure** for the company's affairs;
- Replaces the summary winding-up procedure.

(See the consolidated text at [jerseylaw.je][cjl].)

## Why this matters

Jersey insolvency law has **two parallel insolvency regimes**:

- **Creditors' winding up** under the Companies Law (Chapter 4
  of Part 21);
- **Désastre** under the Bankruptcy (Désastre) (Jersey) Law 1990
  (the older, originally personal-bankruptcy regime, extended
  to companies).

Where summary winding up turns out insolvent, the choice between
converting to creditors' winding up (Article 151) and désastre
(Article 154A) is **strategic**:

- Désastre brings in the **Viscount's office** with established
  procedures;
- Creditors' winding up keeps the **company-law framework** with
  a private liquidator.

Practical considerations: relative cost, asset-realisation
strategy, complexity of claims, cross-border issues.
