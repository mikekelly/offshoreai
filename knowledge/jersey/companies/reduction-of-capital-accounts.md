# Article 61 — Reduction of Capital Accounts

Article 61 of the **[Companies (Jersey) Law 1991][cjl]** is the
**substantive gateway** to capital reduction in a Jersey company.
A company may, by **special resolution**, reduce its capital
accounts — paid-up capital, share premium, or stated capital.

## What Article 61 says

A company **may by special resolution** reduce its capital
accounts, in ways including:

- **Cancelling paid-up capital** that is no longer needed;
- **Extinguishing liabilities** on partially-paid shares;
- **Returning capital** to members.

(See the consolidated text at [jerseylaw.je][cjl].)

## What can be reduced

The Article applies to all **capital accounts**:

- The **nominal capital account** (for par-value companies);
- The **share-premium account** (for par-value companies — see
  [Article 39](share-premium.md));
- The **stated-capital account** (for no-par-value companies —
  see [Article 39A](stated-capital.md)).

## The two routes — solvency statement or court

Article 61 reduction requires **either**:

- **The Article 61A solvency-statement route**
  ([`solvency-statement.md`](solvency-statement.md)) — directors
  declare solvency post-reduction; reduction takes effect on
  registration; **fast and cheap**; or
- The **court-confirmation route**
  ([`court-confirmation.md`](court-confirmation.md) — Article 62)
  — application to the Royal Court; required where no solvency
  statement is made.

The **solvency-statement route is the modern default**. Court
confirmation is used where:

- The directors cannot make the solvency statement (e.g., the
  company is marginally solvent);
- The reduction involves complex creditor or class-rights issues
  needing court oversight;
- The deed specifically requires it.

## Common reduction scenarios

| Scenario | Typical method |
|---|---|
| Capital return to shareholders (excess capital) | Article 61A solvency statement |
| Re-balancing capital structure pre-IPO | Article 61A solvency statement |
| Distressed-company restructuring | Court confirmation |
| Complex multi-class reduction | Court confirmation, possibly with class-rights variation |
| Cross-border reorganisation involving Jersey reductions | Either — driven by group strategy |

## Special-resolution requirement

Reduction always requires **special resolution** of the members —
typically a two-thirds majority (or the deed's higher threshold).
Where the reduction affects different classes differently,
**class-rights variation procedure** under
[Article 52](variation-of-class-rights.md) is required in
addition.

## Carve-out for Part 11 transactions

[Article 58C](not-a-reduction.md) provides that **redemption,
buyback, and cancellation under Part 11 are not Part 12
reductions**. Routine treasury operations don't trigger Article
61 — they have their own Part 11 procedure.

## Effect of reduction

Once the reduction takes effect:

- The company's **paid-up capital base is reduced** by the
  reduction amount;
- The reduction **counts as a distribution** to shareholders to
  the extent capital is returned (with foreign-tax implications);
- The **company's accounts** reflect the reduced capital
  position;
- The **filed memorandum and capital records** are updated.

## Pitfalls

- **Reduction without solvency confirmation** — invalidates the
  reduction. Directors must make the statement if relying on
  Article 61A.
- **Class-rights effects** — a reduction that affects classes
  differently is a class-rights variation needing Article 52
  procedure.
- **Creditor concealment** — directors who hide creditors from
  the solvency analysis face the
  [Article 66](concealing-creditor-penalty.md) offence.
