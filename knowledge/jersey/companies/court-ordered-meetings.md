# Article 94 — Power of Court to Order Meetings

Article 94 of the **[Companies (Jersey) Law 1991][cjl]** is the
**Royal Court's power** to order a Jersey company to hold a
meeting where the company fails to do so or members cannot convene
one themselves.

## What Article 94 says

The Court may **order a company to hold a meeting** if:

- the company **fails** to do so; or
- **members cannot convene** one themselves.

The Court can direct:

- **Meeting procedures**;
- **Notice requirements**;
- **Voting arrangements**.

The provision ensures **members can exercise governance rights**
even when **management obstructs** normal procedures.

(See the consolidated text at [jerseylaw.je][cjl].)

## When the Court is invoked

Common scenarios:

- **Directors refuse** to call a meeting despite a valid Article
  89 requisition;
- **Members are deadlocked** and cannot agree on convening
  procedures;
- **Notice failures** have prevented a previously-scheduled
  meeting from being valid;
- **Activist members** seek the Court's help to circumvent
  entrenched management.

The power runs in parallel with the JFSC's similar power under
[Article 88](./jfsc-meeting-power.md) — applicants typically choose
the forum based on the nature of the dispute.

## Court's discretion

The Court has wide discretion to:

- **Order the meeting** to be held on specified terms;
- **Direct notice procedures** (potentially shorter or longer
  than the Article 91 default);
- **Specify voting procedures** including any departures from the
  Article 92 default;
- **Appoint a chair** for the meeting if the Court considers
  this necessary;
- **Make consequential orders** about the company's affairs
  pending the meeting.

## Procedure

Application by **Representation** in the Royal Court (Samedi
Division). Supporting evidence:

- The **company's deed**;
- Evidence of the **default** or obstacle to convening;
- The **proposed business** for the meeting;
- The **applicant's standing** (typically a member, sometimes a
  director).

## Pitfalls

- **Approaching the Court too quickly** — without first
  attempting normal procedures, the Court may refuse.
- **Forum choice** between JFSC and Court — consider which is
  more appropriate for the specific dispute.
