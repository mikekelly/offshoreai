# Article 31 — Exemption from Liability to Pay Compensation

Article 31 of the **[Companies (Jersey) Law 1991][cjl]** sets out
the **defences** to a prospectus-compensation claim under
[Article 30](./prospectus-compensation.md). A defendant who can
show one of the listed grounds escapes liability.

## What Article 31 says

A defendant may **avoid compensation liability** by demonstrating
any of:

- **Circulation without consent** — the prospectus was issued
  without the defendant's authority or consent;
- **Reasonable investigation and belief** — the defendant
  conducted reasonable investigation and reasonably believed the
  statement was accurate (the "due diligence" defence);
- **Reasonable corrective steps on discovery** — the defendant
  took reasonable steps to correct the misstatement once it was
  discovered;
- **Reasonable belief in expert qualifications** — for
  statements by experts (e.g., reporting accountants, valuers),
  the defendant reasonably believed the expert was qualified to
  make the statement;
- **Claimant's knowledge** — the claimant knew of the falsity or
  omission at the time of investing.

(See the consolidated text at [jerseylaw.je][cjl].)

## The due-diligence defence

The most-relied-on defence is the **reasonable investigation and
reasonable belief** ground — the "due diligence" defence in
ordinary prospectus practice.

A defendant successfully invokes the defence by showing:

- A **systematic verification process** for the prospectus
  contents was carried out (typically a **verification
  exercise** in advance of issue);
- The process was **reasonable in scope** — proportionate to the
  importance of the statement and the resources available;
- The defendant **reviewed the verification** and reasonably
  believed each material statement was accurate at the time of
  issue.

The verification exercise — typically run by the issuer's lawyers
— produces a **verification file** documenting the supporting
evidence for each prospectus statement. The file is the
defendant's best evidence in any subsequent claim.

## Reasonable corrective steps

If a misstatement is discovered **before the prospectus is
withdrawn**, the defendant must take **reasonable corrective
steps** — typically a **supplementary prospectus** correcting the
error. Doing so preserves the defence; failing to do so loses it.

## Expert statements

A prospectus often contains statements by **experts** — for
example, a reporting accountant's confirmation of financial
statements, a valuer's confirmation of asset values. The
defendant (typically the issuer or director) can rely on the
expert's statement if they reasonably believed the expert was
**qualified** and **competent** to make the statement.

The defence applies to the **defendant** (issuer / director); the
expert themselves can be liable under
[Article 30](./prospectus-compensation.md) as a person who has
accepted responsibility for that part of the prospectus.

## Claimant's knowledge

If the claimant **knew** of the misstatement or omission at the
time of investing, they cannot recover compensation for the loss
caused by that misstatement. Knowledge can be:

- **Actual knowledge** — the claimant was told;
- Or, in appropriate cases, **constructive knowledge** —
  information widely available that a reasonable investor would
  have known about.

The burden is typically on the defendant to show the claimant
knew.

## No-consent defence

If the defendant did not **authorise or consent** to the
prospectus's circulation, they are not liable. This is a narrow
defence — applicable to someone whose name appeared in the
prospectus without their authority. Directors and offerors who
authorised circulation cannot rely on this.

## Pitfalls

- **Inadequate verification file** — the defendant who can't
  produce contemporaneous evidence of due diligence has a weak
  defence.
- **Failure to correct on discovery** — a defendant who knows
  of an error and doesn't issue a supplementary loses the
  defence.
- **Over-reliance on experts** — the defendant must reasonably
  believe the expert was qualified; uncritically accepting
  expert statements may not be reasonable.
