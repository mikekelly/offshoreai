# Article 55 — Power to Issue Redeemable Shares

Article 55 of the **[Companies (Jersey) Law 1991][cjl]** permits a
Jersey company to issue **redeemable shares** — shares that are
issued on the basis they will be **bought back by the company**
at a future date or on the occurrence of a stated event.

## What Article 55 says

A company may issue shares **expressly made redeemable**.

The redeemable shares:

- must be **fully paid before redemption**;
- have **redemption terms** (timing, price, mandatory or optional)
  set by the **articles**;
- can be redeemed from **distributable profits** or from a
  **fresh share issue**.

(See the consolidated text at [jerseylaw.je][cjl].)

## How redeemable shares work

A redeemable share is fundamentally a **temporary share** —
issued with a clear path to **redemption** (the company buys it
back, typically extinguishing the share).

Typical features:

- **Mandatory redemption** at a stated date or event (the company
  must buy back);
- **Optional redemption** — the company or holder may trigger
  redemption (sometimes called a put / call structure);
- **Redemption price** specified in the articles (par + premium,
  market value, formula-based);
- **Redemption funding** — typically distributable profits or
  new-issue proceeds.

## Common uses

- **Fund vehicles** — investor classes redeemable at NAV on
  notice, the standard open-ended fund structure;
- **Preference share structures** — preference shares with
  mandatory redemption at a stated date;
- **Vehicle-to-vehicle restructurings** — redemption as part of
  a planned reorganisation;
- **Exit-ready financings** — investors with stated redemption
  rights against the company.

## Funding redemption

The Article specifies redemption can be funded by:

- **Distributable profits** — accumulated profits available for
  dividend equivalent;
- A **fresh share issue** — the company issues new shares and
  uses the proceeds to redeem the redeemable shares.

Where redemption would otherwise reduce paid-up capital below
historical levels, the **Part 12 reduction-of-capital
procedure** (with Article 61A solvency statement) may also be
relevant — but
[Article 58C](./not-a-reduction.md) generally provides that Part 11
redemptions are **not treated as reductions of capital**.

## Treasury treatment

Once redeemed, the company may either:

- **Cancel** the shares (the historical default);
- **Hold them as treasury shares** under
  [Article 58A](./treasury-shares.md) (the modern alternative).

## Pitfalls

- **Underfunded redemption** — calling redemption without
  adequate distributable profits or new-issue proceeds is a
  Companies Law breach.
- **Drafting traps** — redeemable-share terms in the articles
  must align with deed-level provisions on dividends, voting,
  and wind-up distribution to avoid internal inconsistency.
- **Cross-border tax** — the tax characterisation of redemption
  proceeds in the holder's home jurisdiction needs pre-modelling
  (dividend vs capital event).
