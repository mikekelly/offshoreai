# Article 2 — Subsidiary, Wholly-Owned Subsidiary, Holding Body

Article 2 of the **[Companies (Jersey) Law 1991][cjl]** defines the
group-relationship terminology: **subsidiary**, **wholly-owned
subsidiary**, and **holding body**. These definitions thread through
the rest of the Law in voting, accounting consolidation, takeover,
and prohibition-on-cross-holdings contexts.

## What Article 2 says

A body corporate is a **subsidiary** of another body if that other
body:

- **holds a majority of the voting rights** in the first body; or
- meets one of the other **control criteria** specified in the
  Article (typically: holds shares in the first body and has the
  right to appoint or remove a majority of its directors; or
  controls a majority of voting rights through agreement with
  other shareholders).

A **wholly-owned subsidiary** has **no members except** the
parent body or its nominees (and any wholly-owned subsidiaries of
the parent).

A **holding body** is the converse — the body that has the
subsidiary as its subsidiary.

(See the consolidated text at [jerseylaw.je][cjl].)

## Why this matters

Group-relationship definitions are operationally significant in:

- **Cross-shareholding prohibition** ([Article 26](holding-company-membership.md))
  — a subsidiary cannot be a member of its holding body, subject to
  specific exceptions;
- **Group accounting** ([Part 16](accounts.md)) — accounting
  consolidation rules engage the subsidiary/holding body framework;
- **Takeover and merger** ([Part 18 and 18B](takeover-offers.md))
  — squeeze-out and merger thresholds reference group structures;
- **Distributions** ([Part 17](distribution-restrictions.md)) — the
  Article 115 solvency test for a parent company must consider the
  position of subsidiaries;
- **Disclosure** ([Articles 75, 83, 84](director-disclosure.md))
  — directors' interests and group disclosures.

## The control limbs

### Majority of voting rights

The cleanest case: A holds more than 50% of the votes in B. B is a
subsidiary of A.

The voting-rights test is **substantive**: it looks at the rights
attaching to the shares held, not just the formal shareholding
percentage. A company holding shares with weighted voting rights
that produce a majority of voting power is a holding body even if
the formal equity percentage is below 50%.

### Right to appoint or remove majority of directors

A holds shares in B and has the right (under the constitutional
documents or by contract) to appoint or remove a majority of B's
directors. B is a subsidiary.

This captures the **control-by-board-composition** structure that
many Jersey holding structures use — the parent's right to control
the board is the substantive control.

### Control through agreement with other shareholders

A and other shareholders together control a majority of B's votes
under a binding agreement (e.g., a shareholders' agreement, voting
trust, or related arrangement). B is a subsidiary of A.

This catches **acting-in-concert** structures and complex
shareholder agreements.

## Wholly-owned subsidiary

The **wholly-owned subsidiary** definition is stricter — the parent
(or its nominees, or its wholly-owned subsidiaries) must be the
**only members**. Even a single minority member outside the group
removes wholly-owned status.

Why this matters: certain provisions of the Law — notably the
**Article 127FA simplified merger approval** for mergers involving
wholly-owned subsidiaries — apply only to wholly-owned subsidiaries.

## Article 2A — further provisions

[Article 2A](subsidiary-further-provisions.md) supplements Article 2
with detail on:

- attribution of holdings (when shares held by nominees count to the
  parent);
- treatment of bearer shares and ADRs;
- multi-tier subsidiary structures;
- specific exceptions for fiduciary holdings (a trust holding shares
  for trust purposes is not normally a "holding body" of the
  underlying company).

## Pitfalls

- **Voting-rights inflation.** A small-percentage equity holder
  with disproportionate voting rights can be a holding body — don't
  rely on shareholding percentages alone.
- **De facto control.** Article 2 deals with *legal* control. *De
  facto* control without the rights specified is not a subsidiary
  relationship under Article 2, even if the practical reality is
  parent-style control. Other Articles may engage on the *de
  facto* relationship.
- **Trust-held shares.** Where a trust holds the shares of a
  company, the trustee (as legal owner) is not automatically a
  "holding body" of the underlying company in the Article 2 sense
  — see [Article 2A](subsidiary-further-provisions.md). The trust
  framework is treated specially.
