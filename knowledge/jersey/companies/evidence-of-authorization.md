# Article 67C — Evidence of Authorization

Article 67C of the **[Companies (Jersey) Law 1991][cjl]** is an
**evidentiary rule**: documents bearing the company seal or
signed by authorised officers are **prima facie evidence** of
proper authorisation.

## What Article 67C says

Documents bearing the **company seal** or **signed by authorised
officers** constitute **prima facie evidence** of proper
authorisation for corporate transactions.

(See the consolidated text at [jerseylaw.je][cjl].)

## Why this matters

When a Jersey company signs a contract or other document:

- A **counterparty** wants to know the company is bound;
- A **third party** later may need to prove the document was
  validly executed;
- **In litigation**, the document needs evidential weight.

Article 67C says: the **seal or authorised signature** is
**prima facie evidence** of authorisation. Counterparties can
rely on a properly-signed-or-sealed document without separately
proving the underlying authorisation.

## "Prima facie" not conclusive

The evidential effect is **prima facie**, not conclusive:

- A document with the seal or authorised signature is **presumed**
  to be properly authorised;
- The **burden shifts** to the challenger to show it wasn't;
- If actual authorisation **didn't exist**, the document may
  still be unenforceable — but the burden of proving the defect
  is on the party asserting it.

## Combination with Article 20

[Article 20](./form-of-contracts.md) governs the **substantive
authority** of a person to bind the company. Article 67C governs
the **evidential weight** of a document executed under that
authority. Together:

- Article 20 makes a properly-authorised act binding;
- Article 67C makes the document evidence that the act was
  properly authorised.

## Combination with [Article 19](./no-implied-notice.md)

[Article 19](./no-implied-notice.md) abolishes **constructive
notice** of public records. So a counterparty:

- Is **not deemed to know** the company's deed contents (Article
  19);
- Can **rely on the evidential effect** of the seal or signature
  (Article 67C);
- Without needing to **investigate** the authorisation chain
  (Article 20 actual / apparent authority).

The three Articles work together as the **modern third-party-
protection regime** for dealing with Jersey companies.

## Pitfalls

- **Treating the evidential effect as conclusive** — it's not.
  Where there are red flags about the document's authorisation,
  the counterparty's reliance may not survive challenge.
- **Forgery** — a forged seal or signature is **not** properly
  authorised; Article 67C doesn't cure forgery.
