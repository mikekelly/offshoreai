# Article 75 — Duty of Directors to Disclose Interests

Article 75 of the **[Companies (Jersey) Law 1991][cjl]** is the
**disclosure duty** that operationalises the broader Article 74
conflict-of-interest rules. Directors must **disclose their
interests** in proposed or existing company contracts.

## What Article 75 says

Directors must **disclose interests** in:

- **proposed** company contracts; or
- **existing** company contracts;

to **fellow directors** **before authorisation**, **unless**:

- the interest is **remote**; or
- the director **reasonably believes disclosure is unnecessary**.

(See the consolidated text at [jerseylaw.je][cjl].)

## What must be disclosed

A director's **interest** in a company contract includes:

- **Direct** personal interests — e.g., a contract where the
  director is the counterparty;
- **Indirect** interests through
  [connected persons](./connected-persons.md) (Article 74ZA);
- **Material** interests in counterparties (e.g., a substantial
  shareholding in a counterparty company);
- **Fiduciary** interests where the director acts for someone
  with an interest in the counterparty.

## Timing — before authorisation

Disclosure must happen **before authorisation** of the contract.
A director who:

- **Discloses after** the contract is signed is in breach;
- **Realises a conflict** mid-transaction must disclose
  promptly;
- **Becomes aware** of a previously-unknown conflict in an
  existing contract must disclose on becoming aware.

## To whom — fellow directors

Disclosure is to **fellow directors**, not to members or to the
counterparty. The mechanism:

- **Board meeting** — the director discloses at the relevant
  board discussion;
- **Written disclosure** — the director writes to fellow
  directors before the meeting;
- **General notice** — for ongoing interests (e.g., the
  director's spouse owns shares in a counterparty), a general
  notice declares the interest standing.

## The "remote" exception

The Article excuses disclosure where the interest is **remote**.
What's remote:

- A **trivial** financial interest (e.g., a few shares in a
  large counterparty's publicly-traded stock);
- An **attenuated** indirect interest (e.g., the director's
  cousin's distant business connection);
- Interests **clearly not affecting** the director's judgement on
  the contract.

The exception is **narrow**. Most interests should be disclosed
even if the director thinks they're not material — leave the
materiality assessment to fellow directors.

## The "reasonably believes disclosure unnecessary" exception

A second exception: where the director **reasonably believes**
disclosure isn't needed. This typically applies where:

- The interest is **already known** to fellow directors (e.g.,
  the director has previously declared it as a general notice);
- The interest is **structurally obvious** (e.g., the company's
  director who is also CEO of a known counterparty);
- The transaction is so **routine** that disclosure adds nothing.

But the **belief must be reasonable** — a director who
optimistically assumes disclosure isn't needed when fellow
directors didn't know is in breach.

## Consequences of non-disclosure

[Article 76](./failure-to-disclose-consequences.md) sets out the
consequences:

- The contract may be **voidable** at the company's discretion;
- The director may be **liable** for profits from the
  undisclosed transaction.

## Pitfalls

- **Forgetting connected-person disclosure** — disclosure
  extends to interests of connected persons, not just personal
  interests.
- **Over-reliance on general notices** — for **specific
  material transactions** the director should make a specific
  disclosure even if a general notice covers the general
  interest.
- **Mid-transaction conflicts** — disclosing only at the start
  doesn't excuse later breach if circumstances change.
