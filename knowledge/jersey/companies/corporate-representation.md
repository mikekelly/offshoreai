# Article 93 — Representation of Body Corporate at Meetings

Article 93 of the **[Companies (Jersey) Law 1991][cjl]** allows
a **corporate member** of a Jersey company to attend and vote at
meetings through an **authorised representative** (distinct from
a proxy).

## What Article 93 says

A **body corporate which is a member** of a company is **entitled
to attend and vote** at any meeting of the company through an
**authorised representative**.

The representative must **produce a certificate of authorisation**
before voting.

**Multiple representatives** may attend if appointed for different
meetings or different classes of shares.

(See the consolidated text at [jerseylaw.je][cjl].)

## Corporate representative vs proxy

A **corporate representative** is distinct from a **proxy**:

| Feature | Corporate representative | Proxy |
|---|---|---|
| Available to | Corporate members only | Any member |
| Status | Acts in the corporate member's stead | Acts as agent |
| Authority | Resolution of the corporate member's board | Appointment by the member |
| Powers | Can do anything the member could do | Limited to specific authorisation |

Corporate representatives have **broader authority** than proxies —
they can effectively act as the corporate member would, including
on matters that would be hard to anticipate in a proxy
appointment.

## When this matters

Common scenarios:

- A **parent company** holding shares in a Jersey subsidiary —
  the parent's representative attends and votes;
- A **trust-company business** holding shares in trust — the
  TCB's representative attends as the trustee;
- An **institutional investor** in a Jersey listed company — the
  institution's representative attends.

## Certificate of authorisation

The representative must produce a **certificate** showing
authorisation. Typically:

- A **resolution of the corporate member's board** (or equivalent
  governance);
- Signed by an **officer of the corporate member**;
- Identifying the **person authorised** and the **meeting(s)
  covered**.

## Multiple representatives

A corporate member can appoint **different representatives** for
different meetings or share classes. This is operationally useful
where the corporate member has internal divisions of
responsibility for different holdings.

## Pitfalls

- **Inadequate authorisation certificate** — the company may
  refuse to allow voting until proper authorisation is shown.
- **Confusing with proxy procedures** — the corporate
  representative isn't a proxy; different paperwork applies.
