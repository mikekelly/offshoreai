# Article 134 — Failure to Co-operate with Inspectors

Article 134 of the **[Companies (Jersey) Law 1991][cjl]** creates
the **offence of failing to cooperate** with an inspector — the
passive counterpart to Article 133 obstruction.

## What Article 134 covers

A person commits an offence if, without **reasonable excuse**,
the person:

- **Fails to produce** documents required under Article 130;
- **Fails to attend** before the inspector when required;
- **Refuses to answer** questions on examination;
- **Fails to provide** explanations or other assistance required.

Penalties include **fines** and, in serious cases, **imprisonment**.

The Royal Court may also **deal with** the failure as contempt
of court where the inspector's powers are formally enforced
through the court.

(See the consolidated text at [jerseylaw.je][cjl].)

## Why this matters

Many investigations fail not through obstruction but through
**passive non-cooperation**: people who don't refuse outright but
simply don't respond, don't produce, don't attend.

Article 134 makes that passive resistance an offence — equivalent
in legal force to failing to answer a court order.

## Self-incrimination

A practical question: can a person refuse to answer on the
ground that the answer would incriminate them in a criminal
proceeding?

Investigative regimes vary on this. Jersey's regime typically:

- **Requires the answer** under compulsion;
- **Restricts use of the answer** against the person in
  subsequent criminal proceedings (a "use immunity" mechanic).

The detail is in the Article and related case law.
