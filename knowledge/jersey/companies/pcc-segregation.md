# Article 127YT — Liability of Protected Cell Company and its Cells

Article 127YT of the **[Companies (Jersey) Law 1991][cjl]** is
the **central segregation rule** for PCCs — the provision that
makes the regime work.

## What Article 127YT covers

Where a PCC has incurred a liability:

- If the liability was incurred **on behalf of a specific cell**,
  it is a **cellular liability** of that cell and is satisfied
  out of the **cellular assets attributable to that cell**;
- If the liability was incurred **not on behalf of any cell** (or
  on behalf of the PCC generally), it is a **non-cellular
  liability** of the PCC and is satisfied out of **non-cellular
  assets**;
- **Cellular assets of one cell are not available** to satisfy
  cellular liabilities of another cell;
- **Cellular assets are not available** to satisfy non-cellular
  liabilities (subject to specific exceptions);
- **Non-cellular assets** are generally available to satisfy
  cellular liabilities of a cell whose cellular assets are
  insufficient (the "primary recourse" rule), unless specifically
  excluded.

(See the consolidated text at [jerseylaw.je][cjl].)

## Why this matters

This is the **statutory equivalent of separate legal personality**
— but achieved through statutory operation rather than corporate
structure.

Practical implications:

- A **fund cell** that has performed badly cannot be ruined by
  the failure of another cell;
- A **special-purpose vehicle** issued as a PCC cell can be
  ring-fenced from the rest of the PCC's business;
- An **insurance cell** holds reserves available only for that
  cell's claims.

The "**recourse**" question — whether non-cellular assets back
cellular liabilities — depends on the **express terms** of the
PCC's articles. PCCs are typically structured with **clear
exclusion** of non-cellular recourse, making each cell fully
ring-fenced.

## Recognition risk

Article 127YT works **in Jersey law**. Whether a **foreign court**
will respect the segregation depends on:

- The foreign court's familiarity with PCC structures;
- Whether the foreign jurisdiction has equivalent statutory
  segregation;
- The wording of the cellular contract (does it disclose that
  the PCC is contracting on behalf of the cell?);
- Whether directors have maintained operational segregation
  (Article 127YR).

In **uncooperative foreign jurisdictions**, ICC structures are
sometimes preferred because each cell's separate legal
personality is universally recognised.
