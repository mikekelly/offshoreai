# Article 22 — Company Seals

Article 22 of the **[Companies (Jersey) Law 1991][cjl]** governs
the **company seal** — the physical or virtual stamp historically
used to authenticate company documents. In modern Jersey practice
seals are largely vestigial, but the rules still apply where used.

## What Article 22 says

A company seal must have the company's **name engraved in legible
characters**.

**Duplicate seals** are permitted.

Using or authorising use of a seal that lacks the proper
identification is an **offence**.

(See the consolidated text at [jerseylaw.je][cjl].)

## When seals are used

In modern Jersey practice, ordinary contracts are signed by
authorised officers under [Article 20](form-of-contracts.md)
without applying a seal. Seal use is **typically reserved** for:

- **Documents the deed requires to be sealed** (e.g., specific
  share certificates, instruments under specific deed provisions);
- **Documents another jurisdiction's law requires to be sealed**
  (some foreign-property transfer instruments);
- **Statutory instruments under the Companies Law** that
  expressly require the seal.

A company is **not required to have a seal**. Many modern Jersey
companies do not maintain one at all; ordinary business is
conducted on Article 20 authority.

## Format requirement

If a Jersey company has a seal, it must:

- bear the **company's name**;
- in **legible characters**;
- as **engraved** (typically — modern equivalents like high-
  quality stamped reproductions are accepted in practice).

A seal that **doesn't show the company's name** is non-compliant.
Using or permitting use of such a seal is an offence under the
Law.

## Duplicate seals

A company may have **more than one seal**, provided each
satisfies the Article 22 format. Common reasons:

- A **head-office seal** plus a **branch seal** for use abroad
  (specifically authorised by [Article 23](official-seal-abroad.md));
- A **general seal** plus a **securities seal** specifically for
  sealing securities documents ([Article 24](official-seal-securities.md));
- Operational redundancy.

Each duplicate seal must independently satisfy Article 22's
format requirements.

## Operational practice

Where a Jersey company uses a seal:

- The deed should specify **who has custody** of the seal and
  **who may authorise its application** (typically directors or
  designated officers);
- A **seal register** records each application of the seal,
  identifying the document and the authorisation;
- The **physical seal** is held under appropriate security.

## Pitfalls

- **Using a seal with the wrong company name** (e.g., after a
  name change without updating the seal) — an offence and may
  cast doubt on the validity of the sealing.
- **Multiple seal uses without authorisation chain** — should
  be documented and authorised.
- **Foreign-jurisdiction insistence on sealing** when Article
  20 plain signing would suffice for Jersey-law purposes. Some
  jurisdictions (especially civil-law systems) still expect a
  seal; check the foreign requirement.
