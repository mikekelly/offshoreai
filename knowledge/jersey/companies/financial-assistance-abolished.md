# Article 58 — Rule on Financial Assistance for Share Purchase Abolished

Article 58 of the **[Companies (Jersey) Law 1991][cjl]** **abolishes
the common-law prohibition on "financial assistance"** for share
purchases. A Jersey company may now lend, guarantee, or otherwise
assist in the acquisition of its own shares — within the bounds of
directors' duties.

## What Article 58 says

The historical common-law prohibition on "**financial assistance**"
for share purchases is **eliminated**.

Companies may now provide:

- **loans**;
- **guarantees**;
- **other assistance**;

to facilitate **share acquisition**, without legal constraint
from this former doctrine.

(See the consolidated text at [jerseylaw.je][cjl].)

## What the old rule was

At common law, a company was prohibited from providing
**financial assistance** for the acquisition of its own shares.
The mischief targeted:

- Companies funding the **purchase of their own shares** in ways
  that effectively returned capital to selling shareholders
  through the back door;
- **Asset-stripping LBOs** where the target company's resources
  were used to fund the acquisition;
- **Insider self-dealing** where management funded their own
  share purchases through the company.

The rule was widely criticised as:

- Overly broad (catching legitimate commercial transactions);
- Mechanically applied (catching transactions that posed no
  substantive risk);
- A planning nuisance (requiring "whitewash" procedures or
  artificial restructuring).

## Why Jersey abolished it

Jersey's policy: the underlying mischief is better addressed by:

- **Directors' duties** under
  [Article 74](directors-duties.md) *(planned)* — directors who
  authorise financial assistance must act in the company's best
  interests;
- **Solvency tests** under [Article 115](distribution-restrictions.md)
  *(planned)* and Part 12 capital-reduction rules — preventing
  asset-stripping that leaves the company insolvent;
- **Insolvency-act clawbacks** under Part 21 — voiding
  transactions at undervalue or preferences in wind-up.

These provisions catch the substantive harm without the
mechanical rigidity of the financial-assistance prohibition.

## Practical consequences

A Jersey company can now:

- **Lend money to a buyer** of its own shares;
- **Guarantee** an acquisition loan taken by a buyer of its own
  shares;
- **Provide security** for the buyer's debt;
- **Subordinate its own claims** to facilitate the acquisition;
- Participate in **leveraged-buyout structures** without
  whitewash procedures.

This is a major Jersey-law selling point for:

- **LBO transactions** — leveraged acquisitions of Jersey
  TopCos;
- **MBOs and management buyouts** — where the target company
  funds management's acquisition;
- **Restructuring** — where the company helps facilitate share
  transfers among existing or new shareholders.

## Directors' duties remain

The abolition **does not** release directors from their
fiduciary duties. A director authorising financial assistance must
still:

- Act in **good faith** in the best interests of the company
  (Article 74);
- Exercise the **proper-purpose** doctrine;
- Avoid **conflicts of interest** (Article 75);
- Ensure the company remains **solvent** after the assistance.

Where the directors fail in these duties, they remain
**personally liable** regardless of Article 58's abolition of the
specific financial-assistance rule.

## Cross-border considerations

A Jersey-law financial-assistance position does not necessarily
travel to the **foreign jurisdictions** in which the company
operates. UK, US, EU member-state, or other regimes may still
impose financial-assistance rules on:

- **Subsidiaries of the Jersey company** that are foreign-
  incorporated;
- **Operations** in jurisdictions with their own rules;
- **Listed securities** subject to market-rules constraints.

Jersey-law freedom is one component; cross-border modelling is
still required.

## Pitfalls

- **Treating abolition as carte blanche** — directors' duties
  remain, and a financial-assistance transaction that
  prejudices the company can still ground a breach claim.
- **Forgetting solvency review** — financial assistance that
  leaves the company insolvent invites Part 21 clawback in
  wind-up.
- **Cross-border whitewash needs** — foreign subsidiaries may
  still need to follow local financial-assistance procedures.
