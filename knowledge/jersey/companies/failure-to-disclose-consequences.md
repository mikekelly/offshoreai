# Article 76 — Consequences of Failure to Disclose Interests

Article 76 of the **[Companies (Jersey) Law 1991][cjl]** specifies
the consequences when a director **fails to comply with the
[Article 75](./director-disclosure.md) disclosure duty**.

## What Article 76 says

**Failure to disclose interests** may result in:

- the **contract being voidable** at the company's discretion;
- the director's potential **liability for profits** earned
  through the breach.

(See the consolidated text at [jerseylaw.je][cjl].)

## Contract voidable at company's option

The contract is **not automatically void** — it is **voidable at
the company's option**. This means:

- The company **can choose** to accept the contract (e.g., where
  it remains commercially beneficial despite the disclosure
  failure);
- The company **can choose** to unwind it (e.g., where the
  failure has harmed the company);
- The company's choice typically expressed by **board
  resolution** or, in serious cases, by **member resolution**.

The company's right to avoid is **subject to limits** — typically
the avoidance must be made within a reasonable time of discovery,
and rights of bona fide third parties may need to be respected.

## Director liability for profits

The director may be required to **account for profits** made
from the undisclosed transaction. This is the equitable
**disgorgement** principle:

- The director **disgorges** any benefit received from the
  transaction;
- The disgorgement amount is **paid to the company** (it is the
  company that has been wronged);
- The disgorgement is **separate from** any contract-avoidance
  remedy — both can be pursued.

## Personal liability beyond profits

In serious cases, the director may also face:

- **Personal liability** for losses the company suffered as a
  result of the breach;
- **Disqualification** under
  [Article 78](./disqualification-orders.md);
- **Damage to professional reputation** and potential JFSC
  regulatory consequences for professional directors.

## Cure by post-disclosure

A director who realises they failed to disclose may sometimes
**cure** the breach by:

- **Disclosing promptly** on discovery;
- Offering to **disgorge** any benefit;
- Cooperating with the company in any remedial action.

The Royal Court may take the cure into account in setting
remedies, though it doesn't automatically eliminate the breach.

## Pitfalls

- **Treating voidability as automatic invalidity** — the company
  must elect; the contract isn't automatically void.
- **Disgorgement gaps** — failing to capture all profits
  attributable to the breach.
- **Failure to act promptly** on discovery — undermines the
  company's avoidance position.
