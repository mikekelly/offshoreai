# Article 114 — Meaning of "Distribution"

Article 114 of the **[Companies (Jersey) Law 1991][cjl]** defines
"**distribution**" for the purposes of Part 17 — the **restriction
regime** for payments out of a Jersey company to its members.

## What Article 114 says

"**Distribution**" encompasses any **transfer of assets** by a
company to its **members**, whether by:

- **dividend**;
- **share redemption**;
- **capital reduction**;
- or other means;

**excluding specified exceptions**.

(See the consolidated text at [jerseylaw.je][cjl].)

## Why the definition matters

The Article 114 definition is the **gateway** to
[Article 115](distribution-restrictions.md) — the solvency-test
restriction on distributions. Anything that counts as a
"distribution" must satisfy Article 115; anything that doesn't is
outside the regime.

The definition is **broad** — it captures all economic transfers
to members, regardless of the legal form used:

- **Cash dividends** (the typical case);
- **In-specie distributions** (e.g., distributing assets in
  kind);
- **Share buybacks** that involve a payment to a member;
- **Capital reductions** that return value to members;
- **Other transactions** with members that have the economic
  substance of returning company value.

## Excluded categories

Specific exceptions take certain transactions outside the Part 17
regime:

- **Arm's-length commercial transactions** where the member
  receives goods or services at fair value (not a distribution
  in substance);
- **Salary or fees** to members who happen to be directors or
  employees (paid for services, not as members);
- **Specific statutory transactions** carved out in
  Regulations;
- **Returns of capital** under separate statutory authority
  (e.g., Part 12 reductions that are themselves subject to
  Article 61A solvency).

The exact list of exceptions evolves with Regulations.

## Substance over form

The Article catches transactions by their **economic substance**,
not just their formal label. A transaction labelled as
"commercial" but in substance returning company value to a
member is a distribution. The Royal Court has applied
substance-over-form analysis in distribution cases.

## Pitfalls

- **Substance-over-form** treatment of "commercial"
  transactions that are really distributions — the test catches
  the substance.
- **Hidden distributions** through related-party transactions —
  payments to members in their member capacity through related
  parties may still count.
