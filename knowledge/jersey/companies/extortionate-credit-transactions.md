# Article 179 — Extortionate Credit Transactions

Article 179 of the **[Companies (Jersey) Law 1991][cjl]** is a
specialised claw-back provision targeting **extortionate credit
transactions** — credit on grossly excessive terms.

## What Article 179 covers

The liquidator may apply to the Royal Court for orders affecting
a credit transaction (loan, factoring, finance lease, etc.)
where:

- The transaction was entered into within the **relevant period**
  before winding up (typically **3 years**);
- The transaction was **extortionate** — the credit provider
  exacted **grossly exorbitant payments** or otherwise grossly
  contravened ordinary principles of fair dealing.

The Court may make orders:

- **Setting aside** the transaction in whole or in part;
- **Varying** the terms;
- **Requiring repayment** of excess payments;
- **Surrendering** security taken in connection with the
  transaction.

(See the consolidated text at [jerseylaw.je][cjl].)

## Why this matters

Distressed companies are vulnerable to **predatory lending** —
finance providers exploit the company's desperate position to
exact:

- **Punitive interest rates**;
- **Onerous covenants**;
- **Excessive security**;
- **Fees and charges** out of proportion to the credit provided.

Article 179 lets the liquidator **unwind these terms** for the
benefit of the creditor body, preventing predatory lenders from
keeping windfall profits at the expense of unsecured creditors.
