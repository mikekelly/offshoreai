# Article 4C — Memorandum or Articles of Company of Limited Duration

Article 4C specifies the content required where a company is to be a
**limited-life company** under [Article 3H](./limited-life-companies.md).

## What Article 4C says

Where a company is to be dissolved:

- at a **specified time**; or
- on the occurrence of a **specified event** (or events);

the **memorandum or articles** must specify those terms.

(See the consolidated text at [jerseylaw.je][cjl].)

## Where the terms go

Article 4C is permissive about which document the limited-duration
terms live in: either the **memorandum** or the **articles**. In
practice:

- Where the limited duration is a **fundamental commercial
  feature** of the company (e.g., a fund-vehicle company whose
  life matches the fund's term, a JV company with a planned
  wind-up), the terms typically live in the **memorandum** to
  signal their importance.
- Where the limited duration is a **secondary administrative
  provision**, the **articles** are appropriate.

The choice affects how the terms can later be altered:

- Memorandum amendments require special resolution under
  [Article 11](./alteration-of-memorandum-articles.md) *(planned)*;
- Articles amendments also require special resolution but have a
  different procedural feel.

## Common formulations

| Trigger | Drafting form |
|---|---|
| Fixed term | "The company shall be wound up on the [X]th anniversary of its incorporation" |
| Stated event | "The company shall be wound up on the completion of [defined transaction]" |
| Discretionary trigger | "The company may be wound up on a resolution of the holders of [class] shares" |
| Combination | "The company shall be wound up on the earlier of [date] and [event]" |

The deed should also specify the **wind-up procedure** — typically
the summary winding-up under Article 144 (limited-life) /
Article 145 — and the **distribution order** for remaining assets.

## Extension

A limited-life company can typically be **converted to indefinite
life** by amending the memorandum or articles (whichever holds the
terms) before the trigger event. Special resolution required;
member or class-rights consents may also be needed depending on
the deed.

## Pitfalls

- **No extension mechanism** drafted — leaves the company
  inflexible if circumstances change.
- **Ambiguous trigger event** — "completion of the transaction"
  needs defining; subjective triggers invite dispute.
- **Failure to align the trigger with the underlying commercial
  arrangement** — e.g., a fund-vehicle company's term should
  match the fund's life including any extension periods.
