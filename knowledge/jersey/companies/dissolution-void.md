# Article 213 — Power of Court to Declare Dissolution of Company Void

Article 213 of the **[Companies (Jersey) Law 1991][cjl]** gives
the **Royal Court** power to **declare the dissolution of a
company void** — effectively **restoring** the company to the
register.

## What Article 213 covers

On application of any **interested person** (creditor, member,
former director, beneficial owner, etc.) within a prescribed
period after dissolution, the Royal Court may declare the
dissolution **void**.

Once declared void:

- The company is **deemed never to have been dissolved**;
- It is restored to the **active register**;
- **Assets** that may have escheated as bona vacantia are
  returned;
- **Litigation** and obligations resume as if the dissolution
  had not occurred.

The Court will consider:

- The **grounds** for restoration (e.g. discovery of assets,
  ongoing litigation, regulatory enforcement need);
- The **interests** of those who relied on the dissolution;
- **Practical implications** of restoration.

(See the consolidated text at [jerseylaw.je][cjl].)

## Why this matters

Companies are sometimes **prematurely dissolved**:

- Through erroneous **strike-off** under Article 205;
- After **winding up** but before all claims emerged;
- Where **assets are subsequently discovered**.

Article 213 lets these errors be **corrected** by restoring the
company. Common scenarios:

- A **forgotten bank account** containing significant funds;
- A **claim against the company** by a previously unknown creditor;
- A **claim by the company** against a third party that emerges
  post-dissolution;
- A **tax-recovery action** requiring the company as a respondent.
