# Article 127YJ — Application of Part 21 to Cell Companies

Article 127YJ of the **[Companies (Jersey) Law 1991][cjl]**
applies the **Part 21 winding-up regime** to cell companies (both
ICCs and PCCs) with **appropriate modifications**.

## What Article 127YJ covers

Part 21 (Winding Up — Articles 144–194A) applies to cell companies
subject to modifications including:

- **Incorporated cells** can be wound up **separately** from
  their ICC — the cell is a separate company, so it can be the
  subject of summary winding-up, just-and-equitable, or
  creditors' winding-up in its own right;
- **An ICC** can be wound up while its cells continue (or with a
  separate winding up of the cells);
- **A PCC** as a single legal entity is wound up as a single
  company, but the **liquidator must respect** the protected-
  cell asset segregation (cellular assets of cell A are not
  available to satisfy claims against cell B or non-cellular
  liabilities);
- **A protected cell** of a PCC can be wound up as a separate
  matter while the PCC and its other cells continue (a
  prescribed procedure modelled on summary winding-up).

(See the consolidated text at [jerseylaw.je][cjl].)

## Why this matters

Winding-up is the **stress test** of the cell structure. If a
single cell becomes insolvent:

- For an **ICC**: the cell can be wound up like any company. The
  ICC and other cells continue.
- For a **PCC**: the assets of the failed cell are segregated by
  Article 127YT — the PCC and the other cells continue, but the
  liquidator deals with only the failed cell's cellular assets
  (plus a contribution from non-cellular assets, depending on
  the structure).

Article 127YJ is the **conceptual bridge** between the cell-
company regime and the general insolvency law.
