# DPL 2018 Article 43 — Personal Data Breach Notification

Article 43 establishes the **breach notification** regime —
**72-hour** notification to the JOIC and (in some cases)
data subjects.

## What is a "personal data breach"

A **personal data breach** is a breach of security leading
to:

- **Accidental or unlawful destruction**;
- **Loss**, **alteration**;
- **Unauthorised disclosure** of, or **access to**, personal
  data;
- **Transmitted, stored, or otherwise processed**.

Examples:

- **Cyber attack** exposing data;
- **Lost / stolen device** containing data;
- **Misdirected email** containing data;
- **Unauthorised internal access**;
- **Ransomware** affecting data integrity;
- **Insider theft**;
- **Inadequate disposal** of records.

## Notification to JOIC

Controllers must **notify the JOIC** of a personal data
breach:

- **Without undue delay**;
- And **where feasible, within 72 hours** of becoming aware;
- **Unless** the breach is **unlikely to result in risk** to
  data subjects.

### Content of notification

- **Nature** of the breach;
- **Categories and approximate numbers** of data subjects and
  records affected;
- **Likely consequences**;
- **Measures taken or proposed** to address the breach;
- **Contact details** of DPO / other contact;
- **Phased reporting** acceptable for evolving incidents.

### Late notification

- Notification **after 72 hours** must be **accompanied by
  reasons** for the delay;
- **Delay** itself is not a breach if good reason and prompt
  remediation.

## Notification to data subjects

In addition to the JOIC, the controller must **notify data
subjects** when:

- The breach is **likely to result in high risk** to data
  subjects' rights and freedoms;
- **Without undue delay**.

### Content of subject notification

- **Clear language** description of the breach;
- **Likely consequences**;
- **Measures** taken or proposed;
- **Contact** for further information.

### Exceptions to subject notification

Subject notification is **not** required if:

- The controller has **appropriate technical / organisational
  protection** (e.g., encryption) that renders the data
  **unintelligible**;
- The controller has taken **subsequent measures** ensuring
  the high risk is **no longer likely**;
- **Disproportionate effort** (in which case **public
  communication** or equivalent is acceptable).

## Processor obligations

A **processor** must notify the **controller** of a breach
**without undue delay**:

- The **controller** then assesses and notifies the JOIC /
  subjects as appropriate;
- **Contractually**, processors typically have **shorter**
  internal deadlines (24 hours or less) to enable the
  controller's 72-hour timeline.

## Documentation

The controller must **document** all breaches:

- **Including** non-notifiable breaches (those without
  significant risk);
- **Documentation** available to JOIC;
- **Internal review** of patterns and lessons learned.

## Practical implications

For Jersey businesses:

- **Incident response plan** essential;
- **Breach detection** systems;
- **Decision framework** for notifiability;
- **Coordination** with cyber insurer, legal counsel;
- **Cross-border** considerations (concurrent EU GDPR
  notification);
- **Reputational** management.

### Cross-border breaches

A single incident affecting **Jersey and EU data subjects**:

- **JOIC** notification under DPL;
- **EU lead supervisory authority** notification under GDPR;
- **Coordination** between authorities;
- **Consistent** messaging required.
