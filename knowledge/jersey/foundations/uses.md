# Uses of Jersey Foundations

Jersey foundations are used in a range of **structuring
situations**, generally where:

- The founder prefers a **body-corporate** vehicle to the
  relational structure of a trust;
- **Civil-law foundation experience** in the founder's home
  jurisdiction makes a foundation more comfortable than a trust;
- The vehicle needs to be **publicly registered** without
  revealing beneficial interests (charter is public; regulations
  are private);
- The vehicle must **own assets in its own name** (a foundation
  is a legal person; a trust is a relationship);
- **Long-term continuity** beyond any single individual is
  needed.

## Common use cases

### 1. Philanthropic vehicles

Foundations are well-suited to **charitable or quasi-charitable
purposes**:

- Holding **philanthropic capital** for distribution over
  generations;
- **Family philanthropic** vehicles dedicated to specified
  causes;
- **Operating charities** (where the foundation does the
  charitable work itself rather than granting funds).

The **purpose-focus** of foundation law fits naturally with
charitable mission.

### 2. Orphan SPVs

A foundation makes an excellent **orphan vehicle** for
structured-finance and securitisation transactions:

- The foundation **owns the SPV shares**;
- The foundation has **no beneficial owners** beneficially
  entitled to its assets — true orphan status;
- This achieves **bankruptcy remoteness** of the SPV from any
  sponsor;
- Common alternative is the **purpose trust** under Article 12
  of the Trusts Law.

### 3. Family-governance vehicles

Wealthy families use foundations as **governance overlays**:

- The foundation holds **family-wealth structures** (PTCs,
  investment companies, trustee shares);
- The foundation's **council** acts as a "family-office
  governance board";
- **Multi-generation family members** can serve as council
  members or guardians;
- The foundation provides **continuity** beyond any one
  generation.

### 4. Private Trust Company (PTC) structures

A foundation commonly holds the **shares of a PTC**:

- The PTC acts as **trustee** for one or more family trusts;
- The foundation owns the PTC shares (PTC shares are otherwise
  hard to place without compromising the trust structure);
- The foundation's council provides **governance oversight** of
  the PTC trustees.

### 5. Succession and estate-planning

Founders can use foundations for **succession planning**:

- The foundation **continues to exist** after the founder's death
  (no rule against perpetuities to worry about);
- **Family members** can be beneficiaries or potential
  beneficiaries;
- **Conditional benefits** can be built into the regulations.

### 6. Heritage / legacy structures

For founders wanting to **preserve a specific business or
asset** indefinitely:

- A foundation can hold the asset perpetually;
- The regulations can specify **continuing operating purposes**;
- The foundation **never distributes the underlying asset** if
  the regulations so provide.

## Why not a trust instead?

Trusts can do most of what foundations do, with significant
differences:

| Factor | Foundation | Trust |
|---|---|---|
| Familiarity to civil-law founder | Higher | Lower |
| Body-corporate legal personality | Yes | No |
| Public charter | Yes | No |
| Beneficiary information rights | None default (charter / regs may grant) | Schmidt v Rosewood plus Article 29 |
| Purpose trust equivalent | Native | Article 12 |
| Article 9 firewall protection | Foundations-Law equivalent (different scope) | Strong |
| US tax characterisation | Frequently company-like | Often grantor or foreign-grantor trust |

The **right choice** depends on:

- Founder background and preference;
- Tax characterisation in relevant home jurisdiction;
- Desired beneficiary-information regime;
- Use of public-charter / private-regulations split.
