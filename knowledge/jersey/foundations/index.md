# Foundations — Jersey

## What this section is about

This section covers the **Jersey foundation** — a
civil-law-flavoured wealth-structuring vehicle introduced by the
**[Foundations (Jersey) Law 2009][fl]**.

A Jersey foundation is structurally distinct from both a company
and a trust:

- **Like a company**: it is a **body corporate** with its own legal
  personality. It can own property in its own name, contract, sue,
  and be sued. It has no shareholders.
- **Like a trust**: it must have one or more **objects** —
  charitable or non-charitable purposes, beneficiaries, or both —
  and its assets are dedicated to those objects.
- **Unlike a trust**: beneficiaries of a Jersey foundation **do
  not own the foundation's assets as of right** and (unless the
  charter or regulations provide otherwise) **do not have
  information rights** equivalent to *Schmidt v Rosewood*.
- **Unlike a company**: there are no shareholders; the foundation
  is governed by a **council** under a **charter** (public,
  registered) and **regulations** (private), with mandatory
  oversight from a **guardian**, and one council member must be a
  **JFSC-regulated qualified member**.

([JFSC — About foundations][jfsc-fdn]; [Carey Olsen — Jersey
foundations][co])

## Foundation vs trust — quick comparison

| Feature | Jersey foundation | Jersey trust |
|---|---|---|
| Legal personality | Body corporate (yes) | No — trust is a relationship, not an entity |
| Owner of assets | The foundation itself | The trustee, in trust capacity |
| Beneficiary rights | None as of right (unless charter / regulations grant) | Schmidt v Rosewood supervisory jurisdiction; Article 29 disclosure |
| Founder retained powers | Possible under charter / regulations | Article 9A reserved powers |
| Governance | Council + guardian + qualified member | Trustee(s), possibly protector |
| Constituting documents | Charter (public) + regulations (private) | Trust deed (private) |
| Registration | Registered with JFSC; charter is public | Not registered |
| Statutory firewall | Foundations Law has its own provisions | Trusts Law Article 9 (extensive) |
| Common law / equity overlay | Limited — civil-law flavour | Substantial — Trusts Law is expressly not a codification |
| Use for orphan structures | Yes — common | Yes — non-charitable purpose trust under Article 12 |
| Use for philanthropy | Yes | Yes (charitable trust) |
| Use for family-office structures | Yes | Yes (more common) |
| Use for US-grantor-trust planning | Possible but unusual | Common (with Article 9A reserved revocation power) |

The choice of vehicle is highly fact-specific. Common drivers:
- **Civil-law settlor preference** for a body-corporate vehicle
  → foundation;
- **Desire for trust-style firewall protection (Article 9)** →
  trust;
- **Pre-modelled foreign tax characterisation** → check both in
  the foreign tax regime;
- **Privacy of beneficial-interest detail** → both work
  (foundation's regulations are private; trust deed is private),
  but foundation's charter is public.
