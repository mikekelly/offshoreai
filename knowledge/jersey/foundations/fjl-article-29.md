# FJL 2009 Article 29 — No Entitlement of Beneficiary

Article 29 is **the defining feature** of a Jersey foundation
when compared with a trust.

## The provision

A beneficiary of a Jersey foundation **does not have, by
reason only of being a beneficiary**:

- Any **legal or equitable interest** in the foundation's
  property;
- Any **right** to a distribution;
- Any **right** to demand information (subject to Article 30
  and the charter / regulations);
- Any **right** to enforce the charter / regulations as
  against the council, save as the Law specifically provides.

## Why this matters

In a **trust**, the beneficiary has:

- An **equitable interest** in trust property (defeasible,
  contingent, or vested);
- **Standing** to enforce trustee duties;
- A **Schmidt v Rosewood** supervisory information right;
- **Article 29** trust disclosure rights;
- **Standing** to apply for trustee removal, Beddoe orders,
  etc.

In a **foundation**, none of these arise automatically. The
beneficiary's position is closer to that of a **purpose-trust
object** or a **company shareholder before distribution** — a
contingent benefit holder rather than an equitable owner.

## What beneficiaries do have

Notwithstanding Article 29, beneficiaries have:

- **Standing** under Article 46 to apply to the Royal Court in
  specified circumstances;
- **Information rights** to the extent granted by Article 30
  and the charter / regulations;
- **Rights** under the charter / regulations themselves (if
  drafted to give such rights);
- **Standing** to challenge council acts that are **ultra
  vires** (Article 13);
- **Distribution entitlement** if the charter / regulations
  give fixed (rather than discretionary) entitlement.

## Charter / regulations can vary

The charter / regulations can:

- **Grant** information rights to beneficiaries;
- **Create** fixed distribution entitlements (turning the
  beneficiary into a quasi-creditor);
- **Specify** consultation or consent rights;
- **Establish** family-council mechanisms.

The default is **no rights**; everything else is bespoke
drafting.

## Foreign tax characterisation

The "no entitlement" feature can be:

- **Useful** — for foreign tax purposes where settlor-
  controlled trust treatment is unwanted (the beneficiary has
  no equitable interest to bring into the home tax base);
- **Awkward** — for foreign tax purposes where beneficiary
  entitlement is needed for relief (e.g., remittance-basis
  beneficiaries struggle to demonstrate entitlement);
- **Variable** — depends on foreign-jurisdiction look-through
  rules.

Always model the foreign tax characterisation in the relevant
jurisdiction before establishing.
