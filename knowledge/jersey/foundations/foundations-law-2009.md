# Foundations (Jersey) Law 2009

The **[Foundations (Jersey) Law 2009][fl]** governs the formation,
administration and dissolution of Jersey foundations. It came into
force on **17 July 2009** and provides a vehicle that sits between a
company and a trust in its mechanics.

## What a Jersey foundation is

A Jersey foundation is similar to a company in that:

- it is a **body corporate** with separate legal personality from its
  founder and council members; and
- it is governed by a **council** (analogous to a board of directors)
  in accordance with its **charter** and **regulations**, in much the
  same way a company is managed by its board under the articles of
  association.

It is similar to a **trust** in that:

- it must have one or more **objects**, which may be a **purpose**
  (charitable or non-charitable) and/or be for the benefit of one or
  more **beneficiaries**;
- the assets of the foundation are dedicated to those objects and do
  not belong to any member of the council, the guardian, the founder
  or the beneficiaries.

Crucially — and this is the most-asked-about feature — beneficiaries
of a Jersey foundation **do not have beneficial ownership of the
foundation's assets as of right**, and (unless the foundation's
charter or regulations provide otherwise) **do not have a right to
information** about the foundation. This distinguishes a foundation
from a trust, where beneficiaries' equitable interest and information
rights are foundational.

## Governance — council, qualified member, guardian

The Law requires:

- A **council** of one or more members responsible for managing the
  foundation in accordance with its charter and regulations.
- A **qualified member** of the council — a Jersey **qualified
  person** within the meaning of the Law (in practice, a Jersey
  regulated **trust company business** entity). The qualified member
  is the regulatory and substantive Jersey nexus.
- A **guardian**, mandatory, whose function is "to take such steps as
  are reasonable in all the circumstances to ensure that the council
  carries out its functions". The guardian is not a council member
  and is not personally liable for council decisions, but supervises
  and can apply to the Court.

(Both council and guardian roles are summarised on the [JFSC
foundations page][jfsc-fdn].)

For more detail see
[council-and-guardian.md](./council-and-guardian.md) and

## Charter and regulations

A foundation has two constitutional documents:

- A **charter** — public, registered with the Registrar of
  Foundations (a function within the JFSC). The charter contains
  the foundation's name, objects, the initial council, and certain
  required matters.
- **Regulations** — private (held by the foundation, not on the
  public register) and dealing with the more sensitive governance
  rules: who the beneficiaries are, the foundation's dispositive
  rules, how council members are appointed and removed, etc.

This split — a public charter and a private set of regulations —
makes the foundation an attractive option for clients who want a
**body-corporate** vehicle (rather than a trust) but who do not want
all governance details to be on a public register.

## How a foundation comes into existence

1. The **founder** (which can be an individual, a body corporate, or a
   nominee — and need not be Jersey-resident) endows the foundation
   with property and signs (or instructs an agent to sign) the
   charter and regulations.
2. The qualified member files the charter with the Registrar of
   Foundations and pays the prescribed fee. There is no
   consent-to-incorporate prerequisite analogous to the Companies-Law
   consent for share-capital companies; the registration is the
   constitutive act.
3. On registration the foundation becomes a body corporate from the
   date in the Registrar's certificate.

## Typical use cases

- **Philanthropic / family-purpose** structures, where the absence of
  fixed beneficiaries with information rights is desirable.
- **Orphan structures** in finance transactions — holding assets in
  a vehicle that is not "owned" in any conventional sense, with
  governance directed at a specific purpose.
- **Succession** planning where the founder wants company-style
  governance combined with trust-like dedication of assets to
  objects.
