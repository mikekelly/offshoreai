# FJL 2009 Part 3 — Capacity and Powers

Part 3 governs the **legal capacity** of a foundation and the
**powers** it can exercise.

## Article 12 — Capacity

A foundation has the **capacity** of an individual of full age
and capacity, **except** as restricted by:

- The **Law** itself (e.g., the foundation must act for its
  objects);
- The **charter** (the objects clause is a real restriction);
- The **regulations** (which may further restrict council
  powers).

## Article 13 — Ultra vires

Acts **outside the objects** of the foundation are **ultra
vires** and may be **set aside** by the Court on application
by:

- A **council member**;
- The **guardian**;
- The **Attorney General** (in charitable / public-purpose
  cases);
- A **person specified** in the charter.

In practice the ultra-vires doctrine here is more limited
than it once was for companies — the foundation's capacity
itself is wide (Article 12); only specific acts that exceed
the objects are reachable.

## Article 14 — Powers

A foundation has all the **powers** of a natural person:

- To **acquire, hold, dispose of** property;
- To **contract**;
- To **borrow / lend**;
- To **issue debt securities** (but not equity — the
  foundation has no shareholders);
- To **act as trustee** of a trust;
- To **act as a council member** of another foundation
  (subject to qualified-member rules);
- To **engage in any lawful activity** consistent with its
  objects.

Foundations are often used as:

- **Holding vehicles** for shares in operating companies;
- **PTC** (private trust company) — the foundation holds the
  shares of the PTC;
- **Purpose-trust analogues** — orphan SPV in finance
  transactions.
