# FJL 2009 Article 47 — Mistake / Hastiness Powers

Article 47 is the **foundation analogue** of Article 47 of the
Trusts (Jersey) Law 1984 — the provision allowing the Royal
Court to **set aside** a transfer to, or by, a foundation made
under mistake.

## The provision

The Royal Court may, on application, declare that a **transfer
or disposition** of property to or by a foundation is **void**
on the grounds of:

- **Mistake** by the transferor as to the effect of the
  transfer;
- **Mistake** by the council as to the effect of an exercise
  of power;
- **Hastiness** in the sense of *Pitt v Holt* and *In re
  Hastings-Bass* — failure to take relevant considerations
  into account, or taking irrelevant considerations into
  account.

## Mistake jurisdiction

The classic *Pitt v Holt* mistake jurisdiction applies:

- The mistake must be **causative** (had the truth been
  known, the transfer would not have been made);
- The mistake must be of a **type that justifies relief** —
  typically substantial, not merely a tactical regret;
- The mistake commonly relates to **tax consequences**, but
  can extend to other material matters;
- The Court has **discretion** to grant or refuse relief.

Common scenarios:

- **Foreign-tax** mistake — the transferor establishes a
  foundation on the understanding that it is tax-neutral in
  their home jurisdiction, and later discovers a tax
  liability;
- **Charter / regulations** mistake — the council exercises
  a power on the basis of a mistaken understanding of the
  charter;
- **Legal effect** mistake — the transferor or council
  misunderstands a fundamental legal feature of the
  transaction.

## Hastiness jurisdiction

The *Hastings-Bass / Pitt v Holt* "rule" — that a fiduciary's
decision is void / voidable if material considerations were
not taken into account — applies to council decisions:

- The council member must have **failed to take into account**
  what they ought to have taken into account (or vice versa);
- The failure must have **affected the outcome**;
- The court has **discretion** to set aside or refine the
  decision.

In Jersey the **Article 47 (Trusts) / Article 47 (FJL)**
formulation **expressly preserves** a broader "hastiness"
jurisdiction than the post-*Pitt v Holt* English common-law
position, which is more restricted.

## Procedure

Application is by **representation**:

- Brought by the **transferor**, **council**, **guardian**, or
  another interested party;
- **Evidence** of the mistake / hastiness (affidavits from
  the transferor / council, professional advisers, etc.);
- **Convenors** include all affected parties (other
  beneficiaries, the foundation itself);
- **Hearing** in the Family Division or Samedi.

The Court can:

- **Declare** the transfer void (with retrospective effect);
- **Order** restitution;
- **Refuse** relief in the exercise of discretion;
- **Impose terms** (costs, conditions).

## Practical effects

For foreign tax authorities:

- A successful Article 47 application typically **retrospectively
  unwinds** the foundation transfer;
- For **foreign tax** purposes, the home jurisdiction may
  accept the unwind (often does, for substantive mistakes) or
  refuse it (rarely);
- Practitioners should **coordinate** the Article 47
  application with home-jurisdiction tax counsel.
