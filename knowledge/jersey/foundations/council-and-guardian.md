# Foundation Council and Guardian

A Jersey foundation has **two principal governance organs**:

- The **council** — manages the foundation;
- The **guardian** — supervises the council.

## The council

The council is the **management body** of the foundation,
analogous to a company's board of directors.

### Composition

- The council must have **at least one council member**;
- Most foundations have **3–5 council members**;
- **At least one council member** must be a **qualified
  member** — a person regulated by the JFSC under the trust
  company services regime;
- Council members may be **natural persons** or **bodies
  corporate**;
- The founder may be a council member;
- Council members are listed in the **charter** (public
  document).

### Powers

The council:

- **Manages** the foundation's assets and affairs;
- **Makes decisions** in accordance with the **charter** and
  **regulations**;
- **Acts** on behalf of the foundation in dealings with third
  parties;
- **Exercises discretion** where the regulations permit;
- **Reports** to the guardian as the regulations require.

The council's authority is **broad and flexible**, modelled on
company-board powers but adapted to the foundation's purpose-
driven mission.

### Duties

Council members owe duties to the **foundation itself**
(comparable to directors' duties to the company under
Article 74 of the Companies Law):

- **Act honestly and in good faith**;
- Act with the **care, diligence and skill** of a reasonable
  person;
- **Act in the best interests of the foundation** in the pursuit
  of its objects;
- **Avoid conflicts of interest**;
- **Maintain proper records and accounts**.

Significantly, council members **do not owe fiduciary duties to
beneficiaries** in the way trustees do to trust beneficiaries —
unless the charter or regulations expressly provide otherwise.
This is one of the central distinctions between the foundation
and trust regimes.

## The guardian

The guardian is the **supervisory body**:

### Role

- **Supervises** the council's adherence to the foundation's
  objects;
- **Ensures** the council acts in accordance with charter and
  regulations;
- **Has standing** to bring proceedings against the council for
  breaches;
- **Approves** specified decisions (where the regulations so
  provide).

### Composition

- The guardian must be a **separate person from any council
  member**;
- The founder may be the guardian (initially);
- The guardian may be a **natural person** or **body
  corporate**;
- The guardian is named in the **charter** (so guardian identity
  is public).

### Mandatory presence

The guardian role is **mandatory** for most foundations — there
must always be a guardian:

- Where the foundation has **non-charitable purposes**;
- Where the regulations require independent oversight;
- In **almost all practical** foundation structures.

A vacancy in the guardian role triggers obligations to fill it.

### Why the guardian matters

The guardian is the **structural protection** for the
foundation's objects. Without it:

- Council members could **drift** from the foundation's purpose;
- **Self-dealing** by founder-council-members could go
  unchecked;
- **Beneficiaries** (who generally lack standing to enforce)
  would have no way to ensure performance of the foundation's
  objects.

The guardian's standing to bring proceedings is the **practical
enforcement** of the foundation's purpose.

## Relationship between council and guardian

The split is intentionally **company-like**:

- **Council = directors** (management);
- **Guardian = supervisory board / minority-protector** (oversight).

But unlike a company:

- The **guardian's role is statutory**, not optional;
- The **guardian's standing** to enforce the objects is statutory.
