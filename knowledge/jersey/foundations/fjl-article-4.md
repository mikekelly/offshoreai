# FJL 2009 Article 4 — Establishment of Foundations

Article 4 is the **constitutive provision** of the Foundations
(Jersey) Law 2009. It governs how a Jersey foundation comes
into existence.

## The provision

A foundation is **established** when:

1. A **founder** (or a person on the founder's behalf) signs
   the **charter** and **regulations**;
2. The **charter is filed with the Registrar** of Foundations
   together with the prescribed fee and any required
   declarations; and
3. The **Registrar issues a certificate** of establishment.

The certificate is **conclusive evidence** that the foundation
is duly established as of the date stated on it.

## Constitutive registration

Unlike a Jersey trust (which exists from the moment of
declaration, without registration), a Jersey foundation is
**constitutively registered**. The act of registration brings
the legal person into existence; before registration there is
no foundation. This is the same constitutive model as for a
Jersey company under Article 11 of the Companies (Jersey) Law
1991.

## The founder

The **founder** may be:

- An individual;
- A body corporate;
- An unincorporated body; or
- A **nominee** acting for an undisclosed principal.

The founder need not be Jersey-resident. The founder may
endow the foundation initially or later (Article 11), and may
or may not retain reserved powers (Articles 26-27).

## Who actually signs

In practice:

- The **founder** signs the charter and regulations;
- The **qualified member** (TCB firm) usually counter-signs as
  the council member who will file with the Registrar;
- **Founder execution** may be by power of attorney granted to
  the qualified member, where the founder is not physically
  present in Jersey.

## Fee and forms

The Registrar publishes prescribed forms and fees. As of 2026:

- **Application form** for establishment;
- **Charter** in prescribed form (public);
- **Standard fee** for registration;
- **Expedited fees** available.

(Always check the current JFSC published fee schedule.)
