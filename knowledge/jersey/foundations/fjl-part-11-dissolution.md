# FJL 2009 Part 11 — Dissolution

Part 11 governs **dissolution** of a Jersey foundation — the
process by which the foundation ceases to exist.

## Article 41 — Dissolution by council

The council can dissolve the foundation:

- In accordance with the **charter / regulations**
  (typically requiring a council resolution, guardian
  consent, and / or founder consent);
- By **resolution** specifying the dissolution date;
- Subject to **solvency** — the foundation must be able to
  pay debts;
- With **notification** to the Registrar;
- With **distribution** of remaining property per Article 43.

## Article 42 — Court-ordered dissolution

The Royal Court can order dissolution on application by:

- The **council**;
- The **guardian**;
- A **beneficiary** with sufficient standing;
- The **Attorney General** in public-interest cases;
- The **Registrar** for systemic non-compliance;
- A **creditor** in insolvent dissolution.

Grounds for court-ordered dissolution include:

- **Insolvency**;
- **Failure** of objects (impossibility, illegality);
- **Public-policy** concerns;
- **Deadlock** in council or governance failure;
- **Material breach** of charter / regulations.

## Article 43 — Distribution on dissolution

On dissolution, the council (or appointed liquidator) must:

- **Discharge debts and liabilities** of the foundation;
- **Settle** with creditors;
- **Distribute** remaining property per:
  - The **charter / regulations** (typically directing
    distribution to beneficiaries or designated successor
    purposes);
  - Otherwise, per **court order** (Article 42);
  - In default of provision, **the Crown** (rare).
- **File** a final return with the Registrar;
- **Apply** for dissolution to be recorded.

## Article 44 — Striking off

The Registrar can **strike off** a foundation:

- For **failure to file** annual returns;
- For **failure to pay** fees;
- For **failure to maintain** a qualified member;
- After **notice** to the registered office;
- After a **specified period** of non-compliance (typically
  6-12 months).

A struck-off foundation:

- **Ceases to exist** as a legal person;
- Has its **remaining property** vest in the Crown (subject
  to Article 43);
- Can be **restored** by court order on application by an
  interested person, on terms (often including bringing all
  filings up to date and paying penalties).

## Insolvency

Foundation insolvency is **not** dealt with in the same depth
as company désastre. In practice:

- An insolvent foundation typically applies to the **Royal
  Court** under Article 42 for dissolution;
- The Court may appoint a **liquidator**;
- Creditors are paid in **statutory priority** order, broadly
  tracking general principles of Jersey insolvency;
- Remaining assets (if any) go to beneficiaries / objects.

## Practical considerations

- **Plan dissolution** carefully — tax consequences for
  beneficiaries in their home jurisdictions;
- **Address foreign assets** (real property, securities);
- **Notify** all relevant parties (beneficiaries, banks,
  registered agents);
- **Document** the dissolution process for record;
- **Ensure** that all books and records are preserved per the
  10-year obligation, even after dissolution.
