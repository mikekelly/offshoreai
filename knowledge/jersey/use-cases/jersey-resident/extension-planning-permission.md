# Q: Do I need planning permission to build an extension on my house?

## The short answer

**Almost certainly yes, unless the extension is very modest
and falls fully within "permitted development" under the
Planning and Building (General Development) (Jersey) Order
2011.**

A typical sequence:

1. **Check** whether the extension falls within permitted
   development (size, position, height limits set by the
   GD Order 2011);
2. **If yes**, you can proceed without a planning
   application — but you still need **building approval**
   under the Building Bye-Laws 2007;
3. **If no**, you need to submit a full planning
   application under the Planning and Building (Jersey)
   Law 2002;
4. **If unsure**, apply for a **Certificate of Lawful
   Development** for certainty.

Common situations that **need** a full application:

- **Two-storey extension** of any size;
- **Single-storey rear extension** exceeding the size /
  depth thresholds;
- **Side extension** beyond narrow limits;
- **Listed building** — almost always;
- **Conservation area** — exemptions narrower;
- **Green Zone / Coastal National Park** — restricted.

## The proper answer

### The two parallel consents

Even small works typically need **two separate consents**:

- **Planning permission** — about whether you can build at
  all, and the design / impact;
- **Building approval** — about technical standards
  (structure, insulation, fire, drainage).

Permitted-development cover **only relieves** you from
needing planning permission — building approval is still
required.

### Permitted development for extensions

The GD Order 2011 grants automatic permission for **specific
extension categories** subject to detailed conditions.
Typical conditions include:

- **Maximum floor area** added to the original dwelling;
- **Maximum projection** from the original rear wall;
- **Maximum height**;
- **Maximum eaves height**;
- **Distance from boundaries**;
- **Materials** to match the existing dwelling;
- **Not facing a highway** (in some cases);
- **No extension** in front of the main building line.

A small single-storey rear extension on a typical Jersey
dwelling is often within these limits. A two-storey
extension, or anything beyond modest, typically isn't.

**Conservation areas, listed buildings, and Green Zone
properties** typically lose much of the permitted-
development cover — even small extensions usually need
applications.

### When you need a full application

The process is:

1. **Pre-application advice** — free drop-in at the
   Department for the Environment, or paid pre-app for
   detailed feedback;
2. **Engage an architect / agent** for the drawings and
   application documents;
3. **Submit** with fee, drawings, and a design and access
   statement;
4. **21-day public notice and consultation** — your
   neighbours will see the application and can comment;
5. **Officer assessment** against the Island Plan and
   policies;
6. **Decision** — typically 8 weeks for minor
   applications;
7. **28-day appeal window** post-decision;
8. **Apply for building approval** separately under the
   Bye-Laws.

Total realistic timeline from concept to safe-to-start:
**3-5 months** for a minor application; **6-12 months** for
anything larger.

### Common pitfalls

- **Assuming "small" = permitted**: the GD Order has
  specific numeric limits often more restrictive than
  intuition;
- **Starting work** before both planning and building
  consents — risks enforcement, fines, possible
  reversal;
- **Departing** from approved drawings during build —
  unauthorised variation can trigger enforcement;
- **Listed buildings**: virtually everything needs
  consent, including internal works;
- **Conservation areas**: tighter rules, less permitted
  development;
- **Restrictive covenants**: even if planning approves,
  private covenants on the title can prohibit the
  works — see [`../../property/restrictive-covenants.md`](../../property/restrictive-covenants.md).

### If you build without permission

Unauthorised development is a **criminal offence** under
the PBL 2002:

- **Unlimited fines** possible;
- **Imprisonment** for serious cases;
- **Enforcement notice** requiring removal at your cost;
- **Stop notice** halting work immediately;
- **Retrospective application** is possible but not
  guaranteed to succeed;
- **Sale impact** — buyer's solicitor will surface the
  problem at exchange;
- **Mortgage** and **insurance** problems likely.

## What to do tomorrow morning

1. **Read** [`../../planning/permitted-development.md`](../../planning/permitted-development.md)
   and check whether your proposal is likely within the
   GD Order;
2. **Check** if your property is listed, in a
   conservation area, or in the Green Zone — these change
   the analysis substantially;
3. **Visit** or call the Department for the Environment
   for pre-application drop-in;
4. **Engage an architect / planning consultant** for
   anything beyond very minor — fees are usually small
   compared to the cost of getting it wrong;
5. **Check your title** for restrictive covenants —
   private-law restrictions even where planning would
   permit;
6. **If borderline**, apply for a **Certificate of
   Lawful Development** — modest fee, provides
   certainty.
