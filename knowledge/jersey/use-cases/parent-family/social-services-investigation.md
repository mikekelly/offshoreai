# Q: Social Services have started investigating my child. What are my rights?

## The question

You've received a call, a visit, or a letter from
Children's Services / the Minister for Children
indicating concerns about your child. Maybe a referral
has been made by a school, a hospital, a neighbour, or
the police. What happens now? What are your rights?
What's the worst-case scenario?

## The short answer

Take this **seriously and engage early**. The Jersey
public-law child protection regime sits under **Part 4
of the Children (Jersey) Law 2002**. Most cases are
resolved at the pre-court "engagement" stage — but
proceedings, where they happen, can end in care orders
or even adoption.

Your rights at every stage:

- **To be informed** of the concerns;
- **To engage** with services and propose solutions;
- **To legal representation** — typically **publicly
  funded** if proceedings are issued;
- **To attend** all court hearings;
- **To propose** kinship carers (relatives) if removal
  is being considered;
- **To challenge** evidence and assessments;
- **To maintain** contact with your child unless
  welfare strictly requires otherwise.

**Engage. Get legal advice immediately. Do not refuse
to cooperate.**

## The proper answer

### Stage 1 — Initial concern / referral

Children's Services has received information suggesting
concern. They will typically:

- **Make initial enquiries**;
- **Visit** you and the child;
- **Consult** the child's school, GP, health visitor;
- **Convene** a multi-agency strategy discussion if the
  concern is serious.

**Your position**:

- **Engage cooperatively** — refusal to engage is
  itself evidence supporting concern;
- **Ask** for the concerns in writing;
- **Take notes** of every meeting (dates, names, what
  was said);
- **Seek legal advice** even at this early stage —
  Citizens Advice Jersey or a Jersey family-law
  specialist;
- **Consider** what evidence you can provide (photos,
  diaries, character references, records of services
  engaged).

### Stage 2 — Pre-proceedings

If concerns persist, the Minister sends a **letter
before proceedings**:

- Sets out the **concerns** in writing;
- Identifies the **action required** of you;
- Offers **support and services**;
- Warns that **legal action may follow** if engagement
  fails.

**Your position**:

- **Engage a family-law advocate immediately** — public
  funding usually available at this point;
- **Engage** with services proposed (parenting
  programmes, drug testing, mental-health support,
  contact arrangements);
- **Propose** alternatives if the Minister's approach is
  excessive;
- **Suggest** kinship carers (relatives) if temporary
  arrangements are needed;
- **Document** your engagement.

Most cases **resolve at this stage** if parents engage
genuinely. The cases that proceed to court are those
where engagement fails or the concerns are too serious.

### Stage 3 — Application to court

If concerns cannot be resolved, the Minister applies for
a **care order** or **supervision order** under Part 4
of the CJL 2002.

The court considers:

- **Threshold criteria** — is the child suffering, or
  likely to suffer, significant harm attributable to
  inadequate parental care or to being beyond parental
  control?
- **Welfare** — what order best serves the child's
  welfare?

**Significant harm** is a high threshold. It includes:

- **Ill-treatment** — physical, sexual, emotional
  abuse;
- **Impairment** of health or development;
- **Witnessing** abuse of another.

### Stage 4 — Court proceedings

Public-law children cases follow a structured timeline,
aiming for resolution within **26 weeks**:

1. **First hearing** — interim orders considered;
2. **Case management** — assessments commissioned,
   guardian appointed (a JFCAS officer);
3. **Expert evidence** — parenting capacity assessments,
   psychological reports, drug testing;
4. **Issues resolution hearing** — narrowing the issues;
5. **Final hearing** — evidence, findings, order.

**Interim orders** can include:

- **Interim care order** — child taken into care
  pending final hearing;
- **Interim supervision order** — child at home, under
  supervision;
- **Emergency protection order** — short-term, urgent;
- **Child assessment order** — for assessment access.

### Stage 5 — Final orders

If the court is satisfied of threshold and welfare:

- **Care order** — child in the care of the Minister
  (typically with foster carers); **PR shared**;
- **Supervision order** — child at home under
  supervision; up to 3 years;
- **No order** — concerns addressed but no formal
  intervention needed;
- **Placement order** — authorising the Minister to
  place the child for adoption (in serious cases).

A care order continues until **discharged** or until the
child is **18**.

A **placement order** is the most serious — leading to
adoption. The court applies a strict "nothing else will
do" standard (per *Re B-S*).

## Your rights at every stage

### To information

- **Written concerns** — ask for these;
- **Reports and assessments** — entitled to see them
  (with limited redactions);
- **Court documents** — full disclosure to you as a
  party.

### To representation

- **Specialist family-law advocate** — engage early;
- **Public funding** typically available in
  proceedings;
- **At every hearing** — your advocate should attend.

### To participate

- **Attend hearings** (most are in private — only
  parties and lawyers);
- **Give evidence**;
- **Challenge** evidence against you;
- **Propose alternatives**;
- **Suggest kinship carers**.

### To maintain relationship

- **Contact** with your child unless welfare strictly
  requires otherwise;
- **Contact arrangements** are typically set out in
  care plans;
- **Supervised** initially in many cases.

### To appeal

- **Court of Appeal** if you disagree with the order;
- **Time limits** (typically 28 days);
- **Specialist advice** essential.

## What to do tomorrow morning

1. **Don't panic.** Most cases do not end in care orders.
2. **Document** every communication from Children's
   Services in writing.
3. **Engage a specialist family-law advocate immediately**
   — even if proceedings haven't started. The earlier
   they're involved, the better the outcomes typically.
4. **Engage with services** offered — refusal is the
   single strongest predictor of proceedings.
5. **Identify kinship carers** — relatives who could
   support you or care for your child temporarily if
   needed. Discuss this with them in advance.
6. **Gather evidence** of your parenting — photographs,
   school engagement, GP records, character references.
7. **Address** any specific issues raised (e.g.
   substance use, mental health, housing) by accessing
   support voluntarily.
8. **Do not** discuss the case publicly or on social
   media — contempt and confidentiality issues are real.

## Common mistakes

- **Refusing to engage** — perceived as confirming
  concerns;
- **Going adversarial** without specialist advice;
- **Coaching** the child — readily detected by JFCAS;
- **Public disclosure** on social media — risks
  contempt;
- **Failing to address** specific concerns proactively;
- **Missing court hearings** — proceeds in your
  absence and adversely;
- **Withholding** information about kinship carers — the
  court wants to consider all options before final
  removal.

## Support and resources

- **Family-law specialist advocates** — Jersey has
  several specialist firms;
- **Citizens Advice Jersey** — early-stage guidance;
- **JACS** — for related employment matters;
- **Family Foundation** — mediation services where
  parental disputes are entangled;
- **Public funding** — typically available in
  proceedings.
