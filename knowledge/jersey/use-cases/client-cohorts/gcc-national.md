# Client Cohort — GCC National

## Who's in this cohort

Nationals of the **six Gulf Cooperation Council states** —
Saudi Arabia, UAE, Qatar, Kuwait, Bahrain, Oman. The cohort
overlaps but is **distinct from** UAE expats
([`./uae-expat.md`](./uae-expat.md)) — GCC nationals carry
the home-country succession and family-personal-law profile
that expats from non-GCC origins do not.

The HNW portion of this cohort typically holds:

- **Significant family-business interests** (often
  multi-generational);
- **Substantial real-estate assets** across the GCC, UK, and
  Europe;
- **Investment portfolios** across global custodians;
- **Long-standing relationships** with major international
  private banks and trust companies.

## Home-jurisdiction load-bearing facts

### Tax

The GCC tax landscape varies by country:

- **No personal income tax** in any GCC state;
- **No personal CGT / IHT / wealth tax** generally;
- **Corporate tax** introduced in UAE (2023) and increasingly
  in other GCC states under the OECD Pillar Two framework;
- **VAT** in most GCC states (5% generally).

For personal wealth-structuring, the tax framework is light;
the **succession and family-personal-law** dimensions are the
primary drivers.

### Succession — Sharia

GCC nationals are Muslim (with some Christian / other
minorities); Sharia inheritance applies to estates of Muslim
nationals.

**Sharia inheritance** is a **fixed-share** system:

- **Defined heirs** (children, parents, spouse, certain
  relatives) receive defined fractional shares;
- **Sons typically receive twice the share of daughters** in
  classical Sharia (with reform in some jurisdictions);
- **The testator cannot freely dispose** of estate beyond
  one-third by will (the **wasiyyah** limitation) — the
  remaining two-thirds goes to defined heirs per Sharia.

This is **the primary reason** GCC nationals approach Jersey
trusts: the [Article 9 firewall](../../trusts/firewall.md) is
explicitly designed to disapply foreign forced-heirship rules
(including Sharia) at the validity-and-effects level.

### Family business / corporate structure

Many GCC family-business groups have:

- **Holding-company structures** under GCC company law;
- **Cross-generational economic interests** with complex
  family arrangements;
- **Operating businesses** across the GCC and globally;
- **Real-estate portfolios** with assets in the UK, France,
  Spain, US, and elsewhere.

The structuring conversation often spans **family-business
governance**, **succession**, and **asset-protection**
simultaneously.

### Reporting

- **CRS** — all six GCC states are CRS participants;
- **FATCA** — applies to any US-person family members;
- **No domestic personal-tax reporting** in most GCC states.

## Why Jersey

For GCC-national clients, common drivers:

1. **Sharia / forced-heirship defence** — the load-bearing
   structuring motivation;
2. **Multi-generational family-governance vehicles** —
   Jersey trusts and foundations;
3. **Asset protection** for non-GCC assets against potential
   regional risks;
4. **Estate-planning for non-GCC assets** — UK real estate,
   US securities, European art etc. — held in Jersey wrappers
   that allow non-Sharia distribution;
5. **Pre-IPO equity** for entrepreneur clients;
6. **Investment platform** with international banking and
   custodian relationships.

## Common objections and responses

| Objection | Response |
|---|---|
| "Will Sharia courts in our home country enforce this Jersey trust?" | The [Article 9 firewall](../../trusts/firewall.md) is designed to make this question moot at the Jersey-law level — Jersey law governs trust validity and effects, disapplying foreign succession rules including Sharia. Enforcement in the home country is fact-sensitive; in practice, the **trust assets are held in Jersey** and beneficiary distributions are made under Jersey law, so home-country enforcement against the trust is rare. |
| "Can my children of different genders be treated equally?" | The trust deed can specify any allocation the settlor chooses; Sharia's son / daughter ratio does not bind a Jersey-law discretionary trust. **Equality of treatment is a structural choice.** |
| "What about the wasiyyah one-third limit?" | The wasiyyah limit applies to the **estate of a Muslim under Sharia** — it does not directly govern Jersey-trust property where the firewall applies. The structuring is **alongside**, not within, the Sharia framework. |
| "Our family business is too important to put in a trust." | Trust over **holding company** rather than over operating assets directly is a common pattern. The operating business stays under family management; the trust holds the shares. **Governance arrangements** within the trust (protector / family council / reserved powers) maintain family control. |

## Reporting and information-exchange exposure

- **CRS** — Jersey reports financial-account information to
  the GCC tax authority of the relevant client residence.
- **FATCA** — applies to US-person family members.
- **Beneficial-ownership disclosure** — Jersey's non-public
  register applies; access is by authorities only.

## Common red flags / EDD triggers

- **Politically exposed person** status — substantial PEP
  population in GCC ruling families, sovereign-wealth fund
  leadership, and senior political / military roles;
- **Sanctions adjacency** — GCC business relationships with
  sanctioned regional parties (Iran, Syria, Russia) require
  EDD;
- **Source-of-wealth verification** — for very-large GCC
  family wealth, SoW evidence may rely on legacy family-
  business records that require careful primary-source
  evidence;
- **High-risk-country business** — operations in
  jurisdictions on the
  [Handbook country-risk list](../../aml-cft/amlh-high-risk-countries.md)
  that are common GCC trading counterparties.
