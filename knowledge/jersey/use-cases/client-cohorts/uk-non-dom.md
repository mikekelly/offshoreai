# Client Cohort — UK Non-Domiciled

## Who's in this cohort

Until the **UK April 2025 reform**, "non-domiciled" was a
status under English common-law domicile rules — broadly a
person resident in the UK but who maintained a permanent home
elsewhere and had not formed an intention to remain in the UK
indefinitely.

The April 2025 reform **abolished the remittance basis** and
replaced it with the **Foreign Income and Gains (FIG)
regime** — a residence-based regime granting **four years of
relief** from UK tax on foreign income and gains for new
UK arrivers. Domicile-based concepts continue to matter for
**inheritance tax**, with related reforms.

The cohort as practitioners encounter it in 2026 splits into:

- **New arrivers** — under FIG for their first four years of
  UK residence.
- **Mid-status non-doms** — historically on remittance basis;
  now subject to transitional arrangements post-April 2025.
- **Long-residence non-doms** — historically "deemed
  domiciled" after 15 / 20 years; reforms here are still
  evolving and a primary-source check is essential.

## Home-jurisdiction load-bearing facts

For wealth-structuring conversations with this cohort:

### Income and gains

- **FIG regime** (new arrivers) — first four years exempt
  from UK tax on foreign income / gains;
- **Post-FIG window** — taxed on worldwide income / gains on
  arising basis;
- **Historic remittance basis** (mid-status / long-residence)
  — transitional rules with a Temporary Repatriation Facility
  for previously-untaxed remittances.

### Inheritance tax

- **Residence-based** post-reform — long-term residents fall
  within UK IHT scope on worldwide assets;
- **Excluded property** — non-UK assets held by certain trusts
  may be outside scope for IHT in defined circumstances, but
  the rules have tightened materially.

### Trusts

- UK trust taxation is complex and changed substantively in
  the reform. **Settlor-interested** trusts and **transferor**
  trusts have specific anti-avoidance treatment. Pre-reform
  "protected settlements" lost their protections in April
  2025 (subject to transitional provisions).

## Why Jersey

Jersey structuring for UK non-dom clients historically rested
on three pillars:

1. **Trust holding non-UK assets** outside UK IHT scope —
   substantially weakened by 2025 reforms but with continuing
   relevance for some structures and assets;
2. **Article 9 firewall protection** ([`../../trusts/firewall.md`](../../trusts/firewall.md))
   — protecting Jersey trust validity against forced-heirship
   claims under non-UK home-jurisdiction succession rules
   (relevant where the client's domicile is in a forced-
   heirship jurisdiction);
3. **Privacy and confidentiality** — narrower post-CRS and
   BO-register era but still meaningful relative to UK
   onshore structures.

For new arrivers, **FIG-era planning** focuses on the
four-year window and on positioning for the post-FIG period.

## Common objections and responses

| Objection | Response |
|---|---|
| "Won't HMRC see this anyway under CRS?" | Yes — Jersey reports to UK under [CRS](../../international/crs.md). The structure is for **tax efficiency** within reported visibility, not for opacity. |
| "Hasn't the 2025 reform killed offshore trusts for UK non-doms?" | It has materially narrowed the benefits, but **specific structuring use cases remain** — forced-heirship defence, long-term family-governance vehicles, pre-IPO planning, non-UK-asset wrappers. Each needs a current primary-source check. |
| "What about the IHT reform?" | Post-reform IHT scope is **residence-based** for long-term residents. Structuring needs to be calibrated to the client's residence trajectory and to the available transitional provisions. |
| "Will this generate UK reporting obligations?" | Yes — reporting obligations (Form 50-FS, self-assessment trust pages, etc.) attach to certain structures. The reporting is **separate from tax efficiency**; both need attention. |

## Reporting and information-exchange exposure

- **CRS** — Jersey reports financial account information to
  the UK (and to the client's other residence jurisdictions).
- **Beneficial-ownership register** — non-public but available
  to UK authorities on request via inter-governmental
  channels.
- **UK self-assessment** — trust beneficiaries / settlors may
  have reporting obligations (Form 50-FS for non-resident
  trustees, beneficial-interest disclosures, etc.).

## Common red flags / EDD triggers

- Source-of-funds linked to **UK tax under inquiry** or where
  there is HMRC dispute history;
- Client has **historically failed to report** previously-
  remittance-basis structures correctly;
- Structure is being established to **defeat known UK tax
  liability** (this is a major AML/CFT red flag, not just a
  technical issue);
- Family member positions that suggest the structure is
  being used to **circumvent** rather than plan around UK
  rules.

Where any of these appear, EDD per
[`../../aml-cft/amlh-edd.md`](../../aml-cft/amlh-edd.md) is
mandatory, source-of-wealth verification at heightened
standard, and the relationship is unlikely to proceed without
material risk mitigation.
