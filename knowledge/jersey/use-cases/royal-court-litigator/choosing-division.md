# Choosing the Right Royal Court Division

## The question

Royal Court matters proceed in **Samedi**, **Inferior Number**,
or **Superior Number**. Which is appropriate for your matter?

## The short answer

- **Samedi Division** — single-judge civil matters (most civil
  litigation, Article 51 directions, interlocutory matters);
- **Inferior Number** — criminal trials (up to 4-year sentences)
  and some substantive civil matters; Bailiff + Jurats;
- **Superior Number** — the most serious criminal cases
  (sentences exceeding Inferior Number powers); Bailiff + larger
  Jurat panel.

For **civil litigation**: Samedi is the default; some matters
escalate to Inferior Number.

## The proper answer

### Samedi Division

The principal **civil division**:

- **Single judge** (Bailiff, Deputy Bailiff, or Commissioner);
- **No Jurats**;
- **Civil matters** of most types;
- **Article 51 directions**, **Article 47 mistake**,
  **Beddoe applications**;
- **Interlocutory** applications;
- **Most M&A** and **commercial** matters.

#### When Samedi is right

- **Most civil cases**;
- **Trust applications**;
- **Commercial disputes**;
- **Probate** matters;
- **Property** matters.

### Inferior Number

Court of:

- **Bailiff** plus **2 Jurats** (lay judges of fact);
- **Criminal trials** up to **4-year sentences**;
- **Some substantive civil trials**;
- **Major commercial trials** sometimes.

#### When Inferior Number is right

- **Criminal trials** in the prescribed sentence range;
- **Civil trials of substance** where Jurat input on facts
  valuable;
- **Specific** matters where Jurats statutorily required.

### Superior Number

Court of:

- **Bailiff** plus **larger Jurat panel** (5–7 typically);
- **Most serious criminal cases**;
- **Sentences exceeding** Inferior Number power.

#### When Superior Number is right

- **Murder, serious sexual offences, large fraud** etc.;
- **Specific matters** where the law mandates.

### Civil vs Criminal proceedings

#### Civil

- **Standard of proof**: balance of probabilities;
- **Burden** on plaintiff;
- **Civil remedies** (damages, injunctions, declarations).

#### Criminal

- **Standard of proof**: beyond reasonable doubt;
- **Burden** on prosecution;
- **Criminal penalties** (imprisonment, fines, disqualifications);
- **Crown** prosecutes (Attorney General).

### Court of Appeal

Above all three divisions:

- **Senior English / Welsh / Scottish / Northern Irish judges**;
- **Panels of three**;
- **Appeals** from all Royal Court divisions;
- **Sits** in Jersey at the Royal Court building.

### Privy Council

Above Court of Appeal:

- **Final appeal**;
- **Justices of the UK Supreme Court** sitting as Privy
  Counsellors;
- **With leave** of Court of Appeal or Privy Council itself;
- **Sits** in London.

### Practical considerations

#### Forum for hostile matters

- **Samedi**: most hostile civil matters start here;
- **Escalation** to Inferior Number for full trial if needed.

#### Forum for trust applications

- **Samedi**: virtually all trust applications;
- **Article 51 directions**, blessings, set-aside applications.

#### Forum for criminal matters

- **Magistrate's Court**: petty offences;
- **Inferior Number**: most criminal trials;
- **Superior Number**: most serious.

### Filing and listing

- **Judicial Greffe** handles filing for all divisions;
- **Listing** by Greffe;
- **Hearing dates** typically based on judge availability and
  complexity.

### Timing expectations

- **Interlocutory** Samedi: weeks;
- **Substantive civil trial**: 12–24 months;
- **Complex commercial trial**: 18–36 months;
- **Criminal trial**: 6–18 months typically.

### Costs

Cost levels broadly:

- **Samedi interlocutory**: lower;
- **Civil trial**: substantial;
- **Criminal trial**: substantial.

## What to do next

For a new matter:

1. **Identify** matter type;
2. **Confirm** appropriate division;
3. **File** through Judicial Greffe;
4. **Follow** procedural rules for that division.

## Pitfalls

- **Wrong-division** filing causes delay;
- **Procedural-rule** mismatches;
- **Forum** for cross-divisional issues unclear.
