# Article 51 Directions Application — Drafting and Procedure

## The question

You are an Advocate or Solicitor of the Royal Court instructed
to bring an **Article 51 directions** application. What do you
file, how do you frame the application, and what does the
hearing look like?

## The short answer

An Article 51 application is the **trustee's principal route
to court guidance** on questions about trust administration.
It is brought by **Representation** (the Jersey procedural
equivalent of a petition), supported by an **affidavit** from
the trustee explaining the issue and the alternatives, with
**joined parties** typically being the principal beneficiaries
or representative beneficiaries.

The hearing is usually **on the Royal Court Samedi list** with
a **single judge** (Bailiff, Deputy Bailiff, or Commissioner)
unless complexity warrants the Inferior Number.

## The proper answer

### What Article 51 provides

Article 51 of the Trusts (Jersey) Law 1984 allows the **trustee,
beneficiary, settlor, or other person with an interest** to
apply to the Royal Court for **directions** on:

- The **propriety** of a proposed decision;
- The **interpretation** of the trust instrument;
- The **administration** of the trust;
- The **rights** of beneficiaries or other persons.

The Court's jurisdiction is **broad** — it can give the trustee
**comfort** on a momentous decision (Public Trustee v Cooper
category 2) or **resolve** a dispute about interpretation, or
**direct** the trustee on a contested matter.

### Categories of Article 51 application

The most common categories (drawn from English Public Trustee
v Cooper, adopted in Jersey):

- **Category 1**: Is the trustee's proposed action **within its
  powers**? (Pure interpretation / vires question.)
- **Category 2**: The trustee's action is within powers, but
  it's a **momentous decision** — does the Court **bless** it?
- **Category 3**: The trustees are **deadlocked** — does the
  Court resolve?
- **Category 4**: The trustee is **conflicted** and cannot
  exercise its own judgment — does the Court direct?

The procedural shape is similar across categories; the substantive
test the Court applies differs.

### Drafting the Representation

The Representation should:

- **Identify** the trust and trustee;
- **Set out** the relevant trust provisions;
- **Describe** the issue requiring directions;
- **State** the directions sought (with alternatives if
  appropriate);
- **List** the parties to be joined;
- **Confirm** that beneficiary information rights are being
  respected (or seek directions on confidentiality);
- **Pray** for directions and (typically) costs from the trust
  fund.

### The trustee's affidavit

The affidavit should:

- **Lay out the facts** of the trust history and current
  state;
- **Explain** the issue and how it arose;
- **Identify** the parties' interests;
- **Set out** the alternative courses of action considered;
- **Explain** the trustee's preferred course and the reasoning;
- **Exhibit** relevant documents (trust deed, key correspondence,
  expert opinions).

The affidavit is the **substantive evidence** before the Court.
Its drafting is more critical than the Representation in many
cases.

### Beneficiaries' position

Beneficiaries:

- Are typically **served** with the Representation and affidavit;
- May **appear and be heard** at the hearing;
- May **be represented** individually or by a representative
  beneficiary (especially where minor / unborn beneficiaries
  exist);
- Can **support, oppose, or be neutral** on the directions sought.

For minor / unborn / unascertained beneficiaries, the Court will
typically appoint a **representative** (often an Advocate
acting for them) at trustee cost.

### The hearing

Typical hearing flow:

- **Opening submissions** by the trustee's counsel;
- **Beneficiary submissions** in turn;
- **Cross-examination** of affidavit deponents if disputed;
- **Court questions** to counsel;
- **Reserved judgment** typically delivered in writing within
  weeks.

In **uncontroversial** matters, hearings can be **short** (an
hour or less). In **contested** matters or complex blessing
applications, hearings can extend over multiple days.

### Costs

The standard position:

- **Trustee costs** paid from the trust fund on the indemnity
  basis (the *Beddoe* principle adapted);
- **Beneficiary costs** sometimes paid from the trust fund where
  participation was helpful to the Court;
- **Hostile parties** may face personal cost orders if they fail
  on their position.

A **separate Beddoe application** is often made (alongside or in
advance of Article 51) to secure trustee cost-funding from the
fund.

## What to do next

1. **Receive instructions** from the trustee — confirm the
   issue, the desired directions, the beneficiary landscape.
2. **Draft Representation and affidavit** in iteration with the
   trustee.
3. **File** the Representation with the Judicial Greffe.
4. **Serve** on beneficiaries / parties.
5. **Engage** any representative-beneficiary counsel.
6. **Prepare** for hearing — bundle, skeleton argument.
7. **Attend** the hearing.
8. **Implement** the directions following judgment.

## Pitfalls

- **Premature application** — Article 51 is for guidance, not
  for matters the trustee should decide itself.
- **Inadequate affidavit** — the Court can only act on the facts
  presented; a thin affidavit yields thin directions.
- **Failing to join the right parties** — directions binding on
  parties not joined are problematic.
- **Beddoe gap** — failing to secure cost protection before
  incurring substantial litigation costs.
