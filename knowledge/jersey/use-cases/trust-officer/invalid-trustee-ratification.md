# Invalidly Appointed Trustee — Can the Court Ratify?

## The question

You discover that a trustee was **invalidly appointed** — wrong
deed used, missing consent, wrong appointing person. Acts have
been done in the trustee's name. Can the Royal Court **ratify**
the appointment and the acts?

## The short answer

Yes — the Royal Court has broad **inherent and statutory
jurisdiction** to:

- **Validate** an appointment retrospectively;
- **Ratify** acts done by the de-facto trustee where they were
  in good faith;
- **Make a fresh appointment** prospectively;
- **Apply Article 28(2)** validity-of-acts protection where it
  applies.

This is a routine Royal Court application — typically by
**Representation** with affidavit and beneficiary involvement.

## The proper answer

### The defect

Common defects in trustee appointment:

- **Wrong deed**: the appointment was made by deed that didn't
  comply with the trust's power-of-appointment requirements;
- **Wrong appointor**: the person appointing didn't hold the
  power;
- **Missing consent**: a required protector / settlor consent
  was not obtained;
- **Form defect**: the appointment wasn't in the required form
  (e.g. not by deed where deed was required);
- **Out of time**: the appointment was made after the appointor
  ceased to hold the power.

### What the de-facto trustee has done

In the meantime, the de-facto trustee has typically:

- **Accepted office** and acted as trustee;
- **Made distributions**;
- **Entered into contracts** on behalf of the trust;
- **Filed reports** and made decisions;
- **Held trust assets** in trust capacity.

### Article 28(2) — validity of acts

Article 28 of the Trusts (Jersey) Law 1984 provides that acts
of a person purporting to be a trustee are **valid in favour of
third parties** dealing in good faith. So **outside-trust-deed
liability** is generally protected.

The **internal trust** position (between trustee, beneficiaries,
and trust assets) is what needs Court ratification.

### The Court application

The application typically seeks:

- **Declaration** that the prior appointment was invalid /
  defective;
- **Retrospective ratification** of the appointment where
  the Court considers it just;
- **Ratification of acts done** by the de-facto trustee in
  good faith;
- **Confirmation** of any current trustee position;
- **Costs** from the trust fund.

The Court will consider:

- **Good faith** of the de-facto trustee;
- **Prejudice to beneficiaries** of validation vs invalidation;
- **Practicalities** of unwinding the de-facto period;
- **Source of the defect** (administrative slip vs deliberate
  bypass).

### The procedural route

Article 51 directions or general inherent-jurisdiction
applications:

- **Representation** filed at the Judicial Greffe;
- **Beneficiary involvement** as appropriate;
- **Affidavit** evidence of the defect and the de-facto acts;
- **Hearing** typically on the Royal Court Samedi list.

Usually resolved in a single hearing where uncontested.

## What to do next

1. **Identify** the precise defect and date it occurred;
2. **Audit** the trustee acts during the defective period;
3. **Engage Jersey counsel** to draft the application;
4. **Consult** beneficiaries (or representative-beneficiary
   counsel for minors / unborn);
5. **File** the Representation;
6. **Attend** the hearing;
7. **Implement** the resulting order;
8. **Review** appointment procedures to prevent recurrence.

## Pitfalls

- **Trying to fix retroactively without Court input**: a
  unilateral fresh-deed-of-appointment doesn't cure prior acts;
- **Delay**: longer the de-facto period, more complex the
  ratification;
- **Hostile beneficiary**: a beneficiary unhappy with a de-facto-
  trustee decision may object to ratification;
- **Forgetting third-party reliance**: even if internal validity
  is restored, third-party contracts may have separate issues.
