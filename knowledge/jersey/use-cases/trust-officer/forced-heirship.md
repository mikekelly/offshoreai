# Foreign Forced-Heirship Claim — What's the Test?

## The question

The settlor died. The settlor's home jurisdiction has **forced
heirship** rules (typically civil-law countries — France, Italy,
Spain, Saudi Arabia under Shari'a, etc.). The forced heirs claim
an entitlement to a fixed share of the trust property. How do
you respond?

## The short answer

**Article 9 of the Trusts (Jersey) Law 1984** says Jersey does
**not apply** foreign forced-heirship rules to Jersey trusts.
Jersey-law recognises the settlor's freedom to settle property
on trust, and the forced heirs have **no entitlement** under
Jersey law to the trust property.

The trustee should:

1. **Acknowledge** receipt of the claim;
2. **Apply Article 9** as the substantive answer;
3. **Apply for Royal Court directions** if the claim is being
   actively pursued;
4. **Consider** beneficiary impact carefully.

## The proper answer

### What forced heirship is

Many civil-law jurisdictions reserve **fixed shares** of a
deceased's estate for specified heirs (spouse, children,
sometimes parents):

- **France**: *réserve héréditaire* (varies — half to three-
  quarters of estate to children);
- **Italy**: *legittima* (similar concept);
- **Saudi Arabia** under Shari'a: detailed prescriptive shares;
- **Spain**: similar civil-code shares;
- **Many Latin American** countries: variations on the above.

The forced heir has a **direct claim** under home-jurisdiction
law, even if the deceased disposed of property differently
during life or by will.

### Article 9 — the Jersey response

Article 9 says Jersey law applies to **all questions** about a
Jersey trust:

- Forced-heirship rules of the settlor's home jurisdiction are
  **not applied** by Jersey courts;
- The trustee is **not obliged** to make a distribution to
  satisfy a foreign forced-heir claim;
- A foreign judgment based on forced-heirship will **not be
  recognised** in Jersey.

### How the claim typically plays out

Scenario:

1. **Settlor dies** with substantial trust assets in Jersey;
2. **Forced heirs** in the home jurisdiction make a claim
   against the estate, citing local forced-heirship law;
3. **Forced heirs apply** to local court for orders against the
   trust assets;
4. **Local court grants** an order (or threatens to).

The trustee's response:

- **Jersey law applies** to the trust (Article 9);
- The trustee **is not obliged** to comply with the foreign
  order under Jersey law;
- The trustee may, however, **face foreign-court pressure** via
  its personnel or assets in the home jurisdiction.

### Royal Court directions

For a substantial claim, the trustee should typically:

- **Apply for Article 51 directions** confirming the Jersey-
  law position;
- Get **explicit Royal Court endorsement** of Article 9
  application;
- This gives the trustee Jersey-law protection.

### Practical considerations

Even where Article 9 holds:

- **Beneficiaries** in the relevant family may have **inheritance
  hopes** linked to forced-heirship expectations;
- **Family disputes** can spill into the trust;
- **Trustee discretion** to make distributions to forced heirs
  (if beneficiaries) is sometimes used to **soften** the impact
  without compromising Article 9;
- **Settlement** of claims may be possible without admitting
  forced-heirship entitlement.

## What to do next

1. **Receive and document** the claim;
2. **Engage Jersey counsel**;
3. **Identify** the precise home-jurisdiction rules being
   invoked;
4. **Consider Royal Court directions** for trustee protection;
5. **Consult** with beneficiaries (forced heirs may also be
   trust beneficiaries — this is common);
6. **Document** the trustee's Article 9-based position;
7. **Address** any personal-pressure on trustee personnel
   abroad.

## Pitfalls

- **Treating Article 9 as automatic protection**: in practice
  the trustee must actively assert it through Royal Court
  proceedings;
- **Beneficiary confusion**: forced heirs may also be discretionary
  beneficiaries — communications must be careful;
- **Foreign-court contempt risk** for trustee personnel making
  business / personal trips to the relevant jurisdiction.
