# "The beneficiary is asking for the deed / accounts / letter of wishes. What must I provide?"

## The short answer

The beneficiary **does not have an automatic right** to anything. Since
the Privy Council's decision on appeal from the Isle of Man in
**[*Schmidt v Rosewood Trust Ltd* [2003] UKPC 26][schmidt]** — which
the Jersey Royal Court has accepted as a correct statement of Jersey
law — disclosure of trust information is a matter of the Court's
**inherent supervisory jurisdiction** over the administration of
trusts, not an entitlement that flows from any proprietary interest
of the beneficiary in the trust property.

In Jersey practice, that translates into two presumptions:

- **A presumption *in favour of* disclosure** of the **trust deed**
  and **accounts** to a beneficiary who asks, **unless** the trustee
  can show a "good reason why disclosure was not in the interests of
  the beneficiaries as a whole" ([Mondaq — Beneficiaries' rights][mondaq];
  [Dickinson Gleeson — Rights of a beneficiary][dg]).
- **A presumption *against* disclosure** of the **letter of wishes**,
  on the basis that the settlor is likely to have intended it to be
  confidential. ([Mondaq][mondaq])

Where the trustee and the beneficiary disagree, either can apply to
the **Royal Court** for directions on disclosure. The Court will
balance the competing interests of beneficiaries, the trustee, and
third parties, and may order **limited disclosure** or impose
**safeguards** such as redaction, undertakings as to onward use, or
limits on who may see the documents.

## The legal framework

### 1. The *Schmidt v Rosewood* shift

Before 2003, the leading authority on a beneficiary's right to trust
documents was the English Court of Appeal decision in
*Re Londonderry's Settlement* [1965] Ch 918, which approached
disclosure as flowing from the beneficiary's *proprietary* interest
in the trust property.

In ***Schmidt v Rosewood Trust Ltd***, the Privy Council (per Lord
Walker) rejected the proprietary approach:

> The Board cannot regard it as a reasoned or binding decision that a
> beneficiary's right or claim to disclosure of trust documents or
> information must always have the proprietary basis of a
> transmissible interest in trust property.

([Wikipedia summary of *Schmidt*][schmidt-wiki])

Instead, the right to seek disclosure is **an aspect of the Court's
inherent jurisdiction to supervise and, if necessary, to intervene in
the administration of trusts**. ([Wikipedia summary of *Schmidt*][schmidt-wiki])

And critically:

> No beneficiary (and least of all a discretionary object) has any
> entitlement as of right to disclosure of anything which can plausibly
> be described as a trust document.

([Wikipedia summary of *Schmidt*][schmidt-wiki])

### 2. The Court's balancing exercise

Because the right is supervisory, not proprietary, the Court (and the
trustee acting in advance of any application) carries out a
**balancing exercise** of:

- the **interests of the requesting beneficiary**;
- the **interests of other beneficiaries** (whose privacy and
  whose share of the trust fund may be affected by disclosure);
- the **interests of the trustee** in administering the trust
  efficiently;
- the **interests of third parties** named in the documents (e.g.,
  the settlor, the protector, a former trustee, banking
  counter-parties).

(See [Mondaq — Beneficiaries' rights][mondaq])

Where the balance is not clear, **disclosure may be limited** —
e.g., redacted accounts, summary minutes rather than full minutes, or
inspection-only at the trustee's office — or **safeguards** imposed
(undertakings, non-disclosure agreements, restrictions on who can
see).

### 3. Jersey-specific presumptions

The Jersey Royal Court has applied *Schmidt* with two presumptions
that materially shape how trustees should respond to requests:

| Document type | Presumption |
|---|---|
| **Trust deed** (including subsequent amendments) | Presumption **in favour of** disclosure |
| **Trust accounts** | Presumption **in favour of** disclosure |
| **Letter of wishes** | Presumption **against** disclosure (settlor confidentiality) |
| **Trustee minutes / deliberations** | No firm presumption — Court applies the balancing exercise; the *Re Londonderry* concern that disclosing reasons stifles trustee decision-making remains relevant |
| **Lawyers' advice** to trustees | Tilts against disclosure where privilege is preserved; trustee should not waive without thought |
| **Third-party correspondence** | Tilts against disclosure; trustee owes duties of confidence to non-trust counterparties |

([Mondaq][mondaq]; [Dickinson Gleeson][dg])

## Who counts as a "beneficiary" for these purposes

A **fixed-interest beneficiary** has a stronger case for disclosure
than a **discretionary object**, but both are within scope of the
supervisory jurisdiction. A **mere object of a power of addition**
(someone who could be made a beneficiary but is not yet one) has a
weaker case still; *Schmidt* itself was on facts where the claimant
was a discretionary object.

A **protector** is **not** a beneficiary. The protector's right to
information flows from the deed and from the proper performance of
the protector's function, not from *Schmidt*.

A **settlor** is also not a beneficiary unless named as one — although
trustees in practice keep settlors informed, the legal basis is the
deed and accepted practice, not a *Schmidt*-type entitlement.

## What to do — checklist when you receive a request

1. **Categorise the requester.** Beneficiary (fixed / discretionary /
   object of power)? Protector? Settlor? Third party? The category
   sets the strength of the underlying case.
2. **Categorise the document.** Use the table above as a starting
   point. The strongest case is the deed and accounts; the weakest is
   the letter of wishes.
3. **Consider the requester's stated reason.** A beneficiary
   contemplating their tax position has a strong case to see the deed
   and accounts; a beneficiary running a divorce or hostile family
   litigation has a complicating factor that the Court will weigh.
4. **Consider the interests of others.** Are other beneficiaries'
   interests engaged? Is there sensitive third-party material in the
   accounts (e.g., underlying-asset values that other family branches
   would weaponise)?
5. **Draft what you would disclose voluntarily.** Often the answer is
   "the deed, redacted accounts showing the requester's interest,
   summary of recent material decisions". This is often enough.
6. **If you propose to refuse**, articulate the **good reason** in
   writing. *Schmidt* requires it to be a *good reason* in the
   interests of the beneficiaries as a whole, not the trustee's
   convenience.
7. **If the dispute escalates**, do not wait to be sued. Apply to the
   **Royal Court under Article 51** for directions; this protects the
   trustee on costs and shifts the contest into the supervisory
   framework. See
   [distribution-decisions-and-court-blessing.md](distribution-decisions-and-court-blessing.md)
   and
   [`trusts/article-51-directions.md`](../../trusts/article-51-directions.md).

## Interaction with the Data Protection (Jersey) Law 2018

A Jersey-resident beneficiary may make a **subject access request
(SAR)** under the **Data Protection (Jersey) Law 2018** for their
personal data held by the trustee. The trustee's response should be
*Schmidt*-aware:

- The DPJL is an additional access route, not a substitute for the
  supervisory jurisdiction.
- The DPJL contains exemptions that broadly protect third-party
  personal data, professional privilege, and legal proceedings.
- Disclosure under the DPJL can be selective — you can give the
  beneficiary their *personal data* without giving them the full
  trust document set.
- A SAR is not a tool the trustee should use to side-step
  *Schmidt* balancing in respect of trust documents proper.

## Pitfalls

- **Refusing without reasoning.** A bare refusal to a beneficiary's
  reasonable request invites a Court application and may shift the
  costs onto the trustee personally rather than the fund.
- **Disclosing the letter of wishes without thought.** The
  presumption is against; if you disclose, you may be undermining
  the settlor's intent and exposing the trustee to a complaint from
  remainder beneficiaries.
- **Disclosing trustee deliberations.** The *Londonderry* logic still
  holds: making the trustee's reasoning routinely disclosable will
  chill the trustee's decision-making. Treat minutes as more
  sensitive than accounts.
- **Forgetting third parties.** The trustee owes duties of
  confidence to a wide set of third parties (other clients of the
  trust company, counterparty banks, investment managers). Don't
  disclose their information when responding to a beneficiary's
  request.
- **Treating a SAR as a *Schmidt* application.** They are different
  doors; the answers may overlap but the legal tests differ.
