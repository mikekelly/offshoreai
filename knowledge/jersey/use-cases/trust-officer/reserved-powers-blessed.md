# Reserved-Powers Structures the Royal Court Has Blessed

## The question

You're designing a trust structure with **reserved powers**.
Which specific configurations has the **Royal Court accepted**
as valid in case law, and what's the line beyond which the
Court starts to question?

## The short answer

The Royal Court has consistently accepted:

- **Article 9A statutory reservations** (Article 9A list);
- **Settlor as protector** with veto / consent powers;
- **Investment-direction** powers reserved to settlor or
  protector;
- **Power to appoint / remove trustees** reserved;
- **Power to add / remove beneficiaries** reserved;
- **Power to revoke** (with adverse foreign-tax considerations).

The Court starts to question when:

- The trustee has **no real fiduciary function** to perform;
- The arrangement is **manifestly a sham**;
- The reservations **defeat the trust's stated purpose**;
- Creditor / spouse circumstances suggest **abuse**.

## The proper answer

### Accepted configurations

#### Settlor as protector with veto

A common, well-accepted structure:

- Trustee makes distribution and investment decisions in the
  ordinary course;
- Settlor (as protector) has **veto power** on specified
  categories;
- Settlor can **direct** distributions in some scenarios.

This preserves the trustee's fiduciary function while giving
the settlor strategic-level oversight.

#### Investment-direction trust

- Trustee holds legal title;
- Investment manager (often appointed by settlor) directs
  investment decisions;
- Trustee follows directions subject to legality / fiduciary
  duty limits.

Long-accepted in Jersey case law and practice.

#### Power to add/remove beneficiaries

- Settlor / protector can add new beneficiaries during
  settlor's lifetime;
- Removal less common but accepted in some structures;
- Court has not generally objected.

#### Reserved revocation

- Settlor retains power to **revoke** the trust;
- Article 9A explicitly permits;
- **Foreign tax consequences** typically severe (US grantor
  trust, UK transfer-of-asset-abroad);
- Jersey-law-valid but commercially fraught for tax-resident
  settlors.

### Cases worth knowing

#### Re the Esteem Settlement (2003)

- **Snook sham test** adopted into Jersey law;
- Settlor's extensive control didn't make the trust a sham
  where the trustee genuinely accepted fiduciary duty;
- **Substance of trustee role** is what matters.

#### Re A Settlement / Re B Settlement family

- Various Royal Court applications dealing with reserved-
  powers structures;
- Court generally **respects** the structure where validly
  established.

#### MacKinnon v Regent Trust

- Sham analysis applied;
- Trustee genuine acceptance of duty defeats sham allegation.

### Where the Court draws lines

The Court has indicated concerns when:

#### No real trustee role

- Trust deed gives **all material discretion** to settlor /
  protector;
- Trustee is a **pure rubber stamp**;
- This crosses into sham territory.

#### Foreign-jurisdiction interaction

- A reserved-powers structure designed to defeat **foreign
  creditor / spouse claims** can face **public-policy** push-
  back even within Article 9 protection;
- Particularly where the timing suggests anticipation of
  foreign proceedings.

#### Inconsistency with stated purpose

- Trust purports to be for family beneficiaries;
- Reserved powers make beneficiaries' interests illusory;
- Court may scrutinise.

### Practical drafting guidance

For a reserved-powers structure:

- **Use Article 9A list** as the menu;
- **Document the settlor's intention** clearly;
- **Preserve genuine trustee role** in deed drafting;
- **Disclose** the structure for foreign-tax purposes;
- **Avoid** features that suggest sham (sole-beneficiary + revoke
  + protector combination etc. without strong justification).

### Working with reserved-powers trusts

When you administer a trust with significant reserved powers:

- **Document** trustee decisions properly;
- **Decline** instructions that the trustee cannot legitimately
  follow;
- **Engage** the settlor / protector on appropriate matters;
- **Maintain** independent trustee judgement records.

## What to do next

For a new reserved-powers trust:

1. **Map** the proposed reserved powers against Article 9A;
2. **Model** foreign-tax consequences;
3. **Draft** the deed preserving real trustee function;
4. **Document** the settlor's intentions;
5. **Engage** trustee professional governance from outset.

For an existing reserved-powers trust:

1. **Audit** the current arrangements;
2. **Confirm** trustee is exercising real judgement;
3. **Document** trustee decisions properly;
4. **Refresh** any tax positions on material changes.

## Pitfalls

- **Treating Article 9A as a free pass**: Court still requires
  real trustee function;
- **Over-reliance on jurisprudence**: each case is fact-specific;
- **Foreign-tax surprise**: tax consequences can be severe even
  with Jersey-law-validity;
- **Trustee passivity**: a trustee that doesn't exercise judgement
  invites sham challenge.
