# Settlor Wants to be Protector and Sole Beneficiary — Sham?

## The question

The settlor wants to be:

- The **sole beneficiary** during their life;
- The **protector** with appointment / removal / direction
  powers;
- And retain a **revocation** power.

Is this a sham, or can you accept it?

## The short answer

**Not automatically a sham** — Article 9A permits each of these
powers individually. But this configuration is the **classic
sham profile**, and you should approach it with caution:

- The combination signals that the **trustee may have no real
  fiduciary role**;
- The trust must be **operated genuinely** with the trustee
  exercising independent judgement;
- **Esteem** and **MacKinnon** are the leading Jersey authorities
  on the sham line.

## The proper answer

### The Snook sham test

A sham requires:

- A **common intention** between settlor and trustee that the
  documents do **not** reflect the true relationship;
- The **documents are a façade** for an arrangement that is
  really something else.

A sham is **not** found merely because the structure is
unusual or unfavourable to creditors / spouses / tax authorities.
There must be genuine **intention to deceive** about the nature
of the relationship.

### Esteem

In **Re the Esteem Settlement (2003)**, the Jersey Royal Court
considered whether trusts where the settlor exercised extensive
control were shams. The Court applied the Snook test:

- **Both parties** (settlor + trustee) must share the sham
  intention;
- A **unilateral intention** by the settlor doesn't make a sham;
- The **trustee's genuine acceptance** of fiduciary duty defeats
  the sham allegation.

So if the trustee genuinely **acts as trustee** — exercises
judgement, considers beneficiary interests, refuses inappropriate
instructions when needed — the structure is not a sham even with
extensive reserved powers.

### The practical risk

Even where the trust is **not legally a sham**:

- **Foreign courts** in matrimonial / creditor / tax disputes
  may treat it as a sham anyway;
- **Foreign tax authorities** may look through;
- The **trustee's reputation** is on the line if challenged;
- The TCB's **regulatory standing** with the JFSC may be
  affected by accepting questionable structures.

### Trustee diligence

To defend against sham challenges:

- **Document** each significant trustee decision with reasons;
- **Decline** to follow settlor instructions that are
  inappropriate or harmful to beneficiaries;
- **Hold meetings** with proper governance even if the settlor
  is the only beneficiary present in interest;
- **Maintain** the trust assets separately and clearly;
- **Avoid** appearing to take orders rather than exercising
  judgement.

## What to do next

For a current request to set up such a trust:

1. **Probe** the settlor's expectations — do they understand
   that the trustee has real authority?
2. **Model** foreign-tax consequences — they will often be
   adverse;
3. **Discuss with senior management** — sham-profile structures
   may trigger TCB risk-committee review;
4. **Consider declining** if the structure looks predominantly
   tax-avoidance / creditor-defeating;
5. If accepting, **document** the trust's genuine purpose and
   the trustee's intended approach.

## Pitfalls

- **Accepting at face value**: "I just want to keep control"
  is exactly the wrong starting point for a real trust.
- **Treating settlor as principal**: the settlor isn't your
  client in their settlor capacity — the beneficiaries
  (including the settlor as a beneficiary) are.
- **Foreign tax surprise**: settlors often don't understand
  grantor / TOAA / equivalent treatment until after settlement.
