# Jersey Jurisdiction Clause After Crociani

## The question

Your trust deed has a clause selecting **Jersey courts** as the
exclusive forum. The Privy Council's decision in **Crociani v
Crociani** (2014) addressed such clauses. Is the clause still
effective today?

## The short answer

**Yes**, but with important nuances:

- *Crociani* confirmed that Jersey-jurisdiction clauses in
  trust deeds are **valid and enforceable**;
- Foreign courts will give **weight** to such clauses but may
  override in **strong** cases (matrimonial finance, public
  policy);
- The clause is **most effective** when paired with active
  Royal Court engagement and proper Article 9 protection;
- **Post-Crociani drafting** typically includes belt-and-braces
  clauses (exclusive jurisdiction + proper-law + arbitration
  alternatives).

## The proper answer

### What Crociani decided

The Privy Council (on appeal from the Jersey Court of Appeal)
held that:

- A **jurisdiction clause** in a trust deed selecting Jersey is
  **valid and binding** on parties to the trust;
- The clause should be **given effect** unless there are
  **strong reasons** to override;
- The same principles applied by English courts to jurisdiction
  clauses in commercial contracts apply, **adapted** to the
  trust context;
- The fact that a clause was inserted **after** disputes had
  arisen could affect its weight.

### What Crociani didn't decide

- **Matrimonial finance** courts (English Family Division etc.)
  may still take jurisdiction over a settlor / beneficiary
  spouse personally;
- **Public-policy** overrides remain available in extreme
  cases;
- **Recognition of foreign judgments** is a separate question
  (Article 9 protects).

### Drafting implications

Modern Jersey trust-deed drafting:

- **Exclusive jurisdiction** in Jersey for trust questions;
- **Proper law** Jersey;
- **Submission clauses** for beneficiaries (where appropriate);
- **Arbitration alternatives** in some structures;
- **Severability** in case one element is challenged;
- **Express adoption** of Article 9 firewall.

### Forum-shopping risk

The risk is **forum-shopping** by:

- **A beneficiary** seeking a more favourable forum;
- **An ex-spouse** seeking matrimonial-finance relief abroad;
- **A creditor** seeking faster / cheaper recovery elsewhere.

Crociani makes forum-shopping **harder** by giving the
jurisdiction clause real weight. But:

- It doesn't **prevent** foreign courts from taking
  jurisdiction;
- **Personal-pressure** mechanisms (against settlor /
  beneficiary personally) remain available;
- **Recognition** in third jurisdictions may be affected.

### Trustee strategy

When jurisdiction is contested:

- **Cite Crociani** prominently for the clause's weight;
- **Engage** in foreign-court proceedings only to assert
  jurisdiction (not to submit);
- **Apply** for parallel Jersey Royal Court directions;
- **Document** active Jersey-court engagement to support the
  forum argument.

## What to do next

For existing trusts:

1. **Check** the deed for jurisdiction / proper-law clauses;
2. **Assess** whether the drafting reflects post-Crociani best
   practice;
3. If a jurisdiction dispute arises, **engage counsel
   immediately**;
4. **Apply** for Jersey directions in parallel with any foreign
   proceedings.

For new trusts:

1. **Draft** with exclusive jurisdiction + proper law clauses;
2. **Consider** arbitration / mediation provisions;
3. **Include** express Article 9 references;
4. **Confirm** the settlor's understanding of the forum
   selection.

## Pitfalls

- **Treating the clause as bulletproof**: foreign courts may
  still take jurisdiction in matrimonial-finance and other
  strong cases;
- **Inserting post-dispute**: clauses inserted after disputes
  may carry less weight;
- **Failing to engage in Jersey**: a Jersey jurisdiction clause
  is more persuasive when paired with active Royal Court
  proceedings.
