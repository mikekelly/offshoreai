# Foreign Court / Regulator Demands Trust Information

## The question

A foreign court or regulator has issued an order or request for
information about a Jersey trust — bank statements, beneficiary
identities, distribution records. How do you respond?

## The short answer

**Do not comply unilaterally**. Jersey trustees are subject to
**Jersey confidentiality obligations** and the Trusts Law
Article 9 firewall principles. Steps:

1. **Engage Jersey counsel immediately**;
2. **Identify the legal basis** of the foreign request (letters
   rogatory, mutual-assistance treaty, automatic-exchange
   framework);
3. **Determine** whether to comply, resist, or apply to the
   Royal Court for directions;
4. **Notify** the JFSC and affected parties as appropriate.

Direct compliance with foreign orders without Jersey-court
authorisation can be a **breach of trustee duty** to
beneficiaries.

## The proper answer

### Possible legal bases

The foreign request may be made under:

- **Letters rogatory** — formal request from foreign court via
  Jersey diplomatic channels;
- **Mutual Legal Assistance Treaty (MLAT)** — criminal /
  regulatory cooperation;
- **TIEA** — tax-information exchange on request;
- **CRS / FATCA** — automatic exchange already running through
  Revenue Jersey;
- **Direct foreign-court order** — typically not directly
  enforceable in Jersey without Royal Court recognition.

Each has different procedural requirements and trustee
obligations.

### The trustee's competing duties

- **To beneficiaries**: confidentiality and the trust's interest;
- **To the Jersey trust regime**: comply with Jersey law
  including disclosure orders;
- **To AML / CFT regime**: cooperate with legitimate criminal-
  investigation requests;
- **To regulator (JFSC)**: comply with JFSC information requests
  and Code obligations.

These can conflict — Royal Court directions resolve.

### Article 51 directions

A common response is an **Article 51 application** asking the
Royal Court to direct the trustee:

- Whether to comply with the foreign request;
- On what terms (e.g. redactions, beneficiary consent);
- With what protections for the trustee.

This gives the trustee **Court protection** for whatever course
it then takes.

### Direct foreign-court order

A direct foreign-court order is **not directly enforceable** in
Jersey without:

- **Recognition / enforcement** through Jersey procedures;
- **Letters rogatory** for evidence-gathering;
- **Voluntary compliance** by the trustee (which is generally
  not advised without Royal Court input).

The Article 9 firewall and Jersey confidentiality principles
strongly favour **Court guidance** before responding.

### Automatic exchange exceptions

For CRS / FATCA, the trustee already reports through Revenue
Jersey under the automatic framework. **Additional bespoke
requests** outside this framework should be handled with
caution.

## What to do next

1. **Receive and record** the request — date, source, legal
   basis claimed, deadline;
2. **Do not respond** beyond acknowledgement until advised;
3. **Engage Jersey counsel** (often litigation specialist) and
   notify the trust officer's senior management;
4. **Notify** the JFSC if the request involves regulatory or
   AML matters;
5. **Consider** Article 51 directions application;
6. **Notify beneficiaries** (if appropriate / not constrained
   by tipping-off rules);
7. **Respond** in accordance with legal advice and any Royal
   Court direction.

## Pitfalls

- **Direct compliance** without Royal Court input can be breach
  of trust;
- **Outright refusal** without legal basis can attract foreign-
  court contempt risk for the trustee's officers personally;
- **Ignoring AML overlap**: criminal-investigation requests may
  have AML implications independent of the trust position;
- **Confidentiality breach**: disclosure to inappropriate
  parties is itself a breach.
