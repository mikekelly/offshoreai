# Settlor Wants a Distribution to a Non-Beneficiary

## The question

The settlor (or a beneficiary) wants the trust to distribute to
a person who is **not** a beneficiary — a friend, a charity not
in the named class, a new family member. What are your options?

## The short answer

You **cannot distribute to a non-beneficiary** — that would be a
**breach of trust**. Options:

1. **Add the recipient as a beneficiary first** (if the deed
   gives a power of addition) — then distribute;
2. **Distribute to an existing beneficiary** who then gifts on
   (with tax / wishes / liability implications);
3. **Refuse** if the deed doesn't permit addition and the
   distribution isn't otherwise authorised.

The Royal Court will **not** authorise a distribution outside
the beneficial class except in very narrow circumstances.

## The proper answer

### The core principle

A trustee's distribution power is **limited to the
beneficiaries** named or described in the trust deed. Distribution
**outside the class** is a breach of trust regardless of:

- The settlor's wishes;
- A beneficiary's agreement to redirect;
- The economic merits of the proposed recipient.

The trustee is **a fiduciary** for the named class — not a
general philanthropic agent.

### Option 1: Power of addition

If the trust deed contains a **power to add beneficiaries**
(common in modern Jersey trusts), the trustee can:

- **Exercise the power** to add the prospective recipient;
- Then **distribute** to them as a beneficiary.

The power of addition is a **fiduciary power** — it must be
exercised in good faith and for proper purposes. It is not a
mere conduit. Considerations:

- **Is the addition genuinely in the interest of the trust?**
- **Are existing beneficiaries' interests properly considered?**
- **Are there foreign-tax consequences of the addition?**

### Option 2: Existing-beneficiary route

Distribute to an existing beneficiary, who then **gifts** to
the prospective recipient at their discretion:

- The beneficiary becomes the **legal owner** of the distributed
  property;
- The trustee has **no further role** in what they do with it;
- But the trustee should not **engineer the route** to defeat
  the beneficiary-only principle if that creates a sham issue.

The route works for **genuine gifts** but not for arrangements
where the existing beneficiary is functionally a nominee.

### Option 3: Refuse

If neither option above is appropriate, **refuse**:

- Trustees do not have a general power to distribute outside the
  class;
- Settlor pressure does not change this;
- Refusal protects the trust integrity and the trustee's
  position.

### Court applications

The Royal Court will **not** authorise distribution outside
the class as a general rule. Variation under **Article 47** of
the Trusts Law could change the beneficial class but requires:

- A **proper Article 47 application**;
- **Beneficiary consents** or court approval for those who
  cannot consent;
- A **good case** for the variation.

This is the **proper route** if the settlor / family genuinely
want to change the class.

## What to do next

For an immediate request:

1. **Check the trust deed** for power of addition and other
   relevant powers;
2. **Discuss with the trustee committee / decision-makers**;
3. **Consider** whether addition is appropriate and proper;
4. **Document** the decision with reasons;
5. **If declined**, communicate clearly with the settlor /
   beneficiary about the alternatives (Article 47, gift route).

## Pitfalls

- **Routing through a beneficiary to defeat the class**: this
  is a breach of trust dressed up.
- **Settlor pressure**: settlors sometimes treat the trustee as
  a banker who will pay whomever they instruct. Hold the line.
- **Power-of-addition exercised mechanically**: addition must
  be a genuine fiduciary act, not a rubber stamp.
