# SAR Threshold — When Must I File?

## The question

Working as a trust officer, you encounter something that **could
be suspicious** — an unusual instruction, a transaction
inconsistent with the client profile, an inconsistent
explanation. When must you escalate internally, and when must
you (or your MLRO) file a SAR?

## The short answer

The legal threshold is **"knowledge or suspicion"** — a low
threshold short of certainty. Practical operation:

- **Trust officer escalates internally** to the MLRO on
  forming a suspicion (or even uncertainty);
- **MLRO assesses** and decides whether the suspicion crosses
  the SAR threshold;
- **MLRO files** the SAR with the FIU if the threshold is met;
- **Continuing observation** of the client is required after
  SAR.

The trust officer's primary duty is **internal escalation** —
file an internal report. The MLRO's duty is to **assess and
file externally** as appropriate.

## The proper answer

### The legal threshold

Under the Proceeds of Crime (Jersey) Law 1999:

- An offence is committed by **failing to disclose** known or
  suspected money laundering;
- **"Suspicion"** is the operative threshold;
- It is **less than belief** and significantly less than
  certainty;
- It is **more than fleeting concern** — there must be a
  reasonable basis.

The classic articulation: "**something more than fanciful, but
less than 'likely on balance'**".

### Red flags as a trust officer

Common triggers that should prompt internal escalation:

#### Client behaviour

- **Reluctance** to provide CDD information or explanations;
- **Inconsistent explanations** of source of funds / wealth;
- **Unusual instructions** outside the client's typical pattern;
- **Pressure** for urgency, secrecy, or unusual procedures;
- **Last-minute changes** in destinations or counterparties.

#### Transaction profile

- **Pass-through transactions** with no apparent economic
  purpose;
- **Round-number** transactions designed to avoid thresholds;
- **Unexpected sources** of new contributions to the trust;
- **Distribution to non-beneficiaries** (and pressure to add
  them);
- **Counterparties** in high-risk jurisdictions without
  business rationale.

#### Background events

- **Negative media** about the settlor, beneficiary, or related
  party;
- **Sanctions list matches** (even false-positive matches
  warrant review);
- **Foreign court proceedings** suggesting fraud / corruption /
  tax-evasion;
- **Regulatory action** against connected parties;
- **PEP status changes** suggesting elevated risk.

### Internal escalation

When you spot something:

1. **Do not discuss with the client** ("tipping off" is an
   offence);
2. **Document** your observation contemporaneously;
3. **Escalate to MLRO** through internal procedures;
4. **Continue normal interactions** with the client without
   tipping off;
5. **Await MLRO assessment**.

### MLRO assessment

The MLRO:

- **Investigates** the internal disclosure;
- **Reviews** the client file, transaction history,
  counterparties;
- **Decides** whether to file a SAR externally;
- **Documents** the decision and reasons.

If the MLRO **doesn't file**, the trust officer's obligation is
discharged (the legal obligation is on the MLRO at that point,
unless the trust officer has additional independent grounds).

### After filing

If a SAR is filed:

- The trust officer **continues** normal client management;
- **Must not tip off** the client;
- **Records** post-SAR transactions for follow-up;
- **Awaits** any FIU consent decisions for specific transactions.

### Tipping off

This is a serious criminal offence:

- **Don't tell the client** that a SAR has been or might be
  filed;
- **Don't tell the client** their account is under review;
- **Don't share information** with the client that might tip off;
- **Carefully worded** explanations of delays / procedures.

The line can be hard — but the **default position** is **don't
share**.

### The trust officer's protection

Filing or escalating in good faith provides legal protection:

- **Statutory protection** from breach-of-confidence claims for
  the disclosure;
- **No civil liability** to the client for properly-handled
  disclosure;
- **Continuing client management** in line with FIU consent
  process.

## What to do next

For a specific situation:

1. **Document** what you observed and why it concerned you;
2. **Escalate immediately** to MLRO;
3. **Continue normal client engagement** without tipping off;
4. **Cooperate** with MLRO investigation;
5. **Document** further events as they unfold.

For programme readiness:

- **Know your MLRO** and the internal-escalation process;
- **Stay current** on AML training;
- **Read the AML / CFT Handbook** updates;
- **Discuss borderline cases** with the MLRO informally if
  needed.

## Pitfalls

- **Setting the bar too high**: waiting for certainty is wrong;
- **Failing to document**: contemporaneous notes are essential;
- **Discussing with colleagues** outside the proper channels:
  the only path is to the MLRO;
- **Tipping off** by careless explanation to the client.
