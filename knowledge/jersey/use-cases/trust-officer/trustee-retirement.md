# Trustee Retirement — Indemnities and Release

## The question

You (as outgoing trustee) want to retire and hand over to a
successor trustee. What indemnities and releases should you
seek?

## The short answer

A retiring trustee should obtain:

- **Release** from the incoming trustee for past acts as
  trustee;
- **Indemnity** from the incoming trustee (and ideally from the
  trust assets) for **future claims** relating to the trustee's
  period of service;
- **Indemnity for past liabilities** that may surface later
  (tax assessments, beneficiary claims);
- **Right of access** to trust records to defend against future
  claims.

A **deed of retirement and indemnity** is the standard
instrument.

## The proper answer

### The basic position

A retiring trustee:

- Ceases to be trustee from the **effective date** of
  retirement;
- **Remains liable** for breaches committed while in office
  (subject to limitation periods);
- Hands over **trust property** and **trust records** to the
  successor;
- Loses **active access** to the trust unless preserved by the
  retirement deed.

### The retirement deed

A typical deed of retirement and indemnity provides for:

#### Release

- **Outgoing trustee** is released from claims by the
  **incoming trustee** (and, where competent, beneficiaries)
  for past acts within scope of office;
- **Subject to exceptions** for fraud, wilful misconduct, and
  similar.

#### Indemnity

- **Incoming trustee** indemnifies the outgoing trustee against
  third-party claims;
- Indemnity is **typically out of the trust fund** (an
  "indemnity from the fund" not personal to the new trustee);
- Includes **tax liabilities** that may arise after retirement
  relating to the trustee's period of service;
- Includes **legal costs** of defending future claims.

#### Access to records

- The outgoing trustee retains a **right to inspect** trust
  records to mount defences to future claims;
- The incoming trustee agrees to **preserve records** for at
  least the limitation period.

### Beneficiary involvement

- **Beneficiary consents** may be required if the deed releases
  the outgoing trustee from claims;
- Where minors / unborn beneficiaries exist, **Court approval**
  under Article 47 (variation) may be sought to bind them;
- Without beneficiary release, beneficiaries can still sue the
  outgoing trustee for past breaches.

### Limitation periods

Even without express release, beneficiary claims are limited by
**Article 57 of the Trusts Law**:

- **3 years** from constructive knowledge;
- Some exceptions for **fraud** and **property still in trustee
  hands**.

So the **outer-bound exposure** of the outgoing trustee is
limited even without comprehensive release.

### Practical points

- **Outgoing trustee fees** outstanding should be addressed in
  the deed;
- **Pending litigation** (if any) — clarify whether outgoing
  trustee continues to manage or transfers;
- **Underlying-company directorships** held by the trustee or
  its officers — separate resignation steps;
- **Bank mandates and signatories** — operational handover
  steps.

## What to do next

1. **Identify the successor trustee** and confirm willingness
   to accept;
2. **Engage Jersey counsel** to draft the retirement deed;
3. **Negotiate** release, indemnity, and access terms;
4. **Obtain** beneficiary consents where appropriate;
5. **Consider** Court application if beneficiaries cannot
   consent;
6. **Sign and execute** the retirement deed;
7. **Operationally hand over** records, bank mandates, etc.;
8. **Retain** copies of records for defence purposes.

## Pitfalls

- **Inadequate indemnity**: a successor-trustee personal
  indemnity is worthless if the successor has no assets;
  indemnity from the trust fund is what matters;
- **Missing beneficiary consents**: future beneficiary claims
  can revive issues thought resolved;
- **Records-access defect**: without retained access, the
  outgoing trustee can't defend later claims effectively;
- **Tax-assessment surprise**: tax liabilities can emerge years
  after retirement; indemnity must cover this.
