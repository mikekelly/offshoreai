# CDD on a Foundation — What's Different from a Trust?

## The question

You're asked to act as **qualified member** on a Jersey
foundation council. CDD obligations mirror those for a trust
client, but with **structural differences** because a foundation
is a body corporate, not a relationship. What's different?

## The short answer

Foundation CDD is structured similarly to trust CDD but with
**different parties**:

- **Founder** (corresponds to settlor) — full CDD;
- **Council members** — CDD on each individual member;
- **Guardian** — CDD;
- **Beneficiaries / class of objects** — identified and CDD on
  principal beneficiaries;
- **Source of foundation property** — equivalent to source of
  trust property;
- **Public charter** is on the registry; **private regulations**
  must be obtained.

The **public-private split** is the key structural difference.

## The proper answer

### Founder

The founder is the **establishing party** — equivalent to a
trust settlor:

- **Full identification** and verification;
- **Source of wealth** and **source of funds** for endowment;
- **PEP screening**;
- **Tax residence** for CRS / FATCA;
- **Purpose** of the foundation.

### Council members

Each council member needs CDD:

- Including the **qualified member** (your firm, but verified as
  part of own KYC);
- **External council members** (founder's representatives,
  family members, professional advisers);
- Standard individual CDD on each;
- **PEP screening** on each.

### Guardian

The guardian needs CDD:

- The guardian has **statutory standing** to enforce the
  foundation;
- **Independent of the council** typically;
- **Important to identify** because of the supervisory role.

### Beneficiaries / objects

Foundations may have:

- **Named beneficiaries** (CDD on each);
- **Class beneficiaries** (identify principal members);
- **Charitable / purpose objects** (no beneficiaries — but
  identify the **purpose**).

For non-charitable purpose foundations (analogous to Article 12
purpose trusts), the **purpose** must be **clear and
documented**.

### The charter / regulations split

A distinctive feature:

- **Charter** is **public** — registered with the JFSC;
- **Regulations** are **private** — internal operational rules;
- Both are needed for CDD;
- **Always obtain** a copy of the regulations.

The regulations may contain:

- **Beneficial-interest** detail not in the charter;
- **Discretionary-distribution** rules;
- **Council governance** detail;
- **Amendment procedures**.

### Source of foundation property

Equivalent to source of trust property:

- The **initial endowment** — what was contributed to set up the
  foundation;
- **Subsequent additions** — who contributed when;
- **Beneficial origin** of the property — same SoW analysis as
  trusts.

### Purpose

Foundations have **specific purposes** in their charter:

- **Charitable** purposes (most generous regulatory treatment);
- **Family / private** purposes;
- **Mixed** purposes;
- **Commercial / structural** purposes (orphan SPV, PTC holding).

The purpose should be **commercially / personally
reasonable** — not pure tax avoidance or asset shielding.

### Differences from trust CDD

| Aspect | Trust | Foundation |
|---|---|---|
| Identifier of the parties | Trust deed | Charter + regulations |
| Public information | None registered | Charter registered, council members named |
| "Settlor" equivalent | Settlor | Founder |
| Fiduciary | Trustee | Council |
| Supervisory party | Protector (optional) | Guardian (often mandatory) |
| Beneficiaries' rights | Schmidt-Rosewood | Limited / per regulations |
| CDD complication | Hidden beneficiaries in class | Some council info public |

### Ongoing CDD

Same principles as for trusts:

- **Refresh** at intervals;
- **Update** on material changes;
- **Trigger reviews** on adverse events;
- **EDD** for PEPs, high-risk jurisdictions, complex structures.

## What to do next

For a new foundation engagement:

1. **Obtain** the proposed charter and regulations;
2. **Identify** founder, council members, guardian, beneficiaries
   or objects;
3. **Conduct** CDD on each individual;
4. **Verify** source of foundation property;
5. **Risk-assess** and apply EDD if needed;
6. **Document** the CDD file;
7. **Obtain** senior approval;
8. **Set up** ongoing monitoring.

## Pitfalls

- **Treating charter as sufficient**: must obtain regulations;
- **Council-member-only CDD**: must also CDD founder, guardian,
  and beneficiaries / objects;
- **Charitable foundation laxity**: don't relax CDD because
  "it's charitable" — verify the charitable status;
- **Public-information complacency**: just because the charter
  is public doesn't reduce CDD on the founder.
