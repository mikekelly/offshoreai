# Beneficiary Suing the Trust Abroad — Is Article 9 a Defence?

## The question

A beneficiary has commenced proceedings against the trustee in
a **foreign court** seeking trust assets or relief. Can the
trustee rely on the **Article 9 firewall** of the Trusts
(Jersey) Law 1984 as a defence?

## The short answer

Article 9 is a **Jersey-law rule** about **what law applies**
to Jersey-trust questions — it is not directly a defence in a
foreign court. But it has several practical effects:

- The trustee can **resist foreign jurisdiction** on the basis
  that Jersey is the proper forum;
- The trustee can **stay** Jersey proceedings pending foreign
  outcome (rarely advised);
- Jersey will **not recognise** foreign judgments that violate
  Article 9 principles;
- The trustee in Jersey has a **duty to comply with Jersey
  law** — including Article 9 — regardless of foreign orders.

## The proper answer

### What Article 9 says (recap)

Questions about Jersey trusts are determined by **Jersey law**,
regardless of:

- Settlor's domicile / residence;
- Asset situs;
- Forum hearing the dispute.

Foreign **forced heirship, matrimonial regime, and creditor
recovery** rules are not applied to Jersey trusts even if a
foreign court would otherwise apply them.

### Practical responses

#### 1. Challenge foreign jurisdiction

The first practical step in a foreign-court proceeding is
typically:

- **Argue** that the foreign court lacks jurisdiction or that
  Jersey is the **forum non conveniens**;
- **Rely on** any **jurisdiction clause** in the trust deed
  selecting Jersey courts;
- **Cite** *Crociani* and similar authorities supporting
  Jersey-court jurisdiction over Jersey-trust matters.

Outcome varies by forum. **English Family Division** for
matrimonial finance is famously resistant to jurisdictional
deflection. **Commercial-court** proceedings are often more
receptive.

#### 2. Don't submit to foreign jurisdiction

If the trustee **submits** to foreign jurisdiction, that has
serious implications:

- The trustee may be **bound** by the foreign judgment;
- The trustee's defence becomes harder under Article 9 in
  Jersey;
- Trust assets become exposed.

Carefully **calibrated participation** — appearing for
jurisdictional argument without conceding to merits — is often
the approach.

#### 3. Apply for Jersey directions

In parallel:

- **Article 51 application** in the Royal Court for directions
  on how to handle the foreign proceedings;
- **Court guidance** on whether to participate, whether to
  defend, on what terms;
- **Cost protection** under Beddoe.

The Royal Court's directions give the trustee Jersey-law
protection regardless of the foreign court's view.

#### 4. Refuse to honour foreign judgments

If a foreign judgment is rendered against the trust contrary to
Article 9:

- The trustee can **resist enforcement** in Jersey;
- The Royal Court will typically **refuse to recognise** a
  judgment that violates Article 9 principles;
- The trustee's duty under Jersey law is to **the trust as
  Jersey-law governed**.

### The personal-pressure problem

The trustee or its officers may face personal pressure abroad:

- **Contempt sanctions** for non-compliance with foreign orders;
- **Travel restrictions** on personnel;
- **Asset seizure** in foreign jurisdictions where the trustee
  has assets.

These personal-pressure mechanisms are how foreign courts often
effectively bypass Article 9 — the trustee may comply with the
foreign order to protect personnel even though the trust
position is defended.

The Royal Court has acknowledged this in cases like *Re B
Trust* and others — the firewall is **strong but not absolute
in practice**.

## What to do next

1. **Engage Jersey counsel immediately** on receipt of foreign
   proceedings;
2. **Engage foreign counsel** in the foreign jurisdiction;
3. **Plan** the jurisdictional response — challenge or
   participate-without-submitting;
4. **Apply for Article 51 directions** for Jersey-court
   guidance;
5. **Apply for Beddoe protection** for legal costs;
6. **Notify the JFSC** if the matter is material to the
   trustee's regulatory standing;
7. **Document** the trustee's reasoning at every step.

## Pitfalls

- **Treating Article 9 as a direct shield**: it controls Jersey
  law, not foreign-court behaviour;
- **Submitting to foreign jurisdiction inadvertently**:
  appearance for any merit purpose can be deemed submission;
- **Inadequate cost provision**: cross-border trust litigation
  is expensive;
- **Beneficiary leakage**: a beneficiary on both sides of the
  dispute can be a source of pressure / information leak.
