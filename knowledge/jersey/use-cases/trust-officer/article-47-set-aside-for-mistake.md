# "We made a mistake on a previous distribution / appointment. Can we undo it?"

## The short answer

In many cases, yes. Jersey has a **statutory mistake regime** under
**Articles 47B to 47H** of the [Trusts (Jersey) Law 1984][tjl],
introduced by the **Trusts (Amendment No. 6) (Jersey) Law 2013**. The
Royal Court can **declare voidable** a transfer or other disposition
of property to a trust, or the **exercise of a fiduciary power** in
relation to a trust, where it was made under a **qualifying mistake**.
Once voidable, the Court can set the transaction aside, with effect
typically *ab initio*, restoring the parties to where they would have
been but for the mistake.

A common — and successful — application is **set-aside for an
unforeseen tax consequence**, for example a UK or US tax charge that
the settlor or trustee did not know about and would have planned
around had they known.

The Court will apply a **three-limb test** (per *S & T Trusts* and the
authorities following it):

1. **Was there a mistake** (within the wide Article 47B definition)?
2. **Would the disposition or power-exercise have been made
   "but for" the mistake**?
3. **Is the mistake so serious** that it would be **just** for the
   Court to make a declaration?

([Viberts — S & T Trusts][viberts]; [Carey Olsen — No re-writing
history][co])

---

## What counts as a "mistake"

Article 47B of the Trusts Law defines mistake broadly. It covers a
mistake as to:

- the **effect** of a transaction,
- its **consequences** (including its tax consequences),
- any **advantage** the person believed they would gain from the
  transaction, and
- any **fact existing before or at the time of** the disposition or
  power-exercise.

([Mondaq — Mistake in Jersey Trust Law][mondaq]; [Lexology —
Setting aside subsequent transfers (Ogier)][lexology])

Crucially, **the *manner* of the mistake is irrelevant** under Jersey
law. The Royal Court has held that, under Articles 47C–H, the Court is
to decide whether there was *any* mistake within the wide statutory
definition; it does **not** apply the English law distinction in
*Pitt v Holt* [2013] UKSC 26 between a "mistake" and "mere
inadequate deliberation" or "mere ignorance". A Jersey mistake claim
is therefore **easier to make out** than its English counterpart.
([Carey Olsen][co]; [Viberts][viberts])

This Jersey/English divergence is the policy reason an English
adviser will sometimes prefer a Jersey-law trust where the principal
substantive risk to be hedged is future application of a tax rule the
adviser cannot yet see clearly.

## Mistake regarding **the trust itself** vs mistake regarding a
**transfer into the trust** vs mistake regarding **exercise of a power**

The Trusts Law treats three different kinds of mistake separately:

| Mistake about | Article | Effect |
|---|---|---|
| The validity of the trust as a whole (the settlor's intention to settle at all) | **Article 11** | Court may declare the trust invalid (or partially invalid) on grounds of mistake. |
| A subsequent **transfer or disposition of property into** an otherwise valid trust | **Article 47E** | Court may declare the transfer voidable; setting it aside returns the property to the transferor. |
| **Exercise of a fiduciary power** in relation to a trust (e.g., a trustee's appointment of capital, a protector's consent) | **Article 47G** (and accompanying provisions) | Court may declare the exercise voidable; setting it aside reverses the action. |

([Mondaq][mondaq]) Choosing the right Article is the threshold
question — pleading the wrong one is the most common pitfall.

## When the Court will / will not grant relief — practical pattern

The Royal Court has set aside transactions in a wide range of fact
patterns:

- **Tax-driven mistakes** — settlor did not know a settlement would
  trigger an immediate inheritance tax or capital gains tax charge in
  another jurisdiction. ([Viberts][viberts])
- **Unforeseen second-order consequences** — e.g., a distribution
  causing the loss of a foreign residency or other tax status.
- **Mistakes about facts existing at the time** — e.g., the existence
  of a creditor's claim, the value of the underlying asset, the
  meaning of a foreign-law concept.

The Court has *declined* relief where:

- The applicant could not show that the disposition **would not have
  been made** but for the mistake (limb 2 fails). ([Collas Crill —
  M Settlement][m]).
- The mistake was a **commercial misjudgement** rather than a mistake
  about effect, consequences or fact.
- The relief sought was **partial rescission** of a transaction in a
  way the Court considered substantively a re-writing of history
  rather than an unwinding. ([Collas Crill — M Settlement][m]; [Carey
  Olsen — No re-writing history][co])

The recent **In re M Settlement** decision is a useful negative
authority on these limits — read it before assuming a partial unwind
will be granted.

## Effect of an Article 47 set-aside

A successful declaration under Articles 47C–H typically:

- **Voids the transaction *ab initio***, so the property returns to
  the transferor (Article 47E) or the position immediately before the
  power was exercised is restored (Article 47G).
- **Does not** make the trust itself void (unless an Article 11
  application is also made and granted).
- **Does not** automatically purge foreign-law tax consequences that
  have already crystallised — relief turns on whether the foreign
  revenue authority recognises the Jersey set-aside as restoring the
  *status quo ante*. Some recognise it (HMRC has historically given
  effect to a Jersey rescission); some do not. Take cross-border tax
  advice.

## What to do — checklist

1. **Identify the transaction(s)** to be challenged. Date them.
   Identify which Article (11, 47E, 47G) is in play.
2. **Identify the alleged mistake**, framed against Article 47B's
   four limbs (effect / consequences / advantage / fact).
3. **Gather contemporaneous evidence** of the trustee's or settlor's
   state of mind at the time — file notes, board minutes, e-mail
   correspondence with advisers, the original advice given.
4. **Test the three-limb test** internally:
   (i) mistake; (ii) but-for; (iii) seriousness.
5. **Identify cross-border consequences** of an unwind — does the
   foreign revenue authority give effect to the rescission? Will the
   foreign court enforce the Royal Court order?
6. **Consider Article 51 alternative**. Sometimes the appropriate
   relief is not set-aside but a **direction** under Article 51
   blessing a remedial step. See
   [distribution-decisions-and-court-blessing.md](./distribution-decisions-and-court-blessing.md)
   and
   [`trusts/article-51-directions.md`](../../trusts/article-51-directions.md).
7. **Issue Representation** in the Royal Court (Samedi Division)
   joining as respondents the persons affected — including, where
   relevant, the trustee that took the impugned decision, the
   beneficiaries, and any third party that received property under
   the impugned transaction.
8. **Costs**. The application is typically a friendly proceeding and
   trustee costs come from the trust fund; the *applicant settlor*
   in a personal-mistake case bears their own costs. Confirm before
   filing.

## Pitfalls

- **Article 47 was renumbered.** "Article 47" historically referred
  to the older variation jurisdiction (which still exists at Article
  47, with Article 47A covering charitable/non-charitable purposes).
  The **modern mistake regime** is at **Articles 47B–47H**, inserted
  by the 2013 amendment. Citing "Article 47" alone in a brief is
  ambiguous; cite the sub-Article.
- **Pitt v Holt is *not* Jersey law.** Some English advisers default
  to the *Pitt v Holt* test. Jersey is wider; you may be over-
  qualifying your client's case if you import the English narrowing.
  ([Carey Olsen][co])
- **Lost-tax-advantage** as the only "mistake" is sometimes not
  enough — the Royal Court has been reluctant to treat the loss of a
  hoped-for *future* tax advantage (as opposed to the suffering of an
  unforeseen *charge*) as a qualifying mistake. ([Walkers — Recent
  mistake cases][walkers])
- **Partial unwinds.** The Court is wary of being asked to re-write
  history. Frame the relief as unwinding the impugned step in full,
  not as a selective rewrite. ([Collas Crill — M Settlement][m])
