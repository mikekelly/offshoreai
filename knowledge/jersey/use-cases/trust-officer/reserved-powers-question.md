# Can the Settlor Reserve [X] Power Without Invalidating the Trust?

## The question

The settlor wants to **retain** a power — revoke, vary, direct
investments, appoint trustees, change beneficiaries, or
something else. Will the reserved power **invalidate the trust**
or expose it to sham challenge?

## The short answer

**Article 9A** of the Trusts (Jersey) Law 1984 says that a trust
is **not invalidated** by the settlor reserving specified
powers, including the power to **revoke or vary** the trust,
**add or remove beneficiaries**, **direct investments**,
**appoint or remove trustees** (including a corporate
trustee-protector), and **be a beneficiary**.

So the answer for most reserved powers is **yes, the settlor
can reserve it**. But:

- A power **so extensive** that the trustee has no real
  fiduciary role can still be a **sham**;
- **Foreign tax characterisation** may be adverse (US grantor
  trust, UK transfer-of-asset-abroad regime);
- Article 9A doesn't protect a trust that **never was a trust**.

## The proper answer

### Powers expressly permitted by Article 9A

Article 9A(2) gives a non-exhaustive list of reservable powers:

- **Revoke or vary** the trust (in whole or part);
- **Direct or veto trustee decisions** on investment, management,
  distribution;
- **Appoint or remove** trustees, protectors, enforcers;
- **Add or remove beneficiaries**;
- **Change the proper law** of the trust;
- **Be a beneficiary**;
- **Hold an office** as protector, enforcer, or otherwise.

### The sham line

Even with Article 9A, a trust can be a **sham** where:

- The **trustee had no real intention** of acting as a
  fiduciary;
- The **trust documentation does not reflect** the parties'
  actual intentions;
- The parties **intended throughout** for the assets to remain
  the settlor's property.

Jersey case law (**Esteem**, **MacKinnon v Regent Trust**)
applies the **Snook** sham test: a common intention to deceive
that the documents do not reflect.

### Foreign tax characterisation

Reserved-powers trusts often have **adverse foreign tax
characterisation**:

- **US** — a settlor's power to revoke makes the trust a
  **grantor trust** (all income taxed to the settlor);
- **UK** — a settlor-interested trust can trigger **transfer of
  asset abroad** rules and other anti-avoidance;
- **Civil-law jurisdictions** may treat the trust as having no
  effect at all.

Always model the foreign tax position **before** building in
reserved powers.

### The Jersey-law conclusion

For Jersey-law-validity purposes: **reserved powers are fine**
under Article 9A, even very broad ones.

The questions worth asking are:

1. **Sham**: Is there genuine trustee involvement, or is this a
   nominee arrangement dressed up as a trust?
2. **Foreign tax**: Has the foreign-tax position been modelled
   and accepted?
3. **Practical operability**: Can the trustee actually act, or
   does the settlor's power make the trustee's role
   meaningless?

## What to do next

1. **Identify** the specific powers the settlor wants;
2. **Confirm** they fit within Article 9A;
3. **Model** foreign-tax consequences with home-jurisdiction
   advice;
4. **Document** the trust deed clearly;
5. **Confirm** with the trustee that they retain a real
   fiduciary role.

## Pitfalls

- **Reserved revocation + sole beneficiary**: this is the
  classic sham profile — be cautious;
- **No-trustee-discretion clauses**: powers so extensive that
  the trustee never decides anything risk sham reclassification;
- **Tax surprise**: clients sometimes insist on broad reserved
  powers without realising the tax cost.
