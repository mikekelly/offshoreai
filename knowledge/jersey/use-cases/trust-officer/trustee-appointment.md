# Appointment of a New Trustee — Process

## The question

A new trustee needs to be appointed — to replace a retired,
removed, or deceased trustee, or to add to the existing trustees.
What's the process?

## The short answer

The route depends on the trust deed:

1. **Deed power**: most trust deeds give the **settlor, a
   protector, or appointor** a power to appoint trustees;
2. **Statutory power**: where no deed power exists or the
   power-holder cannot act, **Article 17** of the Trusts (Jersey)
   Law 1984 gives statutory appointment power to specified
   persons;
3. **Court appointment**: the Royal Court can appoint trustees
   on application where other routes are unavailable or
   inappropriate.

The appointment must be **by deed** (typically) and the new
trustee must **accept the office** in writing.

## The proper answer

### Deed-based appointment

Modern Jersey trust deeds commonly give:

- **Settlor** a power to appoint trustees (often subject to
  conditions, sometimes terminating on settlor's death);
- **Protector** a power to appoint trustees (often the most
  durable power);
- **Existing trustees** a power to appoint additional trustees
  in some structures;
- **Other named appointors** under bespoke arrangements.

The deed will specify:

- **Who** holds the power;
- **Form** of exercise (deed, written instrument, etc.);
- **Conditions** of appointment (consents, eligibility
  criteria);
- **Whether** the appointor can also be the trustee.

### Article 17 — Statutory power

Where the deed is silent or the power-holder is unable / unwilling:

- **Article 17** allows specified persons (continuing trustees,
  personal representatives of the last surviving trustee, etc.)
  to appoint a replacement;
- **In writing**, by deed;
- Subject to the requirements of the trust.

### Court appointment

The Royal Court can appoint trustees:

- **Article 20** and **Article 51** and inherent jurisdiction
  combine to give wide power;
- Used where: deed power can't be exercised, dispute about
  identity, beneficiary application, foreign-court order, etc.;
- **Court appointment** carries Court endorsement — useful in
  contested situations.

### Eligibility of new trustee

The new trustee must:

- Be **of full age and capacity**;
- Not be **disqualified** by the trust deed;
- For a **regulated TCB context** — be JFSC-licensed for trust
  company business;
- **Accept** the office in writing;
- Provide **CDD documentation** to the existing trustee /
  service provider.

### Trustee residence

Subtle but important:

- **At least one Jersey-resident trustee** is typically required
  if the trust intends to retain Jersey-law governance and
  Jersey-resident character;
- A wholly **foreign-trustee** Jersey trust risks
  characterisation issues;
- Most Jersey trusts have a **regulated Jersey corporate
  trustee** as the primary or sole trustee.

### Deed of appointment

Standard appointment deeds include:

- **Identity** of appointor, retiring / removed trustee (if
  any), and new trustee;
- **Vesting** of trust property in the new trustee
  (automatic under Jersey law in most cases, but often
  documented);
- **Acceptance** by the new trustee;
- **Indemnity** terms (if combined with retirement);
- **Effective date**.

### After appointment

Operational steps:

- **Update** trust register / trustee records;
- **Update** bank mandates and signatories;
- **Notify** counterparties (banks, custodians, fund managers);
- **Update** AML / CFT and CRS / FATCA classifications;
- **Notify** the JFSC if the appointment changes a registered
  TCB's key persons.

## What to do next

1. **Identify** the appointment route (deed power / statutory /
   Court);
2. **Confirm** the appointor's authority;
3. **Identify** and confirm acceptance of the new trustee;
4. **Confirm** new trustee's regulatory standing (where TCB);
5. **Draft** the deed of appointment;
6. **Execute** and effect;
7. **Operationally implement** the change.

## Pitfalls

- **Defective appointment**: missing required consents or wrong
  appointor invalidates;
- **Foreign-resident-only trustees**: can compromise Jersey-
  trust characterisation;
- **No JFSC TCB licence** for trustee acting commercially:
  regulatory breach;
- **Inadequate handover**: gaps in records / authority leave
  the new trustee exposed.
