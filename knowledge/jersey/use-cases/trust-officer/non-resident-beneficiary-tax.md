# Non-Resident Beneficiary — Is the Trust Caught by Jersey Tax?

## The question

Your trust's beneficiaries are all **non-Jersey-resident**.
What Jersey tax applies (if anything) on:

- Trust income;
- Distributions to the beneficiaries;
- Realised capital gains within the trust?

## The short answer

Generally **little to no Jersey tax**:

- **No Jersey income tax** on foreign-source income of a trust
  with non-resident beneficiaries;
- **No Jersey withholding** on distributions to non-resident
  beneficiaries;
- **No Jersey CGT** on capital gains;
- **Schedule A** income (Jersey real estate) is the principal
  Jersey-tax exposure of trusts.

The Jersey tax-friendliness is a key driver of Jersey's
position as an offshore trust jurisdiction for international
families. Foreign tax in the beneficiary's home jurisdiction is
a different matter.

## The proper answer

### The basic position

Jersey income tax is a **schedular system** charging:

- **Jersey-source income** (Schedule A property; certain Schedule
  D categories);
- **Worldwide income** of **Jersey-resident persons**.

A trust with non-Jersey-resident beneficiaries:

- Is **not a Jersey-resident person** in the personal-tax sense;
- Has **no Jersey income tax** on foreign-source income;
- Is **not subject** to capital gains tax (Jersey has none).

### Distribution mechanics

For distributions to non-resident beneficiaries:

- **No Jersey tax** on the distribution at trust level;
- **No Jersey withholding** to deduct;
- **No Jersey return** on the distribution by the trust;
- **Foreign tax** in beneficiary's home jurisdiction is for
  them to handle.

### Underlying-company position

If the trust holds Jersey companies:

- The companies are **Jersey-tax-resident** under Article 123;
- They pay zero-ten (0% for most);
- Dividends paid up to the trust are also generally 0%-rate
  under zero-ten;
- The **trust** receives those dividends without Jersey tax
  burden.

### Schedule A (Jersey real estate)

If the trust holds **Jersey real estate** directly (rather than
through a Jersey company):

- **Rental income** is **Schedule A** income;
- Taxed at 20% on net rental profits;
- The trust (or its trustees) is liable for the Jersey tax;
- A Jersey tax return covering the property income is required.

This is the **principal Jersey-tax exposure** of foreign-
beneficiary trusts.

### Schedule D specific cases

For most Schedule D income types:

- **Trading income** from Jersey activity (rare for trusts)
  would be Jersey-taxable;
- **Foreign-source** Schedule D income of a trust with non-
  resident beneficiaries is generally outside Jersey tax.

### Article 81 distributions regime

The Income Tax Law's distribution regime (Article 81 onwards)
targets **Jersey-resident shareholders** of zero-rate Jersey
companies — it does not extend to non-resident beneficiaries
of trusts holding the same Jersey companies.

### Practical implications

For a typical Jersey trust with foreign-resident family
beneficiaries:

- **Annual administration** — CRS / FATCA / beneficial-ownership
  filings;
- **No Jersey tax return** unless Jersey real estate is held
  directly;
- **Jersey companies underneath** file their own returns at 0%
  rate;
- **Foreign tax** in beneficiaries' jurisdictions is the active
  consideration.

This is **why Jersey is attractive** for international family
trust planning.

## What to do next

For each trust:

1. **Identify** the residence of all beneficiaries;
2. **Identify** any Jersey-source income (especially Jersey
   real estate);
3. **File** Schedule A returns if Jersey real estate income;
4. **Ensure** underlying-company returns filed;
5. **Provide** statements to beneficiaries for their foreign-
   tax filings;
6. **Maintain** CRS / FATCA / beneficial-ownership filings.

## Pitfalls

- **Missing Schedule A** on Jersey real estate income;
- **Assuming no Jersey tax means no tax**: foreign tax in
  beneficiary jurisdictions is often the larger exposure;
- **Underlying-Jersey-company-Pillar-Two** for large groups: if
  the structure is part of a Pillar-Two group, the 15% top-up
  may apply at the underlying-company level;
- **Forgetting** that beneficiaries may need detailed source
  information for their own returns.
