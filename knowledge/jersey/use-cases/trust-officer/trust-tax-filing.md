# Does the Trust Itself Have a Jersey Tax Return?

## The question

You administer a Jersey trust. Does the trust have to file a
**Jersey tax return**? On what income? At what rate?

## The short answer

A **Jersey trust** is **not a separate taxpayer** in the Jersey
income-tax system in the way a company is. The Jersey tax
position depends on the nature of the income and the residence
of the beneficiaries:

- **Income from Jersey sources** is generally taxed at the
  source (e.g. by a Jersey company under zero-ten);
- **Foreign-source income** of a trust with **non-Jersey-
  resident beneficiaries** is typically outside Jersey tax;
- **Jersey-resident beneficiaries** are taxed on their share /
  distribution.

**Trust-administration** filings (CRS, FATCA, beneficial-
ownership) are separate from income-tax filings and **are**
required.

## The proper answer

### The trust as a non-taxpayer

Unlike a Jersey company (which is a body corporate taxpayer
under Article 123), a **Jersey trust is not a body corporate**:

- It is a **relationship**, not an entity;
- It does **not** have separate tax personality;
- Its income is **looked through** to the relevant taxable
  party.

### Where Jersey tax can arise

#### Jersey-source income of the trust

If the trust earns income from Jersey sources (Jersey real
estate, Jersey-trading-company shares):

- The **source** is typically taxed (e.g. the Jersey company
  pays at zero-ten rate);
- The **trust itself** may have residual tax in limited cases
  (e.g. real-estate income — see Schedule A in ITL 1961).

#### Jersey-resident beneficiaries

If the trust **distributes** to a Jersey-resident beneficiary:

- The beneficiary is taxed on the distribution as their income;
- The character of the distribution (Jersey-source vs foreign-
  source, income vs capital) matters;
- The trustee provides a **statement** to the beneficiary for
  their return.

#### Jersey-resident trustee

The trustee is a separate **Jersey-tax-resident company**
(typically a TCB):

- Pays Jersey tax on its **fees** earned (zero-ten 0% rate for
  most TCBs);
- Is **not taxed** on trust income — that's the trust's, not the
  TCB's.

### When Jersey trust filings are required

#### Filings the trust itself makes

- **Schedule A** filings for Jersey real estate income, if any
  (the trust as proprietor);
- **Specific filings** for unusual income types as required.

#### Filings the trustee makes (on behalf of trust)

- **CRS report** for non-Jersey-resident reportable persons;
- **FATCA report** for US persons;
- **Beneficial-ownership** filings under the FS(DPI)(J)L 2020;
- **Specific filings** the trust may have because of underlying
  Jersey companies (whose returns the trustee oversees as
  director / shareholder).

### Foreign-tax-resident beneficiaries

For non-Jersey-resident beneficiaries:

- **No Jersey tax** on the distribution at the trust level
  (assuming no Jersey-source component);
- **Foreign tax** applies under the beneficiary's home
  jurisdiction;
- See [`distribution-uk-resident.md`](distribution-uk-resident.md)
  and [`distribution-us-person.md`](distribution-us-person.md)
  for jurisdiction-specific implications.

### Underlying Jersey companies

A common structure: Jersey trust holds shares in a Jersey
holding company. The Jersey company is the **primary taxpayer**:

- Files its own corporate tax return;
- Pays zero-ten (0% for most cases);
- Distributes dividends up to the trust.

The trustee oversees this filing through its **director
appointment** to the underlying company.

### Practical compliance

For a trust officer administering a typical Jersey trust:

- **Personal trust-level return**: rarely required;
- **CRS / FATCA**: annually through portal;
- **Beneficial-ownership** filings: maintain;
- **Underlying companies' returns**: ensure filed;
- **Beneficiary information**: provide for their home-
  jurisdiction filings.

## What to do next

For each trust under administration:

1. **Identify** the income types and sources;
2. **Map** which filings are required;
3. **File** annually as required;
4. **Coordinate** with underlying-company returns;
5. **Provide** statements to beneficiaries for their use.

## Pitfalls

- **Treating trust as a taxpayer**: it isn't, in the income-tax
  sense; but it does have its own administrative obligations;
- **Missing Schedule A** on Jersey real-estate income;
- **Missing CRS / FATCA**: these aren't strictly "income tax"
  but are core trust-administration filings;
- **Underlying-company filing failures**: the trustee is
  responsible for ensuring underlying-Jersey-company filings.
