# Subject Access Request from a Beneficiary — DPJL 2018 vs Schmidt

## The question

A beneficiary has issued a **subject access request (SAR)**
under the **Data Protection (Jersey) Law 2018** seeking personal
data the trustee holds. How does this interact with the *Schmidt
v Rosewood* trust-information regime?

## The short answer

The two regimes **overlap** but are **not coextensive**:

- **Schmidt-Rosewood**: discretion-based supervisory regime for
  **trust-administration information**;
- **DPJL 2018 SAR**: statutory right to **personal data** of
  the data subject (the beneficiary themselves);

A beneficiary cannot use a DPJL SAR to **circumvent** Schmidt
disclosure principles. Trust-administration information (letters
of wishes, internal trustee deliberations) generally remains
protected under Schmidt analysis even when sought via DPJL.

## The proper answer

### What DPJL gives

A data subject is entitled under DPJL 2018 to:

- **Information about** the data the trustee holds about them;
- **Access to** a copy of their personal data;
- **Information about** how that data is processed and shared;
- Subject to **exemptions** including legal privilege, third-
  party information protection, and **confidential references**.

### What Schmidt gives

Under *Schmidt v Rosewood* (and Jersey's *Re Internine* line):

- The Royal Court has **supervisory jurisdiction** over the
  trust;
- Beneficiaries can apply to the Court for **disclosure of
  trust documents** as part of that supervision;
- The Court applies **discretion**, balancing:
  - Beneficiary's interest in information;
  - Trustee's confidentiality obligations;
  - **Other beneficiaries' interests**;
  - Specifics of the document type (deed FOR disclosure; letters
    of wishes AGAINST).

### Where they overlap

The same beneficiary may have:

- **DPJL standing** as a data subject for their personal data;
- **Schmidt standing** as a beneficiary for trust-administration
  information.

The same document may contain:

- Personal data **of the beneficiary** (potentially DPJL-
  accessible);
- **Other beneficiaries' personal data** (DPJL third-party
  exemption);
- **Trustee deliberations** (Schmidt principles favour
  confidentiality);
- **Letters of wishes** (Schmidt presumption against
  disclosure).

### How to handle a DPJL SAR

A trustee receiving a DPJL SAR should:

1. **Acknowledge** the request and timeframe (typically 1 month
   under DPJL);
2. **Identify** what personal data of the requester the trustee
   holds;
3. **Apply DPJL exemptions** — third-party data, legal privilege,
   etc.;
4. **Apply Schmidt overlay** — even where DPJL would technically
   require disclosure, ask whether disclosure undermines trust
   administration;
5. **Provide** personal data the beneficiary is entitled to;
6. **Decline** or redact where DPJL exemptions apply;
7. **Document** the analysis.

### If beneficiary escalates

The beneficiary can:

- **Complain to the Office of the Information Commissioner**
  (OIC) for DPJL non-compliance;
- **Apply to the Royal Court** under Schmidt for trust-document
  disclosure.

In a Schmidt application, the Court will look at the substance
of what is sought — using DPJL as a workaround to get Schmidt-
protected information is **likely to be rejected**.

### The settled position

The Jersey Royal Court has signalled (in cases including
*Re S Trust* and similar) that DPJL is **not a route around
Schmidt** for trust information. The trustee should:

- **Comply with DPJL** on truly personal data;
- **Defend Schmidt confidentiality** on trust administration
  documents;
- **Document** the line drawn.

## What to do next

1. **Acknowledge** the request promptly;
2. **Engage** Jersey counsel familiar with DPJL and Schmidt;
3. **Map** what data the trustee holds and what categories it
   falls into;
4. **Apply** DPJL exemptions and Schmidt overlay;
5. **Respond** to the beneficiary with the disclosed material
   and explanation of redactions / non-disclosures;
6. **Document** the analysis for trustee defence.

## Pitfalls

- **Over-disclosure under DPJL** that compromises Schmidt
  protection for other beneficiaries;
- **Under-disclosure** that breaches DPJL and exposes to OIC
  enforcement;
- **Ignoring** the third-party-data exemption (other
  beneficiaries' data);
- **Failing to document** the reasoning — defence depends on a
  clear analytical trail.
