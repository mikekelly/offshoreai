# Non-Charitable Purpose Trust — Workable?

## The question

The settlor wants a trust **without beneficiaries** — perhaps
for an **orphan SPV**, a **PTC holding** structure, an
**asset-holding** vehicle, or a **family-governance** purpose.
Is a **non-charitable purpose trust** under Article 12 the right
tool?

## The short answer

**Yes**, Article 12 of the Trusts (Jersey) Law 1984 permits
**non-charitable purpose trusts** that are not subject to the
common-law beneficiary principle. The conditions:

- The purposes must be **certain, reasonable, and possible** to
  carry out;
- The purposes must **not be unlawful, immoral, or contrary to
  public policy**;
- An **enforcer** must be appointed to police the trust;
- The trust must be **created in writing**.

Common uses: orphan SPVs in structured finance, PTC share-
holding, family-governance vehicles.

## The proper answer

### What Article 12 changed

At common law, a private trust required **identifiable
beneficiaries** to enforce it (the beneficiary principle). A
non-charitable trust **with no beneficiaries** was void.

Article 12 expressly permits Jersey **non-charitable purpose
trusts** with no beneficiaries, provided an **enforcer** is
appointed.

### The enforcer

The enforcer is the **person with standing** to enforce the
trust:

- **Not a trustee**;
- **Not a beneficiary** (there are no beneficiaries by
  definition);
- Has **statutory standing** to bring proceedings to enforce
  the trust's purposes;
- Owes **fiduciary duties** to the purpose, not to any
  individual.

The enforcer can be:

- An **individual** (often professional);
- A **body corporate** (typically a service provider);
- **More than one** enforcer.

### Permitted purposes

Article 12 purposes can include:

- **Holding shares** of a PTC for the benefit of family-trust
  structures;
- **Holding shares of an orphan SPV** in a securitisation;
- **Operating a family-governance** mechanism;
- **Holding heritage assets** (art, real estate) with specified
  rules;
- **Carrying on a charitable-adjacent purpose** that doesn't
  meet strict charity tests.

### Restrictions

Purposes must be:

- **Certain** — clearly defined so the enforcer / Court can
  determine whether they are being carried out;
- **Reasonable** — not capricious or absurd;
- **Possible** — physically and legally achievable;
- **Not unlawful, immoral, or contrary to public policy**.

A purpose trust to "build a monument to me at any cost" might
fail the reasonableness limb. A purpose trust to "hold shares in
X PTC for the benefit of the Y family's trust structures" is
fine.

### Compared to foundation

Article 12 trust vs Jersey foundation as orphan / purpose
vehicle:

- **Foundation**: body corporate, public charter, more familiar
  to civil-law parties;
- **Article 12 trust**: trust relationship, private trust deed,
  more familiar to common-law parties.

Both serve orphan / purpose use cases. The choice often turns
on **counterparty preference** and **foreign-law recognition**.

## What to do next

1. **Confirm** the purposes can be drafted with certainty;
2. **Engage** Jersey counsel to draft the trust deed and
   identify suitable enforcer;
3. **Consider** which enforcer profile fits — professional
   independent, related-party, or service-provider entity;
4. **Document** the trust's intended operation in clear terms;
5. **Set up** ongoing administration including enforcer
   reporting / reviews.

## Pitfalls

- **Vague purposes**: "for the benefit of the family" is too
  vague — it's a purpose without specific definition;
- **Treating enforcer like beneficiary**: the enforcer has
  enforcement standing but not economic interest;
- **Forgotten enforcer**: a long-running trust whose enforcer
  has died / been dissolved is unenforceable — replacement
  mechanism essential.
