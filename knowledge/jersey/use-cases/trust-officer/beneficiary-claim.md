# Beneficiary Alleges Breach of Trust — Procedure

## The question

A beneficiary alleges the trustee has **breached duty** — wrong
investment, improper distribution, conflict of interest,
inadequate accounting. What's the procedure?

## The short answer

The beneficiary can:

- **Demand information** under their *Schmidt v Rosewood*
  rights to substantiate the allegation;
- **Apply to the Royal Court** for an account or for
  declarations and remedies;
- **Sue the trustee** directly for breach;
- **Apply for trustee removal** under Article 21 / inherent
  jurisdiction.

For the trustee, the priorities are:

1. **Engage legal counsel immediately**;
2. **Consider Beddoe protection** for legal costs from the fund;
3. **Preserve all records**;
4. **Avoid further actions** that could compound the alleged
   breach;
5. **Respond proportionately** rather than defensively.

## The proper answer

### The substantive cause of action

A breach-of-trust claim alleges that the trustee:

- **Failed to perform** a duty (act / decision required);
- **Performed a duty negligently** or improperly;
- **Acted outside powers** (ultra vires);
- **Took unauthorised personal benefit** (self-dealing /
  conflict);
- **Failed to maintain proper accounts**.

Remedies can include:

- **Equitable compensation** to restore the trust fund;
- **Account of profits** (where trustee benefited);
- **Removal** of the trustee;
- **Tracing** of misappropriated assets.

### The procedural starting point

The beneficiary typically:

- **Requests information** under Schmidt-Rosewood and Article
  29 to substantiate;
- **Writes a letter of claim** identifying the alleged breach;
- **Issues Order of Justice** if not resolved.

The trustee should:

- **Acknowledge** receipt and engage legal counsel;
- **Provide reasonable information** to enable assessment
  (subject to confidentiality from other beneficiaries);
- **Avoid** combative posture before facts are clear;
- **Document** the trustee position contemporaneously.

### Beddoe protection

A trustee facing or initiating litigation should seek **Beddoe
relief** — a Court order authorising the trustee to use trust
assets for legal costs:

- **Application by Representation**;
- **Court reviews** the strength of the trustee position and
  whether litigation is justified;
- If granted, **legal costs are paid from the fund** on the
  indemnity basis;
- If refused (rare), the trustee bears costs personally.

Beddoe protects the trustee from **personal cost exposure** in
defending the trust. It is usually sought **early** in the
process.

### Common scenarios

**Investment underperformance**:
- Generally not a breach per se — investment is a discretionary
  judgement;
- Becomes a breach if trustee **failed to take advice** or made
  decisions outside reasonable parameters.

**Improper distribution**:
- Distribution outside the class is a breach;
- Distribution within class but contested — usually defensible
  if proper process followed.

**Conflict of interest / self-dealing**:
- Strict liability — the trustee should not have benefited
  personally;
- Disclosure and beneficiary consent can cure some conflicts.

**Inadequate accounting**:
- Trustees must keep proper accounts;
- Beneficiaries can demand an account; failure to provide is
  itself a breach.

### Limitation

Jersey limitation rules apply (Article 57 of the Trusts Law):

- **3 years** from constructive knowledge for typical breach
  claims;
- **No limitation** for fraud / property still in trustee
  hands.

Some breach claims may be **out of time** — an important early
defensive consideration.

## What to do next

1. **Engage legal counsel** (specialist trust litigation);
2. **Notify** professional-indemnity insurer;
3. **Apply for Beddoe protection** for legal costs;
4. **Audit** the trustee record on the relevant decisions;
5. **Engage** with the beneficiary in good faith;
6. **Consider** alternative dispute resolution if appropriate;
7. **Defend** if necessary while keeping options open.

## Pitfalls

- **Combative early stance**: positions on day one are often
  regretted on day fifty when the facts are clearer.
- **No Beddoe protection**: large legal-cost exposure absent
  Court authorisation.
- **Trustee continuing to make decisions** while accused:
  consider pausing material decisions until the issue is
  resolved.
- **Communication leaks**: keep matters confidential to
  professional advisers.
