# Jersey Trust or Foundation — How to Choose?

## The question

Your client wants to establish a Jersey-based wealth-structuring
vehicle. Should it be a **Jersey trust** or a **Jersey
foundation**?

## The short answer

For most clients, the **Jersey trust** remains the default — it
has the deepest body of case law, the strongest **Article 9
firewall**, and broad international tax familiarity.

A **Jersey foundation** is the better choice when:

- The client has a **civil-law background** and is more
  comfortable with a body-corporate vehicle than a trust
  relationship;
- The structure benefits from a **public charter / private
  regulations** split;
- The vehicle is for **orphan / purpose** rather than
  beneficiary use (foundation has native non-beneficiary
  purpose capability);
- The **home-jurisdiction tax characterisation** of a
  foundation works better than a trust (rare but possible).

## The proper answer

### Conceptual comparison

| Feature | Trust | Foundation |
|---|---|---|
| Legal nature | Relationship — not a legal entity | Body corporate — own legal personality |
| Asset ownership | Trustee, in trust capacity | The foundation itself |
| Constituting party | Settlor (transfers assets to trustee) | Founder (endows the foundation) |
| Governance | Trustee(s), possibly Protector | Council + Guardian + Qualified Member |
| Beneficiaries | Have *Schmidt v Rosewood* supervisory jurisdiction; Article 29 disclosure | No automatic information rights (unless charter / regs grant) |
| Public document? | No (trust deed private) | Yes (charter is public) |
| Private document? | No statutory split | Yes (regulations are private) |
| Reserved powers | Article 9A — broad reservations possible | Charter / regulations can reserve broadly |
| Statutory firewall | Article 9 (very strong) | Foundations-Law equivalent (different scope) |
| Founder retained role | Possible via Article 9A | Can be council member or guardian |
| Common-law overlay | Substantial (Trusts Law not a codification) | Limited (more civil-law flavour) |

### Cultural and home-jurisdiction factors

- **UK / US / Commonwealth-domiciled settlors** typically prefer
  trusts (familiar mechanism, well-developed jurisprudence);
- **Civil-law-jurisdiction founders** (France, Germany, Italy,
  Switzerland, Latin America) often prefer foundations
  (familiar civil-law concept of *Stiftung* / *fondation*);
- **Asian families** vary — some prefer trusts following common-
  law influence, others prefer foundations.

### Tax characterisation matters

The **home-jurisdiction tax treatment** of the vehicle can be
the deciding factor:

- **UK IHT**: trust treatment is well-defined; foundation is
  newer and less predictable;
- **US tax**: trusts can be **grantor or non-grantor**;
  foundations are typically **corporate** in US characterisation
  (which may or may not be desired);
- **Continental Europe**: foundations often **avoid** trust-
  hostile rules (some civil-law jurisdictions don't recognise
  trusts but readily recognise foundations).

Always **model both vehicles** in the home jurisdiction
specifically.

### Specific use-case considerations

#### Family wealth-management vehicle (general)

- **Default to trust** unless specific factors suggest
  foundation.

#### Private Trust Company (PTC) holding structure

- **Foundation works well** — the foundation holds the PTC
  shares, provides governance oversight, sits orphan above the
  family-trust structure;
- Trust alternative: a **non-charitable purpose trust** under
  Article 12 of the Trusts Law holding the PTC shares.

#### Charitable / philanthropic vehicle

- **Both work**;
- **Foundation** if a body-corporate operating vehicle is
  needed;
- **Charitable trust** if the trustees structure suits;
- Modern Jersey charity registration sits over both.

#### Orphan SPV (structured finance)

- **Foundation** or **non-charitable purpose trust** both serve;
- Choice driven by **counterparty preference** and **foreign-
  jurisdiction recognition** of the structure.

#### Succession planning

- **Trust** if discretionary distributions to family members
  are central;
- **Foundation** if a **multi-generational governance** structure
  is needed beyond the founder's lifetime.

## What to do next

1. **Model tax characterisation** in the home jurisdiction(s)
   of the founder/settlor and beneficiaries.
2. **Consult the trust-officer / foundation-administrator** at
   the chosen Jersey service provider.
3. **Draft** the trust deed or foundation charter / regulations.
4. **CDD** through the service provider.
5. **Fund** the vehicle.
6. **Establish** ongoing administration arrangements.

## Pitfalls

- **Choosing on Jersey-law factors alone**: the home-
  jurisdiction tax treatment usually drives the answer.
- **Misjudging beneficiary information rights**: a foundation
  with no information rights for beneficiaries can be
  unexpectedly opaque from the beneficiaries' point of view.
- **Cultural mismatch**: a civil-law founder forced into a trust
  may not engage properly with the structure; conversely, a
  trust-familiar settlor may struggle with foundation mechanics.
