# Working with a Jersey Trust Officer

## The question

You're a family-office adviser. How do you work effectively
with the **Jersey trust officer** administering the family's
trust(s)?

## The short answer

Effective working with a Jersey trust officer requires:

- **Clear roles** — adviser advises; trustee decides;
- **Regular communication** — scheduled meetings, prompt
  responses;
- **Letters of wishes** from the settlor for trustee guidance;
- **Structured information flow** to support trustee decisions;
- **Respect for trustee discretion** even when family wants
  more control;
- **Cooperation on AML / regulatory** matters.

The trustee is **not** the family's employee — they have
fiduciary duty to the trust and beneficiaries. Working with
them on that basis produces better outcomes.

## The proper answer

### Roles

#### Family-office adviser

- **Advises** the family on wealth strategy;
- **Coordinates** across vehicles;
- **Communicates** family priorities to trustees;
- **Implements** family decisions on aspects within family
  authority.

#### Trust officer / trustee

- **Holds** trust assets in fiduciary capacity;
- **Makes** trustee decisions independently;
- **Considers** family wishes but isn't bound by them;
- **Manages** compliance, accounting, distributions.

#### Working effectively

- **Distinguish** family's role from trustee's role;
- **Don't expect** trustee to be a rubber stamp;
- **Don't override** trustee's fiduciary judgement.

### Communication cadence

Typical engagement:

- **Quarterly** meeting between family-office and trustee;
- **Monthly** report on trust accounts / activities;
- **Annual** comprehensive review;
- **Ad-hoc** consultation on specific decisions.

### Letters of wishes

The settlor (or surviving family principals) typically provide
**letters of wishes** to the trustee:

- **Non-binding** statements of family preference;
- **Guide** trustee discretion on distributions;
- **Refresh** as circumstances change;
- **Trustee considers** but is not bound;
- **Schmidt v Rosewood** principles apply — beneficiaries may
  seek disclosure but presumption is against (see
  [`../trust-officer/beneficiary-information-rights.md`](../trust-officer/beneficiary-information-rights.md)).

### Information to provide

To support trustee decisions:

- **Family circumstances** — health, employment, life events;
- **Beneficiary financial position** for assessing need;
- **Family priorities** for distribution / investment;
- **Foreign-tax implications** for proposed distributions;
- **Beneficiary requests** with appropriate context.

### Discretionary distributions

Distribution requests:

- **Submit** through proper channels (typically through family-
  office to trustee);
- **Provide context** for trustee consideration;
- **Allow time** for trustee review (often a board / committee
  meets quarterly);
- **Accept** trustee decisions even when disappointing;
- **Discuss** alternatives if a request is declined.

### Investment decisions

For trust investments:

- **Family input** on investment preferences;
- **Investment-manager engagement** typically separate from
  trustee but coordinated;
- **Trustee oversight** of overall investment strategy;
- **Periodic review** with family-office.

### Tax and regulatory

The trustee handles:

- **Trust-level tax** filings;
- **CRS / FATCA** reporting;
- **Beneficial-ownership** filings;
- **Regulatory** compliance;
- **AML / CFT** on family members.

The family-office:

- **Coordinates** with family-member tax advisers;
- **Provides** information for trustee's filings;
- **Supports** AML refresh cycles;
- **Aggregates** reports for family overview.

### Common friction points

#### Family wants distribution trustee won't approve

- **Discuss** the trustee's concerns;
- **Provide** additional information;
- **Consider** alternative approaches;
- **Respect** the trustee's ultimate decision;
- **Consider** Article 51 application if material disagreement.

#### Family wants control trustee won't cede

- **Trustee discretion** is genuine — not a courtesy;
- **Reserved powers** in deed may allow more control;
- **PTC structure** if family wants direct governance;
- **Don't pressure** trustee inappropriately.

#### Information requests / Schmidt issues

- **Beneficiary information rights** through trustee — not
  through family-office;
- **Coordinate** but respect trustee's role.

#### Pricing disagreements

- **Engagement-letter** terms govern;
- **Fee discussions** can be raised;
- **Re-tendering** is the ultimate sanction.

### Healthy working relationship

Indicators of a healthy relationship:

- **Mutual respect** between family-office and trustee;
- **Clear communication**;
- **Trustee adds value** beyond mechanical administration;
- **Family understands** and accepts fiduciary boundaries;
- **Issues resolved** at appropriate levels.

Indicators of trouble:

- **Surprise** distributions outside agreed framework;
- **Trustee inattention** to family priorities;
- **Family pressure** for non-fiduciary decisions;
- **Communication breakdowns**;
- **Frequent re-tendering** or trustee changes.

## What to do next

For an existing engagement:

- **Review** the working relationship periodically;
- **Address** issues proactively;
- **Maintain** professional communication;
- **Refresh** letters of wishes regularly.

For a new engagement:

- **Set expectations** at the outset;
- **Establish** communication cadence;
- **Document** roles and responsibilities;
- **Onboard** smoothly with proper information sharing.

## Pitfalls

- **Treating trustee as servant**: invites pushback or worse;
- **Over-pressuring** for specific outcomes;
- **Information starvation**: trustee can't decide well without
  context;
- **Bypass attempts** — going around the trustee to lawyers,
  banks, etc.
