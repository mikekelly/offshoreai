# Choosing a Jersey Trustee

## The question

The family is establishing a Jersey trust. Who should be the
**trustee** — a major trust company, a boutique TCB, a PTC, or
an individual?

## The short answer

Options:

- **Major trust company** (Carey Olsen / Mourant / Ogier-
  affiliated, Bedell / Walkers / similar TCB arms, IQ-EQ,
  Vistra, Sanne, etc.) — strong infrastructure, scale, but
  more institutional;
- **Boutique TCB** — more personal service, family-office feel,
  but smaller scale;
- **Family PTC** — direct family control with regulatory wrapper
  (see [`when-ptc.md`](./when-ptc.md));
- **Individual** — only for non-commercial single-trust
  arrangements; not common.

Key factors: **family scale**, **complexity**, **service level
needed**, **continuity requirements**, **family preference**.

## The proper answer

### Major trust company

Large Jersey trust companies — both standalone and law-firm-
affiliated — offer:

#### Strengths

- **Scale infrastructure** — risk, compliance, technology;
- **Multi-jurisdictional reach** (Jersey, Guernsey, Cayman, etc.);
- **Strong reputation** with foreign tax authorities and
  counterparties;
- **Continuity** through institutional structure;
- **Specialist teams** (PTC support, structured-finance, fund
  trustees);
- **Capacity** for complex / large trusts.

#### Weaknesses

- **Less personal** — relationship-officer turnover;
- **More expensive** (typically 0.5–1.5%+ of assets, scaled);
- **Process-driven** approach;
- **Family-specific knowledge** less embedded.

Suits: **complex / large** trusts, **multi-jurisdictional**
families, **structured-finance**, **fund trustees**.

### Boutique TCB

Smaller / mid-tier Jersey trust companies offer:

#### Strengths

- **Personal service** — senior people doing the work;
- **Relationship continuity**;
- **Flexibility** for family-specific needs;
- **Lower-cost** typically;
- **Boutique** feel and decision-making speed.

#### Weaknesses

- **Smaller infrastructure**;
- **Capacity** constraints for very large or complex trusts;
- **Risk concentration** — fewer parallel relationships;
- **Succession risk** if key person leaves.

Suits: **mid-sized family** trusts, **single-family wealth**,
**boutique service** preference.

### Family PTC

A family Private Trust Company:

#### Strengths

- **Direct family control**;
- **Family-specific knowledge** embedded in directors;
- **Continuity** across generations;
- **Coordinated** governance of multiple family trusts.

#### Weaknesses

- **Fixed costs** higher than a single-trust independent trustee
  arrangement;
- **Family-governance capacity** required;
- **Family-PTC exemption** must be maintained;
- **Regulatory exposure** of family directors.

Suits: **substantial multi-trust** families with **engaged
directors** (see [`when-ptc.md`](./when-ptc.md)).

### Individual trustee

An individual (typically a family member or family friend) acts
as trustee:

#### Strengths

- **Minimal cost**;
- **Direct family involvement**;
- **Personal commitment**.

#### Weaknesses

- **Regulatory exposure** if commercially provided (may be TCB
  unintentionally);
- **Continuity** dependent on individual;
- **Independence concerns**;
- **Lack of professional infrastructure**;
- **Difficulty** with AML, CRS, FATCA, and other regulatory
  obligations.

Rarely appropriate for **commercial** trust arrangements.

### Factors in choosing

#### Family scale

- Small / simple family: boutique TCB or smaller trustee;
- Medium: boutique or major trust company;
- Large: major trust company or PTC.

#### Asset complexity

- Simple investments: any option;
- Real estate, operating businesses, art, complex structures:
  trust company with relevant expertise;
- Funds, structured finance: specialist provider.

#### Service expectations

- High-touch relationship: boutique or PTC;
- Institutional service: major trust company.

#### Cost

- PTC: high fixed cost, scales well;
- Major trust company: typically higher per-asset fees;
- Boutique: often lower per-asset fees;
- PTC vs trust company crosses at **£20–50m+** assets typically.

#### Family preference

- Engaged family: PTC may suit;
- Hands-off family: independent trust company;
- Cultural fit: matters more than spec sheets.

### Practical evaluation

When evaluating candidates:

- **Site visit** to the trust company / TCB office;
- **Meet** the proposed relationship team;
- **Discuss** specific family scenarios;
- **References** from existing clients;
- **Pricing** comparison;
- **Service-level commitments**;
- **Continuity** plans;
- **Specialist expertise** (international families, specific
  asset types).

### After choosing

- **Engagement letter** clearly setting out scope;
- **Service-level expectations**;
- **Pricing**;
- **Exit terms** for future flexibility;
- **Relationship cadence** (meetings, reporting).

## What to do next

For trustee selection:

1. **Assess** family-specific factors;
2. **Shortlist** trustees (typically 3–5);
3. **Evaluate** each against criteria;
4. **Meet** senior people;
5. **Choose** with clear engagement terms;
6. **Establish** relationship.

## Pitfalls

- **Choosing on cost alone**: long-term partnership matters
  more;
- **Ignoring continuity risk** in smaller trustees;
- **Mismatch** between family culture and trustee style;
- **Inadequate due diligence** before engagement.
