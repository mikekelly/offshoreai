# Intergenerational Transfer Through Jersey Vehicles

## The question

How are Jersey vehicles used to **transfer wealth between
generations**? What are the typical structures and considerations?

## The short answer

Jersey vehicles support intergenerational transfer through:

- **Discretionary trusts** holding wealth across generations,
  distributing to family as needed;
- **Foundation governance overlay** persisting beyond founder
  death;
- **PTC structures** keeping family governance consistent;
- **Family-holding companies** for operating businesses passed
  down;
- **Lifetime gifts** rather than testamentary transfers (timing
  flexibility).

Jersey provides **no Jersey IHT** at the trust / foundation / 
company level, allowing wealth to **accumulate generationally**
without Jersey-tax friction. Foreign tax in family members'
jurisdictions is the substantive consideration.

## The proper answer

### Generational accumulation in trust

A typical Jersey discretionary trust:

- **Settled** by Generation 1 (founder);
- **Beneficiary class** includes Generation 1, 2, 3, and
  descendants;
- **Distributes** to family members across generations as
  appropriate;
- **No fixed-rule transfer** between generations — discretionary
  power of trustee;
- **Continues** beyond founder's death (no rule against
  perpetuities in Jersey trust law as restrictive as
  elsewhere);
- **Adapts** to family circumstances over time.

This **multi-generational** approach is one of the **central
benefits** of Jersey trust structures.

### Foundation generation-spanning

Similarly with foundations:

- **Founder** establishes the foundation;
- **Council members** include family across generations;
- **Beneficiaries / objects** include broad family;
- **Foundation continues** beyond founder;
- **Council succession** through generational handover;
- **Family-values** articulated in charter.

### PTC continuity

PTC structures:

- **PTC owned** by orphan vehicle (foundation / purpose
  trust);
- **PTC directors** include senior family members;
- **Director succession** through generations — Generation 2
  takes seats from Generation 1, etc.;
- **Trusts under PTC** continue to administer family wealth;
- **Continuity** of governance across decades.

### Operating-business succession

For family operating businesses:

- **Jersey holding company** often holds the operating shares;
- **Holding company shares** held by family trust / foundation;
- **Generational transfer** through shareholding evolution;
- **Management transition** separate from ownership transition;
- **Tax-efficient** under zero-ten at holding level.

### Lifetime gifts vs testamentary

Many wealthy families prefer **lifetime structures** over
testamentary transfer:

- **Reduced** estate-tax exposure in some jurisdictions;
- **Witness** the generational handover and family response;
- **Adjust** terms based on observed family behaviour;
- **Avoid** probate process;
- **Reduce** family disputes through clear lifetime arrangement.

But:

- **Foreign tax** consequences vary;
- **Loss of control** for founder;
- **Family member readiness** to receive.

### Common scenarios

#### Operating-business founder approaching retirement

- **Transfer** operating-business shares to family holding
  structure;
- **Adult children** take governance roles;
- **Trust / foundation** holds wealth long-term;
- **Founder retains** consulting / advisory role.

#### Multi-generation family with established wealth

- **Existing trust** continues distributing;
- **New trusts** for specific family branches;
- **PTC governance** evolves;
- **Strategic asset-allocation** by family-office.

#### Patriarch / matriarch with limited willing successors

- **Professional trustees** continue governance;
- **External adviser** roles for family-office;
- **Trust** distributes to family without intensive family
  governance;
- **PTC** may not be appropriate if family lacks engaged
  members.

### Tax considerations across generations

#### Settlement tax

- **Jersey**: zero on entry to trust / foundation;
- **Foreign tax** (UK IHT, US gift tax, etc.) varies by settlor
  jurisdiction.

#### Ongoing tax

- **Jersey**: zero-ten 0% on company holding;
- **Foreign tax**: continues per beneficiary jurisdiction.

#### Beneficiary tax

- **Jersey**: zero on distributions to non-residents;
- **Foreign tax**: varies by beneficiary jurisdiction (often
  significant).

#### Succession events

- **Death of settlor** doesn't typically end Jersey-tax-free
  status of trust;
- **Trust distributions** in subsequent decades can continue;
- **Long-term planning** material to outcome.

### Common structures

#### Multi-generational discretionary trust

- **Settlor** (Gen 1);
- **Beneficiaries**: settlor, spouse, children, grandchildren,
  remoter descendants;
- **Trustee**: professional or PTC;
- **Protector** (optional): senior family member;
- **Discretionary distributions** as circumstances warrant.

#### Family-foundation overlay

- **Foundation** holds PTC and operating structures;
- **Council**: senior family + advisers;
- **Beneficiaries / objects**: family + charitable goals;
- **Generational transition** through council renewal.

### Family governance enabling

Long-term success requires:

- **Articulating** family values / mission;
- **Engaging** next generation early;
- **Educating** family members on wealth and structures;
- **Building** governance habits;
- **Adapting** as family evolves.

The Jersey structures **enable** generational transfer; the
family must **make use** of the enablement.

## What to do next

For intergenerational planning:

1. **Assess** family-wealth structure and generational reach;
2. **Identify** vehicles serving generational transfer;
3. **Engage** next generation in planning;
4. **Document** family-governance philosophy;
5. **Implement** with Jersey counsel and tax advisers.

## Pitfalls

- **Founder-control trap**: refusing to release control hampers
  transition;
- **Next-generation disengagement**: structures without family
  buy-in fail;
- **Tax surprise** in beneficiary jurisdictions;
- **Inadequate family communication**: silent wealth transfer
  generates disputes.
