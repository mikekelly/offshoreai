# US Grantor-Trust Treatment of Jersey Vehicles

## The question

The settlor / family includes **US persons** — citizens, green-
card holders, or US-residents. How is a Jersey trust **treated
under US tax**? Will it be a **grantor trust** or **non-grantor
trust**? What's the difference?

## The short answer

For US tax purposes, a Jersey trust is classified as:

- **Foreign Grantor Trust (FGT)** — where the **grantor**
  (settlor) retains specified powers under US tax law and is
  themselves a US person, **or** under specific rules for
  non-US grantors;
- **Foreign Non-Grantor Trust (FNGT)** — most Jersey trusts
  settled by **non-US persons**.

**FGT**: trust income taxed to the **grantor** as if directly
earned.
**FNGT**: trust income accumulates; **distributions to US-person
beneficiaries** trigger US tax (including throwback tax on
accumulated income — see distribution-us-person.md).

US tax modelling is essential before settlement.

## The proper answer

### What grantor trust means

A "**grantor trust**" under US tax law (IRC §§ 671–679) is a
trust where the **grantor** (settlor) is treated as the **owner**
of trust assets for US income-tax purposes:

- **Income** is taxed to the grantor (not the trust);
- **Gains** taxed to the grantor;
- **Beneficiary distributions** generally have no separate US
  tax consequence (income already taxed at grantor level);
- **Estate-tax** consequences depend on US estate-tax rules.

### When grantor-trust treatment arises

#### US grantor with retained powers

- **Reversionary interest** over 5% retained;
- **Power to revoke** (Article 9A reserved power);
- **Power to direct distributions**;
- **Power to direct investments** (in some cases);
- **Power to add / remove beneficiaries**;
- Various **other specified powers**.

Many **Article 9A reserved-powers** Jersey trusts settled by
US persons are **grantor trusts** under US tax law.

#### Foreign-grantor specific rules

Even **non-US grantors** can have FGT classification:

- **IRC §679(c)**: foreign-grantor trust if the trust has at
  least one US-person beneficiary;
- Generally **5-year rule** for foreign-grantor trusts settled
  by non-US persons becoming US residents;
- **Pre-immigration** trust planning common.

### FGT consequences

For a foreign grantor trust:

- **Income** taxed to grantor;
- **Form 3520-A** filed annually for the trust;
- **Form 3520** by US-person beneficiaries on distributions;
- **No throwback tax** on FGT distributions (income already
  taxed);
- **PFIC** issues at grantor level for underlying investments.

### FNGT consequences

For a foreign non-grantor trust:

- **Trust accumulates** income at the trust level;
- **No US tax** during accumulation (trust is non-US);
- **Distributions to US-person beneficiaries**:
  - **DNI** for current-year distributions (current-year
    income);
  - **UNI** for accumulated-income distributions —
    **throwback tax** at highest US individual rates plus
    interest;
- **Form 3520** by beneficiaries.

The **throwback regime** is one of the harshest features of US
tax — long-accumulated income distributions can be effectively
taxed at 70%+.

### Common scenarios

#### US-citizen settlor with reserved revocation power

- **FGT** under US tax;
- Income taxed to settlor;
- Annual Form 3520-A;
- No throwback issues but ongoing reporting burden.

#### Non-US settlor with US-person children as beneficiaries

- **Often FNGT** initially;
- **Throwback risks** as accumulation continues;
- **Planning** to avoid harsh distribution outcomes.

#### Pre-immigration trust by non-US person becoming US resident

- **5-year FGT** rule under IRC §679 sometimes applies;
- **Pre-immigration window** for grantor-trust treatment;
- **Becomes FNGT** after the rule period.

#### Trust settled long ago with accumulated income

- **Throwback exposure** for future US-person distributions;
- **Asset-allocation** decisions affect future tax;
- **Planning** to limit accumulation harm.

### PFIC issues

Where the trust holds **non-US investment funds** or shares in
companies classified as PFICs:

- **US-person beneficiaries** face PFIC taxation on underlying;
- **QEF / mark-to-market elections** can mitigate but require
  setup;
- **Default PFIC** taxation is punitive.

### Form 3520 / 3520-A

- **Form 3520-A**: annual reporting by foreign trust;
- **Form 3520**: US-person beneficiaries' reporting of gifts /
  distributions / trust ownership;
- **Penalties** for failure substantial (35% of distribution /
  asset amounts);
- **MLRO / trustee** typically assists US beneficiaries with
  required information.

### Planning considerations

- **Pre-settlement modelling** essential for any US person
  involvement;
- **Asset selection** to limit PFIC issues;
- **Beneficiary classes** structured to manage attribution;
- **Distribution timing** to manage UNI build-up;
- **Trust-level investments** managed for US-tax efficiency.

### Jersey's role

Jersey provides:

- **No Jersey tax** at trust or beneficiary level;
- **Mature trust law** with strong firewall;
- **Article 9A reserved powers** for grantor-trust elections
  where desired.

But **US tax overlay** is dominant — US-person involvement
requires US tax advice at every step.

## What to do next

For a US-person-related Jersey trust:

1. **Engage** US tax adviser at structure design stage;
2. **Classify** the prospective trust (FGT / FNGT);
3. **Model** US tax consequences;
4. **Plan** asset selection and beneficiary structuring;
5. **Set up** US tax-compliance arrangements (Form 3520-A,
   Form 3520);
6. **Coordinate** with Jersey trustee on US reporting.

## Pitfalls

- **Treating Jersey trust as US-tax-free**: rarely is for US
  persons;
- **PFIC surprise** on underlying investments;
- **Throwback shock** when distributing long-accumulated
  income;
- **Form 3520** late filing penalties.
