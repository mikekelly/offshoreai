# How Will My Structure Be Characterised in [Home Country]?

## The question

The family is establishing a Jersey vehicle (trust, foundation,
company). How will it be **characterised in the family's home
country** for tax purposes — and is that good or bad?

## The short answer

Each home jurisdiction characterises Jersey vehicles
differently. Broad patterns:

- **Jersey company**: typically a **foreign corporation** in
  home jurisdictions; foreign-corporation tax rules apply
  (CFC, PFIC for US, foreign-company rules elsewhere);
- **Jersey trust**: characterisation varies — **grantor trust**
  in US for retained-power cases, **non-grantor** otherwise;
  **relevant property trust** in UK typically;
- **Jersey foundation**: often **treated as corporate** in
  common-law countries, sometimes **as trust** in civil-law
  countries, **as fiscal nullity** in some.

Model **specifically for the family's home jurisdiction(s)** —
generic Jersey-law description doesn't suffice.

## The proper answer

### Why characterisation matters

The home-country tax treatment depends on how the vehicle is
**classified** in that jurisdiction:

- **Corporate** classification: usually CFC / PFIC / similar
  anti-deferral rules apply;
- **Trust** classification: grantor-trust / non-grantor-trust
  rules apply;
- **Partnership** classification: tax-transparent in many
  jurisdictions;
- **Nullity** classification: vehicle ignored; assets treated
  as the family's;
- **Hybrid** classification: complex mixed treatment.

The same Jersey-law structure can produce **radically different
tax outcomes** depending on home-country classification.

### Common Jersey vehicles and characterisations

#### Jersey company

- Almost universally treated as **foreign corporation**;
- Subject to home-country **CFC** rules (UK, US, Germany,
  France, etc.);
- **PFIC** in US if passive-income / passive-asset thresholds
  met;
- **Distributions** taxed as foreign dividends;
- **Underlying gains** may be attributed to home-country
  shareholders under CFC.

#### Jersey discretionary trust

- **US**: FGT or FNGT depending on grantor circumstances;
- **UK**: typically **relevant property trust** subject to
  10-year / exit / IHT charges (post-2025 reforms changed
  some excluded-property positions);
- **Civil-law jurisdictions**: variable recognition;
  sometimes "**fiscal nullity**" (treated as direct settlor
  ownership);
- **Some jurisdictions** (e.g. France) historically anti-trust.

#### Jersey foundation

- **Less established characterisation** than trusts;
- **US**: often treated as **corporate** (foreign corporation);
- **UK**: HMRC guidance treats as **trust-like** for some
  purposes;
- **Civil-law countries**: variable, often closer to civil-law
  *Stiftung* / *fondation* concepts.

#### Jersey limited partnership

- Typically **tax-transparent** in major investor jurisdictions;
- **US**: partnership treatment by default;
- **UK**: partnership treatment;
- **EU**: variable but often transparent.

### Specific jurisdiction examples

#### UK

- **Companies**: foreign-company, CFC rules, dividend taxation;
- **Trusts**: relevant property regime, TOAA, s.87 attribution;
- **Foundations**: HMRC guidance evolving;
- **Partnerships**: tax-transparent typically.

#### US

- **Companies**: foreign corporation, CFC if controlled, PFIC
  if passive;
- **Trusts**: FGT / FNGT; throwback tax for FNGT distributions
  of accumulated income; PFIC issues for underlying;
- **Foundations**: often treated as corporation;
- **LPs**: tax-transparent.

#### EU (varies by country)

- **France**: trust-hostile historically; specific reporting
  rules (trust declarations);
- **Germany**: trust treatment difficult; foundation more
  comfortable;
- **Italy, Spain**: similar to France in trust treatment;
- **Netherlands**: more accommodating;
- **Luxembourg, Ireland**: tax-treaty network often used for
  intermediate structuring.

### Modelling approach

For each family:

1. **Map** family members by tax residence;
2. **Identify** the proposed Jersey structure;
3. **Model** characterisation in each relevant jurisdiction;
4. **Calculate** tax consequences (entry, ongoing, distribution,
   exit);
5. **Compare** with alternative structures;
6. **Document** the modelling and decision.

### Engagement with foreign tax advisers

Essential:

- **Foreign tax adviser** must be engaged at structure-design
  stage;
- **Don't rely** solely on Jersey counsel for foreign tax;
- **Joint planning** between Jersey and foreign;
- **Periodic refresh** as rules change.

### Common surprises

- **PFIC** on underlying investments;
- **Throwback tax** for FNGT US-person distributions;
- **CFC inclusion** in UK / US shareholders' personal income;
- **UK IHT** entry / 10-year / exit charges;
- **Civil-law fiscal-nullity** treatment.

### Documentation

Maintain:

- **Tax-position memo** for each jurisdiction;
- **Filing obligations** identified;
- **Update** for regulatory / law changes;
- **Family-member-specific** considerations.

## What to do next

For new structures:

1. **Identify** all relevant jurisdictions;
2. **Engage** foreign tax advisers;
3. **Model** characterisations and outcomes;
4. **Choose** vehicle based on aggregate tax + non-tax
   considerations;
5. **Implement** with proper documentation;
6. **Establish** ongoing compliance.

For existing structures:

- **Review** characterisation periodically;
- **Refresh** for rule changes (e.g. 2025 UK IHT reforms);
- **Update** modelling for life events.

## Pitfalls

- **Jersey-law-only thinking**: fatal for cross-border
  structures;
- **Stale modelling**: tax rules evolve;
- **Single-jurisdiction focus**: international families
  multi-juris by definition;
- **Inadequate documentation**: hard to defend later.
