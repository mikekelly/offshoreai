# What a Foreign-Lawyer in [Home Jurisdiction] Needs to Know

## The question

You're advising a family with a Jersey-trust / foundation
structure. The family also has a **lawyer in their home
jurisdiction** (UK, US, EU, etc.) advising on broader matters.
What does that lawyer need to know about Jersey trustees and
foundations?

## The short answer

The home-jurisdiction lawyer needs to understand:

- **Jersey trustees / council members are fiduciaries** —
  not the family's agents;
- **Discretionary decisions** are the trustee's / council's,
  not the family's;
- **Article 9 firewall** affects how Jersey trusts interact
  with foreign-court proceedings;
- **Article 9A reserved powers** allow some retained settlor
  control;
- **Procedural pathways** for Jersey-court matters (Article 51
  directions, Article 47 mistake);
- **Tax characterisation** in the home jurisdiction is the
  active question;
- **Information rights** of beneficiaries (Schmidt v Rosewood);
- **Service-provider regulation** under FS(J)L 1998.

## The proper answer

### Cultural orientation

Many foreign lawyers initially treat Jersey vehicles as
**straightforward instruments under family control**. This
misreads:

- The **trustee is a fiduciary** with independent duties;
- **Family wishes** are guidance, not direction;
- **Trustee decisions** are independent professional judgement;
- **Trustee discretion** is real, not formal.

Setting this expectation **at the start** of engagement avoids
many later issues.

### Key Jersey-law concepts to communicate

#### Article 9 firewall

- Jersey applies **Jersey law** to all questions about Jersey
  trusts;
- Foreign **forced-heirship, matrimonial, creditor** rules don't
  apply;
- **Foreign judgments** based on those rules not recognised;
- **Practical effect**: foreign litigation against trust assets
  faces uphill battle.

See [`../trust-officer/firewall-question.md`](../trust-officer/firewall-question.md)
for deep coverage.

#### Article 9A reserved powers

- Settlor can **reserve** specified powers (revoke, direct,
  appoint) without invalidating;
- **Doesn't make the trust a sham**;
- **Foreign tax** characterisation may be adverse — model
  carefully.

#### Article 47 mistake

- **Wider** than English *Pitt v Holt*;
- Jersey **sets aside** transactions made under mistake;
- **Tax-trigger mistakes** can be unwound;
- **Useful tool** for correcting errors retrospectively.

#### Schmidt v Rosewood

- **Beneficiary information rights** are discretionary, not
  absolute;
- **Royal Court supervisory jurisdiction**;
- **Document categories** treated differently (trust deed FOR;
  letters of wishes AGAINST);
- **Different** from data-protection-law rights.

### Procedural orientation

#### Article 51 directions

- The trustee's principal route to court guidance;
- **Public Trustee v Cooper** categories adopted in Jersey;
- **Blessings** of momentous decisions common;
- **Beddoe cost protection** for trustee.

#### Royal Court

- **Same court** for civil and criminal;
- **Bailiff** (judge of law) + **Jurats** (judges of fact);
- **Appeals** to Jersey Court of Appeal, then Privy Council.

#### Service of foreign proceedings

- **Letters rogatory** for evidence;
- **Recognition** of foreign judgments limited (especially
  under Article 9);
- **Personal service** on trustees / officers possible.

### Tax characterisation

This is where home-jurisdiction lawyers add the most value:

- **UK IHT**: relevant-property regime applies to most
  discretionary trusts;
- **UK income / CGT**: TOAA and s.87 attribution to UK
  residents;
- **US**: FGT vs FNGT classification, throwback tax, PFIC;
- **Other EU**: civil-law treatment varies;
- **2025 UK reforms** changed the residence / domicile
  landscape.

The Jersey vehicle's Jersey-tax-light position **doesn't
overcome** home-jurisdiction tax.

### Information flow

For productive collaboration:

- **Brief** home-jurisdiction lawyer on the structure;
- **Provide** trust deed / foundation documents (subject to
  trustee consent);
- **Coordinate** on family-member-specific tax planning;
- **Engage** Jersey counsel for Jersey-law-specific advice;
- **Don't substitute** foreign-law advice for Jersey-law advice.

### Common foreign-lawyer mistakes

#### Treating trustee as nominee

- **Inviting sham allegations**;
- **Undermining** the trust's effectiveness;
- **Foreign tax** characterisation may worsen.

#### Drafting beneficiary letters expecting compliance

- **Trustee discretion** means letters are guidance;
- **Pressure tactics** counterproductive;
- **Proper channels** (deed mechanisms, letter of wishes) work
  better.

#### Foreign-court strategy underestimating Article 9

- **Article 9** is a significant obstacle to foreign-court
  attacks;
- **Plan accordingly** rather than assuming foreign judgment
  enforceable.

### Working together

Best practices:

- **Joint planning** at structure setup;
- **Regular updates** on home-jurisdiction tax / regulatory
  changes;
- **Coordinated communication** with the family;
- **Mutual respect** between Jersey and foreign counsel.

## What to do next

For an active engagement:

1. **Identify** the family's home-jurisdiction lawyer;
2. **Brief** on key Jersey concepts;
3. **Provide** structure documentation;
4. **Establish** coordination cadence;
5. **Address** any misunderstandings proactively.

## Pitfalls

- **Cultural mismatch**: foreign lawyer treating Jersey as
  paper-only;
- **Information gaps**: trustee, family, and foreign lawyer
  out of sync;
- **Tax-surprise**: foreign tax not properly modelled.
