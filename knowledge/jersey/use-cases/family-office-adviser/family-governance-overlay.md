# Family-Office Governance Overlay — Vehicle Choice

## The question

The family wants a **governance overlay** vehicle — sitting
above operating wealth, family trusts, PTCs, etc. — to provide
**family-level coordination**. Should this be a **foundation**,
**non-charitable purpose trust**, or **holding company**?

## The short answer

For most family-governance situations:

- **Foundation** — preferred when the family wants a body
  corporate with public-charter / private-regulations split;
- **Non-charitable purpose trust** — preferred for common-law-
  familiar families wanting a trust-based vehicle;
- **Holding company** — works if **direct family ownership** is
  acceptable and the company has commercial substance.

The decision turns on **family preference**, **tax
characterisation** in relevant jurisdictions, and **structural
purpose**.

## The proper answer

### What governance overlay does

The governance vehicle typically:

- **Holds shares** in the family PTC;
- **Holds shares** in family operating / holding companies;
- **Owns** family-office service entities;
- **Coordinates** family decisions across multiple vehicles;
- **Persists** across generations;
- **Articulates** family values and priorities.

### Foundation option

#### Strengths

- **Body corporate** — clean ownership of underlying entities;
- **Charter** can articulate family values publicly;
- **Regulations** keep operational details private;
- **Council** structure familiar to civil-law families;
- **Guardian** provides supervisory oversight;
- **Continuity** beyond founder.

#### Weaknesses

- **Civil-law character** less familiar to common-law families;
- **Public charter** — some families prefer privacy;
- **Beneficiary rights** muted — may not suit families wanting
  participation.

### Non-charitable purpose trust

#### Strengths

- **Trust-based** — familiar to common-law families;
- **Private trust deed** — privacy preserved;
- **Article 12 framework** well-established in Jersey law;
- **Enforcer** provides supervisory structure;
- **Flexible** purpose definition.

#### Weaknesses

- **Trust complexity** for civil-law families;
- **No body-corporate** identity;
- **Beneficial-ownership** discussions complex for AML.

### Holding company

#### Strengths

- **Simple** — a Jersey company is widely understood;
- **Direct family ownership** if family members hold shares;
- **Operational** — the company can directly do things;
- **Tax-clear** in most jurisdictions.

#### Weaknesses

- **Family-shareholder** taxation in their home jurisdictions;
- **No governance structure** beyond standard company directors;
- **Generational transitions** complex (share transfer);
- **Less family-specific** governance flexibility.

### Comparison table

| Feature | Foundation | Purpose Trust | Holding Company |
|---|---|---|---|
| Legal form | Body corporate | Trust relationship | Body corporate |
| Owner of structure | Foundation itself | Trustees | Family (directly / via trust) |
| Family role | Council, guardian | Trustees, enforcer | Directors, shareholders |
| Public vs private | Charter public, regs private | Trust deed private | Memorandum public |
| Beneficiaries | Defined (or purpose) | Generally purpose (no beneficiaries) | None / shareholders |
| Civil-law fit | Strong | Weaker | Variable |
| Common-law fit | Workable | Strong | Strong |
| Generational continuity | Strong | Strong | Weaker (without trust above) |
| Tax characterisation (typical) | Often corporate | Trust treatment | Corporate |

### Specific scenarios

#### Wealthy European family with international children

- **Foundation** often natural fit;
- Civil-law parents comfortable with foundation;
- Public charter for family-values articulation;
- Council includes adult children;
- Guardian provides independent oversight.

#### UK / US family with established trust tradition

- **Purpose trust** often fits cultural preference;
- Common-law jurisprudence familiar;
- Trustee + enforcer structure works;
- Family-specific arrangements via trust deed.

#### Operating-business family with active commercial role

- **Holding company** often preferred;
- Direct ownership of business interests;
- Family directors actively managing;
- Tax-driven decisions specific to family-member jurisdictions.

#### Hybrid family (mixed civil-law / common-law backgrounds)

- **Foundation** often chosen for cross-cultural acceptability;
- Body corporate familiar to civil-law side;
- Council governance familiar to common-law side.

### Holding underlying vehicles

The governance overlay typically holds:

- **Family PTC shares** (the PTC then trustees family trusts);
- **Family-office service company shares**;
- **Investment holding company shares**;
- **Heritage asset entity shares** (real estate, art);
- **Operating-business shares** (sometimes).

### Setting up

1. **Family discussion** on values / governance philosophy;
2. **Choose** vehicle type based on factors above;
3. **Engage** Jersey counsel + home-jurisdiction tax advisers;
4. **Establish** the chosen vehicle;
5. **Transfer** ownership of underlying structures;
6. **Establish** ongoing governance (regular meetings,
   reporting);
7. **Document** family priorities in vehicle's terms.

## What to do next

For an active family-office consideration:

1. **Discuss** family values and governance preferences;
2. **Model** tax characterisation of each option;
3. **Choose** vehicle type;
4. **Engage** counsel for structure design;
5. **Implement** with proper documentation.

## Pitfalls

- **Form over substance**: choosing the wrong form for cultural
  reasons;
- **Tax surprise**: characterisation in some jurisdictions
  unexpectedly adverse;
- **Generational gap**: structure that works for founder doesn't
  for children;
- **Governance neglect**: vehicle established but not actively
  used.
