# Landlord Q: My tenant isn't paying rent — can I evict them?

## The question

You're a Jersey residential landlord. Your tenant has
stopped paying rent. Can you just evict them? What
protections do they have?

## The short answer

**No, not "just".** You cannot:

- Change the locks;
- Remove their possessions;
- Cut off utilities;
- Physically remove the tenant;
- Refuse access to the property.

All of those are unlawful and may be **criminal offences**.

You **can** initiate the **statutory breach procedure**:

1. **Serve a 7-day written breach notice** specifying the
   unpaid rent and requiring payment within 7 days.
2. If unpaid, **apply to the Petty Debts Court** for an
   order terminating the tenancy and evicting the tenant.
3. If granted (often with a stay of execution), the
   **Viscount** enforces the order — attending the
   property, removing the tenant if necessary, and
   restoring possession to you.

Realistic timeline: **2-4 months** from first missed
payment to actual possession, longer if contested. Cost:
court fees + Viscount's fees + your time. Tenant
protections are substantive — discussed below.

## The proper answer

### Step 1 — The 7-day breach notice

Non-payment is a **breach of the tenancy agreement** under
the **Residential Tenancy (Jersey) Law 2011** (RTJL).
Termination is conditional on serving statutory notice and
the tenant failing to remedy.

The notice must:

- Be **in writing** and **signed**;
- **Identify the breach** — specifically, the rent due
  (which period(s), how much);
- **Require remedy** — payment of arrears — within at
  least **7 days** of service;
- **Be delivered** properly (per the agreement or by
  personal service / recorded post).

A defective notice means you start over.

If the tenant pays in full within the 7 days, the breach is
remedied. You cannot proceed on the same arrears.

### Step 2 — Apply to the Petty Debts Court

If unpaid after 7 days, apply to the **Petty Debts Court**
(which has exclusive original jurisdiction). You'll need:

- The tenancy agreement;
- The condition report;
- Evidence of arrears (rent ledger, bank statements);
- A copy of the breach notice with proof of service;
- A schedule of communications with the tenant.

You may also ask the court for a money judgment for the
arrears.

### Step 3 — The hearing

The court considers:

- Was the breach made out? (Rent due, unpaid, after 7 days
  of notice?)
- Does the tenant have a defence?
- Is the procedure compliant?
- Should there be a stay of execution?

Common tenant defences:

- **Set-off** for landlord disrepair (a documented unfit
  property, unrepaired despite requests);
- **Discrimination** (eviction is in substance
  retaliatory or discriminatory);
- **Harassment** during the notice period (you cut off
  utilities, entered without consent, made threats);
- **Procedural defects** in your notice.

If the court is satisfied, it grants:

- **Termination** of the agreement;
- **Eviction** order;
- Often a **stay of execution** (commonly 14-28 days,
  longer for families with children);
- **Money judgment** for arrears (if sought);
- **Costs** (usually).

### Step 4 — Viscount enforcement

After any stay has expired, you apply to the **Viscount's
Department** for execution. The Viscount:

- Attends the property at a scheduled time;
- Removes the tenant (with force if needed);
- Inventories possessions, stores or sells them;
- Restores possession to you.

Viscount fees: typically several hundred to several
thousand pounds. Recoverable from the tenant per the
lease / judgment but often in practice borne by you.

## What "protections" does the tenant have?

Material ones:

- **Procedural** — your notice and procedure must be
  perfect;
- **Set-off** — if you've failed to repair, the tenant can
  reduce the arrears claim by the diminution in value;
- **Discrimination** — if your eviction is on a protected
  ground (race, sex, disability, etc.), you face a
  parallel discrimination claim;
- **Retaliation** — if the eviction follows a complaint /
  report to authorities, the court will scrutinise;
- **Vulnerability** — children, mental health, pregnancy,
  disability all attract stays of execution;
- **Time** — the 7-day notice + court + stay + Viscount
  scheduling adds up.

## Common mistakes to avoid

- **Self-help** — never. Costs you the case and exposes
  you to damages and criminal liability.
- **Accepting partial payment** as "remedy" — clarify
  whether partial payment is acceptable; otherwise
  insist on payment in full.
- **Defective notice** — get the form, signature, period,
  and service right.
- **Accepting rent after the termination date** — may
  waive the termination.
- **Not engaging early** — courts dislike landlords who
  go straight to litigation without trying to resolve.
- **Discriminatory or retaliatory language** in
  correspondence — gold for the tenant's defence.
- **Misunderstanding** RTJL vs commercial-lease procedure
  — different procedure entirely.

## What to do tomorrow morning

1. **Calculate** arrears precisely.
2. **Check** the tenancy agreement and condition report.
3. **Send** the tenant a written, formal notice giving 7
   days to remedy. Use a template; sign it; serve it
   properly.
4. **Document** service (recorded post receipt /
   photograph of personal delivery).
5. **Open communication** with the tenant in parallel —
   offer a payment plan; many cases resolve here.
6. If unresolved after 7 days, **gather your evidence**
   and prepare the Petty Debts Court application.
7. **Engage a Jersey advocate** if the matter looks
   contested or if the tenant is represented — fees
   are usually recoverable.
