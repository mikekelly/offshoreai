# Worked Example: Contentious Trust Litigation in the Jersey Royal Court

## The scenario

You're an English-qualified solicitor running a contentious-
trust matter with a Jersey dimension. Your client is a
beneficiary of a long-established Jersey discretionary
trust. The settlor — your client's late grandfather —
made a substantial appointment from the trust shortly
before his death. That appointment:

- Distributed £15 million to a specific branch of the
  family;
- Was based on professional tax advice that proved
  **materially wrong** (an HMRC ruling now indicates the
  appointment crystallised a significant unforeseen UK
  tax charge);
- Has been challenged by other beneficiaries on
  procedural grounds.

The trustee is a Jersey-licensed Trust Company Business
(TCB). The trustee is now uncertain how to proceed — face
beneficiary litigation in Jersey, deal with the HMRC tax
charge, or seek to unwind the appointment under Jersey's
statutory mistake regime.

This worked example walks the litigation strategy and
mechanics.

## The structural options

For your client and the trustee, the principal routes
are:

| Route | Mechanism | Outcome |
|---|---|---|
| **Set-aside** the appointment under Article 47G TJL 1984 | Royal Court Representation; void the appointment ab initio | Property returns to trust; tax charge unwound; family branch loses the £15m appointment |
| **Article 51 directions** | Royal Court application by trustee | Court blesses a proposed restructuring or course of action |
| **Beneficiary suit** for breach of trust | Order of Justice in Royal Court | Damages against trustee; possible removal of trustee |
| **Negotiated** rectification by family agreement | Variation of trust by all beneficiaries (Saunders v Vautier-equivalent) | Consensual restructuring without court order |

Each has different procedural mechanics, costs,
beneficiary-relations consequences, and tax
implications.

## Step-by-step — Article 47B mistake set-aside

The strongest legal route given the facts is to apply for
the appointment to be set aside on mistake grounds.

### Step 1 — Standing and parties

Under Article 47C of the TJL 1984, standing to apply
includes:

- The **trustee** (typical applicant);
- The **settlor** (often deceased or otherwise unable);
- An **affected beneficiary**;
- The **Attorney General** in narrow public-interest
  cases.

The natural applicant here is the **trustee**, who can
apply by **Representation** rather than the more
adversarial **Order of Justice** route. This recognises
the friendly-proceedings nature of mistake applications —
the trustee isn't suing anyone, it's asking the court to
unwind a vitiated power-exercise.

See [`../../legal-system/royal-court-rules.md`](../../legal-system/royal-court-rules.md)
for the Representation procedure.

### Step 2 — The legal test

The Royal Court applies the **three-limb test** from
*Re S&T Trusts* [2015] JRC259:

1. **There was a mistake** within Article 47B's wide
   definition;
2. **But for the mistake**, the power would not have
   been exercised in the form it was;
3. **The mistake was of so serious a character** that
   it would be just for the Court to grant relief.

### Step 3 — Article 47B's wide definition

Article 47B defines "mistake" broadly:

- Mistake as to the **effect** of the transaction;
- Mistake as to the **consequences** (expressly including
  **tax consequences**);
- Mistake as to **any advantage** believed to flow;
- Mistake as to **any fact existing** before or at the
  time;
- **Manner of the mistake is irrelevant** — Jersey
  rejects the *Pitt v Holt* distinction between
  "operative mistake" and "inadequate deliberation".

For your case, the **tax-consequences limb** is
directly engaged: the appointment was made on
professional advice that proved wrong; HMRC has issued
a ruling crystallising the unforeseen tax charge. This
is precisely the situation Article 47B was designed for.

See [`../../trusts/article-47-set-aside.md`](../../trusts/article-47-set-aside.md).

### Step 4 — Procedure

The application proceeds as follows:

1. **Representation filed** with the Royal Court
   (Samedi Division typically) by the trustee;
2. **Supporting affidavit** documenting:
   - The decision-making context;
   - The professional advice received;
   - The mistake (the wrong tax advice);
   - The causative link to the appointment;
   - The materiality (the HMRC ruling and consequences);
3. **Service** on affected beneficiaries (those who
   received the £15m appointment, and those whose
   interests will be affected by reversion);
4. **Convening order** specifying who must be
   represented;
5. **First hearing** for directions;
6. **Substantive hearing** — affidavit evidence + brief
   submissions; sometimes oral evidence;
7. **Judgment** — typically reserved briefly;
8. **Order** declaring the appointment set aside ab
   initio.

Timeline: typically **3-6 months** from filing to
judgment in straightforward cases; longer if contested.

### Step 5 — The "just to grant relief" test

The third limb (justice) requires the court to consider:

- **Reliance** by recipient beneficiaries — has the
  appointment been spent? Has anyone changed their
  position relying on it?
- **Innocent third parties** — would set-aside affect
  them?
- **Public-policy** considerations (e.g., evasion of
  proper tax);
- **Family** dynamics — are the recipients consenting
  or contesting?

If the £15m has been received but not yet substantially
spent, and the recipients are family members aware of
the family-trust context, the court is more likely to
grant relief. If the £15m has been used to acquire
third-party assets that cannot easily be unwound, the
court will scrutinise harder.

## Step-by-step — Article 51 directions

In parallel or instead, the trustee can apply for
**Article 51 directions** seeking court blessing on a
proposed course of action.

### When Article 51 is right

- The trustee wants **insurance** against future
  beneficiary suit;
- A proposed action is **complex / contestable**;
- **Beneficiary disagreement** exists;
- The trustee wants **specific Court approval** of a
  decision under *Public Trustee v Cooper* category
  analysis.

### Procedure

- **Representation** to the Royal Court;
- **Supporting evidence** on the proposed action and
  rationale;
- **Convening** of affected beneficiaries;
- **Court analyses** under *Public Trustee v Cooper*
  categories (typically Category 2 — where the trustee
  has reached a decision but wants Court blessing);
- **Order** approving (or refusing) the proposed
  action.

The Article 51 route is **standard practice** for
contentious-trust matters. It provides the trustee
substantial **liability protection** — having acted on
Court direction, the trustee is shielded against
beneficiary claims later.

See [`../../trusts/article-51-directions.md`](../../trusts/article-51-directions.md).

## Combined strategy

In practice, the trustee here might:

1. **Apply for Article 47B set-aside** as the principal
   relief;
2. **Conjoin Article 51 directions** for ancillary
   matters (treatment of any interim income, expenses,
   future distribution policy);
3. **Notify HMRC** of the application and outcome;
4. **Coordinate** with the recipient beneficiaries
   (ideally obtaining their consent or non-opposition);
5. **Document** the entire process for the trustee's
   own records and future audit.

## Tax outcome on set-aside

If the appointment is set aside ab initio:

- The £15m **never left** the trust as a matter of
  Jersey law;
- The **HMRC ruling** is potentially neutralised
  retrospectively — HMRC typically respects Jersey-court
  declarations of mistake-based rescission for tax
  purposes, though the exact treatment depends on the
  precise nature of the tax charge;
- The **recipient family branch** loses the
  appointment but retains discretionary-beneficiary
  status going forward;
- The **trustee** can subsequently make a different
  appointment, properly advised.

## What this teaches a foreign lawyer

Key Jersey-specific points for foreign-lawyer
counterparts:

- **Procedure** is Representation, not Order of Justice
  — friendly proceedings;
- **Jersey rejects Pitt v Holt** distinction — Jersey is
  wider than English law on mistake;
- **Re S&T Trusts** three-limb test is the operative
  standard, not English common-law rescission;
- **Costs** typically come from the trust fund (trustee's
  reasonable costs) — not the contentious-litigation
  costs-follow-the-event rule;
- **Article 51 directions** are routinely combined with
  Article 47 set-aside;
- **Privy Council** is the final appeal — case law
  developed there influences both Jersey and Guernsey
  trust practice.

## Costs

- **Trust costs** — the trustee's reasonable costs of the
  proceedings are typically borne by the trust fund;
- **Beneficiaries' separate costs** — typically each
  party bears their own, with exceptions for unreasonable
  conduct;
- **Total** legal costs for a contested Article 47 +
  Article 51 combined application: typically **£100k -
  £500k** depending on complexity and contesting
  parties' presence;
- **Court fees** — modest by Royal Court standards.

## Appeals

If the Royal Court refuses set-aside:

- **Court of Appeal** within 28 days;
- **Privy Council** with leave for points of law of
  general importance.

If the Royal Court grants set-aside:

- **Other beneficiaries** can appeal;
- **HMRC** typically does not (lacks standing in Jersey
  to challenge Jersey-law mistake declarations directly).

## Practical recommendations

- **Engage Jersey advocates early** — Jersey-procedural
  expertise is critical;
- **Document the mistake** carefully — contemporaneous
  records of professional advice, board minutes,
  decision-making process;
- **Coordinate with the recipient family** — consent
  helps;
- **Plan the post-set-aside structure** — what happens
  with the £15m once it reverts? Don't litigate without
  a forward plan;
- **Manage HMRC** in parallel — keep them informed of
  the application;
- **Consider** confidentiality — Jersey trust
  proceedings are typically heard in private and
  judgments anonymised.
