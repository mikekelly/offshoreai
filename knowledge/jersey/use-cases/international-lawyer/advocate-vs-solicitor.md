# When Do I Need a Jersey Advocate vs Jersey Solicitor?

## The question

Jersey's legal profession splits into **Advocates** and
**Solicitors**. Which one do you need for your matter?

## The short answer

- **Jersey Advocate**: required for **Royal Court advocacy** —
  any contested hearing, application, or trial;
- **Jersey Solicitor**: handles **transactional work,
  conveyancing, probate, advisory matters**, and many
  out-of-court engagements;
- **Both** often work together on litigation (Solicitor for
  case preparation, Advocate for advocacy);
- **Major Jersey firms** have both Advocates and Solicitors —
  you instruct the firm, who allocates internally.

For most matters: **instruct a major Jersey firm** — they
allocate the appropriate fee-earners.

## The proper answer

### Advocate role

Jersey Advocates:

- **Equivalent** in some respects to English **barristers**,
  but **employed** by firms (not independent like UK barristers
  in chambers);
- **Right of audience** before the Royal Court, Court of Appeal,
  and (with leave) Privy Council;
- **Specialised** in advocacy and complex legal advice;
- **Higher tier** of the profession in Jersey tradition.

### Solicitor role

Jersey Solicitors:

- **Equivalent** to English Solicitors;
- **Right of audience** in lower courts and on specified
  matters;
- **Transactional work** — corporate, property, probate;
- **Advisory** work;
- **Case preparation** for Advocate-led matters.

### When you need Advocate

- **Royal Court hearings**;
- **Court of Appeal** advocacy;
- **Privy Council** appeals;
- **Complex litigation strategy** advice;
- **Hostile applications**;
- **Trust applications** (Article 51, Article 47).

### When Solicitor suffices

- **Corporate transactions** (M&A, financing);
- **Real-estate** conveyancing;
- **Probate** of estates;
- **Trust drafting** (deed of trust);
- **Foundation** establishment;
- **Compliance** advice;
- **Regulatory** matters not involving the Royal Court.

### Instructing structure

#### Firm model

Major Jersey firms (Mourant, Carey Olsen, Ogier, Bedell,
Walkers, Appleby, etc.):

- **Have both** Advocates and Solicitors;
- **Allocate internally** based on matter;
- **Single point of contact** for you;
- **Coordinated** team approach.

#### Boutique firms

- **Specialist firms** in specific areas;
- **May lack** in-house Advocate capability;
- **Instruct external Advocate** for advocacy needs;
- **Lower-cost** for non-litigation work.

### Working with the team

For a Royal Court matter:

- **Lead Advocate** runs advocacy;
- **Solicitor** prepares the case (witnesses, documents,
  procedure);
- **You as foreign lawyer** liaise with both;
- **Coordinated** with foreign-court strategy.

### Cost differences

- **Advocate**: typically £500–800+ per hour (senior);
- **Solicitor**: typically £400–600 per hour (senior);
- **Junior fee-earners**: lower rates;
- **Total cost**: matter-driven, not profession-driven.

### Engagement-letter

Standard engagement-letter terms:

- **Scope** of work;
- **Fee rates** by fee-earner;
- **Estimated cost** (if practicable);
- **Conflict checks**;
- **Confidentiality**;
- **Termination** rights.

### Choice of firm

Factors:

- **Subject-matter expertise** in the relevant area;
- **Senior fee-earner** seniority and reputation;
- **Conflict** considerations (firm may have conflicting
  matter);
- **Pricing**;
- **Working relationship** quality.

For most international-lawyer engagements: **established major
firm** with appropriate specialist expertise.

## What to do next

For a matter:

1. **Identify** matter type (litigation / transaction / advisory);
2. **Choose** Jersey firm based on expertise;
3. **Engage** with clear scope and fee discussion;
4. **Coordinate** with foreign counsel;
5. **Work** through Jersey team.

## Pitfalls

- **Wrong-tier instruction**: instructing Solicitor for matter
  needing Advocate (or vice versa) wastes time and money;
- **Firm conflicts**: check before engaging substantive work;
- **Cost surprises**: clear engagement-letter upfront;
- **Multiple instructions**: instructing multiple Jersey firms
  on related matters creates inefficiency.
