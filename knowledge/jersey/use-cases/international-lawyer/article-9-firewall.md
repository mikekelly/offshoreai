# Article 9 Firewall — How Strong Is It?

## The question

You are an English (or other foreign) lawyer in a contentious
trust dispute. The trust is governed by Jersey law. Your client
(perhaps a beneficiary, perhaps a spouse, perhaps a creditor)
wants the Jersey trust **set aside, varied, or attacked** based
on foreign-law principles. How does **Article 9 of the Trusts
(Jersey) Law 1984** affect your strategy?

## The short answer

Article 9 is **one of the strongest trust-firewall provisions
globally**. It:

- **Forces Jersey-law analysis** of all questions about the
  Jersey trust's validity, administration, and operation;
- **Refuses to apply** foreign succession laws, foreign matrimonial
  laws, and foreign forced-heirship rules;
- **Refuses to recognise** foreign judgments inconsistent with
  these principles.

It is **not absolute** — it has limits — but it is the **single
biggest obstacle** to a foreign attack on a Jersey trust.

## The proper answer

### What Article 9 says

In simplified form, Article 9 provides that:

- **Any question** concerning the validity, interpretation,
  effect, administration, variation, or termination of a Jersey
  trust is determined **according to the law of Jersey**;
- This applies **regardless of**:
  - The law of the settlor's domicile, residence, or
    nationality;
  - The law of the trust's situs of assets;
  - The forum hearing the dispute (Jersey, or wherever);
- **Foreign rules** that would invalidate the trust or claw back
  assets are **not applied**;
- **Foreign judgments** based on inconsistent principles are
  **not recognised**.

The original text is in the Trusts (Jersey) Law 1984 — see
[`../../trusts/firewall.md`](../../trusts/firewall.md) for the
detailed Jersey-law treatment.

### Specific issues Article 9 addresses

#### Foreign succession / forced heirship

A common attack: a settlor's heirs in a civil-law jurisdiction
claim that **forced-heirship** rules entitle them to a fixed
share of the trust assets.

Article 9 says: **Jersey doesn't apply foreign forced-heirship**.
The settlor's freedom to settle on trust is governed by Jersey
law, which gives broad freedom.

#### Foreign matrimonial regime

Another common attack: a settlor's (ex-)spouse claims that
foreign matrimonial-property rules give them ownership of trust
assets.

Article 9 says: **Jersey doesn't apply foreign matrimonial-
property rules** to override the trust. The settlor's right to
settle property on trust is recognised.

(Foreign courts may, of course, make orders **personal to the
settlor** that require them to do something — but Jersey
trustees are not bound to comply with such orders against the
trust assets.)

#### Foreign creditor claims

A third attack: a foreign creditor of the settlor claims the
trust was a **fraudulent conveyance** under foreign law.

Article 9 says: Jersey applies Jersey rules on fraudulent
conveyance. **Article 4(3)** of the Trusts Law provides Jersey's
test (focused on intent to defraud creditors known at the time).
Foreign creditor-protection rules don't override.

### Limits and exceptions

Article 9 is **not absolute**:

- **Sham trusts** — if the trust was never genuinely intended to
  be a trust, Article 9 doesn't help (a sham is not a trust);
- **Capacity-to-settle issues** — these are determined by the
  settlor's personal law, not Jersey law (Article 9 doesn't
  override capacity);
- **Specific Article 4 grounds for invalidity** — Jersey applies
  its own Article 4 grounds for trust invalidity;
- **Public policy** — Jersey courts retain a residual public-
  policy override (rarely deployed).

### How foreign courts react

Foreign courts often **reject the Article 9 firewall**:

- **English courts** in matrimonial-finance proceedings have
  ordered settlors to procure trust distributions despite
  Article 9;
- **US courts** in tax / creditor disputes have similarly looked
  through Article 9 in some cases;
- **Civil-law courts** may simply refuse to recognise the trust
  altogether.

But these foreign orders are typically **personal to the
settlor / beneficiary** — they don't directly bind the Jersey
trustee. The trustee must comply with **Jersey law** on the
question.

The **practical result**:

- A foreign judgment **against the settlor** (e.g. for
  matrimonial finance) can be **personally enforceable** but the
  trustee is not bound to make trust distributions to satisfy it;
- The settlor / beneficiary may face foreign-court contempt
  consequences for not procuring distributions;
- The trustee's duty is **to Jersey law** — and Jersey law,
  through Article 9, doesn't recognise the foreign claim.

### Strategy implications

For an **attacker** of a Jersey trust:

- **Article 9 makes Jersey-court litigation difficult** — the
  Jersey court will apply Jersey law;
- **Foreign-court personal orders** against the settlor are
  often the only viable route;
- **Contempt sanctions** in the foreign court are the pressure;
- **Trustee replacement** is sometimes attempted (a settlor-
  compliant trustee might be replaced with a more responsive one
  through court order in the home jurisdiction);
- **Settlor pressure** to vary or revoke is the practical
  endpoint of many disputes.

For a **defender** of a Jersey trust:

- **Article 9 is your friend** — invoke it early and prominently;
- **Insist on Jersey jurisdiction** for any trust-related
  proceedings;
- **Keep the trustee's discretion neutral** — don't let the
  trustee be seen as carrying out the settlor's wishes
  improperly;
- **Document** the trust's independent operation.

## What to do next

For an attacker:

1. **Assess feasibility** of foreign-court personal orders
   against the settlor;
2. **Consider** sham-trust arguments if facts support;
3. **Plan for** Article 9 pushback at every step;
4. **Engage Jersey counsel** to test specific lines of attack.

For a defender:

1. **Map Article 9 onto specific contested issues**;
2. **Plan for Jersey-court intervention** (Article 51 directions
   are routinely sought);
3. **Coordinate** with foreign counsel where personal-order
   exposure exists.

## Pitfalls

- **Treating Article 9 as a foreign-judgment shield only**:
  Article 9 controls **what law applies**, not just **which
  judgments are enforced**. The substantive analysis matters.
- **Overlooking the sham question**: Article 9 doesn't protect
  a sham; verify the trust's genuineness.
- **Foreign-court personal pressure**: Article 9 doesn't stop a
  foreign court from imprisoning a settlor for contempt; that
  pressure can drive practical outcomes despite Article 9.
