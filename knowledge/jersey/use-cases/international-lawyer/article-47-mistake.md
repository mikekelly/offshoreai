# Article 47 Mistake — Wider Than English Pitt v Holt

## The question

A trust decision (typically a distribution or transfer)
produced **unintended consequences** — usually adverse tax.
English *Pitt v Holt* (2013) constrains relief. How does Jersey
compare?

## The short answer

Jersey's **Articles 47B–H** of the Trusts (Jersey) Law 1984
provide a **wider mistake regime** than English *Pitt v Holt*:

- **Trustee mistake** (Article 47B): set-aside if mistake of
  serious character;
- **Settlor mistake** (Article 47G): set-aside of disposition
  by settlor;
- **No requirement of "operative" mistake** matching Pitt v
  Holt's restrictive test;
- **Tax-trigger mistakes** routinely unwound under Article 47;
- **Royal Court actively** applies the regime.

For UK / cross-border practitioners: Jersey is a **friendly
venue** for mistake claims that would fail in English law.

## The proper answer

### English Pitt v Holt (recap)

The English position post-Pitt v Holt:

- **Operative mistake** required — mistake must be of such
  serious character that to leave it standing would be
  unconscionable;
- **Tax avoidance** as primary purpose may disqualify relief;
- **Inadequate advice** without operative mistake doesn't
  qualify;
- **Restrictive** application.

### Jersey Article 47 regime

The Trusts Law's **Articles 47B–47H** provide:

#### Article 47B — Trustee mistake

- **Set aside** trustee decisions made under mistake;
- **Mistake of serious character** required;
- **Wider** than English approach;
- **Procedural** application to Royal Court.

#### Article 47G — Settlor mistake

- **Set aside** of trust dispositions made by settlor under
  mistake;
- **Including** trust settlements themselves;
- **Mistake** at the level of the settlor's decision;
- **Powerful** tool for unwinding tax-driven settlements.

### Jersey's three-limb test

Jersey's *S & T Trusts* (2018) Royal Court decision articulates
the test:

1. **Was there a mistake?** — operative mistake at the relevant
   moment;
2. **What was the nature** of the mistake? — sufficient
   character?
3. **Would it be just and equitable** to grant relief?

This is **less restrictive** than the English Pitt v Holt test.

### Specific differences from English law

#### Tax-trigger mistakes

- **Jersey**: routinely unwound where tax consequence was not
  foreseen and would not have occurred if known;
- **English**: harder — Pitt v Holt suggests tax avoidance as
  primary purpose disqualifies relief.

#### Inadequate advice

- **Jersey**: if proper advice would have prevented the
  decision, set-aside often available;
- **English**: must rise to "operative mistake" standard.

#### "Just and equitable" approach

- **Jersey**: balancing test gives flexibility;
- **English**: more rule-based approach.

### Procedural

#### Application

- **Representation** to Royal Court;
- **Affidavit** evidence on circumstances of mistake;
- **Beneficiary involvement** as appropriate;
- **Tax authorities** sometimes notified;
- **Court application** by Article 51 directions framework.

#### What the Court considers

- **Specifics** of the mistake;
- **Consequences** of not setting aside;
- **Beneficiary impact**;
- **Tax-authority response**;
- **Third-party interests**.

#### Outcome

- **Rescission** of the affected decision;
- **Retroactive** effect typically;
- **Tax-authority recognition** in some jurisdictions (varies).

### Foreign tax-authority recognition

A Jersey set-aside doesn't automatically bind foreign tax
authorities. Practice:

- **UK HMRC**: generally **respects** Jersey set-aside but
  case-by-case;
- **US IRS**: more **mixed** — set-aside may not be respected
  if seen as retroactive tax planning;
- **Other tax authorities**: vary.

The Jersey set-aside provides the **strongest possible basis**
for the foreign-tax position; foreign-tax recognition is then a
separate engagement.

### Common scenarios

#### UK-resident settlor's tax-mistake

- **Settlor** made decision without realising UK IHT
  consequence;
- **Jersey Article 47** set-aside available;
- **HMRC** may respect the unwinding.

#### Trustee distribution producing US tax shock

- **Trustee** distributed without realising US throwback tax
  consequence;
- **Article 47B** set-aside possible;
- **IRS recognition** more uncertain — but Jersey provides the
  cleanest basis.

#### Tax-planning gone wrong

- **Settlor / trustee** acted on incorrect tax advice;
- **Adverse consequence**;
- **Article 47** set-aside available if mistake of serious
  character.

### Why Jersey for difficult cases

Cross-border practitioners increasingly:

- **Restructure** to involve Jersey trust before risky
  decisions;
- **Use Jersey** as remedial-jurisdiction even where main
  trust elsewhere;
- **Engage** Jersey courts for set-aside relief unavailable
  elsewhere.

### Practical advice

For mistake-relief scenarios:

- **Move quickly** — delay can harm the application;
- **Engage Jersey counsel** familiar with Article 47 regime;
- **Document** the mistake comprehensively;
- **Plan** foreign-tax-authority engagement post-set-aside;
- **Cooperate** with all parties (beneficiaries, tax
  authorities).

## What to do next

For a mistake situation:

1. **Engage Jersey counsel** immediately;
2. **Document** the mistake and circumstances;
3. **Prepare** Article 47 application;
4. **File** with Royal Court;
5. **Engage** beneficiaries and tax authorities as
   appropriate;
6. **Implement** the set-aside.

## Pitfalls

- **Delay** weakening the application;
- **Inadequate evidence** of mistake;
- **Foreign-tax-authority complications** post-set-aside;
- **Third-party rights** affected by set-aside.
