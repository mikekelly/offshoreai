# Enforcement of Foreign Judgments in Jersey

## The question

You have a **foreign court judgment** that you want to enforce
against Jersey assets / persons. How?

## The short answer

Foreign judgments enforced in Jersey through:

- **Judgments (Reciprocal Enforcement) (Jersey) Law 1960** for
  judgments from designated reciprocating jurisdictions
  (registration regime);
- **Common-law enforcement** by **action on the judgment** for
  non-reciprocating jurisdictions;
- **Article 9 firewall** may **prevent** recognition of trust-
  related judgments inconsistent with Jersey law;
- **Public-policy** override available.

Engage **Jersey Advocate** early — enforcement varies by
judgment origin and subject matter.

## The proper answer

### Statutory enforcement — 1960 Law

The **Judgments (Reciprocal Enforcement) (Jersey) Law 1960**
allows registration and enforcement of judgments from
**designated reciprocating jurisdictions**:

#### Designated jurisdictions

Includes:

- **United Kingdom**;
- **Various Commonwealth** jurisdictions;
- **Some EU** countries (under specific arrangements);
- **Other** as designated by Order.

#### Registration process

1. **Apply** to Royal Court for registration;
2. **Submit** authenticated foreign judgment;
3. **Pay** prescribed fee;
4. **Court** registers if conditions met;
5. **Registered judgment** has the force of a Jersey judgment.

#### Conditions for registration

- **Final and conclusive** in the foreign court;
- **For a sum of money** (not in respect of taxes or fines
  typically);
- **From a court of competent jurisdiction**;
- **Not obtained by fraud**;
- **Not contrary to public policy**;
- **Defendant** properly served or appeared;
- **Time-limit** for registration met (typically 6 years from
  judgment).

### Common-law enforcement

For judgments **not** under the 1960 Law:

- **Bring action** in Royal Court based on the foreign judgment;
- **Foreign judgment** treated as **evidence of debt** owed;
- **Jersey court** gives **summary judgment** if conditions
  met;
- **Subject** to same fraud / public-policy / jurisdiction
  conditions.

### Defences to recognition

The defendant can resist recognition on grounds:

- **Foreign court lacked jurisdiction**;
- **Defendant not given fair opportunity to defend**;
- **Fraud** in obtaining judgment;
- **Contrary to public policy**;
- **Subject matter** outside enforceable categories;
- **Time-bar**.

### Article 9 firewall

For **trust-related** judgments:

- **Article 9** of the Trusts (Jersey) Law 1984 says **Jersey
  law applies** to Jersey trusts;
- **Foreign judgments** based on **forced-heirship,
  matrimonial-regime, or creditor-recovery** rules **not
  recognised** if inconsistent with Article 9;
- **Personal judgments** against settlor / beneficiary may
  still be enforceable against their **personal assets**, but
  not against trust assets.

This is **a significant defence** for trust-related foreign
judgments.

### Enforcement against assets

Once judgment recognised / registered:

#### Asset types

- **Bank accounts**: garnishee / arrêt orders;
- **Real estate**: judicial sale (with Jersey-customary-law
  procedures);
- **Securities**: court-ordered transfer / sale;
- **Other property**: as appropriate.

#### Process

- **Execution** through bailiff;
- **Jersey procedural** rules apply;
- **Defendant** may apply to set aside.

### Specific scenarios

#### UK judgment

- **1960 Law** typically available (UK is reciprocating);
- **Registration** straightforward usually;
- **Trust-related** judgments may face Article 9 defence.

#### US judgment

- **Not under 1960 Law** typically;
- **Common-law action** required;
- **Royal Court** considers all conditions;
- **More variable** outcome.

#### EU judgment

- **Brussels Recast Regulation** doesn't directly apply
  (Jersey not EU member);
- **Pre-Brexit UK-EU** judgments may have residual treatment;
- **1960 Law** designations or common-law action.

#### Trust-related judgment (any source)

- **Article 9 firewall** significant defence;
- **Recognition resistance** more likely;
- **Personal-pressure** on settlor / beneficiary more
  effective than direct enforcement.

### Time limits

- **1960 Law**: typically 6 years from judgment for registration;
- **Common-law action**: 10 years from judgment usually for
  bringing action;
- **Statute of limitations** in Jersey or foreign jurisdiction.

## What to do next

For an enforcement action:

1. **Engage** Jersey Advocate;
2. **Determine** route (1960 Law or common-law action);
3. **Verify** conditions met;
4. **Apply** to Royal Court;
5. **Address** any defences raised;
6. **Execute** against assets once recognised.

## Pitfalls

- **Wrong route**: 1960 Law vs common-law action;
- **Article 9 surprise** for trust-related judgments;
- **Public-policy challenge** in egregious cases;
- **Time-bar** issues for delayed enforcement;
- **Procedural compliance** failures in registration.
