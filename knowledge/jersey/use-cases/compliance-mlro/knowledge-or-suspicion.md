# What is "Knowledge or Suspicion" of Money Laundering?

## The question

The Proceeds of Crime (Jersey) Law 1999 requires reporting on
**knowledge or suspicion** of money laundering. What does
"suspicion" actually mean as a legal threshold?

## The short answer

**Suspicion** is a **low threshold** significantly below
**belief** or **certainty**:

- **More than fanciful** but **less than balance of probabilities**;
- A **reasonable basis** to **wonder** whether money laundering
  is occurring;
- The classic English authorities (R v Da Silva, K Ltd) apply
  in Jersey;
- The threshold is **subjective** — the actual suspicion of the
  individual — but with an **objective test** of reasonableness.

When in doubt, **err toward disclosure** — failure-to-disclose
is an offence at this low threshold.

## The proper answer

### The legal threshold — Da Silva

The English Court of Appeal's R v Da Silva (2006) is the leading
authority, adopted in Jersey:

- Suspicion means **more than a vague feeling of unease** but
  **less than certainty or even balance of probabilities**;
- It is a **genuine doubt**, not a mere theoretical possibility;
- The standard is **subjective** — the person's actual state of
  mind matters;
- But the question is **whether they had a basis** for that
  state of mind.

### Practical formulations

Different ways the threshold is articulated:

- **More than a fleeting thought**;
- **A reasonable basis to wonder**;
- **Genuine doubt about legitimacy**;
- **Something to investigate**;
- **A foot in the door of concern**.

### Distinction from belief

- **Belief** = significantly higher than suspicion;
- A finance professional **believes** something when they would
  act on it as a known fact;
- **Suspicion** can coexist with belief in the contrary —
  alternative explanations exist but money laundering is one
  of them.

### Distinction from certainty

- **Certainty** = near-100% confidence;
- Not required for SAR;
- Waiting for certainty defeats the regime.

### Red flags that warrant escalation

Common indicators that should at least prompt **internal
review**:

#### Behavioural

- **Reluctance** to provide CDD information;
- **Inconsistent** explanations;
- **Pressure for secrecy** or urgency;
- **Unusual instructions** outside profile;
- **Avoidance** of standard procedures.

#### Transactional

- **Pass-through** transactions with no apparent purpose;
- **Round numbers** designed to avoid thresholds;
- **Unusual counterparties**;
- **Geographic anomalies**;
- **Inconsistent volumes / patterns**.

#### Background

- **Adverse media** about the customer or related parties;
- **Sanctions match** (even false-positive);
- **Foreign criminal proceedings**;
- **Regulatory action** in another jurisdiction;
- **Source-of-wealth gaps**.

### How to handle the threshold question

When something concerning happens:

1. **Document** your observations factually;
2. **Investigate** as far as appropriate — talk to the
   relationship manager, review records;
3. **Consider** alternative explanations — is there a benign
   reason?
4. **Form a view** on whether suspicion is reasonable;
5. **If suspicion exists**: escalate internally to MLRO;
6. **If suspicion does not exist** but doubt persists: still
   document and consider whether to consult.

### MLRO's role

The MLRO receives internal disclosures and:

- **Investigates** further if appropriate;
- **Forms a view** on whether the threshold is met;
- **Decides** to file a SAR or close internally;
- **Documents** the decision and reasoning.

The MLRO's decision should reflect **the threshold being
reasonably met**, not a lower test like "convenience to file" or
a higher test like "certainty of money laundering".

### Defensive reporting

A practical reality: **defensive reporting** is common:

- Firms file SARs **more readily** than strictly required to
  avoid risk;
- The FIU receives many SARs that are **false positives**;
- This is **acceptable** within reason — over-reporting is
  better than under-reporting.

But filing **without any reasonable basis** is also
inappropriate:

- It floods the FIU with noise;
- It can be **bad faith** disclosure (rare but possible);
- It risks **tipping off** the subject if discovered.

The goal is **reasonable judgement** — neither paranoid nor
complacent.

### Consequences of inadequate threshold

#### Under-disclosure

- **Failure to disclose** is a criminal offence under POCL;
- **Penalties** up to 5 years imprisonment;
- **Personal liability** of the MLRO and others with the
  information;
- **Firm exposure** through regulatory action.

#### Over-disclosure

- **Tipping off** if the SAR or its consequences become known
  to the subject;
- **FIU swamping** with low-value reports;
- **Reputational risk** of being known as a heavy filer.

The balance is **considered judgement** at each case.

## What to do next

For a specific situation:

1. **Document** the observation;
2. **Apply** the suspicion test;
3. **Investigate** as appropriate;
4. **Escalate** to MLRO if suspicion exists;
5. **MLRO decides** SAR or close.

For programme:

- **Train** staff on the threshold;
- **Provide** clear escalation channels;
- **Calibrate** firm-wide approach (defensive vs reserved);
- **Document** decisions consistently.

## Pitfalls

- **Threshold too high**: waiting for certainty;
- **Threshold too low**: filing on mere theoretical
  possibilities;
- **Inconsistent application**: different officers applying
  different thresholds;
- **Documentation gaps**: undocumented decisions difficult to
  defend later.
