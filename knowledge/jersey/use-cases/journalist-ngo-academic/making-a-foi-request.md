# Q: How do I make a Freedom of Information request to a Jersey public body?

## The short answer

Under the **Freedom of Information (Jersey) Law 2011**
(FOI 2011), you can ask any **Scheduled Public Authority
(SPA)** for recorded information they hold. They must:

- **Acknowledge** your request promptly;
- **Respond substantively within 20 working days**;
- **Disclose** the information unless an exemption
  applies;
- **State reasons** if refusing.

The basic procedure:

1. **Identify the right SPA** (only scheduled bodies are
   subject to FOI);
2. **Frame** a specific written request;
3. **Submit** by email or post;
4. **Track** the 20-working-day timeline;
5. If refused or unsatisfied: **internal review** →
   **Information Commissioner** → **Royal Court**.

You **don't** have to state a reason. The SPA generally
shouldn't ask why you want the information.

## The proper answer

### Step 1 — Identify the right SPA

Only **Scheduled Public Authorities** are subject to FOI:

- **Government departments** (Customer and Local Services,
  Health and Community Services, Treasury and Exchequer,
  etc.);
- **States Assembly** services;
- **Police authorities**;
- **Parishes** for some functions;
- **Statutory bodies** named in the schedule;
- Various **regulators** and offices.

Bodies **not** on the schedule (e.g. some private-sector
bodies performing public functions) are not subject to
FOI in Jersey — a meaningful difference from broader UK
practice. Check the schedule before sending the request.

### Step 2 — Frame the request

The request should:

- Be in **writing** (email is fine);
- **Identify yourself** sufficiently to receive a response
  (you don't need to state purpose);
- **Specify** the information sought — be **specific**;
- **Avoid** overly broad "give me everything about X"
  framings — they invite cost refusals;
- **Cite** "Freedom of Information (Jersey) Law 2011" to
  trigger formal handling;
- Set a **preferred format** for response (PDF, email,
  copies).

Vague requests cost more and are slower; narrow requests
get faster answers.

### Step 3 — Submit and track

- Most SPAs accept FOI requests via a **dedicated email
  address** or through gov.je;
- **Record the date** of submission;
- **20 working days** is the target response window;
- **Extension** to a "reasonable" further period only on
  specific grounds (complexity, third-party consultation,
  large volume);
- **Acknowledge** the response (or non-response).

### Step 4 — Understand the response

The SPA may:

- **Disclose** the information requested;
- **Disclose in part** (with redactions for exempt material);
- **Refuse** under specified exemption(s);
- **Refuse for cost** if compliance would exceed the
  cost threshold;
- **Refuse as vexatious or repeated**;
- **Confirm or deny** whether the information exists
  (sometimes even confirmation is exempt — the
  "neither confirm nor deny" position).

Refusals must:

- **State the exemption** claimed;
- **State reasons** with sufficient particularity;
- **Conduct a public-interest test** for qualified
  exemptions;
- **Notify rights of appeal**.

### Step 5 — Appeal if needed

If unsatisfied:

| Stage | Forum | Time limit |
|---|---|---|
| Internal review | The SPA itself (different officer) | Typically 28 days |
| Decision notice | Information Commissioner (JOIC) | Typically 28 days from internal review |
| Final appeal | Royal Court (on points of law) | Statutory limit |

The Royal Court's decision is **final**.

The JOIC is typically the most active appeal layer.
Their decisions are binding on the SPA.

## Exemptions in practice

Some common exemption claims:

- **Personal data** of others → typically absolute (use
  DPL routes for your own data);
- **Crime detection / law enforcement** → qualified;
  public-interest balancing;
- **Free and frank advice** in government → qualified;
- **Commercial confidentiality** of third parties →
  qualified;
- **JFSC functions** → qualified;
- **Specific statutory bars** → absolute.

A **well-targeted request** for factual information
(spending data, decision rationales, statistics) is
typically harder to refuse than a request for opinions,
draft documents, or advice.

## Cost

- **Most requests** are answered free;
- **Cost threshold** for refusal — substantial volume of
  work invites fee or refusal;
- **Re-framing** narrower requests can avoid cost
  refusals.

## Specific situations

### "I want my own data"

Use **DPL subject access** instead. FOI typically
absolutely exempts personal data of the requester
(directing them to DPL).

### "I want personal data about someone else"

Typically absolutely exempt. Anonymised statistics
sometimes available. Legitimate journalistic / academic
purposes may justify specific approaches; the SPA may
disclose with redactions.

### "I want documents the SPA says don't exist"

The SPA can **confirm or deny** whether information
exists. Sometimes even this is exempt. If you believe
information is being concealed, the **JOIC** can
investigate via its decision-notice powers.

### "I want commercially-sensitive contracts"

Procurement and contract details are commonly requested.
Commercial confidentiality is a **qualified** exemption —
public interest in transparency of public spending often
prevails for headline figures, while detailed pricing
schedules may be protected.

## What to do tomorrow morning

1. **Read** [`../../civil-liberties/foi-law-2011.md`](../../civil-liberties/foi-law-2011.md)
   if you want fuller doctrinal detail;
2. **Identify** the SPA holding the information you want;
3. **Frame** a specific request;
4. **Send** by email, citing the FOI 2011;
5. **Track** the 20-working-day timeline;
6. **Escalate** through internal review → JOIC → Royal
   Court if needed;
7. **Document** all responses for any future challenge.

## Pitfalls

- **Requesting** from non-scheduled bodies — no statutory
  right of access;
- **Broad / unfocused** requests — invite cost refusal;
- **Asking** for opinions / advice — often exempt;
- **Mixing** FOI and DPL requests — separate them;
- **Missing** appeal deadlines;
- **Assuming** the JOIC will always side with the
  requester — case-by-case.
