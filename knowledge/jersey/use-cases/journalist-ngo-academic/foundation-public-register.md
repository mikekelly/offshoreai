# Are Foundation Registers Public?

## The short answer

**Partly**. Jersey foundations have a **partially public**
register:

- **Charter** (constitutive document) is **public**;
- **Council members** are **public** (their names listed);
- **Guardian** is **public**;
- **Qualified member** (regulated TCB) is **public**;
- **Registered office** is **public**;
- **Annual confirmation** filings are **public**.

**Not public**:

- **Regulations** (internal operational rules);
- **Beneficial-interest** detail;
- **Beneficiaries** (typically named in regulations, not
  charter);
- **Internal financial information**.

Compared to trusts, foundations are **more publicly visible**
because of their body-corporate registered status.

## The proper answer

### The charter / regulations split

A distinctive feature:

- **Charter** establishes the foundation publicly;
- **Regulations** provide internal operating rules privately.

This **partial transparency** lets the public see basic
identity while protecting operational privacy.

### What the charter typically shows

- **Foundation name**;
- **Charitable / non-charitable** purpose;
- **Founder** (usually named);
- **Council members** at incorporation;
- **Guardian** identified;
- **Qualified member** identified;
- **Registered office**;
- **General purpose** statement;
- **Duration** (perpetual or limited).

### What the regulations contain (private)

- **Detailed operational** rules;
- **Specific beneficiaries** or beneficiary classes;
- **Distribution mechanics**;
- **Council governance** detail;
- **Specific powers** of council, guardian;
- **Amendment** procedures.

### Comparison with Jersey trusts

| Feature | Foundation | Trust |
|---|---|---|
| Registered with JFSC? | Yes | No |
| Charter / deed public? | Charter yes; deed no | Deed no |
| Council / trustees named? | Council public | Trustees not (privately) |
| Beneficiaries named? | Typically in private regulations | Privately in trust deed |

Foundations are **more publicly visible** than trusts as a
class.

### Why partial transparency

The split reflects policy choice:

- **Public establishment** for legal certainty and recognition;
- **Private operations** for legitimate family / business
  privacy;
- **Council identification** for AML / accountability;
- **Beneficiary privacy** for protection.

### What journalists can find

#### From public register

- **Foundation exists** and basic identity;
- **Council members** — potentially family members, advisers,
  qualified-member TCB;
- **Founder** named;
- **Service provider** identified through qualified member.

#### From public Royal Court records

- **Court applications** involving the foundation;
- **Disputes** if any;
- **Specific facts** disclosed in litigation.

### Cross-jurisdictional comparison

#### Liechtenstein

- **Stiftungen** traditionally private;
- **Reforms** increased transparency;
- **Foundation register** with some public elements.

#### Panama

- **Private foundations** traditionally very private;
- **Recent reforms** increasing transparency.

#### Other civil-law jurisdictions

- Various; Jersey is **moderately transparent** by comparison.

### For investigators

#### What to do

- **Search JFSC Registry** for foundation charter;
- **Note** council members and guardian;
- **Pattern recognition** with service providers;
- **Cross-reference** with foreign records.

#### Limitations

- **Beneficiary identification** typically not possible from
  public records;
- **Internal operations** opaque;
- **Foundation purpose** in charter may be broad / generic.

### Beneficial ownership of foundations

The **BO register** (FS(DPI)(J)L 2020):

- **Foundations report** beneficial ownership centrally;
- **Same authority-only** access as for companies;
- **Council, guardian, beneficiaries** identified;
- **Not publicly accessible**.

## What to do next

For investigation:

1. **Search** JFSC Foundations Registry;
2. **Read** the charter;
3. **Note** council, guardian, qualified member;
4. **Cross-reference** publicly available information;
5. **Recognise limits** of public information.

## Pitfalls

- **Assuming** public visibility extends to beneficiaries;
- **Privacy law** considerations in reporting;
- **Inferential** beneficiary identification often unreliable.
