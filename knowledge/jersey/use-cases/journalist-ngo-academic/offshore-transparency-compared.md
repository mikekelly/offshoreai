# Offshore Transparency Compared

## The question

You're a journalist / NGO researcher / academic comparing
**offshore jurisdictions** on **transparency** — what public
information is available, what's behind closed doors, and how
the regimes differ?

## The short answer

**All** major offshore jurisdictions have **substantially
improved transparency** since 2010s:

- **Central beneficial-ownership registers** in most
  jurisdictions;
- **Tax transparency** through CRS / FATCA;
- **AML transparency** through FATF compliance;
- **Substance** transparency through annual returns;
- **Public access** to BO information **varies** — most
  jurisdictions limit public access, with authority access.

**Specific** differences exist between jurisdictions in:

- Companies register publicity;
- Trust registration (or its absence);
- Foundation register;
- Charity register publicity;
- Court records publicity.

## The proper answer

### Companies registers

| Jurisdiction | Companies info publicly searchable | BO public access | Trust register |
|---|---|---|---|
| Jersey | Yes (name, directors, status, registered office) | Limited public | No trust registration |
| Guernsey | Yes | Limited public | No trust registration |
| IoM | Yes | Limited public | No trust registration |
| Cayman | Limited | Authority only | No trust registration |
| BVI | Limited | Authority only | No trust registration |
| Bermuda | Yes | Limited public | No trust registration |

### Beneficial ownership

#### Authority access — universal

**All** offshore jurisdictions now have **authority access**
to beneficial ownership through:

- **Central BO register** maintained by regulator;
- **Information** provided by registered agents / TCBs;
- **Authorities** access through specific protocols;
- **FATF Recommendation 24** compliance.

#### Public access — limited

**Most** offshore jurisdictions limit **public access** to BO:

- **Jersey**: not generally public;
- **Guernsey**: not generally public;
- **IoM**: not generally public;
- **Cayman**: not public;
- **BVI**: not public;
- **Bermuda**: not public.

This is **distinct from UK PSC register** which has **full
public access**.

#### Future direction

International pressure (especially FATF and EU) is pushing
for:

- **Wider public access** to BO information;
- **Specific** legitimate-interest access;
- **Specific** Jersey / Guernsey / IoM **public access**
  developments in some configurations.

### Trust transparency

**No major offshore jurisdiction** has a **public trust
register**:

- **Trusts are not registered**;
- **Settlor / trustee / beneficiary** information not
  generally public;
- **Authority access** through specific BO and MLA channels;
- **Specific** Royal Court / equivalent court process for
  disclosure in litigation.

This is consistent across **Jersey, Guernsey, IoM, Cayman,
BVI, Bermuda**.

### Foundation transparency

For jurisdictions with foundation regimes:

- **Jersey**: charter public, regulations private;
- **Guernsey**: similar structure;
- **Specific** other jurisdictions: vary.

### Charity transparency

| Jurisdiction | Charity register publicity |
|---|---|
| Jersey | Three sections — general (public), restricted (limited), historic |
| Guernsey | Similar structure |
| IoM | Public register |
| Cayman | Limited charity transparency |
| BVI | Limited |
| Bermuda | Specific Bermuda rules |

### Tax transparency

**All** offshore jurisdictions participate in:

- **CRS** — automatic exchange of financial-account
  information;
- **FATCA** — exchange with US;
- **CbCR** — country-by-country reporting for large groups;
- **Substance** annual reports.

This is **comprehensive** but **not public** — authorities
exchange, not public.

### Court records

| Jurisdiction | Court record publicity |
|---|---|
| Jersey | Limited public; some judgments published |
| Guernsey | Similar |
| IoM | More public English-style |
| Cayman | Limited; FSD judgments published selectively |
| BVI | Limited |
| Bermuda | Limited; some publication |

### Financial-services licensing

**All** offshore jurisdictions have **public lists** of
licensed firms:

- **Names**;
- **Categories** of authorisation;
- **Status**;
- **Specific** licence conditions in some cases.

### Comparison with onshore

**Offshore transparency** lags UK / EU in specific areas:

- **Public BO access** (UK has it; offshore generally not);
- **Court records** (UK / US more open);
- **Trust information** (similar offshore / onshore);
- **Tax transparency** (similar — through CRS / FATCA).

But offshore transparency has **substantially improved**:

- 1990s/2000s: significant opacity;
- 2010s: AML / FATF improvements;
- 2020s: BO registers, substance, Pillar Two;
- Now: substantial transparency to authorities, limited
  public.

### Practical implications

For researchers / journalists:

#### What you can get publicly

- **Companies** basic information in most jurisdictions;
- **Licensed firms** lists;
- **Charities** in general-section registers;
- **Foundations'** charters (Jersey, Guernsey);
- **Court** judgments selectively;
- **Major** enforcement actions.

#### What you cannot get publicly

- **Beneficial ownership** generally;
- **Trust information**;
- **Foundation** regulations;
- **Specific** transaction terms;
- **Customer-level** financial data;
- **Internal** business records.

#### Authority-mediated access

For specific information:

- **Foreign tax authority** → home authority → offshore
  authority through MLA channels;
- **Foreign court** → letter of request → offshore court;
- **Foreign regulator** → MoU → offshore regulator;
- **Foreign AML** → Egmont → offshore FIU.

These channels are **closed to public researchers**.

### Strategies for researchers

For investigating offshore structures:

1. **Identify** the jurisdiction(s);
2. **Search** publicly available registers;
3. **Cross-reference** licensed firms involved;
4. **Look for** court judgments and enforcement actions;
5. **Track** through onshore connections;
6. **Use** Panama Papers / Pandora Papers leaks where
   relevant;
7. **FOI requests** in source jurisdictions if applicable;
8. **Engage** with offshore investigative journalism
   networks.

## What to do next

For a transparency investigation:

1. **Map** what's publicly available across the relevant
   jurisdictions;
2. **Use** consolidated tools (e.g., OCCRP, ICIJ Offshore
   Leaks Database);
3. **Cross-reference** with onshore sources;
4. **Specific** licensed-firm searches;
5. **Court records** searches;
6. **Document** the public-record findings.

## Pitfalls

- **Expecting** full transparency in offshore;
- **Missing** the authority-vs-public distinction;
- **Not** cross-referencing across jurisdictions;
- **Specific** transparency improvements not appreciated;
- **Specific** access routes through FOI / public records
  unused.
