# Beneficial-Ownership Transparency vs Financial-Privacy Balance

## The short answer

Jersey's position on BO transparency vs financial privacy:

- **Strong AML / CFT compliance** with authority-access BO
  central register;
- **Privacy considerations** preserved through non-public
  default;
- **International pressure** ongoing for public access;
- **Industry concerns** about over-transparency cited;
- **NGO advocacy** for full public access continues;
- **Likely evolution** depending on international consensus.

The **policy debate** is genuine — both transparency and privacy
have legitimate values; the balance is calibrated through
political process.

## The proper answer

### Two values in tension

#### Transparency

Arguments for public BO transparency:

- **AML / CFT effectiveness** improved by public scrutiny;
- **Tax-evasion** harder to hide;
- **Corruption / financial crime** investigated more easily;
- **Public-interest journalism** facilitated;
- **Investor confidence** in markets;
- **Democratic** accountability for offshore finance.

#### Privacy

Arguments for limited public access:

- **Legitimate** business confidentiality;
- **Family security** for HNW individuals;
- **Commercial competition** protection;
- **Privacy** as a value in itself;
- **Misuse risk** of public BO (kidnapping, extortion in some
  jurisdictions);
- **Data-protection** principles.

### Jersey's current calibration

- **Authority access**: full;
- **Public access**: limited (no general public BO register);
- **Litigation access**: through court orders in proper cases;
- **Foreign-authority cooperation**: through proper channels.

### Pressure for change

#### EU

- **5th MLD** required public BO in EU member states;
- **Jersey not EU** but EU exemplar influential;
- **Code of Conduct Group** engagement.

#### UK

- **Public PSC register** since 2016;
- **Domestic UK** standard for comparison;
- **Crown Dependencies** under UK political pressure for
  similar.

#### Court decisions

- **CJEU 2022** decision restricting EU public BO access
  partially (privacy ruling);
- **Complicating** the EU public-access mandate;
- **Specific** Jersey impact limited.

#### NGO advocacy

- **Transparency International**;
- **Tax Justice Network**;
- **Open Ownership**;
- **Argue** for full public access.

### Jersey's specific approach

#### Authority access — robust

- **JFSC, Revenue Jersey, law enforcement**: full access;
- **Foreign authorities**: through proper channels;
- **Updated** as ownership changes;
- **Verified** by service providers.

#### Public access — limited

- **Specific** categories may be public (Persons with
  Significant Control for some entities);
- **General** BO not public;
- **Information requests** specific procedure.

#### Litigation access

- **Court order** in proper cases;
- **Adverse party** can seek disclosure;
- **Specific** procedural requirements.

### Industry perspective

#### Concerns about full public access

- **Privacy** of legitimate clients;
- **Reputational** spillover to legitimate uses;
- **Cost** of compliance higher;
- **Competitive disadvantage** vs less-transparent jurisdictions;
- **Specific** security concerns for HNW individuals.

#### Industry as constructive

- **Cooperation** with current authority-access regime;
- **Compliance** with CRS / FATCA;
- **Engagement** with regulatory consultations;
- **Reputation** management.

### NGO perspective

#### Arguments for public access

- **Authority-only** insufficient — civil society needs access;
- **Investigations** depend on data accessibility;
- **Corruption** thrives in opacity;
- **Public-interest** justifies privacy compromise.

#### Acknowledgement of progress

- **Reform trajectory** in Jersey acknowledged;
- **Continuing gaps** highlighted;
- **Comparative** with other jurisdictions noted.

### Possible future evolution

#### Drivers

- **International consensus** evolution;
- **EU policy** developments;
- **Specific** scandals or events;
- **Domestic political** considerations.

#### Possible paths

- **Full public** BO access;
- **Verified-applicant** access (broader than authority but
  not fully public);
- **Sector-specific** transparency (e.g. real estate);
- **Continuation** of current model.

### For journalists / NGOs

#### Working within current framework

- **Public records** for what's available;
- **Authority cooperation** through proper channels;
- **Court records** where litigation exists;
- **Leaked records** when authoritative;
- **Cross-jurisdictional** triangulation.

#### Advocacy

- **Public-policy** engagement through proper channels;
- **Consultation responses** to JFSC papers;
- **International** advocacy through FATF / OECD;
- **Reporting** that highlights specific cases for context.

### Reporting context

#### Avoid

- **Overstating** Jersey opacity;
- **Conflating** with worse jurisdictions;
- **Ignoring** reform trajectory.

#### Include

- **Specific transparency** measures in place;
- **Specific gaps** acknowledged;
- **Reform trajectory**;
- **Cross-jurisdictional** comparison.

### Critical perspectives

There are **legitimate** different positions:

- **Privacy advocates**: emphasise non-public default;
- **Transparency advocates**: emphasise public access;
- **Industry**: emphasises balance;
- **Government**: emphasises sovereign calibration;
- **NGOs**: emphasise civil-society needs.

Accurate reporting acknowledges all sides.

## What to do next

For research / reporting:

1. **Understand** specific transparency measures;
2. **Identify** specific gaps if relevant;
3. **Acknowledge** policy debate;
4. **Report** with appropriate context.

## Pitfalls

- **One-sided** framing on either side;
- **Outdated** information on actual transparency;
- **Stereotyping**;
- **Privacy / personal data** concerns in reporting.
