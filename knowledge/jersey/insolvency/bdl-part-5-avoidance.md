# BDL 1990 Part 5 — Avoidance Powers (residual)

Articles 17A (preferences) and 17B (transactions at
undervalue) have their own deep files. This cluster covers
Articles 17C-17D.

## Article 17C — Fraudulent transfers

A **fraudulent transfer** is one made with **intent to
defraud creditors**:

- **Intent** is the key element — the transferor must have
  intended to put property beyond reach of creditors;
- **Insolvency** at the time is **not required** (unlike
  preferences and undervalue) — the actio Pauliana basis is
  broader;
- **No statutory lookback** in the strict sense — the
  challenge can be made under common-law / customary-law
  fraudulent-transfer doctrines as well as statutory powers.

### Examples

- **Transfers to a trust** specifically to defeat a known
  creditor;
- **Asset transfers** at the threshold of insolvency to
  family members;
- **Disposing of assets** while litigation is being prepared
  by creditors.

### Evidence of intent

Indicators:

- **Timing** — close to insolvency or known dispute;
- **Connected party** transferees;
- **Continued use** by debtor after transfer (settlor of
  trust still benefits);
- **Concealment** — transfer not disclosed;
- **Inadequate** consideration.

### Effect of avoidance

If proven:

- The transfer is **set aside**;
- Property is **restored** to the estate;
- **Third-party recipients** (subsequent purchasers) may have
  defences as BFPV without notice.

### Customary-law base

Jersey's **Pauliana** doctrine (from Roman/Norman customary
law) supports fraudulent-transfer claims **independently** of
the statute — relevant for:

- Transactions outside statutory lookback periods;
- Foreign-jurisdiction transfers with Jersey nexus;
- Non-désastre actions by individual creditors.

## Article 17D — Floating charges

Floating charges granted in the period before désastre may be
**invalidated**:

- **Lookback period** — typically **12 months** for
  unconnected creditors, longer for connected;
- **No new value** rule — the charge is invalid to the
  extent it secures **pre-existing** rather than new debt;
- **Specific protections** for charges granted for **new
  value** at or about the time the charge is granted.

Floating charges over Jersey property are relatively
uncommon (Jersey law has its own security interest rules
under the Security Interests (Jersey) Law 2012), but where
present, Article 17D applies.

## Coordination with Companies Law

For **company debtors**:

- BDL avoidance powers apply if désastre is the chosen route;
- **Equivalent** powers exist under Companies Law Part 21 if
  creditors' winding-up is chosen;
- **Substantive tests** are aligned but procedural vehicles
  differ.

## Practical implications

For practitioners advising:

- **Pre-insolvency planning** needs careful consideration of
  lookback periods;
- **Transfers** to family or related parties carry heightened
  scrutiny;
- **Documentation** of commercial purpose and value crucial;
- **Asset-protection** structures should be established well
  ahead of any known insolvency risk (years, not months).
