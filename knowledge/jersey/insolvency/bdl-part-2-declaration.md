# BDL 1990 Part 2 — Declaration of Désastre (residual)

Articles 3, 4, and 6 have their own deep files. This cluster
covers Articles 5, 7-9.

## Article 5 — Court's discretion

Even where grounds for désastre exist (Article 4), the Court
**may refuse** the declaration if:

- The application is an **abuse of process**;
- The debtor can **pay all debts** despite some being
  unsatisfied;
- A **less drastic alternative** is available;
- The application is **vexatious** or **oppressive**;
- For other **substantial reasons**.

In practice the discretion is **rarely exercised against
declaration** for voluntary applications, and reasonably
sparingly for creditor applications where grounds are clearly
made out.

## Article 7 — Pre-declaration matters

Between application and declaration:

- The **debtor** retains capacity to deal with property
  (subject to specific freezing orders);
- The **applicant** may apply for **interim orders** to
  preserve assets pending the hearing;
- **Other creditors** may join in the application;
- **Notice** is given to specified persons.

## Article 8 — Date of declaration

The declaration takes effect:

- On the **date specified** in the Order;
- Typically the **date of the Order** itself;
- **Sometimes** retrospective to the date of application
  in specified cases.

The date is significant for:

- **Avoidance** lookback periods (preferences,
  undervalue transactions);
- **Priority** of claims (post-désastre debts treated
  differently);
- **Discharge** timing.

## Article 9 — Recall / setting aside

The Court may **recall** or **set aside** a declaration:

- For **error** in the declaration;
- If the **grounds** were not established (in retrospect);
- For **change of circumstances** (debtor pays debts);
- For **fraud** in obtaining the declaration;
- For other **just cause**.

Setting aside is **exceptional** — once désastre is in
progress, unwinding it is complex.
