# BDL 1990 Article 17A — Preferences

Article 17A is one of the **avoidance powers** — the Viscount
can apply to **set aside preferences** given by the debtor in
the period before désastre.

## The provision

A transaction is a **preference** if:

- The debtor **does anything** (pays a debt, grants security,
  transfers property);
- That puts a **creditor** (or guarantor of a debt) in a
  **better position** than they would otherwise be in on
  désastre;
- The debtor was **influenced** by a desire to put the
  creditor in that better position; and
- The transaction was made within the **lookback period**.

## Lookback periods

The lookback period depends on the relationship between the
debtor and the recipient:

- **Connected parties** (relatives, associates, controlled
  entities) — **12 months** or longer in some cases;
- **Unconnected creditors** — **6 months**;
- **Connected** status is **presumed** for related parties;
- **Influence** is **presumed** for connected parties (rebuttable).

## The "influenced by desire" test

The Article 17A test focuses on the **debtor's motivation**:

- The debtor must have been **influenced** by a desire to
  prefer the creditor;
- **Ordinary course** payments to creditors are generally
  not preferences (no preferring intent);
- **Pressure** from a creditor does not establish a
  preference (the debtor's intent is paid under pressure, not
  to prefer);
- **Last-minute** payments to favoured creditors before
  désastre commonly satisfy the test.

## Examples of preferences

### Clear preferences

- **Paying** a particular creditor in full while leaving
  others unpaid;
- **Granting security** for a previously unsecured debt;
- **Discharging** a guarantor's exposure by paying the
  principal creditor;
- **Transferring** assets to a connected party in
  satisfaction of an old debt.

### Not preferences

- **Ordinary trade** payments in the normal course;
- **Payments compelled** by litigation / judgment;
- **Payments under** existing security held by the creditor;
- **Cash sales** for value received.

## Effect of avoidance

If the Court declares a preference:

- The **transaction is set aside**;
- **Property** is restored to the estate;
- **The Viscount** can recover the value from the
  recipient;
- **Recipient's position** reverts to creditor status, proving
  in the désastre alongside others.

## Defences

The recipient may defend on grounds:

- The debtor was **not influenced** by a desire to prefer
  (rebutting the presumption for connected parties);
- The transaction was **in good faith** ordinary course;
- **Value** was given for the property received;
- The recipient was a **BFPV without notice** of insolvency
  / preferring intent.

## Comparison with company creditors' winding-up

Similar preference rules apply to companies in **creditors'
winding-up** under Companies Law Part 21. The substantive
test is broadly the same; the procedural vehicle differs
(liquidator rather than Viscount).
