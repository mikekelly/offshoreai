# Bankruptcy (Désastre) (Jersey) Law 1990 — Overview

The **Bankruptcy (Désastre) (Jersey) Law 1990 (BDL)** is
Jersey's principal **statutory insolvency regime**. It applies
to:

- **Individuals** (natural persons);
- **Companies** (Jersey-incorporated and certain foreign);
- **Other legal persons** (foundations, certain partnerships).

The procedure is **court-supervised** — the Royal Court
declares the debtor's estate "*en désastre*" ("in disaster"),
which triggers a statutory process of asset realisation and
distribution to creditors.

## How désastre works (in summary)

1. **Application** for declaration of désastre by:
   - The **debtor** (voluntary);
   - A **creditor** with a substantial claim;
   - Specific other parties.
2. The **Royal Court declares** the debtor's estate en
   désastre on satisfaction of statutory grounds.
3. The **Viscount** (the office of the executive of the Royal
   Court) takes control of the debtor's assets.
4. The Viscount **collects** assets, **realises** them, and
   **distributes** proceeds to creditors per statutory
   priority.
5. **Avoidance** powers apply to set aside certain pre-
   désastre transactions (preferences, undervalue
   transactions, fraudulent transfers).
6. The debtor is **discharged** at the end of the process
   (subject to statutory exceptions for certain debts).

## Désastre vs creditors' winding-up

For **insolvent companies**, the practitioner choice is
between désastre and creditors' winding-up under the
Companies Law 1991 Part 21:

| Feature | Désastre (BDL) | Creditors' winding-up (CJL Part 21) |
|---|---|---|
| Applies to | Individuals + companies | Companies only |
| Officeholder | Viscount | Liquidator (private practitioner) |
| Court supervision | Significant | More limited |
| Speed | Slower (court process) | Faster (creditor-led) |
| Cost | Lower legal cost typically | Higher legal cost (liquidator fees) |
| Specialist liquidator | No (Viscount) | Yes (chosen by creditors) |
| Cross-border recognition | Established | Established |
| Avoidance powers | Yes | Yes |

The choice often turns on the **complexity** of the
insolvency, the **scale** of the assets, and **creditor
preferences**.

## Key concepts

### "En désastre"

The legal status declared by the Royal Court — analogous to
"bankruptcy" (individual) or "compulsory liquidation"
(company) in English law.

### The Viscount

The **Viscount** is the Royal Court's executive officer with
historic origins. In désastre, the Viscount:

- **Takes control** of the debtor's estate;
- **Collects** assets;
- **Realises** assets through sale;
- **Investigates** the debtor's affairs;
- **Distributes** proceeds to creditors per priority;
- **Reports** to the Royal Court;
- **Investigates** suspected wrongdoing.

The Viscount is **not a private liquidator** — it is a public
office paid through statutory fees, not creditor-funded
remuneration.

### Avoidance powers

The Viscount can apply to set aside:

- **Preferences** — payments / transactions favouring one
  creditor over another in the period before désastre;
- **Transactions at undervalue** — sales at less than market
  value to remove assets from the estate;
- **Fraudulent transfers** — transfers with intent to defeat
  creditors;
- **Floating charges** created shortly before désastre.

### Priority of claims

Statutory priority (approximate):

1. **Costs and expenses** of the désastre;
2. **Preferential creditors** — recent employee wages,
   certain tax obligations;
3. **Secured creditors** to the extent of their security
   (with reservation of equity above);
4. **Unsecured creditors** pari passu;
5. **Subordinated creditors** (e.g., shareholder loans
   contractually subordinated);
6. **Members / shareholders** (residual).

### Discharge

For individual debtors:

- **Automatic** after a prescribed period (typically 4 years);
- **Subject to** certain debts being unrecoverable (recent
  fraud, court fines, family-court orders);
- **Restrictions** on future borrowing during désastre;
- **Restoration** of capacity after discharge.

For companies, désastre typically ends in **dissolution**
rather than discharge — the company ceases to exist.

## Foreign insolvency

The BDL provides for:

- **Recognition** of foreign insolvency proceedings;
- **Cooperation** with foreign courts and officeholders;
- **Cross-border** asset recovery;
- **Royal Court inherent** jurisdiction supplementing
  statutory provisions.

Jersey is not a party to the UNCITRAL Model Law on Cross-
Border Insolvency, but its Royal Court has shown willingness
to cooperate with foreign proceedings through inherent
jurisdiction and treaty arrangements.
