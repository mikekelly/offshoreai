# ITL Article 134A — Anti-Avoidance

Article 134A of the **[Income Tax (Jersey) Law 1961][itl]** is
Jersey's **general anti-avoidance rule (GAAR)** — giving the
**Comptroller of Revenue** power to address tax-avoidance
arrangements.

## What Article 134A covers

The Comptroller may, in defined circumstances, **disregard or
recharacterise** transactions or arrangements where:

- They were entered into for the **purpose** (or one of the
  main purposes) of obtaining a **tax advantage**;
- The advantage would **defeat the intention** of the Law;
- The arrangement is **artificial or contrived**.

The Comptroller may then **adjust** the tax position of affected
persons to **counteract** the avoidance.

## Why this matters

Modern tax systems supplement specific anti-avoidance provisions
with **general anti-avoidance rules** to catch arrangements that
technically comply with specific provisions but defeat their
purpose. Article 134A is Jersey's GAAR equivalent.

Practical implications:

- **Aggressive tax planning** beyond clear legal entitlements
  faces Comptroller challenge;
- **Specific avoidance rules** elsewhere in the Law (e.g.
  distribution rules under Article 81A–81P; transfer-pricing
  approaches) operate alongside the GAAR;
- The **purpose test** is key — legitimate commercial
  arrangements with incidental tax benefits are generally
  outside the GAAR; arrangements where the tax benefit is the
  **principal purpose** are at risk.

## Procedural protections

The GAAR is not unilateral Comptroller power:

- **Right of appeal** to the Commissioners of Appeal and Royal
  Court;
- **Reasoned determination** required from the Comptroller;
- **Disclosure** of the basis for the GAAR-based adjustment;
- **Due process** in the application.
