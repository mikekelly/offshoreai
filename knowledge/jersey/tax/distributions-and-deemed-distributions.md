# Distributions and Deemed Distributions

The **distribution rules** govern how income flowing from a
Jersey company to its **Jersey-resident shareholders** is taxed —
filling the gap left by the 0% corporate rate under zero-ten.

## The policy problem

Under zero-ten, most Jersey companies pay **0% corporate tax**.
If shareholders could leave income in those companies indefinitely
and access it tax-free, Jersey-resident individuals would have
an obvious way to escape the **20% personal income tax** rate.

The distribution rules solve this by taxing the **shareholder**
on company income — either when distributed or, in some cases,
when **deemed** distributed.

## Distributions to Jersey-resident shareholders

When a Jersey-resident individual receives a **distribution** from
a Jersey company:

- The distribution is **taxable income** of the individual at the
  20% personal rate (subject to marginal relief);
- The company is **not** charged a separate distribution tax
  under zero-ten (Article 123C(2) companies do not deduct);
- For **Article 123D 10% companies** (financial services), a
  10% credit may apply to recognise the tax already paid at the
  company level.

## Deemed distributions

In some cases, Jersey law **deems** that a distribution has
occurred, even though no actual distribution has been made:

- **Excess retentions** by a closely-held company that should be
  distributing but isn't;
- **Loans to participators** in certain circumstances;
- **Specific anti-avoidance** triggers in the Income Tax Law.

The deemed-distribution rules are **anti-avoidance** measures
designed to prevent Jersey-resident shareholders from leaving
income trapped in a 0%-rate company.

## Distributions to non-resident shareholders

Distributions from a Jersey company to a **non-resident**
shareholder are **not taxed** by Jersey:

- No Jersey withholding (Article 123C(2) companies — no deduct);
- No Jersey personal tax (the recipient is non-resident).

The non-resident recipient may be taxed by their **home
jurisdiction** on receipt — that is a question for foreign tax
law, not Jersey law.

## Practical implications

- **Family companies** with Jersey-resident shareholders need to
  consider the distribution regime carefully — undistributed
  earnings can trigger deemed-distribution tax.
- **Offshore holding companies** owned by foreign shareholders
  can accumulate tax-free under zero-ten without Jersey
  consequences.
- **Group structures** with mixed residence of shareholders need
  bespoke analysis.
