# Zero-Ten — Jersey Corporate Income Tax

The Jersey corporate income-tax regime is called **"zero-ten"** because
its headline rate is **0%** and the next rate (for financial-services
companies) is **10%**. The full picture is more complicated than that
shorthand suggests: there is also a 20% rate that applies to a defined
list of categories, and a small group of special regimes. This file sets
out the structure, the rates, the categories, and the principal Articles
of the [Income Tax (Jersey) Law 1961][itl] under which they are imposed.

## The headline rates

| Rate | Applies to | Statutory basis |
|---|---|---|
| **0%** | The default rate for every company that is not in one of the lists below | [Article 123C(2)][itl] |
| **10%** | "Financial services companies" — a defined list of regulated activities | [Article 123D][itl] |
| **20%** | Utility companies | [Article 123C(3)][itl] |
| **20%** | Large corporate retailers (with a taper between £500,000 and £750,000 of taxable profits, plus a £2m turnover-in-Jersey gateway) | [Article 123I][itl]; [Revenue Jersey LCR guidance PDF][lcr] |
| **20%** | Importation or supply of hydrocarbon oil ("hydrocarbon trade") | [Article 123CAA(1)][itl] |
| **20%** | Cannabis-industry trade | [Article 123DA][itl] |
| **20%** | Profits from Jersey land and property (income from Jersey land and from Jersey property development) | [Income Tax (Jersey) Law 1961][itl] (see Revenue Jersey guidance) |
| **0%** | "Registered pension company" status — separate concession under [Article 131B][itl] | [Article 131B][itl] |

Two further regimes sit beside the headline rates rather than as part
of them:

- **Public collective investment funds.** Treated separately under the
  Collective Investment Funds (Jersey) Law 1988 mechanism: see the
  Revenue Jersey corporate-tax guidance and the [funds/](../funds/index.md)
  section.
- **Private collective investment funds** and **eligible investment
  schemes** — separate treatment under [Article 118C][itl].

Notations on rates always citing the article number lets the reader
walk back to the statute on jerseylaw.je in one click.

## The 0% standard rate (Article 123C)

Under **Article 123C(2)** of the Income Tax (Jersey) Law 1961, a company
is charged to Jersey income tax at the rate of **0%** on its profits and
income — unless it falls within one of the categories below. In Revenue
Jersey's words on its public corporate-tax page, a company "subject to
tax at the rate of 0% in accordance with Article 123C(2)" is in
substance the default state for a Jersey company. ([Revenue Jersey
guidance notes — section 1][rj-guidance])

In practical terms:

- A Jersey trading company that is not a financial-services company,
  utility, large retailer, hydrocarbon trader, cannabis trader, or
  Jersey-property income-earner is taxed at **0%** on its profits.
- An ordinary Jersey holding company likewise sits at **0%**.
- A non-Jersey-resident company is not within Jersey corporate tax
  on profits other than profits from a Jersey establishment or from
  Jersey land. (See [company-residence.md](company-residence.md).)

A company within Article 123C(2) "shall not be entitled, when paying a
dividend, to make any deduction" of tax from the dividend, by contrast
with a company within Article 123D (which deducts at 10%) — see
[distributions-and-deemed-distributions.md](distributions-and-deemed-distributions.md).

## The 10% rate — "financial services companies" (Article 123D)

**Article 123D** of the Income Tax (Jersey) Law 1961 charges
"financial services companies" at a rate of **10%** on their profits.
The definition of "financial services company" is the gateway and it is
narrower than the lay term suggests: it covers companies *regulated under
the relevant Jersey financial-services Laws* in the specified classes,
not finance-related businesses in general.

A company falls within Article 123D where it is:

- **Registered under the [Financial Services (Jersey) Law 1998][fsjl]**
  to carry on (i) **investment business**, (ii) **trust company business**,
  (iii) **fund services business** as administrator, custodian or
  registrar in relation to an unclassified or unregistered fund, or
  (iv) **general insurance mediation business** — see the Article
  123D(4) sub-paragraphs and the JFSC class definitions in the
  [financial-regulation/](../financial-regulation/index.md) section;
- **Registered under the [Banking Business (Jersey) Law 1991][bbl]**
  to take deposits (Article 123D(4)(b)) — covered banks are taxed at
  10% on the whole of their profits; this is the original target of
  the regime; or
- The holder of a **permit under the Collective Investment Funds
  (Jersey) Law 1988** as administrator, registrar or custodian of a
  public fund; or
- The holder of a **Category A or Category B permit under the
  Insurance Business (Jersey) Law 1996**.

A separate category under **Article 123D(4)(e)** brings within the 10%
rate companies "trading in providing credit facilities into the Jersey
domestic finance market" — and Revenue Jersey has published a statement
of practice clarifying that credit offerings *outside* the Jersey
domestic finance market (e.g. to non-Jersey residents, to non-Jersey
businesses, or for non-Jersey real-property acquisition) are *not*
within scope. ([Revenue Jersey Statement of Practice — Article 123D(4)(e)][sop])

> Policy intent: the Article 123D(4)(e) limb was introduced to "level
> the playing field" between deposit-taking banks taxable at 10% and
> non-bank lenders that compete with them in the Jersey domestic
> finance market. [Revenue Jersey statement of practice][sop]

A company within Article 123D may, when paying a dividend, deduct tax
at the rate of 10% — see
[distributions-and-deemed-distributions.md](distributions-and-deemed-distributions.md).

## The 20% rate — utility companies (Article 123C(3))

Under **Article 123C(3)**, a company that is a **utility company** is
taxed at **20%** on its profits. The category captures the regulated
utilities serving the Island — historically Jersey Electricity
Company, Jersey Telecom (JT), Jersey Water (the Jersey New Waterworks
Company), and gas. ([Revenue Jersey guidance notes — section 1][rj-guidance])

The statutory definition is in the Article itself and references
specified regulated utility activities. A company that is a *subsidiary*
of a utility but does not itself supply the regulated service is not
automatically within the 20% rate; the right analysis is by reference
to the activity definition rather than the corporate group, and Revenue
Jersey's guidance notes are the relevant interpretative aid.

## The 20% rate — large corporate retailers (Article 123I, with taper)

**Article 123I** of the Income Tax (Jersey) Law 1961 charges "large
corporate retailers" at **20%** on the whole of their taxable profits,
subject to:

- a **turnover gateway** of **£2 million** in annual turnover **in
  Jersey** (a company below the gateway is not a large corporate
  retailer at all and is taxed at 0%);
- a **profits-band taper** that produces the following effective rates
  ([Revenue Jersey LCR guidance][lcr]):

| Taxable profits | Effective rate |
|---|---|
| **< £500,000** | 0% on all profits |
| **£500,000 – £750,000** | A taper that increases linearly from 0% up to 20% |
| **≥ £750,000** | 20% on **all** profits (not just the excess) |

A "retailer" for this purpose is a company carrying on a retail trade —
the sale of goods to consumers in Jersey. The threshold and taper sit
on top of that activity definition.

## The 20% rate — hydrocarbon and cannabis trades

- **Hydrocarbon trade.** **Article 123CAA(1)** taxes the **importation
  or supply of hydrocarbon oil** at **20%**. The category targets motor
  fuel and home heating oil supplied in Jersey. ([Revenue Jersey
  guidance notes — section 1][rj-guidance])
- **Cannabis-industry trade.** **Article 123DA** taxes the cannabis
  trade at **20%**. The category was added with the legalisation of
  medicinal-cannabis cultivation and processing in Jersey. ([Revenue
  Jersey guidance notes — section 1][rj-guidance])

## Income from Jersey land and property

Profits arising from Jersey land — rental income, the proceeds of
Jersey property development by Jersey-resident companies, and similar —
are taxed at **20%**. The detailed mechanism is in the Income Tax Law's
property provisions; see the Revenue Jersey company-tax guidance for the
return-form treatment. This is the **one major exception** to the
"zero-ten on profits, no tax on capital gain" generalisation: although
there is no Jersey capital-gains tax, *trading* in Jersey property by
a company is taxed at 20% on profits.

## How the regime interacts with shareholder taxation

The 0% rate at company level was the source of the regime's main
political-economy problem in the 2000s: it had to be paired with rules
that brought distributions home to Jersey-resident shareholders so as
not to leak the Jersey tax base. Those rules — **deemed distribution**
and **shareholder-loan** rules — are covered separately in
[distributions-and-deemed-distributions.md](distributions-and-deemed-distributions.md).
For non-Jersey-resident shareholders, a 0% Jersey company typically
involves no Jersey withholding (because Article 123C(2) companies do
not deduct), and Jersey simply does not tax the foreign holder.

## What sits alongside zero-ten

- **Economic substance.** Jersey-resident companies engaged in defined
  "relevant activities" are subject to a separate substance regime
  under the Taxation (Companies — Economic Substance) (Jersey) Law 2019.
  This is in addition to, not part of, zero-ten — see
  [economic-substance.md](economic-substance.md).
- **OECD Pillar Two.** Jersey has implemented the OECD Pillar Two 15%
  minimum tax for large in-scope multinational groups, via a domestic
  top-up. This sits over the top of zero-ten for groups in scope —
  see [pillar-two.md](pillar-two.md).

## Historical note

The zero-ten regime was introduced from **YA 2009** to replace the
pre-existing 20% standard corporate rate combined with "exempt" and
"international business company" regimes that were judged not to be
compatible with the EU Code of Conduct on Business Taxation. The current
shape — 0% standard, 10% for financial-services companies, 20% for
the listed categories — is the result of a series of amendments
between 2008 and the present, including the introduction of the
large-corporate-retailer regime (Article 123I), the hydrocarbon and
cannabis rates, and the Article 123D(4)(e) gateway. The historical
detail is summarised in the *Jersey & Guernsey Law Review* commentary
on jerseylaw.je.
