# ITL Article 123 — Charge of Tax on Body Corporate; Residence

Article 123 of the **[Income Tax (Jersey) Law 1961][itl]** is the
**gateway** to the zero-ten corporate tax regime — it charges
income tax on a body corporate **resident in Jersey** and
defines what "resident" means.

## What Article 123 covers

A body corporate is **resident in Jersey** for the purposes of
the Law if **either**:

- It is **incorporated in Jersey**; **or**
- Its **business is managed and controlled** in Jersey.

A Jersey-resident body corporate is **chargeable to income tax**
under the Law on its income subject to the **zero-ten rate
structure** in Articles 123A–123S.

(See the consolidated text at [jerseylaw.je][itl].)

## The two-limb residence test

The residence test is **broader** than the UK's incorporation-
plus-CMC test:

- **Incorporation** in Jersey = automatic residence (the easy
  case);
- **Management and control** in Jersey = residence even without
  Jersey incorporation (the catch-all).

A foreign-incorporated body managed from Jersey is therefore
**also a Jersey resident** for tax purposes — typically dual-
resident, with treaty tie-breakers (if any) resolving the
position.

## The zero-ten rate structure

Articles 123A–123S then set the rate:

- **Article 123A** — standard **0%** rate (most Jersey
  companies);
- **Article 123C** — **10%** rate for **financial services
  companies** as defined;
- **Article 123D** — **10%** or **20%** rate for specific
  categories (utility companies, large corporate retailers, etc.);
- **Article 123E onwards** — specific provisions, distribution
  rules, group relief, etc.

The vast majority of Jersey companies fall under the 0% rate.

## Why this matters

Article 123 is the **legal foundation** of zero-ten:

- Without Article 123 charge, no Jersey company would be subject
  to Jersey income tax;
- The **0% rate** is the headline feature making Jersey a finance
  centre;
- The **10% and 20% categories** are the carve-outs for
  regulated and large-scale businesses.
