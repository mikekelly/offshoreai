# Company Tax Residence in Jersey

A company's **tax residence** in Jersey is the threshold question
that determines whether the company is **liable to Jersey income
tax at all** under the zero-ten regime.

## The two-limb test

A company is **resident in Jersey for tax purposes** if **either**:

- It is **incorporated in Jersey**; **or**
- Its **management and control** are exercised in Jersey.

(See Article 123 of the **[Income Tax (Jersey) Law 1961][itl]**.)

This is a **wider** test than the UK's pure-incorporation test
— Jersey catches foreign-incorporated companies that are
**managed and controlled** here, in addition to the locally-
incorporated ones.

## The first limb — Jersey incorporation

A company **incorporated under the Companies (Jersey) Law 1991**
is automatically Jersey-resident for tax purposes.

This is the **default** for Jersey holding companies and
operating entities. Most Jersey corporate tax planning starts
from this position.

## The second limb — management and control

A **foreign-incorporated company** can become Jersey-resident if
its **management and control** are in Jersey. The traditional
test, drawn from English case law (De Beers v Howe), looks at:

- **Where directors meet** to decide strategic matters;
- **Where high-level policy decisions** are made;
- **Where authority over the company's affairs** actually resides
  (irrespective of the registered office or where employees
  work).

Practical indicators:

- **Place of board meetings**;
- **Residence of directors** taking key decisions;
- **Approval and execution** of major contracts;
- **Strategic and financial control**.

## Avoiding inadvertent Jersey residence

Foreign-incorporated companies operating with Jersey directors,
or with significant Jersey-based decision-making, can find
themselves **inadvertently Jersey-resident**. Common scenarios:

- A **Cayman or BVI company** with a Jersey-based service provider
  conducting board meetings in Jersey;
- A **UK-domiciled company** that fails to manage its CEO's
  Jersey residency carefully;
- **Trust-owned foreign companies** where Jersey trustees
  effectively make corporate decisions.

The consequence is generally **dual residence** — Jersey-resident
under the management-and-control test, and home-resident under
its incorporation test. **Tie-breaker rules** in any applicable
DTA determine the outcome. For non-DTA jurisdictions, both
jurisdictions can claim taxing rights.

## Becoming Jersey-non-resident

A Jersey-incorporated company **cannot easily become
non-resident** because the incorporation limb of the test pins
residence to Jersey. Options:

- **Continuance out** of Jersey under Part 18C of the Companies
  Law (the company ceases to be Jersey-incorporated);
- **Merger out** of Jersey under Part 18B with a foreign merged
  body;
- **Liquidation** and re-incorporation abroad.

Each of these is a **significant step** with corporate, tax, and
commercial implications.

## Why this matters

Tax residence drives:

- **Whether** the company is subject to Jersey income tax at all;
- **Which rate** applies under zero-ten (0% / 10% / 20%);
- **Whether** the company is in scope for Jersey **economic
  substance** rules (a separate concept that overlaps with but
  isn't identical to tax residence);
- **Whether** the company features in Jersey's **CRS / FATCA**
  reporting;
- **Whether** the company is in scope for Pillar Two **DMT** as a
  Jersey constituent entity.
