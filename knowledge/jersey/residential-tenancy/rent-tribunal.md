# The Jersey Rent Tribunal (2026)

## What it is

The **Rent Tribunal** is an **independent tribunal**
established by the 2025 amendment and operative from 15
April 2026. It is the **dedicated forum for disputes about
rent increases** in private residential tenancies.

It does **not** handle:

- Eviction or breach disputes — these remain with the
  Petty Debts Court (see [`eviction-procedure.md`](eviction-procedure.md));
- Deposit disputes — these go through the Tenants' Deposit
  Scheme (see [`deposits.md`](deposits.md));
- Disrepair / condition disputes — Petty Debts Court;
- Social housing rent — separate regime applies.

## Jurisdiction

The Tribunal has jurisdiction over:

- Whether a **proposed increase exceeds RPI**;
- Whether the **"significantly below market rent"
  carve-out** applies (and to what extent);
- Whether the **once-yearly rule** has been breached;
- Whether **procedural requirements** (2-month notice,
  written form, RPI calculation) have been complied with;
- **Retrospective** challenges to recent increases that
  did not follow the rules.

## Who may apply

Either party to a residential tenancy:

- **Tenant** — challenging a proposed or imposed increase;
- **Landlord** — seeking approval for an above-RPI
  increase under the "below market" carve-out.

## Procedure

The Tribunal operates with intentionally **low procedural
barriers**:

- **Forms**-based application;
- **Modest or no fee** (per ministerial order);
- **No requirement** for legal representation;
- **Written evidence** plus a hearing (which may be by
  telephone / video);
- **Decision** in writing with reasons;
- **Binding** subject to appeal.

## What evidence the Tribunal considers

For an RPI challenge:

- The **notice** of increase;
- The **published Jersey RPI** for the relevant period;
- The **landlord's calculation**;
- The **history** of previous increases (for the
  once-yearly rule).

For a "below market rent" application:

- **Comparable lettings** in the locality (size, type,
  condition);
- **Letting-agent valuations**;
- **Surveyor evidence** (rare in small cases);
- **History** of the rent (was it below market because of
  a special relationship? unimproved condition?);
- **Improvements** by the tenant (deductible from any
  uplift).

## Decisions the Tribunal can make

- **Confirm** the proposed increase as compliant;
- **Reduce** the increase to a Tribunal-set figure;
- **Refuse** the increase entirely;
- **Direct** procedural rectification (re-service of
  notice with correct calculation);
- **Refund** order for excess paid;
- **Declaration** as to the lawful rent going forward.

## Appeals

A party dissatisfied with a Tribunal decision may appeal
to the **Royal Court** — typically on:

- **Point of law**;
- **Procedural unfairness**;
- **Tribunal exceeding jurisdiction**.

Appeals on the **merits** are not generally entertained —
the Tribunal is the specialist fact-finder.

## Effect of a Tribunal application on the tenancy

- The tenancy **continues** while the application is
  pending;
- The **pre-increase rent** continues to be payable;
- The **proposed increase** is suspended pending decision;
- The landlord **cannot terminate** for non-payment of the
  challenged excess.

## Tactical considerations

For tenants:

- **Apply promptly** — delay weakens the position;
- **Pay the pre-increase rent** during the dispute —
  refusal triggers eviction risk;
- **Gather** comparable evidence (Rightmove-equivalent
  local data, your own search of available lets);
- **Document** the property's condition (against a
  "below-market" argument — if it's below market, what
  was the original deal?).

For landlords:

- **Don't** rely on the "below market" carve-out without
  good comparable evidence;
- **Apply** to the Tribunal in advance of imposing an
  above-RPI increase, rather than imposing first and
  defending later;
- **Recognise** the procedural ease of tenant applications
  — the Tribunal sees a lot of these and is quick to spot
  weak landlord cases.

## How this compares to the pre-2025 regime

Before 15 April 2026:

- **No statutory RPI cap** — rent increases governed by
  the contract and market;
- **No Tribunal** — disputes went to Petty Debts Court
  (rare, costly);
- **Rent control** existed only via specific older
  schemes that had largely lapsed.

The Tribunal is therefore a **substantively new
institution** designed to make the rent caps practically
enforceable for ordinary tenants.
