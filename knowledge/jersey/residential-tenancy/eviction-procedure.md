# Eviction Procedure

## Overview

A landlord can only obtain possession of a Jersey
residential unit by **court order**. Self-help — re-entry,
lock-changing, removal of possessions — is unlawful and a
criminal offence under the RTJL. The procedure has three
stages:

1. **Pre-action** — notice (statutory grounds-specific) and
   negotiation;
2. **Court** — application to the **Petty Debts Court** for
   a termination and eviction order;
3. **Enforcement** — execution of the order by the
   **Viscount**.

This file covers stages 2 and 3 — see
[`non-payment-of-rent.md`](./non-payment-of-rent.md) for the
breach-notice (stage 1) procedure for the most common case
(unpaid rent), and [`grounds-for-termination.md`](./grounds-for-termination.md)
for the broader set of grounds.

## Stage 2 — The Petty Debts Court

### Jurisdiction

The Petty Debts Court has **exclusive original
jurisdiction** under the RTJL over:

- Termination of residential tenancy agreements;
- Eviction orders;
- Money judgments for arrears;
- Deposit disputes (subject to the Deposit Scheme rules);
- Declarations as to rights under the agreement.

Appeals from the Petty Debts Court go to the **Royal
Court** on points of law (and limited fact questions).

### Filing the application

The landlord files an application identifying:

- **Parties** — landlord, tenant, any guarantor;
- **Property** — the unit;
- **Tenancy** — date of agreement, term, rent;
- **Ground for termination** — non-payment, other breach,
  expiry, no-fault (12-month notice), etc.;
- **Procedural compliance** — copy of breach notice, proof
  of service, dates;
- **Relief sought** — termination, eviction, arrears
  judgment, costs.

Supporting evidence:

- Tenancy agreement and condition report;
- Rent ledger / bank statements showing arrears;
- Notice and proof of service;
- Correspondence with the tenant;
- Witness statements where conduct is in issue.

### The hearing

Petty Debts Court hearings are **relatively informal**
compared to the Royal Court. The procedure:

- **Reading and Acknowledgement** — short directions
  hearing where the court confirms what's in issue;
- **Substantive hearing** — usually a single day for
  contested cases, much less for undefended;
- **Evidence** — usually documentary; oral evidence on
  contested facts (e.g. condition of premises, harassment
  allegations);
- **Submissions** — landlord first, then tenant; landlord
  reply;
- **Judgment** — usually reserved briefly; sometimes given
  ex tempore.

### What the court considers

For a non-payment / breach-based application:

- **Procedural compliance** with the RTJL — notice,
  service, period;
- **Breach made out** on the evidence;
- **Tenant defences** — set-off, discrimination, harassment;
- **Vulnerability** — children, disability, mental health,
  Income Support status;
- **Proportionality** — is termination a proportionate
  response to the breach in all the circumstances?

For a no-fault termination (12-month notice route):

- **Procedural compliance** — was 12 months' written
  notice given?
- **Compliance** with the RTJL form requirements;
- **Discrimination** or **retaliation** — did the notice
  follow a complaint, repair request, or protected act?

### Orders the court may make

- **Termination** of the tenancy agreement;
- **Eviction** order — usually directing the tenant to
  vacate by a date certain;
- **Stay of execution** — typically 14-28 days, occasionally
  up to 8 weeks, exceptionally longer;
- **Money judgment** for arrears (and accruing rent until
  vacated);
- **Costs** order against the losing party (typically the
  tenant in non-payment cases);
- **Refusal** of the order — usually for procedural defects
  or made-out defences.

### Stays of execution

A stay gives the tenant additional time to vacate. The
court considers:

- **Children** in the unit;
- **Pregnancy**;
- **Disability** or **mental-health** considerations;
- **Income Support** application in progress;
- **Social-housing** placement on offer;
- **Health-related** moves;
- **School-year** considerations.

Stays are **routinely** granted for families with minor
children. Landlords should plan eviction timelines on the
assumption that some stay will be ordered.

### Appeals

Either party may appeal to the **Royal Court** on:

- A point of **law** — yes, as of right within prescribed
  period (typically 28 days);
- A point of **fact** — only with leave, limited grounds.

Appeals do not automatically stay execution — a separate
stay application is needed.

## Stage 3 — Enforcement by the Viscount

### What the Viscount is

The **Viscount** is a senior judicial officer of the Royal
Court, also the principal **enforcement officer** for Jersey
civil judgments. The role goes back centuries and combines
functions handled in England by sheriffs, court bailiffs,
and tipstaffs.

In residential tenancy enforcement the Viscount is the
**only person** lawfully entitled to take possession from a
resisting tenant — neither the landlord nor a private
bailiff service may do so.

### Applying for execution

Once a stay (if any) has expired, the landlord applies to
the Viscount's Department to execute the eviction. The
application is **administrative** — the underlying order
has already been made — and typically requires:

- A sealed copy of the eviction order;
- Confirmation that any stay has expired;
- Property details;
- A deposit / fee on account.

### Attendance at the property

The Viscount schedules an attendance, typically with notice
to the landlord (and sometimes the tenant). On the day:

- The Viscount attends with officers;
- **Knocks** and gives the tenant an opportunity to vacate
  voluntarily, taking their possessions;
- **Removes** the tenant (with force if necessary) if they
  refuse;
- **Inventories** remaining possessions;
- **Restores possession** to the landlord (changing locks
  is often done at this stage with the landlord present).

### Tenant's possessions

The Viscount handles remaining possessions:

- **Removable items** are stored;
- **Items of realisable value** may be sold to recover
  Viscount's fees and any judgment debt;
- **Items not claimed within 15 days** are disposed of;
- **Net proceeds** are accounted to the tenant if they can
  be located;
- **Unclaimed funds** pass to the consolidated fund after
  reasonable efforts to locate the tenant.

### Costs of enforcement

- **Viscount's fees** — set by tariff; typically several
  hundred to several thousand pounds depending on
  complexity (e.g. need for police presence, removal of
  large quantities of possessions);
- **Lock-change** costs;
- **Storage** costs;
- **Recoverable** from the tenant under the lease terms /
  judgment order; in practice often borne by the landlord
  if the tenant is judgment-proof.

### Timeline

Total elapsed time from court order to actual possession
in a straightforward non-payment case is typically:

- **No stay** — 2-6 weeks (court order, Viscount scheduling);
- **Standard stay (28 days)** — 6-10 weeks;
- **Longer stay (8 weeks)** — 10-14 weeks;
- **Contested + appeal** — several months to a year.

## Specific scenarios

### Tenant absconds during proceedings

- Order may be made in default;
- Viscount attends and finds property abandoned;
- Possessions removed and stored;
- Account of net proceeds (if any) to the tenant if
  located.

### Tenant resists physically

- Viscount may use **reasonable force**;
- **Police assistance** may be requested;
- Tenant who assaults or obstructs commits a separate
  offence;
- Cost / time consequences typically against the tenant.

### Tenant refuses to leave with stay still in force

- Stay must be in force — check the order;
- If the stay has expired, the tenant is in contempt;
- Landlord cannot self-help; must wait for Viscount.

### Tenant claims to be unwell on the day

- Viscount may postpone briefly on humanitarian grounds;
- Not a permanent stay — landlord can return.

### Tenant has children present

- Court should have addressed this at the stay stage;
- Viscount may engage Children's Services where children
  appear at risk;
- No automatic right to refuse execution because children
  are present, but the situation is handled sensitively.

## Pitfalls

For landlords:

- **Self-help** — never. Always go through the procedure.
- **Defective notice** — get the notice right; mistakes
  cost months.
- **Under-budgeting** for Viscount fees and stays.
- **Failing to engage** with the tenant before issuing —
  courts dislike landlords who don't try to resolve first.
- **Discrimination / retaliation** — eviction following a
  protected act (race, sex, disability, complaint to
  authorities) is highly contestable.

For tenants:

- **Ignoring** the proceedings — default orders are
  common.
- **Believing self-help** stories from the landlord —
  changing locks etc. is unlawful, report to the police
  and seek immediate court intervention.
- **Not raising defences** — set-off, discrimination,
  procedural — at the hearing; they're much harder to
  raise on appeal.
- **Underestimating** Viscount fees that will be added to
  the judgment debt.
