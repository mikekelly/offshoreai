# Repairs and Habitability Standards

## Two statutes

Jersey rented dwellings sit under:

- **Residential Tenancy (Jersey) Law 2011** — the
  landlord-tenant relationship, including contractual /
  implied repair obligations;
- **Public Health and Safety (Rented Dwellings) (Jersey)
  Law 2018** — public-law minimum standards, enforced by
  Environmental Health.

## RTJL implied obligations

A landlord under the RTJL is obliged to ensure the unit is
**fit for human habitation** at commencement and remains so
during the tenancy. If the unit becomes **uninhabitable
through no fault of the tenant**:

- The **tenant need not pay rent** for the period of
  uninhabitability;
- Either party may apply to the **Petty Debts Court** for
  termination or modification of the agreement.

## Allocation of repair obligations

The agreement allocates specific repair obligations.
Typical structure for Jersey residential tenancies:

### Landlord typically responsible for

- **Structure** — walls, roof, foundations, windows;
- **Heating and hot water** systems;
- **Electrical** wiring and fixed appliances;
- **Plumbing** — pipes, drains, fixed fittings;
- **External** maintenance — gutters, downpipes;
- **Gas** safety (where applicable);
- **Common parts** in shared buildings.

### Tenant typically responsible for

- **Minor day-to-day** maintenance (light bulbs, fuse
  changes, smoke alarm batteries);
- **Tenant-caused damage**;
- **Internal cleanliness** during occupation;
- **Reporting** defects promptly to the landlord;
- **Garden** upkeep (where the agreement requires).

The agreement may shift items between parties but cannot
relieve the landlord of fitness-for-habitation
obligations.

## Public Health Law 2018

The **Public Health and Safety (Rented Dwellings) (Jersey)
Law 2018** establishes minimum standards enforced by
Environmental Health officers:

- **Damp and mould** standards;
- **Heating** adequacy;
- **Electrical** safety;
- **Gas** safety;
- **Fire** safety;
- **Sanitation**.

A tenant who cannot get the landlord to act may report to
Environmental Health, who can:

- **Inspect** the property;
- **Serve** improvement notices;
- **Prosecute** in serious cases;
- **Issue** prohibition notices in extreme cases.

This is a **separate route** to the contractual RTJL
remedies — and an important one when the landlord is
unresponsive.

## What a tenant can do when the landlord won't repair

1. **Report in writing** — date-stamped email, retained
   copy;
2. **Allow a reasonable time** — depending on severity
   (immediate for no-heat-in-winter, days for non-urgent);
3. **Escalate in writing** — formal notice of breach;
4. **Report to Environmental Health** if the issue meets
   the 2018 Law standards;
5. **Consider** a Petty Debts Court application for:
   - **Specific performance** of the repair obligation;
   - **Damages** for diminution in value of the tenancy;
   - **Rent abatement** where uninhabitable;
6. **Consider** "repair and deduct" — but only with
   formal advice, as wrongful self-help may itself be
   breach;
7. **Document everything** — photographs, dates, names.

## What a landlord can do when the tenant causes damage

1. **Document** with photographs and condition-report
   comparison;
2. **Notify** the tenant in writing;
3. **Estimate** repair costs with quotes;
4. **Negotiate** payment or deduction from deposit;
5. **Apply** to Petty Debts Court if disputed.

For damage that is breach of agreement, the **7-day breach
procedure** applies — see
[`non-payment-of-rent.md`](./non-payment-of-rent.md) for the
template.

## Set-off as a defence

A tenant facing an arrears claim may **set off** the
landlord's failure to repair against the rent claim:

- **Documented** repair failure;
- **Quantified** diminution in value or specific cost;
- **Raised** as defence at Petty Debts Court hearing;
- **May** reduce or extinguish the arrears claim.

This is the most common tenant defence to non-payment
eviction.
