# Grounds for Landlord Termination

## The two regimes

Post-15 April 2026, a landlord may end a **periodic
residential tenancy** by either:

- A **3-month justified notice** stating a specific ground;
  or
- A **12-month no-fault notice** without specific ground.

For **breach** by the tenant (including non-payment), the
landlord uses the **breach procedure** rather than a
termination notice — see
[`non-payment-of-rent.md`](./non-payment-of-rent.md).

## Justified grounds (3 months' notice)

### Breach by the tenant

The most common ground. Sub-procedure:

1. **7-day breach notice** requiring remedy;
2. If unremedied, **application to Petty Debts Court**;
3. Court order for termination + eviction.

See [`non-payment-of-rent.md`](./non-payment-of-rent.md) for
the detailed procedure.

### Owner occupation

The landlord (or a defined close family member) intends
to occupy the unit:

- Must be **genuine** intent at the time of notice;
- **Subject to** retaliation / discrimination scrutiny;
- **Tenant may seek** evidence at court if disputed;
- A landlord who serves on this ground and then re-lets to
  a third party may face damages claims.

### Sale with vacant possession

The landlord intends to sell the unit with vacant
possession:

- **Subject to** the same genuineness test;
- **Mere desire** to sell with tenants in situ is not a
  ground;
- **Subsequent failure** to sell does not retrospectively
  invalidate the notice but does invite scrutiny if the
  property is re-let.

### Substantial redevelopment

The landlord intends substantial redevelopment that
requires vacant possession:

- **Specific** Jersey planning context;
- **Genuine** intent at time of notice;
- **Subsequent** evidence of actual redevelopment expected.

### End of employment-linked accommodation

Where the unit is **tied to a contract of employment** and
the employment has ended:

- **Specific** Jersey rules;
- **Reasonable transition period** typical;
- **Distinct from** ordinary tenancy.

### Other prescribed grounds

Ministerial orders may prescribe additional grounds; check
current orders.

## No-fault termination (12 months' notice)

A landlord may end a periodic tenancy on **12 months'
written notice** without stating a specific ground.

This is the "long-notice" route introduced by the 2025
amendment, replacing the prior 3-month no-fault notice. It
gives the tenant a year of certainty to find alternative
accommodation.

### Limits on the no-fault route

Even no-fault notice is subject to:

- **Discrimination overlay** — DL 2013 protected
  characteristics;
- **Retaliation scrutiny** — notice immediately following a
  tenant complaint, repair request, or regulatory referral
  invites adverse inferences;
- **Procedural compliance** — must be written, signed,
  delivered, with correct period.

A "no-fault" notice that is in fact retaliatory may be set
aside by the Petty Debts Court.

## Fixed-term tenancies

For **fixed-term** tenancies (≤ 3 years post-2025), the
landlord cannot terminate before expiry except for breach.
At expiry:

- The tenancy **ends** if the landlord gives 3 months'
  written notice of non-renewal;
- Otherwise, the tenancy typically **converts to
  periodic**.

## What is **not** a ground

- "I want to put the rent up beyond RPI" — not a
  termination ground; the route is a Rent Tribunal
  application for the "below market rent" carve-out;
- "My tenant has been a nuisance to neighbours" — not in
  itself a ground unless it amounts to breach of the
  agreement;
- "I don't like the tenant" — not in itself a ground;
- "My tenant complained about disrepair" — not a ground;
  using it as one is retaliation.

## Procedural overlay

Whichever ground is used:

- **Written notice** with prescribed contents;
- **Correct period** (3 / 7-day-then-court / 12 months);
- **Service** on the tenant;
- **Date** of effect specified;
- **Reference** to the ground (where required);
- **No** waiver during the notice period of the kind that
  would defeat termination (e.g. accepting rent after
  the termination date may waive the notice).
