# Deposits and the Tenants' Deposit Scheme

## The Scheme

The **Tenants' Deposit Scheme** is a statutory third-party
custody scheme: deposits paid by tenants are held by the
Scheme (or a designated custodian), **not by the landlord
or the agent**. The Scheme exists to:

- **Protect** the deposit from misappropriation;
- **Provide neutral custody** during the tenancy;
- **Adjudicate** disputes about end-of-tenancy deductions.

A landlord who takes a deposit must lodge it with the
Scheme within the prescribed timeframe and provide the
tenant a **deposit receipt** "as soon as possible" after
payment.

## What a deposit may cover

A deposit secures the landlord against:

- **Unpaid rent** at end of tenancy;
- **Damage** beyond fair wear and tear (per the condition
  report);
- **Cleaning costs** if the unit is not returned in
  reasonable condition;
- **Unpaid utilities** for which the tenant is contractually
  responsible.

It may **not** cover:

- **Ordinary maintenance** that is the landlord's
  responsibility;
- **Fair wear and tear**;
- **Improvements** to the property;
- **Items not specified** in the condition report.

## Limits on deposit amount

There is no fixed statutory cap, but **excessive deposits**
(more than 2 months' rent is unusual) may be challenged.
Common practice:

- **1 month's rent** — standard;
- **6 weeks' rent** — typical maximum;
- **More** — requires justification (pets, high-value
  contents, exceptional risk).

The post-2025 amendment empowers ministerial orders to set
explicit caps; check current orders.

## End-of-tenancy procedure

1. **Tenant vacates** the unit;
2. **Landlord** inspects within **7 days** and confirms
   condition with reference to the original report;
3. **Landlord** notifies the Scheme of any proposed
   deductions, with itemised evidence;
4. **Tenant** is notified of proposed deductions and may
   **agree** or **dispute**;
5. **Agreed amounts** are released within the prescribed
   period;
6. **Disputed amounts** are adjudicated by the Scheme.

## Disputed deposits

When the tenant disputes a deduction:

- The Scheme **holds** the disputed amount;
- An **adjudicator** considers the evidence (condition
  report start vs end, photographs, correspondence,
  receipts for repairs);
- **Burden** is on the **landlord** to prove the loss and
  that it goes beyond fair wear and tear;
- **Decision** is binding, subject to appeal to the Petty
  Debts Court.

## When a deposit isn't lodged

Where a landlord fails to lodge a deposit:

- **Offence** under the RTJL;
- **Tenant** may apply to the Petty Debts Court for an
  order requiring lodgement;
- **Tenant** may seek damages;
- **Compensation** equal to a multiple of the deposit
  amount in some cases.

## Practical recommendations

For landlords:

- **Lodge** the deposit promptly — within days of receipt;
- **Provide receipt** to the tenant;
- **Document** the condition with photographs;
- **Itemise** any proposed deduction at end with quotes /
  invoices / receipts;
- **Avoid** vague "cleaning" or "redecoration" deductions
  without specifics — these are heavily challenged.

For tenants:

- **Verify** the deposit has been lodged — check the
  Scheme directly;
- **Insist** on a deposit receipt;
- **Photograph** the property at commencement and end;
- **Clean thoroughly** before vacating — many disputes
  arise on cleaning;
- **Dispute** any unsubstantiated deductions — burden is on
  the landlord.
