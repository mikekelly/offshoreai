# Lodging Houses vs Residential Tenancies

## Why the distinction matters

The **RTJL 2011** applies only to **residential
tenancies**. **Lodgers** and **boarders** are excluded.
Whether an arrangement is one or the other determines:

- Whether the RTJL's protections (notice periods, deposit
  scheme, condition report, eviction procedure) apply at
  all;
- Which **registration** regime applies (Lodging Houses
  (Registration) (Jersey) Law 1962 for lodging houses);
- The **standard** of accommodation and rules of conduct;
- Which **court** handles disputes.

## The legal test — exclusive possession

The core test is whether the occupier has **exclusive
possession** of a defined self-contained unit:

| Factor | Tenancy | Lodging |
|---|---|---|
| Self-contained unit | ✅ | ❌ |
| Exclusive possession | ✅ | ❌ |
| Locked door, own key | ✅ | Sometimes |
| Shared kitchen / bathroom | ❌ (usually) | ✅ |
| Landlord can enter freely | ❌ | Often |
| Services provided (cleaning, meals) | ❌ | Often |
| Defined "home" | ✅ | ❌ |

A lodger has a **personal licence** to occupy; a tenant
has a **proprietary interest** in the unit.

## Common arrangement types

### Clearly a tenancy

- A separate flat with its own kitchen, bathroom, lockable
  door, and the tenant's exclusive use;
- A house let entirely to one or more named tenants;
- A bedsit with self-contained facilities.

### Clearly lodging

- A room in the landlord's home, sharing kitchen and
  bathroom with the family;
- "Half board" arrangements with meals provided;
- Short-term arrangements in lodging houses (specifically
  excluded from RTJL).

### Borderline

- **Shared house** with one bedroom each — usually
  tenancy if each tenant has exclusive possession of
  their room and joint use of common parts (joint
  tenancy);
- **Houseshare with one master tenant** subletting — the
  master tenant is the landlord-of-record; their subs
  may be tenants or lodgers depending on arrangement;
- **AirBnB** for under 3 months — usually outside RTJL
  (short holiday let exclusion).

## What governs lodging arrangements

Lodging arrangements are governed by:

- **General contract law** (Pothier, formation,
  performance) — see [`../contract/index.md`](../contract/index.md);
- **Lodging Houses (Registration) (Jersey) Law 1962** —
  for lodging houses (registration, standards, conduct);
- **Public Health** standards for accommodation generally;
- **Tort / délit** for landlord misconduct.

## Notice for lodgers

Without the RTJL's protections, a lodger's notice rights
come from the contract:

- **Express terms** of the lodging agreement;
- **Implied** "reasonable notice" where silent —
  typically much shorter than tenancy notice;
- **No statutory floor** in the way the RTJL provides for
  tenants.

A landlord ending a lodging arrangement does **not** need
to go through the Petty Debts Court eviction procedure.
**Civil claims** (e.g. for unpaid rent or damages) still
proceed in the ordinary civil courts.

## Pitfalls

For occupiers:

- **Assuming** lodger status excludes all protections —
  contract law, public health law, anti-discrimination
  law still apply;
- **Accepting** a "lodger" label when the substance is
  exclusive possession — courts look at substance over
  form;
- **Not recording** the arrangement in writing — disputes
  about what was agreed are hard to resolve without
  documentation.

For owner-occupiers taking lodgers:

- **Drafting** an unclear arrangement that gets
  recharacterised as a tenancy — would then require RTJL
  compliance retrospectively;
- **Granting** what looks like exclusive possession —
  e.g. exclusive use of an annexe with its own kitchen —
  invites RTJL recharacterisation;
- **Failing** to register a lodging house where required.

For master tenants subletting:

- **Head landlord consent** is typically required by the
  head lease;
- **Subletting without consent** is a breach;
- **The sub-relationship** may itself be a tenancy
  (subletting) or lodging — depending on substance.

## Recharacterisation

A court (Petty Debts or Royal) faced with a dispute will
examine the substance, not the label. A "lodging
agreement" that gives the occupier exclusive possession of
a self-contained unit is **a residential tenancy** for the
RTJL — and all its protections apply, including the need
for an eviction order.
