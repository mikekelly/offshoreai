# Residential Tenancy (Jersey) Law 2011 — Overview

## What the law does

The **Residential Tenancy (Jersey) Law 2011** (RTJL) is the
principal statute governing residential tenancies in
Jersey. It:

- Defines what counts as a **residential unit** and a
  **residential tenancy** (and excludes lodgings, hotels,
  short holiday lets, hospitals, nursing homes, etc.);
- Mandates a **written, signed tenancy agreement** with
  prescribed contents;
- Mandates a **condition report** at the start of the
  tenancy;
- Sets statutory **notice periods** for termination;
- Confers **exclusive original jurisdiction** on the **Petty
  Debts Court** for residential tenancy disputes;
- Empowers the **Viscount** to enforce eviction orders;
- Forbids contracting out — any clause purporting to
  exclude the RTJL is void and is itself an offence.

Significant 2025 amendments (in force from 15 April 2026)
introduced rent caps, a Rent Tribunal, and stronger tenant
security of tenure — see [`2025-amendment-law.md`](./2025-amendment-law.md).

## Scope — when does the RTJL apply?

The RTJL applies where **all** of the following are true:

- The agreement is for the lease or sub-lease of a
  **residential unit**;
- The unit is occupied **for value** (rent or equivalent);
- The term is **9 years or less**, or of indefinite duration
  (rolling periodic);
- The unit is a **self-contained private home** with shower
  or bath, washbasin, kitchen, sleeping space, and lavatory.

It does **not** apply to:

- **Hotels** and holiday lets shorter than 3 months;
- **Hospitals**, nursing homes, hostels, shelters;
- **Educational** institutions' student housing;
- **Private clubs** (except where the unit is staff
  housing);
- **Lodgers, boarders, and licensees** — see
  [`lodging-houses-vs-tenancy.md`](./lodging-houses-vs-tenancy.md).

Leases of 9 years or more are **commercial-style contracts
of lease** passed in the Royal Court — see
[`../property/commercial-leases.md`](../property/commercial-leases.md).

## Key concepts

### Residential unit

A self-contained private home meeting the equipment
requirements above. Shared kitchens or bathrooms typically
take the arrangement out of the RTJL and into the
**lodging-house** regime.

### Residential tenancy agreement

A written, signed agreement between landlord and tenant
containing the prescribed information (property, parties,
term, rent, deposit, condition report). Cannot be oral.
Cannot exclude the RTJL.

### Fixed-term vs periodic

A **fixed-term** tenancy has a stated end date. Post-2025
amendment, the initial fixed term is capped at **3 years**
with no successive fixed terms permitted; at expiry, the
tenancy ends or converts to periodic.

A **periodic** tenancy continues from period to period (e.g.
month to month) until terminated by notice.

### Condition report

A document recording the state of the unit at commencement,
agreed between the parties. Mandatory under the RTJL
(Condition Reports Order 2014). See
[`condition-reports.md`](./condition-reports.md).

### Deposit

Held under the **Tenants' Deposit Scheme** — third-party
custody, not the landlord's own account. See
[`deposits.md`](./deposits.md).

## Mandatory and prohibited terms

Some terms are **mandatory implied** in every residential
tenancy and cannot be contracted out:

- The tenant may remove personal fixtures, subject to
  reparing any damage caused;
- The landlord cannot unreasonably withhold consent where
  the agreement makes their consent a condition (e.g. on
  alterations, assignment);
- The tenant has no obligation to buy property items from
  the landlord or pay key money / premiums.

Some practices are **prohibited**:

- Charging premiums or key money on top of rent and deposit;
- Excluding any part of the RTJL by agreement (offence,
  fine);
- Withholding the executed agreement or deposit receipt
  unreasonably.

## Jurisdiction

The **Petty Debts Court** has exclusive original
jurisdiction over residential tenancy matters under the
RTJL — covering termination orders, eviction, deposit
disputes, breach claims, declarations, and ancillary
orders. Appeals lie to the **Royal Court**.

The **Rent Tribunal** (introduced by the 2025 amendment)
handles disputes over rent increases specifically — see
[`rent-tribunal.md`](./rent-tribunal.md).

## Enforcement

A landlord who has obtained an eviction order applies for
the order to be executed by the **Viscount**, who may
attend the premises, remove the tenant and their
possessions (with force if necessary), and put the landlord
back in possession. The Viscount disposes of unclaimed
possessions and accounts to the tenant for the net proceeds
where the tenant can be located. See
[`eviction-procedure.md`](./eviction-procedure.md).

## Offences

Specific RTJL offences include:

- **Failing to complete a condition report** — fine up to
  £10,000;
- **Failing to lodge a deposit** in the Scheme;
- **Excluding the RTJL** by contract;
- **Refusing to provide** a copy of the executed agreement
  to the tenant.

## Common misconceptions

- ❌ "I can evict by changing the locks." — No. Forfeiture
  by re-entry is not available; an eviction order from the
  Petty Debts Court is required.
- ❌ "My tenant didn't pay so I can terminate immediately."
  — No. Non-payment is a breach, requiring 7-day notice to
  remedy before any termination application; see
  [`non-payment-of-rent.md`](./non-payment-of-rent.md).
- ❌ "Oral tenancy is fine." — No. The agreement must be
  written and signed; an oral arrangement risks being a
  lodging arrangement (or no enforceable arrangement at
  all).
- ❌ "I can put any rent-review clause I want." — No
  longer, post-15 April 2026: rent increases capped at RPI
  and limited to once yearly for periodic tenancies. See
  [`rent-and-rent-reviews.md`](./rent-and-rent-reviews.md).
