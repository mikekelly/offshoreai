# Residential Tenancy (Jersey) Amendment Law 2025

## What this is

The most significant Jersey residential-tenancy reform in
over a decade. The **Residential Tenancy (Jersey) Amendment
Law 2025** received Royal Assent in 2025 and came into force
on **15 April 2026** — one month before the current
verification date of this file. From that date, the
**post-amendment regime** applies to new tenancies, and
existing tenancies are subject to specific transitional
rules.

An agent answering any 2026-vintage residential-tenancy
question **must apply the post-amendment regime** to any
tenancy created on or after 15 April 2026.

## Headline changes

### Fixed-term tenancies capped at 3 years

The initial fixed term of a residential tenancy may not
exceed **3 years**. **Successive fixed terms are
prohibited** — a tenancy that has expired at the end of a
fixed term either ends outright or converts automatically
to a periodic tenancy.

This is a significant reduction from the prior 9-year
ceiling (above which a lease becomes a Royal-Court-passed
contract of lease) and is designed to give tenants the
benefit of periodic-tenancy protections sooner.

### Tenant exit on one month's notice

A tenant may terminate **any** residential tenancy on **one
month's written notice**, regardless of any fixed term.
This is the pre-amendment default for periodic tenancies,
now generalised — a tenant who entered a 3-year fixed term
is no longer locked in for the full term.

### Landlord termination — justified or 12 months

A landlord seeking to terminate a **periodic** tenancy must
either:

- **Justify** the termination on a permitted ground
  (breach, sale, owner-occupation, redevelopment — see
  [`grounds-for-termination.md`](grounds-for-termination.md)); or
- Give **12 months' notice** without specific justification
  (the "no-fault long-notice" route).

This replaces the prior 3-month no-fault notice. Landlords
may still serve **3 months' notice** but only on a
justified ground.

### Rent caps and the once-yearly rule

For periodic tenancies, rent may be increased only:

- **Once per year**;
- By **no more than the Jersey Retail Prices Index** (RPI)
  for the relevant period;
- **Unless** the rent is "significantly below market rent"
  (a narrow carve-out, subject to challenge);
- With **at least 2 months' written notice** to the tenant.

Increases above RPI or out of cycle are challengeable at
the new Rent Tribunal.

### The Rent Tribunal

A new **independent Rent Tribunal** handles disputes over:

- Whether a proposed rent increase exceeds RPI;
- Whether the "significantly below market rent" carve-out
  applies;
- Whether the once-yearly rule has been breached;
- Procedural compliance with the 2 months' notice rule.

The Tribunal does **not** handle:

- **Social housing** rent (Andium, housing trusts) —
  separate regime;
- **Employment-linked** accommodation (staff
  accommodation tied to a contract of employment);
- **Eviction** or **breach** disputes — those remain with
  the Petty Debts Court.

See [`rent-tribunal.md`](rent-tribunal.md).

### Strengthened written-form rules

Tenancy agreements must clearly identify **all fees**
chargeable to the tenant. Hidden fees and add-ons are
unenforceable and may constitute an offence.

## Transitional rules

| Tenancy type at 15 April 2026 | Treatment |
|---|---|
| New tenancy (entered on / after that date) | Full post-amendment regime |
| Pre-existing **periodic** tenancy | Converts immediately to the new periodic regime — rent caps, 12-month no-fault notice, etc. |
| Pre-existing **fixed-term** tenancy | Continues under pre-amendment law until expiry; then post-amendment rules apply |
| Pre-existing tenancy with a successive-fixed-term clause | The clause becomes unenforceable at expiry of the current fixed term — converts to periodic |

## What did **not** change

- **Petty Debts Court** retains exclusive original
  jurisdiction over eviction and breach disputes;
- **Viscount** continues to enforce eviction orders;
- **Condition report** regime (2014 Order) unchanged in
  substance;
- **Tenants' Deposit Scheme** regime unchanged (some
  procedural updates expected via order);
- **Lodging-house** exclusion unchanged;
- **9-year ceiling** above which a lease becomes a
  Royal-Court contract of lease unchanged.

## Practical implications

### For landlords

- **Cannot** offer successive 12-month fixed terms anymore
  — must accept either a single ≤3-year fixed term or a
  periodic structure;
- **Cannot** raise rent above RPI annually without a Rent
  Tribunal-tested "significantly below market rent" case;
- **Cannot** terminate periodically without either a
  ground or 12 months' notice;
- **Should** review pre-existing periodic tenancies
  immediately for compliance with the new rent rules.

### For tenants

- **One month** to walk away from any tenancy, including a
  fixed term — material flexibility;
- **RPI cap** caps the annual cost-of-living shock;
- **Rent Tribunal** offers a free / low-cost forum for
  rent disputes — see [`rent-tribunal.md`](rent-tribunal.md);
- **12 months' notice** for no-fault termination by
  landlord — meaningful security of tenure improvement.

### For letting agents

- **Must** update standard agreement templates to reflect
  3-year cap, 12-month landlord notice, RPI cap language,
  Rent Tribunal references;
- **Must** disclose all fees clearly in the agreement.

## Pitfalls

- **Pre-amendment templates** in continued use will silently
  produce unenforceable clauses — review templates now;
- **Above-RPI rent increases** may be challenged at the
  Tribunal; landlords should document the "significantly
  below market rent" case if relying on it;
- **Tenant termination on 1 month's notice** applies even
  to fixed terms post-amendment — landlords cannot rely on
  fixed-term "lock-in" arguments;
- **Existing tenancies** convert immediately if periodic —
  do not assume grandfathering.
