# Notice Periods — Residential Tenancies (Jersey)

## Quick reference (post-15 April 2026)

| Notice giver | Tenancy type | Minimum notice | Conditions |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Tenant** | Any periodic | **1 month** | Written, signed |
| **Tenant** | Any fixed term | **1 month** | Per 2025 amendment — tenant may exit any tenancy on 1 month's notice |
| **Landlord** | Periodic, justified ground | **3 months** | Written, signed; ground stated |
| **Landlord** | Periodic, no-fault | **12 months** | Written, signed; the "no-fault long-notice" route |
| **Landlord** | Fixed term (expiry) | **3 months before end** | Notice of non-renewal |
| **Either** | Breach (7-day cure) | **7 days to cure**, then court | Triggers the breach procedure, not termination by notice |

Mutual agreement may shorten any notice — but cannot
displace the statutory floor without consent.

## Form requirements

Every termination notice must be:

- **In writing**;
- **Signed** by the giver (tenant personally; landlord or
  authorised agent);
- **Delivered** to the other party — typically by the
  method specified in the agreement, or by personal
  delivery or recorded post;
- **Specific** — identifying the property, the parties, the
  ground (where required), and the termination date.

A defective notice is **fatal**. A landlord with a defective
notice must usually start the clock again with a fresh
notice.

## Tenant's notice

A tenant may end **any** residential tenancy on **1 month's
written notice**, regardless of fixed term. This is a
generalisation introduced by the 2025 amendment — under the
prior regime, fixed-term tenancies were not unilaterally
exitable except per break clause.

The notice must:

- Be **clear** about the intention to terminate;
- **Identify** the unit and the parties;
- **Specify** the termination date (not earlier than 1
  month after delivery);
- Be **delivered to the landlord** (not the managing
  agent, unless the agreement explicitly permits agent
  service).

A tenant remains liable for rent up to the termination
date, but **not** for rent for any period after — even if
the landlord cannot relet immediately.

## Landlord's notice — justified ground (3 months)

A landlord seeking to end a **periodic** tenancy on a
**justified ground** must give at least **3 months'**
written notice. Justified grounds include (subject to
detail in [`grounds-for-termination.md`](grounds-for-termination.md)):

- **Breach** of the agreement by the tenant (procedure
  varies — typically the 7-day cure procedure applies
  first);
- **Owner-occupation** — landlord (or close family member)
  wishes to occupy;
- **Sale** with vacant possession;
- **Substantial redevelopment** of the property;
- **End of employment-linked accommodation** (where the
  unit is tied to a contract of employment that has ended).

The notice must **state** the ground. A landlord cannot
later rely on a different ground without serving a fresh
notice.

## Landlord's notice — no-fault (12 months)

A landlord who has **no justified ground**, or who does not
wish to plead one, may end a periodic tenancy on **12
months'** written notice. This is the "no-fault long-notice"
route introduced by the 2025 amendment.

This is **not** a free-for-all retaliatory tool: the
**discrimination** and **harassment** overlays still apply,
and a 12-month notice given immediately after a tenant's
protected act (complaint, repair request, regulatory
referral) may be challenged as retaliatory.

## Fixed-term tenancies and expiry

Under the post-2025 regime:

- The initial fixed term may not exceed **3 years**;
- **Successive** fixed terms are prohibited;
- At expiry, the tenancy either **ends** or **converts to
  periodic** — depending on what the parties agree or the
  default position;
- A landlord who wants to ensure the tenancy ends at the
  fixed-term expiry must give **3 months' notice of non-
  renewal**;
- A landlord who wants the tenancy to continue periodically
  need not give notice — silence by both parties typically
  produces a periodic continuation.

A tenant may exit before fixed-term expiry on **1 month's
notice** (post-2025 generalisation, above).

## When the breach procedure substitutes for notice

For tenant breach (including non-payment of rent), the
landlord does **not** simply give notice of termination. The
procedure is:

1. **7-day written breach notice** — specifying the breach
   and requiring remedy;
2. **Wait 7 days** for remedy;
3. **Apply to the Petty Debts Court** for termination and
   eviction.

See [`non-payment-of-rent.md`](non-payment-of-rent.md) and
[`eviction-procedure.md`](eviction-procedure.md).

A landlord cannot **bypass** the breach procedure by serving
a 3- or 12-month termination notice "to be safe" — the two
routes are not interchangeable, and the breach procedure
is what allows immediate possession on tenant breach
(subject to court).

## Service of notice

| Method | Status under RTJL |
|---|---|
| Personal delivery | Always sufficient |
| Recorded post to the unit | Generally sufficient |
| Email | Sufficient **only** where the agreement provides for it |
| Letting agent | Sufficient only where the agreement provides for it |
| Posting on the door | Not sufficient |
| WhatsApp / text message | Not sufficient |

A landlord serving on a tenant must serve the **tenant**
(not just leave it at the unit if there is doubt about
receipt). A tenant serving on the landlord must serve the
**landlord** — not the agent unless the agreement allows
it.

Date of delivery is **date of receipt**, not date of
posting, for the purpose of computing the notice period.
Practitioners often add a few extra days to be safe.

## Effect of expiry of notice

When the notice expires:

- The tenancy **ends** (subject to court intervention);
- The tenant must vacate;
- If the tenant does not vacate, the landlord must apply
  to the Petty Debts Court for an eviction order — even
  where the notice procedure was followed perfectly;
- The court will normally grant the order if the notice
  was valid and the period has expired, but may impose a
  stay.

## Pitfalls

For landlords:

- **Defective notice** — get the form, signature,
  delivery, and period right;
- **Wrong notice route** — confusing breach procedure with
  termination by notice;
- **Wrong ground** stated — restricts what the landlord can
  plead at court;
- **Retaliatory timing** — serving a 12-month no-fault
  notice immediately after a tenant complaint invites a
  discrimination / retaliation defence;
- **Assuming** notice = possession — court order is still
  required if the tenant doesn't leave.

For tenants:

- **Underestimating** the 1-month-notice flexibility
  introduced in 2026;
- **Serving** notice on the agent rather than the landlord
  (only valid if the agreement allows it);
- **Ignoring** a notice and hoping it goes away — the
  consequences cascade;
- **Failing** to raise procedural defects in the
  landlord's notice at court.
