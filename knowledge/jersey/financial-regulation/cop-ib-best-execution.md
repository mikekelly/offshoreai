# IB Code of Practice — Best Execution

Best execution is the discipline of executing client orders
on the **terms most favourable to the client** at the time
of execution. It applies to every order the firm executes
on behalf of a client and to every order the firm transmits
to another firm for execution.

## The best-execution obligation

The Code expects the firm to take **all sufficient steps**
to obtain the best possible result for the client. This is
not the same as obtaining the lowest price — it is a
multi-factor assessment.

### The factors

Best execution is judged across multiple factors, with
weighting calibrated to the client and the order:

1. **Price** — the headline factor, particularly for
   liquid instruments;
2. **Costs** — explicit (commissions, fees) and implicit
   (spreads, market impact);
3. **Speed** of execution;
4. **Likelihood of execution** — particularly for less
   liquid instruments;
5. **Likelihood of settlement** — counterparty / settlement
   risk;
6. **Order size** — large orders may need different
   handling;
7. **Nature of the order** — limit / market / stop-loss /
   block;
8. **Any other consideration** relevant to the execution of
   the order.

The weighting depends on:

- **Client characteristics** — retail clients are weighted
  more heavily toward total cost; professional clients may
  weight other factors more heavily;
- **Instrument characteristics** — liquid public-market
  securities are typically price-driven; illiquid private
  securities may weight likelihood-of-execution more
  heavily;
- **Order characteristics** — block trades, complex orders.

## The execution policy

The firm operates a documented **execution policy** that:

- Identifies the execution venues / counterparties the
  firm uses for each category of instrument;
- Explains the relative importance of execution factors
  for each category;
- Justifies the choice of venues / counterparties — what
  evidence supports the conclusion that the chosen venues
  / counterparties consistently deliver best execution;
- **Reviewed at least annually** with documented
  conclusions;
- **Disclosed to clients** at onboarding and on material
  amendment.

The execution policy is itself a Code-required artefact;
operating without one is a deficiency.

## Categories of execution

### Direct execution

The firm executes orders directly on a venue (exchange,
MTF, OTF) or with a counterparty. The firm's controls:

- Algorithmic routing or human trader judgement,
  documented;
- Real-time monitoring of execution quality;
- Post-trade analysis against execution benchmarks (TCA —
  Transaction Cost Analysis).

### Transmission to another firm

The firm transmits the order to another regulated firm for
execution. The firm's controls:

- **Selection of the receiving firm** — best-execution
  diligence on the receiving firm itself;
- **Documented arrangement** with the receiving firm
  setting execution standards;
- **Monitoring** of the receiving firm's execution
  performance;
- **Periodic review** of the receiving-firm arrangement.

Where transmission is in fact used, the firm is responsible
for the receiving firm's execution — not insulated from
best-execution obligation by having delegated execution.

### Discretionary portfolio management

Where the firm has discretion over execution as part of a
discretionary mandate, best execution applies to every
order generated by the discretionary process. The firm's
controls include:

- Documented order-generation logic;
- Execution monitoring at portfolio level;
- Periodic execution-quality reporting to clients.

## Aggregation and allocation

Where the firm aggregates orders for multiple clients (e.g.
trading the same security across multiple discretionary
mandates):

- **Documented aggregation policy** disclosed to clients;
- **Fair allocation** of partial fills across clients;
- **No systematic disadvantage** to any client through
  aggregation;
- **Allocation justification** documented per trade.

## Cross-venue and multi-venue considerations

For instruments traded on multiple venues:

- The firm's execution policy explains how multi-venue
  decisions are taken (algorithmic SOR — smart order
  routing, manual venue selection, single-venue routing
  with periodic review);
- Multi-venue execution-quality data is captured for
  periodic review;
- Venue concentration is itself a risk that the firm
  monitors.

## TCA — Transaction Cost Analysis

The Code expects firms with material execution volumes to
operate TCA:

- **Pre-trade TCA** — expected cost / impact of a
  contemplated trade;
- **Real-time TCA** — execution against benchmark in
  flight;
- **Post-trade TCA** — execution outcome vs benchmark and
  vs peer-firm performance.

TCA tooling is standard for institutional firms; the Code
expects proportionate analysis for wealth-management firms
trading on behalf of discretionary mandates.

## Common best-execution failure points

JFSC enforcement and thematic-review themes:

- **No documented execution policy** — operating on
  unwritten conventions;
- **Generic execution policy** not calibrated to the firm's
  actual instrument mix and client base;
- **Single-venue routing** without periodic review against
  alternatives;
- **Receiving-firm selection** done once and never
  reviewed;
- **Aggregation without documented allocation logic**;
- **No post-trade analysis** of execution quality;
- **Conflicts in execution-venue selection** (affiliated
  brokers, inducements affecting venue choice) not
  adequately managed.

## Why this matters for wealth management

For wealth-management firms running discretionary mandates,
best execution is operationally daily — every portfolio
decision that generates a trade is subject to best
execution. The discipline of operating it well:

- Reduces total client cost (the direct benefit);
- Withstands JFSC inspection (the regulatory benefit);
- Differentiates the firm in client-facing
  conversations (the commercial benefit — clients
  increasingly ask about execution quality).
