# FSB Code of Practice — Principles

The JFSC Fund Services Business Code (effective 15 July
2021) opens with **Principles** that frame every other
obligation under the Code. As with the
[TCB Code Principles](./cop-tcb-principles.md) and the
[IB Code](./cop-ib-index.md), the FSB Principles are
**mandatory and enforceable** independently of any specific
subsection — a Jersey FSB whose conduct breaches a
Principle is in breach even where no specific rule subsection
is contravened.

The exact wording of each Principle and any subordinate
guidance must be **verified against the current published
Code on the
[JFSC's website](https://www.jerseyfsc.org/industry/codes-of-practice/)**
before quoting in an authoritative answer.

## The Principles in summary

The FSB Code follows the standard FSL-class Code structure
of **eight Principles**, applied to the fund-services
context. In summary terms:

1. **Integrity** — honest and fair conduct, central to all
   other obligations.
2. **Skill, care and diligence** — competent execution of
   fund-services functions calibrated to the services
   provided.
3. **Organisation and control** — adequate governance,
   systems, controls, and a designated compliance function;
   three-lines-of-defence framing.
4. **Risk management** — proportionate identification,
   assessment, and mitigation of risk; the business-wide
   risk assessment driving operational design.
5. **Investor / fund treatment** — fair treatment of fund
   investors and the funds themselves, including
   disclosure of material information.
6. **Financial soundness** — adequate capital and resources
   to meet liabilities as they fall due; appropriate
   professional indemnity insurance.
7. **Cooperation with the JFSC** — open, accurate and
   timely dealings with the regulator, including
   notification of material events.
8. **Market integrity** — fair dealing in markets; behaviour
   that does not harm the integrity of the markets in which
   the FSB or its funds operate.

## How the Principles apply differently to FSB

The Principles are calibrated to the FSB context:

### Investor treatment vs customer treatment

The TCB's "customer" is typically a trust / settlor / family.
The FSB's "customer" is more layered:

- The **fund itself** is in many cases the proximate
  customer (e.g. a fund administrator's customer is the
  fund);
- The **fund's investors** are the ultimate beneficiaries of
  the FSB's services;
- The **AIFM / manager** is also a relevant party where the
  FSB is providing services to the AIFM's fund.

The Principle 5 fair-treatment obligation applies across
these layers — the FSB must treat the fund fairly, the
investors fairly (via the fund), and not allow the AIFM
relationship to override investor-protection obligations.

### Skill calibrated to fund type

FSB activities span:

- **Fund administration** — bookkeeping, NAV calculation,
  investor services;
- **Fund management** — investment decisions for the fund;
- **Custody / depositary** — safekeeping fund assets;
- **Transfer agency** — investor register administration;
- **Designated Service Provider** for JPFs (see
  [`./cop-fsb-jpf-dsp.md`](./cop-fsb-jpf-dsp.md)).

Each requires different skill profiles. The Principle 2
skill obligation calibrates to the specific services the
FSB provides.

### AIFMD / NPPR interface

For funds marketed into the EU via NPPR, the FSB Code
Principles operate **alongside** the AIFMD-style obligations
captured in
[`./cop-fsb-aifmd-interface.md`](./cop-fsb-aifmd-interface.md).
Where the AIFMD framework imposes specific obligations, the
FSB Principles supplement them with broader conduct
expectations.

## Operational implications

A robust FSB programme satisfies each Principle through:

- **Principle 1 (Integrity)** — culture, training, conduct
  expectations;
- **Principle 2 (Skill)** — recruitment, training,
  qualifications, capacity management;
- **Principle 3 (Organisation and control)** — three-lines-
  of-defence, designated compliance officer, MLRO
  appointment;
- **Principle 4 (Risk management)** — BWRA, customer-risk
  rating, operational risk management;
- **Principle 5 (Investor / fund treatment)** — fair-
  treatment policies, disclosure regimes, suitability for
  the FSB's specific role;
- **Principle 6 (Financial soundness)** — capital adequacy
  monitoring, PI insurance, business-continuity planning;
- **Principle 7 (JFSC cooperation)** — periodic returns,
  material-event notifications, supervisory engagement;
- **Principle 8 (Market integrity)** — market-conduct
  controls particularly for FSBs with dealing /
  execution roles.

## Why the Principles are load-bearing

The JFSC can take enforcement action against an FSB whose
conduct breaches a Principle even where no specific Code
subsection is contravened. Civil financial penalties under
the FSL can be calibrated by reference to which Principles
were breached and how severely. A compliance programme that
satisfies the detailed Code rules but fails on Principle 1
(integrity) or Principle 3 (organisation and control) is
still in breach.

The substantive sections of the Code operationalise the
Principles — investor disclosure operationalises Principle
5, the compliance officer requirement operationalises
Principle 3, etc. The agent answering a Principle-level
question should pair the Principle with the operationalising
section file.
