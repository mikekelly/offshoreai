# TCB Code of Practice — Acting as Professional Trustee

Acting as professional trustee of a Jersey trust is the
**core function** of most Jersey TCBs. The Code's
trustee-conduct section operationalises the standard the
JFSC expects of a regulated trustee — distinct from but
interlocking with the substantive fiduciary duties under
the [Trusts (Jersey) Law 1984](../trusts/trusts-law-1984.md).

Two regimes apply in parallel:

- **The Trusts Law itself** — substantive fiduciary duties
  (see [`../trusts/trustee-duties.md`](../trusts/trustee-duties.md))
  enforceable in the Royal Court at the suit of
  beneficiaries;
- **The TCB Code** — regulatory conduct expectations
  enforceable by the JFSC under the
  [FSL 1998](./financial-services-jersey-law-1998.md).

A trustee can satisfy one and fail the other. A robust
trustee programme satisfies both.

## What the Code expects of a professional trustee

The Code's trustee-conduct section typically covers:

### Fiduciary discipline operationalised

The fiduciary duties under the Trusts Law (act in good faith,
exercise reasonable care, avoid conflicts, act in
beneficiaries' interests) are restated as JFSC-supervised
**operational obligations**:

- **Documented decision-making** — every trustee decision
  rationale recorded;
- **Periodic review** of trust performance, suitability of
  current strategy, and beneficiary circumstances;
- **Conflicts identification** at every decision point;
- **Beneficiary engagement** at appropriate cadence.

The Royal Court's case-law standard (notably *Re S Settlement*
and the various Article 51 cases) on what is expected of a
"prudent person" trustee informs the Code's expectations.

### Distribution decisions

Discretionary distribution decisions are the most
consequential operational trustee activity. The Code expects:

- **Documented assessment** of beneficiary circumstances
  before any material distribution;
- **Letter-of-wishes consideration** — the settlor's wishes
  reviewed and weighed;
- **Multiple-beneficiary fairness** — where the trust has
  multiple beneficiaries with potentially competing
  interests, the trustee documents the balance struck;
- **Tax considerations** — the trustee should understand
  the tax position of the distribution in the recipient
  beneficiary's hands (the trustee does not give the
  beneficiary's tax advice but should not be blind to it);
- **Royal Court blessing** for momentous distributions
  under [Article 51](../trusts/article-51-directions.md)
  where appropriate.

### Investment management

For trusts where the trustee has investment responsibility:

- **Investment policy** documented and reviewed
  periodically;
- **Suitability** of investments for the trust's
  circumstances and beneficiary profile;
- **Diversification** appropriate to the trust's risk
  tolerance and time horizon;
- **Delegation** to investment managers under documented
  arrangements (where the trustee delegates investment
  decisions, the Code expects the delegate to be regulated
  and the trustee to retain oversight);
- **Performance review** against benchmarks;
- **Custody and segregation** of trust assets — see the
  parallel
  [IB Code on client assets](./cop-ib-client-assets.md)
  for the broader framework.

### Beneficiary information rights

Beneficiaries' information rights under Jersey law (see
[`../trusts/article-29-disclosure.md`](../trusts/article-29-disclosure.md)
and [`../trusts/beneficiary-rights.md`](../trusts/beneficiary-rights.md))
are reinforced by the Code's customer-treatment expectations:

- **Fair handling** of beneficiary information requests;
- **Documented decision** when refusing disclosure of
  privileged or sensitive material;
- **Royal Court referral** where the disclosure decision is
  contested (under Article 51).

### Conflicts management

Conflicts are routine in trustee work:

- **Conflicts between beneficiaries** — different family
  branches with different interests in the trust property;
- **Conflicts between trustee and beneficiaries** — fees,
  related-party transactions;
- **Conflicts between the trustee firm's clients** — the
  same TCB serves multiple trusts with overlapping
  beneficiaries or assets.

The Code expects:

- **Conflicts register** maintained at trust level and at
  firm level;
- **Disclosed conflicts** managed with documented
  mitigation;
- **Restricted handling** where the conflict is material —
  separate teams, Chinese walls, or, where mitigation is
  insufficient, declining to act.

## Particular trustee scenarios

### Acting as trustee of a multi-generational family trust

The deepest trustee practice. The Code's expectations
amplify:

- Multi-decade horizon for decision-making;
- Family-governance interface — protector / family council
  / next-generation engagement;
- Periodic review of the trust's continuing fitness for
  purpose (terminating, varying, or migrating where
  appropriate);
- Royal Court engagement under Article 51 for momentous
  decisions ([`../trusts/article-51-directions.md`](../trusts/article-51-directions.md));
- Article 47 mistake-regime considerations
  ([`../trusts/article-47-set-aside.md`](../trusts/article-47-set-aside.md))
  for past decisions challenged by changed circumstances.

### Acting as trustee of a Jersey Private Fund

Some Jersey trusts hold interests in JPFs (typically family-
office co-investment patterns). The trustee's role spans:

- Trust-side fiduciary duties to beneficiaries;
- JPF-side compliance through the
  [DSP framework](./cop-fsb-jpf-dsp.md);
- Investment-suitability for the trust given the JPF's
  strategy.

### Acting as trustee where the settlor is also a beneficiary

Common for "reserved-powers" trusts under
[Article 9A](../trusts/reserved-powers.md). The Code's
expectations:

- **Distinguish settlor-as-settlor (with reserved powers)
  from settlor-as-beneficiary** clearly in decision-making;
- **Document conflicts** carefully — the settlor's reserved
  powers create structural conflicts that need active
  management;
- **Beneficiary class** is identified including the
  settlor where applicable.

### Acting as successor trustee

Where the TCB is replacing a retiring or removed trustee
([`../trusts/outgoing-trustee.md`](../trusts/outgoing-trustee.md)):

- **Predecessor liability** — the successor takes the
  trust as found and should not silently absorb
  predecessor failures;
- **Indemnity** from outgoing trustee documented;
- **Transfer of trust property** structured carefully —
  Royal Court directions where the change is contested.

## Common trustee-conduct failure points

JFSC enforcement and Royal Court case-law surface:

- **Inadequate documented reasoning** for distribution
  decisions;
- **Letter-of-wishes treated as mandatory** when it is
  non-binding (or, conversely, ignored when it is the
  primary guide);
- **Investment delegation without retained oversight** —
  trustee treats delegation as displacing fiduciary
  responsibility;
- **Conflicts under-disclosed** between firm clients;
- **Beneficiary information requests** handled defensively
  rather than under documented framework;
- **Periodic review skipped** — initial discretion exercised
  well, ongoing review absent.

A trustee programme that addresses each of these withstands
both Royal Court and JFSC scrutiny.

## Why this matters for wealth management

For a Jersey TCB integrated with wealth-management services,
the trustee role is the **load-bearing fiduciary commitment**.
Failures here generate both:

- **Royal Court litigation** at the suit of beneficiaries
  (the most serious of which can result in personal
  trustee liability);
- **JFSC enforcement** under the TCB Code (public
  statements, financial penalties, fit-and-proper
  consequences).

The discipline is therefore among the most expensively
operated in the business — and the Code's section here is
where the JFSC's expectation meets the Royal Court's
case-law-developed standard.
