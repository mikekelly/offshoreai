# TCB Code of Practice — Private Trust Companies (PTCs)

A **Private Trust Company (PTC)** is a Jersey company
specifically established to act as trustee of a **single
trust or a family of related trusts**. PTCs are the
preferred structure where a wealthy family wants
**family-control of the trustee** rather than appointing a
commercial Jersey TCB as trustee.

This file is the orientation surface for the regulatory
treatment. For the use-case-level structuring framing, see
[`../sectors/family-business-succession.md`](../sectors/family-business-succession.md).

## What a PTC is

A PTC is:

- A **Jersey-incorporated company** (under
  [CJL 1991](../companies/companies-law-1991.md));
- **Established to act as trustee** of a defined family
  trust or set of family-related trusts;
- **Not generally available to the public** as a trustee
  service — it is family-specific;
- **Often controlled by family members** through its
  directorate and shareholding;
- **Administered by a Jersey-licensed TCB** (the
  "administrator") on behalf of the family.

The PTC structure separates:

- **Strategic trustee decisions** (family-controlled at the
  PTC board level);
- **Operational trustee administration** (delegated to the
  licensed TCB administrator).

## The TCB-registration exemption

The Jersey regulatory framework provides a **carve-out from
full TCB registration** for PTCs that satisfy prescribed
conditions. Without the carve-out, every PTC would itself
need JFSC registration as a TCB — defeating the structure's
flexibility.

### Conditions for exemption (subject to verification)

The carve-out typically requires the PTC to:

- **Act only as trustee of family-related trusts** —
  trusts connected to a single family or settlor group;
- **Not market its services publicly** — no general
  trustee-services offering;
- **Have its administration provided by a Jersey-licensed
  TCB** — the operational discipline is supplied by the
  regulated administrator;
- **Comply with prescribed governance and notification
  requirements** — even where exempt from full TCB
  registration, certain JFSC notifications are required.

The exact conditions are set out in JFSC publications and
should be verified against current materials before
establishing a PTC.

## The administrator's role

The Jersey-licensed TCB administering a PTC carries
**regulatory responsibility for the PTC's operations**, even
though the PTC itself is not a regulated entity. The
administrator:

- **Performs CDD on the PTC's underlying family** rather
  than on the PTC as a standalone entity;
- **Maintains the PTC's records** to standards consistent
  with the administrator's TCB Code obligations;
- **Provides administrative trustee services** —
  bookkeeping, distribution processing, beneficiary
  reporting, regulatory filings;
- **Engages with the JFSC** on the PTC's behalf where
  required;
- **Applies the TCB Code's customer-treatment, conflicts,
  outsourcing, and record-keeping disciplines** to the
  PTC's operations.

The administrator's TCB licence covers the PTC's services
indirectly — the administrator is the regulated entity, the
PTC is the unregulated client.

## PTC governance

The PTC's own governance typically includes:

- **Board of directors** — mix of family members,
  professional advisers, and (often) representatives of the
  administering TCB;
- **Shares** held by a purpose trust (very common — the
  "purpose trust over PTC" structure means no natural
  person owns the PTC, removing it from natural-person-
  beneficial-owner analysis);
- **Constitutional documents** specifying the PTC's purpose,
  the trusts it administers, and the governance
  arrangements.

The purpose-trust-over-PTC structure is a Jersey-specific
elegance: combining the family's PTC with the Jersey
[purpose trust](../trusts/purpose-trusts.md) regime under
the Trusts Law.

## When a PTC is the right choice

For UHNW families (typically £25m+ but often substantially
more):

- **Family control of trustee** is wanted — the family
  participates directly in trustee decisions rather than
  delegating fully to a commercial trustee;
- **Long-term family-governance** — the PTC provides
  structural continuity across generations of family
  trustee involvement;
- **Multi-trust efficiency** — the family has multiple
  trusts (one per branch, one per asset class, etc.) and
  the PTC consolidates trustee administration;
- **Privacy and discretion** at family-board level — sensitive
  trustee decisions discussed in family-only PTC board
  meetings rather than at the administering TCB level.

The cost of operating a PTC structure (administrator fees +
PTC governance overhead) typically only justifies itself
above a meaningful asset threshold.

## When a PTC is NOT the right choice

- **Smaller families** where the cost-benefit ratio doesn't
  work;
- **Families wanting full delegation** of trustee
  responsibility to a commercial trustee (the PTC
  re-introduces family responsibility for fiduciary
  decisions);
- **Conflicts management** — PTC family directors are
  exposed to fiduciary duty to beneficiaries; for some
  families this is unwelcome;
- **International tax complications** — a family-controlled
  PTC may be a tax-residence concern in some
  jurisdictions; specific tax advice is essential.

## Common PTC-administration failure points

JFSC supervisory themes:

- **PTC operating outside the family-trust carve-out
  conditions** — accepting non-family trusts, marketing
  services publicly;
- **Administrator's CDD on family inadequate** — relying on
  the PTC's family identity rather than performing direct
  CDD on natural-person beneficial owners of the family;
- **PTC board operating without minutes / decision
  documentation** — the family-control element is
  documentation-light;
- **Conflicts under-managed** — PTC directors with multiple
  conflicting roles in the family / trust structure;
- **Cross-jurisdictional residence** of PTC directors not
  managed — the PTC's tax residence and management-and-
  control position drifts as family members move.

## PTC-specific TCB Code application

The Code's TCB-administrator-of-PTC application typically
adapts:

- **Customer treatment** — family is the "customer" for
  customer-treatment purposes;
- **Conflicts** — family-board conflicts surface
  prominently;
- **Outsourcing** — the administrator does not "outsource"
  to the PTC; the PTC is the trustee, the administrator
  provides services to it;
- **Reporting** — periodic returns to the JFSC cover the
  PTC alongside the administrator's other clients.
