# IB Code of Practice — Client Categorisation

Client categorisation is the **upstream gate** that
calibrates every other conduct-of-business obligation under
the Investment Business Code. It determines:

- The intensity of the [suitability assessment](./cop-ib-suitability.md);
- The level of disclosure required;
- Whether the appropriateness test applies (for execution-
  only);
- The client's status under information requirements;
- The applicability of investor-compensation arrangements.

Getting categorisation wrong is therefore not a technical
slip — it cascades into every customer-facing obligation
under the Code.

## The three categories

The IB Code recognises three client categories:

### Retail client

**The default**. Any client not formally categorised as
professional or eligible counterparty is retail. Retail
clients are entitled to the **highest level of regulatory
protection**:

- Full suitability assessment for advised relationships;
- Full disclosure regime (fees, charges, conflicts, risks);
- Specific risk warnings on complex products;
- Investor-compensation scheme protection where applicable.

Most individual clients of a Jersey wealth-management
business fall into this category by default.

### Professional client

Clients with sufficient **experience, knowledge, and
expertise** to make their own investment decisions and
properly assess the risks involved. The Code recognises
two sub-categories:

- **Per-se professional** — entities that qualify as
  professional by virtue of what they are (regulated
  financial institutions, large undertakings meeting size
  tests, national / regional governments, public bodies).
- **Elective professional** — clients who do not qualify
  per-se but **opt up** to professional status on the basis
  of meeting prescribed criteria.

#### Elective professional — the opt-up test

The opt-up typically requires the client to satisfy at
least two of three quantitative-qualitative criteria:

1. **Frequency of transactions** in the relevant market of
   the client's intended dealing — average of 10 per
   quarter over the preceding four quarters;
2. **Portfolio size** including cash deposits and financial
   instruments — exceeding a prescribed threshold
   (typically EUR 500,000 or equivalent);
3. **Professional experience** — at least one year working
   in a relevant professional position requiring knowledge
   of the transactions / services envisaged.

Plus:

- The client requests opt-up **in writing**;
- The business provides a **written warning** of the
  protections being lost;
- The client confirms in writing they are aware of the
  consequences.

The business must keep evidence of all three criteria and
of the documentation flow.

### Eligible counterparty

The **lowest protection tier** — for dealings between
regulated financial firms in their professional capacity.
Limited to specific transaction types (dealing, execution,
related services). Suitability and appropriateness do not
apply; the relationship is between regulated peers acting
in professional capacity.

Per-se eligible counterparties include investment firms,
credit institutions, insurance undertakings, certain UCITS,
pension funds, and other regulated entities. Elective
eligible-counterparty status is available subject to opt-up.

## The reverse — opt-down

A client categorised as professional or eligible
counterparty can request to **opt down** to a more
protective category. The business should accommodate this
request and document the change. A per-se professional
client who has not opted up cannot opt-out of retail
treatment — they are already at the most protective
default.

## Categorisation by transaction type

Categorisation can be **service-specific**. A client may be:

- Retail for some services / instruments;
- Professional for others;
- Eligible counterparty for a narrow set of dealing
  services.

The business documents categorisation per service-instrument
combination where relevant. Most retail private-client
relationships are uniformly retail across the entire
engagement; categorisation variation arises mainly in
institutional and family-office relationships.

## When categorisation must be done

- **Onboarding** — initial categorisation documented in the
  client file before any service is provided;
- **Periodic review** — at least annually, the business
  re-examines whether the categorisation remains correct;
- **Change of circumstance** — when the client's
  circumstances change materially (loss of regulated
  status, change in portfolio size, change in qualifying
  experience), categorisation is re-examined.

## Documentation expectations

For every client:

- **Categorisation outcome** recorded in the client file
  (retail / professional per-se / professional elective /
  eligible counterparty per-se / eligible counterparty
  elective);
- **Basis for the categorisation** — for per-se categories,
  the qualifying facts; for elective categories, the
  documented opt-up package;
- **Evidence of warnings provided** to opt-up clients;
- **Client's written confirmation** for opt-ups;
- **Review history** showing periodic re-examination.

## Common categorisation failure points

JFSC enforcement and thematic-review themes:

- **Default-professional treatment** — sophisticated-looking
  clients categorised as professional without the opt-up
  package being documented (or the opt-up criteria being
  met);
- **Categorisation drift** — initial documentation good, no
  periodic review;
- **Generic warnings** — opt-up warnings copy-pasted rather
  than calibrated to the specific protections being lost;
- **Insufficient elective-professional evidence** — the
  three criteria assessed lightly;
- **Mixed-service relationships** without service-specific
  categorisation where appropriate;
- **No documented opt-down handling**.

## Why this matters for wealth management

For a Jersey wealth-management business, the **vast majority
of HNW and UHNW clients are retail** despite their
sophistication. The Code's protection regime is
**calibrated to the legal category, not to commercial
sophistication**. A client with £50m of investable assets
who has never worked in financial services and trades
infrequently is retail under the elective-professional
test — and the business owes them full retail-grade
suitability and disclosure regardless of their wealth.

Mis-categorising HNW clients as professional to streamline
operations is a recurring failure mode that the JFSC has
addressed in enforcement. The right answer is **retail by
default with full retail-grade protections**, with elective
upgrade only where the criteria are genuinely met and
documented.
