# Investment Business Code of Practice — Suitability

Suitability is the **load-bearing obligation** for any Jersey
investment business giving advice to or managing investments
for retail clients. The JFSC Code of Practice for Investment
Business operationalises the Article 19 FSL 1998 power into a
detailed suitability discipline that an investment business
must satisfy.

This file gives the structural answer; cite the JFSC's current
Code publication for authoritative wording on any specific
sub-rule.

## What suitability means

Suitability is the discipline of **matching investment advice
or discretionary decisions to the specific client**. The Code's
suitability standard requires the investment business to:

1. **Know the client** — investment knowledge and experience,
   financial situation (including capacity for loss),
   investment objectives, attitude to risk, time horizon;
2. **Understand the product or service** — risks, costs,
   complexity, liquidity profile, expected returns;
3. **Match the two** — recommend only investments / services
   that are suitable for the specific client given their
   profile;
4. **Document the reasoning** — maintain a suitability record
   that an external reviewer could follow;
5. **Review suitability over time** — periodically reassess
   as client circumstances evolve.

## When suitability applies

The Code distinguishes the **service** being provided:

### Investment advice

Personal recommendations to retail or professional clients
trigger **full suitability** — the business must be satisfied
that the recommendation is suitable before making it. The
suitability letter (or equivalent record) documents the
reasoning.

### Discretionary investment management

Ongoing portfolio management decisions on behalf of a client
trigger **full suitability**, applied to the **portfolio as a
whole** at periodic review intervals rather than transaction-
by-transaction. The investment management agreement (IMA)
captures the mandate; periodic reporting evidences ongoing
suitability.

### Execution-only (non-advised)

Execution-only services trigger an **appropriateness**
assessment rather than suitability — verifying that the client
has the knowledge / experience to understand the product they
are choosing to deal in, without recommending it. See
[`cop-ib-appropriateness.md`](./cop-ib-appropriateness.md)
*(stub)*.

The line between advised and non-advised services is operative
and the Code is attentive to **disguised advice** — services
marketed as execution-only but practically operating as
recommendation. The investment business needs internal controls
to keep the line clear.

## Client categorisation — the upstream gate

Suitability obligations are calibrated by **client
categorisation**. The Code recognises three categories:

| Category | Suitability intensity | Source for documenting |
|---|---|---|
| **Retail client** | Highest; full suitability disclosure including risk warnings and explanatory matter | Cf. [`cop-ib-client-categorisation.md`](./cop-ib-client-categorisation.md) *(stub)* |
| **Professional client** | Reduced; can be presumed to have necessary knowledge / experience for certain categories of investment | |
| **Eligible counterparty** | Minimal; for dealing services between regulated firms | |

The **default categorisation is retail**; uplift to
professional or eligible counterparty requires the client to
meet defined criteria and (for professional uplift) to opt in
on an informed basis. An investment business cannot escape
retail-standard suitability by mis-categorising a client.

## The suitability assessment — the four pillars

Every suitability assessment captures these four pillars,
sized to the client's profile and the product complexity:

### 1. Client knowledge and experience

What the client understands about the relevant investment
categories — the more complex / risky the product, the deeper
the knowledge expected.

- Investment experience by category (equities, fixed income,
  structured products, derivatives, alternatives, complex
  illiquid products);
- Formal qualifications, professional background where
  relevant;
- The investment business validates this through **on-record
  questionnaire responses + corroborating evidence** where
  the recommended product warrants it.

### 2. Financial situation

The client's broader financial position, including the
**capacity to absorb loss** without unacceptable consequences.

- Income and outgoings;
- Assets and liabilities (composition and concentration);
- Other invested wealth (the recommended investment is one
  piece of a portfolio, not a standalone bet);
- Liquidity needs and time horizons.

### 3. Investment objectives

What the client is trying to achieve.

- Capital preservation / income / growth / specific liability
  matching;
- Time horizon;
- Specific constraints (ethical / ESG / family / employment
  restrictions);
- Attitude to drawdown — what loss the client can tolerate
  in pursuit of the objective.

### 4. Risk tolerance and capacity

Distinct concepts:

- **Tolerance** — psychological / behavioural ability to
  withstand loss without changing plan;
- **Capacity** — financial ability to absorb loss without
  affecting essential needs or objectives.

Both must be considered; a high-tolerance, low-capacity
client should not be recommended high-risk products.

## The suitability letter

For a personal recommendation to a retail client, the **suitability
letter** documents the assessment and is provided to the
client. Typical content:

- Summary of the client's profile (the four pillars above);
- The recommendation made;
- Why the recommendation is suitable given the profile;
- Material risks specific to the recommendation;
- Costs and charges (cost / benefit transparency);
- Any inconsistencies with the client's stated objectives
  that the client should be aware of;
- Confirmation of the client's understanding and consent.

The suitability letter is **not** a marketing document; it is
a regulated compliance record. Tone and content are calibrated
to defensibility on subsequent challenge, not to client
persuasion.

## Ongoing suitability

For ongoing relationships (discretionary management,
investment advice retainers):

- **Periodic review** — at least annually; more frequently for
  high-net-worth or higher-risk profiles;
- **Trigger-event review** — material change in client
  circumstance (loss of employment, divorce, inheritance,
  illness) prompts immediate re-assessment;
- **Market-event review** — material market dislocation may
  prompt portfolio-level review against current suitability;
- **Annual suitability statement** — many businesses produce
  a written annual statement re-confirming suitability
  conclusions.

## Common failure points

The JFSC's enforcement record and thematic-review history
surface recurring suitability failure points:

- **Inadequate client information** — categorisation done on
  thin evidence;
- **Generic recommendations** — recommendations that could
  have been made to anyone with similar tickbox responses;
  not specific to the client;
- **Concentration risk overlooked** — recommendation
  unsuitable in the context of the client's broader portfolio
  even if suitable on its own;
- **Drift over time** — initial suitability documented but no
  subsequent periodic review;
- **Disguised advice** — execution-only services in form,
  advice in substance;
- **Inadequate disclosure of complex-product risks** —
  particularly structured products, alternative investments,
  illiquid private funds;
- **Costs and charges opacity** — total cost not transparently
  presented (initial + ongoing + product-level + adviser-
  level).

A suitability programme that addresses each of these points
in its design will pass thematic-review scrutiny.

## Interaction with AML/CFT obligations

Suitability is **independent of** the AML/CFT CDD obligation —
both apply to every advised / discretionary relationship. The
two assessments use overlapping client information but answer
different questions:

| Discipline | Question |
|---|---|
| Suitability | Is this product / service right for this client? |
| CDD ([`../aml-cft/amlh-cdd.md`](../aml-cft/amlh-cdd.md)) | Who is this client and where do their funds come from? |
| EDD ([`../aml-cft/amlh-edd.md`](../aml-cft/amlh-edd.md)) | Is the source of wealth verified to enhanced standard? |

In practice, the investment business gathers client
information **once** at onboarding and uses it across both
disciplines. The records, however, remain distinct: a
suitability file and a CDD file.
