# TCB Code of Practice — Acting as Commercial Director

A common Jersey TCB function is **acting as director of
client-administered companies on a commercial basis**. The
TCB principal (typically a senior individual at the licensed
TCB firm) sits as director — often the only natural-person
director — of Jersey companies that the TCB administers.

This is **within TCB scope** under
[FSL 1998 (Class — TCB)](./fsl-class-tcb.md) precisely
because it is **by way of business**.

## Two regimes apply

When the TCB principal acts as director, **two regimes
apply in parallel**:

- **Company law** — Article 74 of
  [CJL 1991](../companies/companies-law-1991.md)
  fiduciary duties (see [`../companies/directors-duties.md`](../companies/directors-duties.md))
  enforceable at the suit of the company / shareholders;
- **TCB Code** — the JFSC's conduct expectations on the TCB
  principal in their commercial-director capacity,
  enforceable by the JFSC.

A director can satisfy company-law duties and fail Code
expectations, or vice versa. A robust commercial-director
practice satisfies both.

## The Article 74 duties

The CJL 1991 Article 74 statutory duties:

- **Act in good faith** with a view to the **best interests
  of the company**;
- **Exercise the care, diligence, and skill** that a
  reasonably prudent person would exercise in comparable
  circumstances.

These are **owed to the company**, not to the company's
shareholder or to the trust above the company. Where the
TCB principal is a director of a company held by a Jersey
trust, the director's primary duty is to the company itself
(per ordinary company law); the trust's interests are
addressed at trustee level, not at director level.

This **two-tier structure** is fundamental and a frequent
source of confusion.

## The TCB Code overlay

Beyond the Article 74 duties, the Code expects the TCB
principal to:

### Maintain genuine governance

- **Attend board meetings** of every company they direct;
- **Substantively engage** in decision-making, not merely
  rubber-stamp;
- **Document decisions and reasoning** in minutes;
- **Resign** where they cannot effectively perform the role.

The Code's underlying concern: a TCB principal acting as
director of dozens of client companies cannot give each
genuine attention. The Code expects **capacity discipline**
— the TCB takes on directorships only to the extent it can
discharge the role meaningfully.

### Manage conflicts

The TCB principal serving as director of multiple client
companies faces inevitable conflicts:

- **Between companies** — where two TCB-directed companies
  transact with each other;
- **Between company and trust** — where the company's
  director (TCB principal) is also engaged at trust-level
  decisions affecting the company;
- **Between company and TCB firm** — where the TCB firm's
  commercial interests interact with the company's
  position.

The Code expects:

- **Identification** of each material conflict;
- **Disclosure** in board minutes and at company level;
- **Recusal** where the conflict is material;
- **Documented decision** of what was done.

### Apply skill appropriate to the company's activity

The TCB principal must have, or have access to, sufficient
skill for the company's business:

- A TCB principal directing a property-investment company
  needs property-investment competence (or access to it);
- Directing a trading company demands operational-business
  competence;
- Directing a regulatory-licensed entity demands regulated-
  business competence.

The Code disfavours TCB principals taking on directorships
they are not equipped to discharge.

## Particular director scenarios

### Director of a Jersey company held by a Jersey trust

The classic TCB structure. The TCB principal:

- Is director of the company (subject to Article 74 + Code);
- Is also (often) trustee of the trust above, or works for
  the trust's trustee firm;
- Manages the **two-tier interaction** — directorship duties
  to the company, trustee duties to the beneficiaries via
  the trust's holding of the company's shares.

This is a managed conflict requiring documented procedures
for decisions where the two roles point in different
directions.

### Director of a Jersey company in a fund structure

Often the TCB principal is director of a Jersey-incorporated
**General Partner** of an LP, or a Jersey-incorporated
**holding company** within a fund structure. The director's
duties are to the company; the fund's investors are
addressed via the fund's manager / GP arrangements at a
separate level.

### Director of an AIM-listed Jersey plc

Distinct from a private-company directorship. The TCB
principal is unlikely to be a director of an AIM-listed plc
in a fully-commercial-director capacity (that role is
typically taken by company executives or experienced non-
executive directors); but where it occurs, the
[AIM-listed plc operating reality](../use-cases/aim-listed-jersey-plc/index.md)
adds layers of MAR, Takeover Code, DTR, etc. obligations.

### Director of a charitable-purpose entity

For Jersey foundations (or non-charitable purpose trusts
with corporate underlying vehicles), the TCB principal's
director role applies the same Article 74 + Code framework
calibrated to the entity's particular purpose.

## Common director-capacity failure modes

JFSC and Royal Court themes:

- **Excessive directorship count** at a single TCB
  principal — capacity stretched beyond effective
  oversight;
- **Pro-forma board minutes** — meetings recorded but
  substantive decisions not documented;
- **Resolution by rubber-stamp** of decisions effectively
  made by client / trust / external manager — the director
  is a fiduciary, not a formality;
- **Conflicts under-managed** — directors of two
  transacting client companies operating without recusal /
  disclosure;
- **Skill gaps** — director without competence for the
  company's business and without bringing in
  competence;
- **Indemnity reliance** — the director relies on
  client / trust indemnities for personal exposure,
  potentially eroding the substantive duty.
