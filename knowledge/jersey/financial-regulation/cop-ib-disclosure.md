# IB Code of Practice — Disclosure of Fees, Charges and Conflicts

Disclosure is the **mechanism by which the conduct-of-
business regime operates** — informed consent by the client
to fees, charges, conflicts, and material risks. Inadequate
disclosure undermines suitability, prevents informed
decision-making, and is itself a Code breach independently
of any other failure.

## What must be disclosed

The Code expects clear, accurate, and timely disclosure of:

### Fees and charges

The **total cost** of the investment service to the client:

- **Adviser fees** — the firm's own fees for advice /
  management;
- **Platform / wrap fees** where the firm uses third-party
  platforms;
- **Product fees** — fund management charges, performance
  fees, structured-product fees inside investment products
  the firm recommends;
- **Custody / safekeeping charges** for client assets;
- **Transaction costs** — explicit commissions / spreads /
  market-impact costs;
- **Currency-conversion costs** where multi-currency
  arrangements;
- **Any tied / connected-party arrangements** that affect
  cost;
- **Indirect costs** the client may not see directly (e.g.
  inducements received by the firm from product providers).

The Code expects disclosure in a form the client can
understand and use — typically **annual cost summary**
showing total cost as a percentage of assets under
management plus illustrative monetary amounts.

### Conflicts of interest

The Code's conflicts framework operates as:

- **Identify** material conflicts — between the firm and
  the client, between clients, between the firm and its
  related parties;
- **Avoid** where possible — particularly conflicts that
  cannot be effectively managed through disclosure;
- **Manage** through documented procedures (Chinese walls,
  trade allocation rules, personal account dealing
  policies);
- **Disclose** unavoidable conflicts to the client clearly
  enough to allow informed consent.

Specific conflicts the Code addresses:

- **Recommendation of in-house products** vs third-party
  alternatives;
- **Bundled services** with affiliated providers (custody,
  fund management, broker-dealer services);
- **Inducements** received from product providers
  (commissions, soft commissions, rebates, hospitality);
- **Personal account dealing** by the firm's staff in
  recommended securities;
- **Best-execution conflicts** where the firm has
  affiliated counterparties.

### Material risks

Disclosure of risks specific to the service / product:

- **Investment-specific risks** — market risk, credit risk,
  liquidity risk, currency risk, complexity risk;
- **Service-specific risks** — discretionary vs advisory
  mandate boundaries, custody arrangements, default-of-
  intermediary scenarios;
- **Performance risk warnings** — the standard "past
  performance is not a guide to future returns" disclosure
  in marketing material;
- **Complex-product risks** — structured products,
  alternatives, illiquid investments, derivatives.

### Status and protections

- **Regulatory status** of the firm — Jersey-licensed
  investment business under FSL 1998;
- **Investor-compensation arrangements** — applicable
  schemes and limits;
- **Complaints procedure** — how the client raises a
  complaint, and the firm's response framework;
- **Termination terms** — how the client can exit the
  relationship.

## When disclosure must happen

Multiple stages:

### Pre-onboarding / proposal stage

Before the client commits to the relationship:

- **Initial disclosure document** (IDD) or equivalent terms
  document covering the categories above;
- **Fee illustration** based on the client's intended
  portfolio size and service profile;
- **Conflict statement** — known material conflicts
  affecting the proposed relationship.

### Onboarding documentation

In the client agreement:

- **Detailed fee schedule** binding on both parties;
- **Conflicts policy** referenced;
- **Risk disclosures** for the specific services /
  products contemplated;
- **Reporting frequency** and content commitments.

### Ongoing reporting

- **Quarterly portfolio statements** with valuations,
  transactions, fees applied;
- **Annual cost summary** showing aggregate costs;
- **Conflict disclosures** updated when material new
  conflicts arise;
- **Service / product additions** — any material expansion
  of the relationship triggers fresh disclosure.

### Transaction-level

For specific transactions:

- **Pre-trade transaction-cost disclosure** where the
  transaction has material costs the client should be aware
  of;
- **Post-trade confirmation** within prescribed timing;
- **Best-execution evidence** available on request.

## Common disclosure failure points

JFSC enforcement and thematic-review themes:

- **Total-cost opacity** — the headline adviser fee
  disclosed but product-level fees and indirect costs
  buried;
- **Inducements disclosure light** — the firm receives
  payments from product providers that are not surfaced
  meaningfully to the client;
- **Conflicts inventory incomplete** — the conflicts
  register identifies obvious conflicts but misses
  systemic ones (recommendation of in-house products, for
  instance);
- **Generic risk warnings** — boilerplate text rather than
  risk disclosure calibrated to the specific
  recommendation;
- **Drift over time** — initial disclosure good, ongoing
  updates light;
- **Marketing-tone disclosure** — disclosure document
  formatted as marketing material, undermining the
  informed-consent purpose;
- **Affiliated-party arrangements** not disclosed —
  custody, platform, fund management all with affiliated
  firms but client unaware.

A disclosure programme that addresses each of these will
pass thematic-review scrutiny.

## Inducements — particular attention

The Code's treatment of inducements (payments received by
the firm from third parties in connection with services
provided to clients) requires:

- **Identification** of every inducement category — fund
  rebates, commission sharing, soft commissions, hospitality,
  research provision, marketing support, etc.;
- **Quantification** — the firm tracks the value received;
- **Disclosure** to clients of:
  - The fact that inducements are received;
  - The categories of inducement;
  - The value where calculable;
  - How inducements affect the firm's recommendations.
- **Quality enhancement** — the Code expects inducements to
  be permissible only where they enhance the quality of the
  service to the client, and the firm to document the
  enhancement;
- **Periodic review** — inducement arrangements reviewed
  for ongoing acceptability.

This area has been a focus of JFSC enforcement and is
particularly sensitive in wealth-management contexts where
inducements can be material.

## The relationship to suitability

Disclosure and suitability interlock:

- Suitability (see [`./cop-ib-suitability.md`](./cop-ib-suitability.md))
  addresses **whether** a recommendation fits the client;
- Disclosure addresses **whether the client has the
  information** to evaluate the recommendation.

A suitable recommendation poorly disclosed is **inadequately
delivered**. The Code expects both.
