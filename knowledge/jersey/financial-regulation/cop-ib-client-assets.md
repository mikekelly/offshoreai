# IB Code of Practice — Client Assets and Client Money

The protection of **client assets** (securities, financial
instruments) and **client money** (cash held on behalf of
clients) is among the most consequential disciplines in
investment-business regulation. A failure of client-asset
protection is the kind of event that generates immediate
public-statement enforcement and that the JFSC takes
particularly seriously.

This file is the orientation surface for the IB Code's
client-assets section. Cite the JFSC publication for
authoritative requirements.

## The core principle — segregation

The foundational rule: **client assets and client money are
NOT assets of the investment business**. They are held on
behalf of clients and must be:

- **Segregated** from the firm's own assets;
- **Identified** as client property;
- **Protected** against the firm's creditors in the event of
  the firm's insolvency;
- **Returnable** to clients on demand (subject to
  contractual notice / liquidity terms).

This principle is not aspirational; it is the **legal and
operational foundation** of the trust the client places in
the investment business.

## Client money — what it is

The Code typically defines "client money" as money received
or held by the firm in the course of providing investment
services that is **not immediately due and payable** to the
firm (e.g. for fees earned and invoiced).

Common categories:

- **Subscription / investment money** received from clients
  pending allocation to investments;
- **Sale proceeds** received from disposals pending
  distribution or reinvestment;
- **Income** (dividends, interest, distributions) received
  on client portfolios;
- **Cash on a discretionary mandate** held within the
  mandate's authorised cash allocation;
- **Settlements** in transit between counterparties.

## How client money must be held

The standard expectation:

- **Held in client money accounts** at one or more approved
  banks;
- **Separately designated** as client money — typically
  through dedicated account naming, agreement terms, and
  bank acknowledgement;
- **Statutory trust** or equivalent legal arrangement that
  isolates the money from the firm's general estate;
- **Reconciled daily** between firm records and bank
  records;
- **Reported** to the JFSC in the firm's periodic returns.

The choice of approved banks is itself regulated — the firm
maintains a documented approval methodology, applies
appropriate due diligence on banks, and reviews concentration
risk.

## Client assets — what they are

Client assets are typically:

- **Securities** (equities, bonds, fund units) held on behalf
  of clients;
- **Financial instruments** (derivatives, structured
  products);
- **Title documents** to investments where the firm holds
  custody.

Held either:

- **Directly in the client's name** (where the asset
  type and infrastructure permit);
- **In nominee** (typically the firm's nominee company) on
  behalf of the client; or
- **In a sub-custody chain** where the firm has a custodian
  who holds the asset.

## How client assets must be held

The expectations:

- **Held in the client's name** or in a nominee designated as
  holding for the client;
- **Records** identify each client's holding with sufficient
  precision that any client's assets can be specifically
  identified and returned;
- **Reconciliation** between firm records and external
  records (custodian statements, registry confirmations) at
  appropriate frequency;
- **Sub-custody diligence** — where assets are held via a
  sub-custodian, the firm has undertaken capability /
  financial / regulatory diligence on the sub-custodian;
- **Pooling and shortfalls** — where assets are pooled (e.g.
  in an omnibus account), the firm's records identify each
  client's notional share; shortfalls due to sub-custodian
  failure are allocated per a documented methodology.

## Client-asset documentation in the agreement

The investment management agreement (IMA) or terms of
business document:

- **Title** to client assets and money;
- **Use** of client assets (typically prohibits use for the
  firm's own account except in narrow specified
  circumstances);
- **Sub-custody arrangements** disclosed;
- **Pooling** terms disclosed;
- **Statement frequency** and content;
- **Termination and return-of-assets** mechanics.

Clients are entitled to know **where their assets are held**,
**how they are held**, and **what the firm's recovery
arrangements are** in failure scenarios.

## Reconciliation regime

The Code typically expects:

- **Daily reconciliation** of client money between firm
  records and bank statements;
- **Periodic reconciliation** (typically monthly or more
  frequent) of client assets between firm records and
  custodian / registry records;
- **Documented investigation** of discrepancies and timely
  resolution;
- **Material discrepancies escalated** to senior management
  and (where they cross thresholds) to the JFSC.

## Periodic statement obligations

The firm provides clients with:

- **Quarterly portfolio statements** (or more frequent if
  required by the client agreement);
- **Position statements** identifying each holding;
- **Cash statements** showing client money balances;
- **Transaction statements** showing dealings in the period.

Content expectations include valuations, basis of valuation
(market price, model price, last-traded), and changes since
the previous period.

## Common client-asset failure modes

JFSC enforcement records and thematic-review themes:

- **Reconciliation lapses** — daily reconciliations not
  performed or not investigated when discrepancies arise;
- **Inadequate sub-custodian diligence** — sub-custodian
  failure exposes clients to losses the firm did not foresee;
- **Pooling without records** of client-by-client allocation;
- **Client-money buffer thresholds breached** — short-term
  use of client money for firm purposes;
- **Records inadequate to reconstruct positions** after
  system failures;
- **Agreement and disclosure gaps** — clients not informed
  of sub-custody chains.

A client-asset programme that addresses each of these will
withstand thematic-review scrutiny.

## Interaction with FCA CASS (group context)

For Jersey investment businesses in groups with UK FCA-
authorised affiliates — see
[`../external-rules/fca-handbook-jersey-touchpoints.md`](../external-rules/fca-handbook-jersey-touchpoints.md) —
the UK affiliate is subject to **FCA CASS** (Client Assets
Sourcebook). CASS and the JFSC IB Code's client-assets
section are substantively similar but with distinct
documentation, reconciliation, and reporting requirements.
Group operations typically run CASS-grade controls
universally (CASS being the more prescriptive baseline) and
document the application to satisfy both regimes.

## Why this matters for wealth management

For a Jersey wealth-management business — particularly with
discretionary mandates — client-asset protection is the
**most consequential single control**. Failures here:

- Trigger immediate JFSC supervisory intervention;
- Expose the firm to client claims;
- Generate public-statement enforcement;
- Damage the firm's reputation and licence-to-operate.

The discipline of segregation, reconciliation, and
disclosure is therefore among the **most expensively
operated** controls in the business — and the agent
answering a client-asset question for a CCO is in
high-stakes territory.
