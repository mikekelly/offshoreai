# JFSC Codes of Practice

The **JFSC Codes of Practice** are the regulator's class-by-class
conduct standards for businesses registered under the principal
regulatory Laws. There is **one Code per regulated activity class**
plus separate Codes for fund vehicles (Certified Funds and
Alternative Investment Funds).

This file is the **hub**: it lists all nine current Codes with
their effective dates and points at the per-Code index. Each
per-Code index opens the Code's section-by-section coverage.

## Where Codes sit in the regulatory hierarchy

The Jersey financial-regulation framework has four layers:

1. **Principal Laws** — the
   [Financial Services (Jersey) Law 1998](./financial-services-jersey-law-1998.md),
   the [Banking Business (Jersey) Law 1991](./banking-business-law-1991.md),
   the [Collective Investment Funds (Jersey) Law 1988](./collective-investment-funds-law-1988.md),
   the [Insurance Business (Jersey) Law 1996](./insurance-business-law-1996.md),
   and the
   [Financial Services Commission (Jersey) Law 1998](./jfsc-law-1998.md).
2. **Regulations and Orders** — subordinate legislation made by
   the States or the relevant Minister.
3. **JFSC Codes of Practice** — issued by the JFSC under powers
   conferred by the principal Laws (typically Article 19 of the
   FSL for FSL-class Codes). Codes are **mandatory**: breach
   triggers enforcement consequences under the principal Law.
4. **JFSC Guidance** — non-mandatory interpretation and
   application aids. Distinct from the Codes (which are binding)
   and from the
   [AML/CFT Handbook](../aml-cft/aml-cft-handbook.md) (which is
   guidance with a special quasi-binding status — see that file).

Codes are **the operational reality** for a regulated business.
The principal Law sets the perimeter (what's in scope, who must
register, what triggers fit-and-proper); the Code sets the
day-to-day conduct expectations the JFSC supervises against.

## Why an agent should read the per-Code coverage rather than this hub

A wealth-manager CCO or RM dealing with a real operational
question — *"can we outsource X to a non-Jersey service
provider?"*, *"what does the Code require for suitability
documentation?"*, *"who can sign off on the EDD memo?"* — needs
the section-level rule, not the hub-level summary. This hub
exists for orientation; the per-Code indexes and section files
exist for retrieval.

specialist surfaces — read those when the question concerns the
specific class.

## Common Code structure

Most Codes share a recognisable section structure, with class-
specific elaboration:

- **Principles** — the high-level conduct standards every
  registered person must meet (integrity, due care, governance,
  customer treatment, market behaviour, financial soundness,
  regulator cooperation).
- **Senior management responsibilities** — board composition,
  designated principal persons, allocation of accountability.
- **Compliance function** — designated compliance officer,
  reporting lines, independence.
- **Risk management** — three-lines-of-defence framing, risk
  appetite, ongoing monitoring.
- **Customer / client treatment** — onboarding, suitability,
  disclosure, complaints, vulnerable-client handling.
- **CDD and AML/CFT** — interface with the
  [AML/CFT Handbook](../aml-cft/aml-cft-handbook.md) and the
  [Money Laundering (Jersey) Order 2008](../aml-cft/money-laundering-order-2008.md).
- **Outsourcing** — what's permitted, what notifications are
  required, what controls must remain in Jersey.
- **Conflict-of-interest management** — identification,
  disclosure, mitigation.
- **Record-keeping** — content and retention.
- **Reporting to the JFSC** — periodic returns, notifications of
  material events.
- **Capital adequacy / financial resources** — class-specific
  rules.
- **Public statements / advertising** — fair, clear and not
  misleading.

The per-Code indexes record where each Code expresses each
theme; the section files record the substantive rule.

## How the Codes interact with other regulator surfaces

Three surfaces sit alongside the Codes and an operational
question often crosses between them:

- **[AML/CFT Handbook](../aml-cft/aml-cft-handbook.md)** —
  shares the CDD, EDD, PEPs, sanctions and SAR territory with
  every Code's customer-treatment section. Where the Code
  refers to AML/CFT obligations, the Handbook is the operational
  detail.
- **[`licensing.md`](./licensing.md)** — fit-and-proper,
  principal persons, key persons, and registration mechanics.
  The Code is what the registered business must *do* after
  registration; `licensing.md` is what it must *be* to register.
- **[`enforcement.md`](./enforcement.md)** — Code breach is the
  most common enforcement trigger. The Code defines the rule;
  the enforcement file describes the consequences.

## How the Codes update

The JFSC consults on Code updates through formal consultation
papers, industry workshops, and a final feedback statement.
Updates take effect on prescribed implementation dates and the
JFSC publishes a "Code of Practice tracked changes" resource
alongside each revision. Files in this section carry the
**effective date** of the version they describe in their
frontmatter `last_verified` field; the source URL points to the
JFSC's current published version. When a Code is replaced, the
file is updated in place — old versions remain in git history.
