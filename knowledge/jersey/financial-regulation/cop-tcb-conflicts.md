# TCB Code of Practice — Conflicts of Interest

Conflicts of interest are **inherent in TCB work**. A
regulated trustee or trust-company business serves multiple
clients with potentially competing interests, acts in
multiple capacities (trustee, director, administrator,
custody), and operates within a Jersey-finance ecosystem of
related parties. Effective conflict management is therefore
**core to TCB practice**, not exceptional.

## What counts as a conflict

The Code recognises several conflict categories:

### Between the TCB and its client

- **Fees** — the TCB's fees vs the client's interest in cost
  minimisation;
- **Related-party transactions** — the TCB's own group
  providing services to the client (custody, investment
  management, fund administration);
- **Recommendations of in-house products** vs third-party
  alternatives;
- **Personal dealing** by TCB staff in securities the TCB's
  clients hold.

### Between clients of the same TCB

- **Two trusts transacting with each other** where the TCB
  acts as trustee of both;
- **Two trusts holding interests in the same company**
  where the TCB administers both and the company itself;
- **Family branches** with conflicting interests in a
  multi-family-trust structure;
- **PE fund and a fund investor** where the TCB serves
  both;
- **Buyer and seller** in a transaction where the TCB has
  fiduciary roles on both sides.

### Within a single client relationship

- **Settlor and beneficiaries** of the same trust — settlor
  wants control, beneficiaries want benefit;
- **Different beneficiary classes** with competing
  entitlements;
- **Generations within a family trust** — current
  beneficiaries want distribution, future generations
  want preservation;
- **Trustee and protector** with overlapping decision
  rights.

### TCB-staff conflicts

- **Personal account dealing** by TCB officers in client
  positions;
- **Outside-business interests** of officers conflicting
  with TCB duties;
- **Family relationships** of TCB officers with clients or
  client family members;
- **Hospitality and gifts** received from clients or
  related parties.

## The Code's framework — identify, manage, disclose, declare unmanageable

### Identify

The Code expects **systematic conflict identification**:

- **Conflicts register** at firm level;
- **Trust-level conflicts log** for each individual trust;
- **Trigger-event identification** — new transactions,
  appointments, beneficiary changes that surface new
  conflicts;
- **Periodic review** of the conflicts inventory.

A conflict the firm does not identify cannot be managed.
**Inadequate identification** is the most common conflict-
related failure.

### Manage

Where managing is possible, the Code expects documented
mitigation:

- **Information barriers (Chinese walls)** between teams
  serving conflicted clients;
- **Recusal** of specific staff from specific decisions;
- **Independent decision-making** — outside committees,
  Royal Court applications for momentous decisions, third-
  party advice;
- **Separate teams** for the conflicted parties;
- **Conflict-clearance protocols** before action.

### Disclose

Where the conflict cannot be eliminated:

- **Disclose to the affected client(s)** in clear,
  comprehensible terms;
- **Document client acknowledgement** of the disclosure;
- **Inform of mitigations** the TCB will apply;
- **Annual conflict-disclosure** in many cases.

The Code's expectation: disclosure is **specific to the
conflict**, not generic boilerplate. A standard "we may
have conflicts" notice does not satisfy.

### Decline / resign

Where the conflict is **unmanageable**:

- **Decline** to accept the relationship (at onboarding);
- **Resign** from one or more roles (where the conflict
  emerges later);
- **Cease** providing the conflicted service while retaining
  others;
- **Royal Court direction** under
  [Article 51 of the Trusts Law](../trusts/article-51-directions.md)
  where the conflict is contested.

The Code expects firms to genuinely **walk away from
business** they cannot manage. Continuing despite
unmanageable conflict is enforcement-actionable.

## Particular conflict scenarios

### Same TCB serving multiple trusts holding the same asset

A common pattern — the TCB administers two family trusts
that both hold shares in the family operating company.
Decisions about the company affect both trusts. Management:

- **Information sharing** between the two trust files
  carefully controlled;
- **Separate trustee committees** for each trust;
- **Joint decisions** addressed through documented
  procedures with both trusts represented;
- **Royal Court applications** for momentous decisions
  where the trusts' interests are materially divergent.

### TCB as trustee + TCB's group as fund manager

The TCB administers a trust that subscribes to a fund the
TCB's group manages. Management:

- **Disclose** the relationship at the trust's
  subscription;
- **Independent investment-committee oversight** of the
  trust's investment-decision rationale;
- **Fee transparency** — the fund management fee is on
  top of any TCB fee, disclosed in full;
- **Documented suitability** of the fund for the trust
  beyond the in-house relationship.

### Family member of TCB staff as client

A TCB staff member's family member is a client of the TCB.
Management:

- **Disclosure** of the relationship to senior management
  on appointment of either the staff member or the client;
- **Recusal** of the staff member from the client's file
  decisions;
- **Independent handling** of the client relationship.

### PEP family-member relationships

Where the TCB serves multiple members of a PEP family with
potentially-competing interests:

- **Separate teams** for distinct family branches;
- **PEP-EDD compliance** simultaneously across all
  branches;
- **Family-conflict awareness** — disputes among PEP family
  members can have political and AML dimensions.

## Common conflict-management failure modes

JFSC enforcement themes:

- **Conflicts identification light** — register exists but
  is not comprehensive;
- **Disclosure boilerplate** — disclosure form generic
  rather than calibrated to the specific conflict;
- **Continued operation of unmanageable conflicts** —
  firm continues despite the conflict being structurally
  unmanageable;
- **Inadequate information barriers** — Chinese walls
  exist in policy but porous in practice;
- **In-house product recommendations** without
  acknowledgement of the conflict;
- **Staff personal-dealing breaches** without firm
  oversight;
- **Hospitality / gifts** managed without robust
  recording.
