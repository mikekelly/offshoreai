# Lasting Powers of Attorney in Jersey

## What an LPA is

A **Lasting Power of Attorney** is a legal instrument by
which a **donor** (the person making the LPA) gives one
or more **attorneys** authority to make decisions on
their behalf if they lose capacity.

LPAs were introduced in Jersey by the **Capacity and
Self-Determination (Jersey) Law 2016**. They are
significantly different from ordinary **Powers of
Attorney under the Powers of Attorney (Jersey) Law 1995**:

| Feature | Ordinary PoA 1995 | LPA under CSDJL 2016 |
|---|---|---|
| Continues if donor loses capacity | No (revoked automatically) | Yes (the whole point) |
| Scope | Property / finance | Health & welfare OR property & finance |
| Registration | Optional | Mandatory before use |
| Authority over major decisions | Narrow | Broad (subject to limits) |
| Court supervision | None unless misused | Royal Court Protection Division can supervise |

For incapacity planning, the **LPA is the right
instrument** — not an ordinary PoA, which falls away if
the donor loses capacity (exactly when it's needed most).

## Two types of LPA

### Health and Welfare LPA

Covers decisions about:

- **Medical treatment** — consent or refusal of specific
  treatments;
- **Life-sustaining treatment** — including refusal (only
  if the LPA explicitly authorises this);
- **Where the donor lives** — care home, family member's
  home, own home with support;
- **Care arrangements** — daily routine, contact with
  others, social activities;
- **Specific** consent issues.

Effective **only when the donor lacks capacity** for the
specific decision.

**Cannot authorise**:

- **Euthanasia** or assisted suicide (illegal in Jersey);
- **Sterilisation** (specific separate procedure);
- **Marriage / civil partnership** decisions;
- **Voting**;
- **Specific** other personal decisions.

### Property and Finance LPA

Covers decisions about:

- **Bank accounts** — operating, opening, closing;
- **Income** — receiving pensions, benefits, dividends;
- **Bill payment**;
- **Investments** — managing, buying, selling;
- **Property** — including sale of the donor's home
  (subject to specific safeguards);
- **Tax affairs**;
- **Maintenance** of the donor and dependants;
- **Charitable gifts** within reasonable bounds;
- **Personal gifts** within narrow bounds.

Can be effective:

- **Immediately on registration** — donor and attorney
  can both act, useful for those with mild cognitive
  impairment; or
- **Only on incapacity** — typical for forward-planning
  cases.

## Who can be an attorney

The donor's chosen attorneys must:

- Be **aged 18 or over**;
- Have **mental capacity** themselves at the time;
- Not be **bankrupt** (for property and finance LPA);
- **Consent** to the appointment;
- **Sign** acknowledging their duties.

Common choices:

- **Spouse / civil partner** — typical first choice;
- **Adult children** — common;
- **Trusted friend**;
- **Professional** — solicitor or specialist services;
- **Mixed** — family plus professional for property /
  finance.

## Multiple attorneys

The donor can appoint multiple attorneys to act:

- **Jointly** — all decisions require unanimity;
- **Jointly and severally** — each can act independently;
- **Joint for some matters, several for others** —
  specific drafting.

Replacement attorneys can also be named for if a primary
attorney can no longer act.

## Form and execution

### The form

Prescribed forms are published by the Royal Court
Protection Division. Key elements:

- Donor's identification and capacity declaration;
- Type of LPA (health & welfare / property & finance);
- Specific scope and any restrictions;
- Attorney(s) identification and consent;
- Witnesses;
- Notice to specified persons (relatives, where required).

### Signing

The LPA must be signed:

- By the **donor**, with witness;
- By each **attorney**, with witness;
- Specific **certificate** of capacity by an appropriate
  person (in many cases a doctor or independent
  professional);
- All signatures in a specified sequence.

### Registration

After signing, the LPA must be **registered** at the
**Royal Court Protection Division**:

- **Application** with the LPA and supporting documents;
- **Notice** to family / specified persons (objection
  period typically 4 weeks);
- **Registration fee**;
- **Registration date** — LPA becomes operative.

An LPA **cannot be used until registered**. Delay in
registration after capacity loss creates real problems —
which is why making and registering the LPA **while still
capacitous** is essential.

## Using an LPA

### Health and Welfare LPA

- Effective only on **capacity loss** for the specific
  decision;
- Attorney must act in **best interests**;
- Attorney must consult the donor's wishes / values where
  ascertainable;
- Medical professionals will rely on the LPA for consent.

### Property and Finance LPA

- May be effective immediately or on capacity loss
  depending on terms;
- Attorney must keep proper accounts;
- Attorney cannot benefit personally beyond bounds;
- Investment / property decisions must reflect best
  interests.

## Limits and safeguards

The attorney must:

- **Act in the donor's best interests**;
- **Consult the donor** on decisions where capacity
  allows;
- **Respect** the donor's past wishes and values;
- **Avoid conflicts of interest**;
- **Keep proper records** (especially for property /
  finance);
- **Not exceed the LPA's scope**.

Breaches can result in:

- **Court intervention**;
- **Revocation** of the LPA;
- **Damages claims** (civil);
- **Criminal proceedings** for fraud / theft in serious
  cases.

## Revocation

The donor can revoke an LPA at any time **while they have
capacity**:

- **Written revocation** signed and witnessed;
- **Notice** to attorneys and the Court;
- **Updated registration** record.

After capacity loss, only the Royal Court can effectively
revoke (typically on safeguarding grounds).

## What if there's no LPA?

If a person loses capacity without an LPA, family or
others must apply for a **court-appointed delegate** —
see [`delegates.md`](./delegates.md). This is:

- **Slower** than using an LPA;
- **More expensive** (court fees, often legal fees);
- **More restrictive** (court orders specific authorities);
- **More supervised** (annual accounts, etc.).

This is why making an LPA while capacitous is strongly
recommended.

## Cross-border issues

LPAs made in other jurisdictions:

- **English LPAs** may need re-registration / mirror in
  Jersey to operate over Jersey assets;
- **Other foreign powers** subject to recognition rules;
- **Specific advice** essential for cross-border families.

## Cost

Indicative costs (varies):

- **DIY LPA**: forms free; certificate-of-capacity fees;
  registration fee at Court;
- **Solicitor-drafted**: several hundred to a few thousand
  pounds per LPA;
- **Total** for both types via solicitor: typically £1,000-
  £3,000.

Inexpensive relative to the cost of a contested delegate
application later.

## Practical recommendations

- **Make both types** of LPA — health & welfare and
  property & finance;
- **While you have capacity** — even if seems premature;
- **Choose attorneys carefully** — they will have
  substantial power;
- **Discuss your wishes** with chosen attorneys;
- **Consider** life-sustaining-treatment authorisation
  carefully;
- **Register** promptly after signing;
- **Review** periodically (every few years);
- **Update** if family circumstances change.
