# Mental Health (Jersey) Law 2016 — Overview

## What the law does

The **Mental Health (Jersey) Law 2016** (MHJL 2016)
regulates compulsory admission and treatment of persons
with mental disorder. It:

- Defines **mental disorder** for the purposes of
  compulsory measures;
- Provides for **emergency admission** (72 hours);
- Provides for **assessment** detention (up to 28 days);
- Provides for **treatment** detention (up to 6 months,
  extendable);
- Establishes **community guardianship** as an alternative
  to detention;
- Creates the **Mental Health Review Tribunal** as the
  independent appeal forum;
- Defines the role of the **Nearest Relative / Nearest
  Person**;
- Regulates **treatment with and without consent**;
- Provides **safeguards** for patients.

It replaced the earlier outdated regime and brought
Jersey into broad alignment with modern mental-health
human-rights standards.

## Key concepts

### Mental disorder

The MHJL 2016 defines mental disorder as **any disorder
or disability of the mind**. The definition is intended
to be broad and inclusive but specifically excludes:

- **Substance dependence alone** (drugs / alcohol);
- **Sexual behaviour alone**;
- **Specified** narrow categories.

A diagnosed condition is required, supported by clinical
evidence.

### Approved practitioners

Decisions under the MHJL 2016 require involvement of
**approved practitioners**:

- **Approved Medical Practitioner (AMP)** — typically a
  psychiatrist with specific approval;
- **Approved Mental Health Practitioner (AMHP)** — a
  professional with statutory approval to assess and
  apply for admission;
- **Responsible Clinician** — the practitioner in charge
  of a detained patient's care.

These approvals are formal — not every doctor / social
worker can act under the MHJL 2016.

### Nearest Relative / Nearest Person

A statutory family role:

- Established by a defined hierarchy (spouse, parent,
  child, sibling, etc.);
- Has **statutory powers** including:
  - Making applications for assessment / treatment;
  - Discharging the patient in some cases;
  - Receiving information about the patient's care;
  - **Applying** to the Tribunal on the patient's behalf.

The role recognises family involvement while balancing
patient autonomy.

## The compulsory-admission framework

### Article 17 — Emergency admission (72 hours)

For situations of immediate risk:

- **Authorised by an approved practitioner**;
- **Where allowing liberty would endanger** the patient's
  safety or that of others;
- **Up to 72 hours**;
- **Cannot be extended** as such — converts to Article 21
  or 22 detention if longer needed.

### Article 21 — Assessment detention (28 days)

For diagnostic assessment:

- **Mental disorder** of a nature or degree warranting
  detention;
- **Application** by AMHP or Nearest Relative;
- **Supported by** two medical recommendations (one from
  an AMP);
- **Up to 28 days**;
- **Appeal** available within first 14 days to the
  Tribunal;
- **Cannot be extended** beyond 28 days — converts to
  Article 22 if treatment detention is required.

### Article 22 — Treatment detention (6 months, extendable)

For ongoing treatment:

- **Mental disorder** of a nature or degree warranting
  treatment;
- **Necessary** for the patient's health / safety or for
  others;
- **Application** by AMHP or Nearest Relative;
- **Supported by** two medical recommendations;
- **Up to 6 months**;
- **Renewable** for further periods (6 months, then 12
  months);
- **Appeal** to the Tribunal at any time, subject to
  limit of one application every 6 months.

### Guardianship

A community-based alternative:

- **Approved guardian** appointed;
- **Patient required** to reside at a specified place,
  attend treatment, give access to professionals;
- **Less restrictive** than hospital detention;
- **Renewable**.

## Treatment with and without consent

For detained patients:

- **Some treatments** can be given without consent (with
  procedural safeguards);
- **Other treatments** require consent or independent
  authorisation;
- **ECT, sterilisation, specified medications** — subject
  to enhanced safeguards;
- **Code of Practice** sets out detailed rules.

For voluntary patients, ordinary consent rules apply (see
the Capacity and Self-Determination Law 2016).

## Patient safeguards

- **Independent Mental Health Advocate (IMHA)** —
  available on request;
- **Legal representation** for Tribunal hearings;
- **Code of Practice** binding on practitioners;
- **Tribunal review** as of right;
- **ECHR Article 5** scrutiny (incorporated into Jersey
  law);
- **Information** about rights to be provided;
- **Hospital managers** review periodically.

## The Mental Health Review Tribunal

The independent appeal forum — see
[`mental-health-tribunal.md`](./mental-health-tribunal.md).

Composed of:

- **Legally qualified chair**;
- **Medical member**;
- **Lay member**.

Hearings are formal but accessible. Patients are
typically legally represented (publicly funded). The
Tribunal can:

- **Discharge** the patient;
- **Defer discharge** with conditions;
- **Recommend** Code-of-Practice changes;
- **Direct** specific reviews.

## Forensic patients

For patients in the criminal justice context (e.g.
remand mental-health assessment, hospital orders post-
conviction):

- **Royal Court** orders are the principal route;
- **Specific articles** of the MHJL apply;
- **Detention** at appropriate facilities (Jersey lacks
  a high-secure facility, so serious cases are
  transferred to UK facilities under arrangement).

## Code of Practice

The Minister has issued a statutory Code of Practice:

- **Binding** on practitioners;
- **Detailed guidance** on application of the law;
- **Updated** periodically;
- **Reference** in any Tribunal or court review.

## Recent developments

- The 2016 Law has now been in force for several years;
- **Code of Practice** revisions have refined operational
  practice;
- **Resource** and **facility** issues are subjects of
  ongoing scrutiny;
- **Mental-health tribunal** caseload has grown as
  awareness has increased.
