# Capacity and Self-Determination (Jersey) Law 2016 — Overview

## What the law does

The **Capacity and Self-Determination (Jersey) Law 2016**
(CSDJL 2016) is Jersey's modern mental-capacity statute,
broadly modelled on the English Mental Capacity Act 2005.
It:

- Defines a **statutory test of capacity**;
- Provides a **best-interests framework** for decisions
  made on behalf of those who lack capacity;
- Creates **Lasting Powers of Attorney (LPAs)** — health
  & welfare LPA and property & finance LPA;
- Authorises **advance decisions to refuse treatment**;
- Establishes **court-appointed delegates** for those who
  lack capacity and have no LPA;
- Establishes the **Royal Court Protection Division** as
  the principal court forum;
- Sets out **safeguards** for vulnerable persons.

It is the first comprehensive mental-capacity legislation
Jersey has had, replacing a scattered common-law /
old-statute patchwork.

## Key concepts

### The capacity test

A person **lacks capacity** in relation to a specific
matter if at the relevant time they are **unable to make
a decision for themselves** because of an **impairment of,
or disturbance in the functioning of, the mind or brain**
(whether permanent or temporary).

The functional test of inability:

- **Understand** the information relevant to the decision;
- **Retain** that information;
- **Use or weigh** the information; or
- **Communicate** their decision.

Capacity is **decision-specific** and **time-specific** — a
person may have capacity for one decision but not another,
or have capacity today but not tomorrow.

### Five statutory principles

The CSDJL sets out five guiding principles:

1. **Presumption of capacity** — every adult is presumed
   to have capacity unless established otherwise;
2. **Practicable steps** to support decision-making must
   be taken before concluding someone lacks capacity;
3. **Unwise decisions** are not in themselves evidence of
   incapacity;
4. **Best interests** — any act done for someone lacking
   capacity must be in their best interests;
5. **Least restrictive option** — the option chosen should
   be the least restrictive of the person's rights and
   freedoms.

These principles are **binding** and must inform every
decision under the Law.

### Best interests

Where a person lacks capacity for a decision, the
decision-maker must act in their **best interests**.
This involves considering:

- The person's **past and present wishes and feelings**;
- The person's **beliefs and values**;
- Other relevant factors the person would consider;
- The views of **family members, carers, attorneys, and
  delegates**;
- The **least restrictive** option.

A decision in best interests is **not** simply the
"objectively best" decision — it weighs the person's own
perspective.

## Lasting Powers of Attorney (LPAs)

The CSDJL 2016 creates two types of LPA, both available
to anyone aged 18+ with current capacity:

### Health and Welfare LPA

Covers decisions about:

- **Medical treatment** — consent / refusal;
- **Life-sustaining treatment** — including refusal (if
  explicitly authorised);
- **Care arrangements** — including where the donor
  lives;
- **Daily routine** matters;
- **Cannot** authorise euthanasia (illegal in Jersey).

Effective only when the donor **lacks capacity** for the
specific decision.

### Property and Finance LPA

Covers decisions about:

- **Paying debts**;
- **Managing investments**;
- **Receiving income**;
- **Maintenance** of the donor and dependants;
- **Charitable gifts** within reasonable bounds;
- **Property** decisions including sale of the home
  (subject to specific safeguards).

Can be effective:

- **Immediately** on registration (donor retains capacity
  to act in parallel); or
- **Only on incapacity** (depending on terms).

### Registration

LPAs must be:

- **Made in prescribed form**;
- **Signed** by the donor with witnesses;
- **Registered** at the Royal Court before they can be
  used;
- **Updated** if circumstances change materially.

See [`lasting-powers-of-attorney.md`](lasting-powers-of-attorney.md).

## Advance decisions to refuse treatment

A capacitous adult can make an advance decision:

- **Refusing** specified medical treatment;
- **In specified circumstances**;
- **Including** life-sustaining treatment (with specific
  formalities);
- **Effective** only if the donor lacks capacity at the
  time and the decision is applicable to the
  circumstances.

Must be:

- **Made in writing**;
- **Signed and witnessed** for life-sustaining-treatment
  refusals;
- **Specific** about the treatment and circumstances;
- **Not** overridden by subsequent decisions (subject to
  best-interests considerations).

See [`advance-decisions.md`](advance-decisions.md).

## Delegates (court-appointed)

For a person who **lacks capacity** and has **no LPA**,
the Royal Court can appoint a **delegate** to make
decisions on their behalf:

- **Property and finance** delegate;
- **Health and welfare** delegate (rarer; specific
  circumstances);
- **Family member, friend, or professional**;
- **Subject to ongoing supervision** by the court;
- **Annual accounts** for property/finance delegates;
- **Authority limited** to what the order specifies.

Delegate applications are made to the Royal Court
Protection Division and require:

- **Capacity assessment**;
- **Best-interests evidence**;
- **Specific proposed orders**.

See [`delegates.md`](delegates.md).

## The Royal Court Protection Division

The CSDJL 2016 establishes a specific court division for
capacity matters:

- **Hears** delegate applications, LPA disputes, advance-
  decision disputes;
- **Resolves** disputes about capacity / best interests;
- **Supervises** delegates;
- **Makes** declaratory orders.

See [`protection-division.md`](protection-division.md).

## Code of Practice

A statutory Code of Practice supplements the law:

- **Binding** on professionals making decisions;
- **Detailed guidance** on practical application;
- **Reference** in any court or tribunal case;
- **Updated** as practice develops.

## Interaction with Mental Health Law

The CSDJL 2016 and MHJL 2016 **interact**:

- **Both** can apply to the same person at different
  times;
- **MHJL** governs **compulsory mental-health treatment**;
- **CSDJL** governs **other** decisions for those who
  lack capacity;
- **Best-interests** approach under CSDJL doesn't override
  MHJL compulsory powers;
- **Specific** interface rules in the Codes.

## Vulnerable adult protection

Beyond the CSDJL framework:

- **Multi-Agency Adult Safeguarding** procedures
  (separate non-statutory framework);
- **Specific** protections under the Sexual Offences Law
  2018 and other criminal statutes;
- **Civil** remedies for exploitation, undue influence.

## Practical recommendations

For adults planning ahead:

- **Make an LPA** while you have capacity — both types if
  appropriate;
- **Choose attorneys carefully** — they will have
  substantial power;
- **Discuss** your wishes with your attorneys and family;
- **Update** if circumstances change;
- **Review** documentation periodically.

For families of someone losing capacity:

- **Check for existing LPA** as a first step;
- **Consider** delegate application if no LPA;
- **Engage** with capacity assessment;
- **Best-interests** framework guides decisions;
- **Document** the decision-making process.

For professionals:

- **Code of Practice** is mandatory reading;
- **Capacity assessment** for each specific decision;
- **Best-interests** documentation in every case;
- **Engage** with attorneys / delegates if appointed;
- **Court orders** for contested matters.
