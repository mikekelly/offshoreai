# Planning Appeals in Jersey

## Two routes

### Planning appeal under the PBL 2002

The principal route — a formal appeal heard under the
Planning and Building (Jersey) Law 2002. Available to:

- **Applicant** appealing refusal or onerous conditions;
- **Applicant** appealing failure to determine within
  target time;
- **Third party** in narrow circumstances;
- **Recipient** of an enforcement notice.

### Judicial review in the Royal Court

A separate route to challenge a decision on **public-law
grounds**:

- **Illegality** — decision was outside the power;
- **Irrationality** — no reasonable authority could
  reach that decision;
- **Procedural unfairness** — process was flawed;
- **Insufficient reasons** in some cases.

Royal Court judicial review is **not** a re-hearing on
the merits — it tests the legality of the decision.

## Time limits

**Strict** time limits apply:

- **Planning appeal** — 28 days from decision (or
  expiry of target if non-determination appeal);
- **Judicial review** — typically 3 months but often
  shorter in planning context;
- **Missing the deadline** is fatal — there is no
  general extension.

## Grounds for planning appeal

Common appeal grounds:

- The decision **was wrong on planning policy** —
  misapplication of the Island Plan;
- **Conditions are unreasonable** — onerous, vague, or
  unrelated to the application;
- **Procedural error** in the decision process;
- **New material** the decision-maker didn't have;
- **Insufficient weight** given to material
  considerations;
- **Excessive weight** given to immaterial
  considerations.

## The appeal process

A standard planning appeal:

1. **Notice of appeal** filed within time limit;
2. **Statement of case** — appellant's grounds;
3. **Department's response** — defence of the decision;
4. **Third-party representations** — opportunity for
   others to comment;
5. **Hearing** — formal hearing (oral evidence, expert
   evidence in complex cases);
6. **Decision** — appeal upheld, dismissed, or
   modified;
7. **Costs** in some cases.

Appeal decisions are made by an **independent appeal
authority** under the PBL 2002.

## What an appeal can achieve

- **Reversal of refusal** — application granted on
  appeal;
- **Modification of conditions** — onerous conditions
  removed or amended;
- **Direction** to redetermine (procedural fairness
  cases);
- **Reduction** of an enforcement notice's requirements.

## Limitations

- **Cannot** introduce a wholly new application — the
  appeal is on the original;
- **Cannot** typically extend statutory limits;
- **Cannot** override Island Plan in fundamental ways
  without exceptional grounds.

## Costs

- **Appeal fees** modest;
- **Legal / planning consultancy** fees substantial for
  contested cases;
- **Witness** fees;
- **Costs orders** possible but unusual;
- **Cost recovery** rarely matches outlay even in
  successful appeals.

## Third-party appeals

A neighbour or objector unhappy with an **approval** has
limited rights:

- **Specific statutory grounds** apply;
- **Locus standi** required — affected interest;
- **Time-limited** to the 28-day window;
- **Cost risk** if appeal fails.

Many objections that would seem appropriate are not
appealable — the third-party route is narrower than the
applicant's right of appeal.

## Strategy

For an applicant facing refusal:

- **Consider amendment** — a revised application may be
  better than appeal;
- **Take specialist advice** — planning law specialists
  add real value;
- **Frame on policy** — Island Plan / material
  considerations are the language of appeals;
- **Address objections** — comments raised at first
  instance should be tackled;
- **Cost-benefit** — appeal costs vs application costs
  for a revised scheme.

For a third party facing approval:

- **Cite policy specifically** — generic objections
  rarely succeed;
- **Documentary evidence** of impact;
- **Specialist advice** essential — third-party appeals
  are narrow;
- **Realistic expectations** — most third-party appeals
  fail.

## Judicial review specifically

For a Royal Court challenge:

- **Pre-action protocol** — typically required;
- **Permission stage** — must be granted leave;
- **Substantive hearing** if leave granted;
- **Remedies** typically a quashing order requiring
  redetermination, not a substituted decision;
- **Costs risk** is substantial.
