# Permitted Development under the GD Order 2011

## What this is

The **Planning and Building (General Development) (Jersey)
Order 2011** ("GD Order 2011") grants **automatic planning
permission** for specified categories of minor works. These
are the "permitted development" exemptions.

You do **not** need to apply for planning permission for
works that fall fully within a permitted-development class.
You **do** still need to comply with:

- **Building Bye-Laws 2007** (technical building approval);
- **Listed-building consent** if the property is listed;
- **Conservation-area** restrictions if applicable;
- **Restrictive covenants** on the title (a private-law
  matter — see [`../property/restrictive-covenants.md`](../property/restrictive-covenants.md)).

## Common permitted development categories

The GD Order 2011 covers a range of minor works. Indicative
list (always check the current Order for exact wording and
conditions):

### Around the home

- **Boundary enclosures** (walls, fences, gates) — within
  prescribed height limits (typically 2m, lower facing a
  highway);
- **Painting** of an existing dwelling — unless in a
  conservation area;
- **Replacement windows and doors** — like-for-like in
  most cases; specific rules for listed and conservation;
- **Roof lights** — subject to size and projection;
- **Satellite dishes** — typically one allowed within
  size limits, subject to position;
- **Solar panels** — generally permitted within size
  and position limits;
- **Heat pumps** — recently extended exemption, subject
  to noise and position.

### Outbuildings

- **Sheds, greenhouses, summerhouses** — subject to:
  - Height limits (typically 2.5m flat / 4m pitched);
  - Floor area limits;
  - Distance from boundaries;
  - Distance from the dwelling;
  - Not for residential use (a "garden room" for living
    typically needs permission).

### Extensions

- **Single-storey rear extensions** — subject to size,
  depth, and height limits;
- **Side extensions** — narrow side extensions may be
  permitted;
- **Conservatories** — within specific size and design
  limits;
- **Porches** — small porches typically permitted.

**Note**: extensions above the permitted-development
thresholds need a planning application. The most common
mistake is assuming a "modest" extension is automatically
permitted when in fact the area / height / position
exceeds the GD Order limits.

### Internal works

- **Internal alterations** to an unlisted building —
  generally do not need planning permission at all (but
  may need building approval under Bye-Laws);
- **Loft conversions** — subject to specific conditions
  on dormers, height, design.

### Highways and access

- **New vehicle access** — generally needs permission
  (highways implications);
- **Hardstanding** for parking — may be permitted within
  size limits.

### Agricultural land

- Special rules for agricultural buildings, slurry stores,
  and similar — generally narrower than equivalent
  residential exemptions.

## Conditions

Every permitted-development class is subject to **specific
conditions**. Common conditions:

- **Height limits**;
- **Distance from boundary**;
- **Distance from highway**;
- **Maximum coverage** of curtilage;
- **No projection** in front of the main building line;
- **Not in conservation areas**;
- **Not on listed buildings**;
- **Not affecting protected trees**.

Exceeding **any** condition takes the work outside
permitted development — and back into the standard
application route.

## Restrictions in special areas

Permitted-development rights are typically **restricted**:

- **In conservation areas** — many exemptions don't apply
  or are narrower;
- **On listed buildings** — listed-building consent is
  needed even for works that would otherwise be permitted;
- **In the Coastal National Park / Green Zone** —
  restricted to protect landscape;
- **Under Article 31 directions** — the Minister can
  remove permitted-development rights in specific
  locations.

## Verification — Certificate of Lawful Development

If you're unsure whether your proposal is permitted, you
can apply for a **Certificate of Lawful Development**:

- **Confirms** that the proposed (or completed) works
  fall within permitted development;
- **Lower fee** than a full planning application;
- **Provides certainty** for the future — useful on
  sale, refinancing, or in disputes;
- **Protects against** future enforcement.

This is strongly recommended if there's any ambiguity.

## Risks of getting it wrong

If you treat works as permitted development when they're
not:

- **Enforcement** can require you to reverse the works at
  your cost;
- **Retrospective application** may not succeed —
  particularly if the design wouldn't have been approved
  on a fresh application;
- **Sale** of the property is affected — buyers'
  solicitors typically require evidence of
  planning-permission compliance for all works in the
  preceding period;
- **Insurance** may be affected;
- **Criminal liability** for unauthorised development
  in serious cases.

## Practical recommendations

- **Check the GD Order 2011** for the specific category
  and conditions;
- **Read the conditions** carefully — height, distance,
  coverage limits matter;
- **Consider a Certificate of Lawful Development** for
  any non-trivial work;
- **Engage** the planning department for pre-application
  advice — free drop-in service available;
- **Don't assume** "small" or "minor" = permitted;
- **Conservation area or listed?** — most exemptions
  don't apply; always check.
