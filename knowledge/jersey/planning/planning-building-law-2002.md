# Planning and Building (Jersey) Law 2002 — Overview

## What the law does

The **Planning and Building (Jersey) Law 2002** (PBL 2002)
is the principal Jersey statute regulating use and
development of land. It:

- Defines **development** and which acts require permission;
- Establishes the **planning permission** regime;
- Creates the **Minister for the Environment's** decision-
  making role and delegates routine decisions to the
  Department;
- Establishes the **Planning Committee** for politically-
  significant applications;
- Provides for the **Island Plan** as the policy framework;
- Sets up **listed buildings** and **conservation areas**
  regimes;
- Creates **enforcement** powers — enforcement notices,
  stop notices, prosecutions;
- Establishes **appeals** procedures;
- Creates **building standards** (although technical
  building bye-laws sit in a separate instrument).

## Related instruments

| Instrument | Function |
|---|---|
| **Planning and Building (General Development) (Jersey) Order 2011** | Specifies "permitted development" — categories of works granted permission automatically (small extensions, sheds, satellite dishes, etc.) |
| **Building Bye-Laws (Jersey) 2007** | Technical building standards (structure, insulation, fire, drainage) — separate from planning permission |
| **The Island Plan** | Policy framework; current Island Plan is the **2022 Island Plan**, with periodic reviews and updates |
| **Various sub-orders** | Listed buildings, advertisements, conservation areas, environmental impact assessment |

## Two parallel regimes

A development typically needs **two separate consents**:

1. **Planning permission** — under the PBL 2002 and the
   policies of the Island Plan;
2. **Building approval** — under the Building Bye-Laws
   2007, against the technical standards.

Either can be refused independently. Getting planning
permission does not mean you have building approval, and
vice versa. Both must be obtained before work starts.

## Key concepts

### Development

"Development" is defined broadly in the PBL 2002:

> the carrying out of building, engineering, mining or
> other operations in, on, over or under land, **and**
> the making of any material change in the use of any
> building or other land.

It includes:

- **New build**;
- **Extensions** (above certain thresholds);
- **Demolition**;
- **Changes of use** (commercial to residential, residential
  to short-let, etc.);
- **New access** (driveways, etc.);
- **Removing hedges or trees** in some contexts;
- **Advertisements**.

See [`development-defined.md`](development-defined.md).

### Permitted development

Some categories of development are **automatically
permitted** under the General Development Order 2011 —
the "permitted development" exemptions. These are minor
works that don't need a planning application:

- Boundary enclosures (within height limits);
- Painting (subject to conservation-area exceptions);
- Vents and flues;
- Some window and door replacements;
- Some small extensions and outbuildings;
- Roof conversions (subject to specific limits).

The exemptions have **conditions** — even a permitted
development can require permission if a condition is
exceeded (e.g. height, distance from boundary).

See [`permitted-development.md`](permitted-development.md).

### Material change of use

Some changes of use require permission even if no
building work is involved:

- Residential to short-term let (e.g. AirBnB);
- Commercial to residential or vice versa;
- Agricultural to residential;
- Splitting one dwelling into two (or merging).

The "material" threshold means the change must be
substantive — a marginal change of use within the same
broad category typically does not need permission.

### Listed buildings

Buildings of special architectural or historic interest
are **listed** under the PBL 2002:

- **Grade 1** — buildings of exceptional importance;
- **Grade 2** — buildings of special interest;
- Additional categories for ancillary structures.

Listed buildings require **listed building consent** for
alterations affecting the special character, in addition
to ordinary planning permission.

See [`listed-buildings.md`](listed-buildings.md).

### Conservation areas

Areas of special architectural or historic interest may
be designated as **conservation areas**:

- Additional planning controls apply;
- Permitted-development rights may be **restricted**;
- Trees in conservation areas have special protection;
- Demolition typically requires consent.

See [`conservation-areas.md`](conservation-areas.md).

## Decision-making hierarchy

| Decision-maker | What they decide |
|---|---|
| **Department for the Environment** (planning officers) | Routine applications under delegated authority |
| **Planning Committee** (panel of States members) | Politically-significant applications, applications that have generated public interest, and applications where officers recommend refusal but the applicant has appealed to Committee |
| **Minister for the Environment** | Departures from the Island Plan; called-in applications |

The **Independent Architecture Commission** may be
consulted on applications with significant design or
architectural impact, providing non-binding advice.

## Application process

Typical sequence (covered in detail in
[`application-process.md`](application-process.md)):

1. **Pre-application advice** — free drop-in or paid pre-app;
2. **Application submitted** with fee, drawings, supporting
   statements;
3. **Validation** by the Department;
4. **Public notice** — 21 days on-site sign + Jersey
   Gazette listing;
5. **Consultation** — 21 days for objections from
   neighbours, statutory consultees, parishes;
6. **Officer assessment** — against Island Plan, policies,
   site visit, design considerations;
7. **Decision** — 8 weeks (minor) or 13 weeks (major)
   target;
8. **Decision notice** issued;
9. **28-day appeal window** for third-party or applicant
   appeals.

## Enforcement

The PBL 2002 gives the Minister enforcement powers:

- **Enforcement notice** — requiring specified works to
  cease, or unauthorised works to be reversed;
- **Stop notice** — immediate cessation pending
  enforcement;
- **Prosecution** — for failure to comply, or for
  unauthorised development carried out;
- **Direct action** — Department can carry out
  remedial works and recover costs.

**Unauthorised development is a criminal offence** —
unlimited fines and possible imprisonment for the most
serious cases. **Retrospective applications** are possible
but not guaranteed to succeed; building first and asking
permission later is risky.

See [`enforcement.md`](enforcement.md).

## Appeals

From a Department / Committee / Minister decision:

- **Planning Appeals** — formal appeal hearing process
  under the PBL 2002;
- **Royal Court** — judicial review on points of law;
- **Strict time limits** apply.

See [`appeals.md`](appeals.md).

## Recent reforms

The Island Plan has been refreshed periodically — the
**2022 Island Plan** is the current framework as of May
2026. Specific policies are updated by ministerial
direction.
