# CL 2014 Article 6 — Public Benefit Test

Article 6 establishes the **public benefit test** — the
**second pillar** of charitable status alongside charitable
purposes (Article 5).

## The provision

To be charitable, a purpose must provide **public benefit**:

- There must be an **identifiable benefit**;
- The benefit must be **to the public** or **a sufficient
  section** of the public;
- The benefit must **outweigh** any detriments;
- The benefit must be **of the right kind** for the
  particular purpose.

## The two elements

### Identifiable benefit

The charity must show **what** benefit it provides:

- **Tangible** — direct services to beneficiaries;
- **Intangible** — community awareness, capacity building,
  cultural enrichment;
- **Indirect** — research benefiting future generations,
  environmental conservation benefiting all;
- **Documented** through reports, activity descriptions,
  outcomes measurement.

The benefit must be **real**, not merely notional or
hypothetical.

### Public (or sufficient section)

The benefit must be **to the public** or **a section** that
is **public-spirited**:

- **General public** — broadest reach (community amenity);
- **Section of public** — specific groups (poor, ill,
  disabled, victims of disaster);
- **Not** a closed group of **named individuals**;
- **Not** primarily for the benefit of **private founders**
  or a small circle.

### Detriment

The benefit must **outweigh** any detriments:

- **Some activities** have downsides (e.g., environmental
  cost of buildings);
- **Charity** activities **specifically harmful** to others
  may fail (e.g., overly restrictive religious groups
  causing harm);
- **Balance** of benefits vs detriments.

## The "rich person's club" problem

A purpose that **only benefits the wealthy** is unlikely to
satisfy public benefit:

- **Fee-paying schools** — public benefit depends on bursaries,
  community access, etc. (UK case law extensive);
- **Private clubs** — generally not charitable;
- **Specific** Jersey nuances.

## The "founder benefit" problem

A purpose that **principally benefits the founder / family** is
not charitable:

- A **family foundation** with **only** family beneficiaries
  is **not** a charity;
- A foundation with **family + significant external
  beneficiaries** may be charitable;
- The **restricted section** of the register accommodates
  some private-charity-like vehicles;
- **Distinction** from family-discretionary trusts.

## Demonstrating public benefit

In practice:

- **Initial application** — describes proposed activities and
  public benefit;
- **Constitutional documents** — drafted to ensure public-
  benefit operation;
- **Annual reporting** — demonstrates actual public benefit
  delivered;
- **Outcomes measurement** — increasingly expected by
  Commissioner;
- **Periodic review** by Commissioner.

## Specific tests by purpose

### Education

- Public benefit easy for **schools serving the public**;
- More **complex** for **fee-paying** with bursaries;
- **Research** must have wider benefit beyond the
  researchers.

### Religion

- Public benefit requires more than **internal congregation**
  benefit;
- **Religious activities** must be **accessible** to a
  reasonable section of the public;
- **Specific** considerations for minority religions.

### Sport

- **Amateur** sport (not professional);
- **Open** to the public (or qualifying section);
- **Specific** considerations for sports clubs.
