# Health and Safety at Work (Jersey) Law 1989 — Overview

## What the law does

The **Health and Safety at Work (Jersey) Law 1989**
(HSWL 1989) is Jersey's principal workplace safety
statute. It:

- Imposes **general duties** on employers, employees, and
  the self-employed;
- Empowers the **Health and Safety Inspectorate** to
  inspect, investigate, and prosecute;
- Provides for **improvement notices** and **prohibition
  notices**;
- Establishes a system of **detailed regulations** for
  specific hazards;
- Creates **criminal offences** for breach;
- Provides **safeguards** for whistleblowers and
  cooperators.

It is broadly modelled on the **UK Health and Safety at
Work etc. Act 1974** but adapted for Jersey conditions.

## General duties

### Employer to employees

Every employer must, **so far as is reasonably
practicable**:

- Ensure the **health, safety, and welfare at work** of
  all employees;
- Provide and maintain **safe plant and systems of
  work**;
- Make arrangements for **safe use, handling, storage,
  and transport** of articles and substances;
- Provide **information, instruction, training, and
  supervision**;
- Maintain a **safe workplace** with means of access and
  egress;
- Provide a **safe working environment**;
- Have a **written safety policy** (in prescribed
  circumstances).

### Employer to non-employees

Employers must also conduct undertakings so as not to
expose **non-employees** (visitors, contractors,
neighbours) to risks to their health or safety.

### Self-employed

Self-employed persons must conduct undertakings so as
not to expose themselves or others to risks.

### Employees

Employees must:

- **Take reasonable care** for their own health and
  safety and that of others;
- **Cooperate** with the employer to enable compliance;
- **Not interfere** with safety equipment or
  arrangements.

## Specific regulations

Detailed regulations supplement the HSWL for specific
sectors and hazards:

- **Construction**;
- **Manual handling**;
- **Display screen equipment**;
- **Personal protective equipment**;
- **Hazardous substances** (COSHH-equivalent);
- **Noise**;
- **Asbestos**;
- **Lifting equipment**;
- **Working at height**;
- **Confined spaces**.

Many regulations adopt UK / EU standards by reference.

## Enforcement

The **Health and Safety Inspectorate**:

- **Inspects** workplaces (notice given except in
  emergencies);
- **Investigates** accidents and complaints;
- **Issues improvement notices** for non-compliance
  short of immediate danger;
- **Issues prohibition notices** stopping work where
  imminent serious risk;
- **Prosecutes** for serious breaches;
- **Cooperates** with police on the most serious cases
  (corporate manslaughter, etc.).

### Improvement notice

- **Specifies** the breach;
- **Specifies** action required;
- **Sets time** for compliance;
- **Appeal** to the Royal Court available.

### Prohibition notice

- **Effective immediately**;
- **Stops** specified activity;
- **Until** remedied;
- **Appeal** to the Royal Court available.

## Reporting requirements

Employers must report specified categories of incident:

- **Fatal accidents** at work;
- **Major injuries** as defined;
- **Dangerous occurrences** (specific list);
- **Occupational diseases**.

These are reported via the Inspectorate, similar in scope
to the UK RIDDOR regime.

## Offences

Breaches of:

- **General duties**;
- **Specific regulations**;
- **Improvement notice / prohibition notice**;
- **Reporting obligations**;

are **criminal offences**, attracting:

- **Fines** (substantial for corporate defendants in
  serious cases);
- **Imprisonment** for individuals in serious cases;
- **Personal liability** for company directors / officers
  who consented, connived, or were negligent;
- **Aggravating factors** (death, serious injury, prior
  breaches) significantly increase penalty.

## Whistleblowing and worker protection

Workers who **raise concerns** about workplace safety:

- **Cannot be dismissed** or suffered detriment for
  raising legitimate concerns;
- Protected by the **Employment (Jersey) Law 2003** and
  specific HSWL provisions;
- Can **contact the Inspectorate** directly and
  anonymously;
- **Safety representatives** in larger workplaces have
  statutory roles.

See [`el-articles-index.md`](./el-articles-index.md) for
employment-law protections.

## Civil claims

Beyond criminal liability, an injured worker has:

- **Civil claim** for negligence and breach of statutory
  duty against the employer;
- **Specific** Jersey rules on causation, contributory
  negligence, damages;
- **Employers' Liability Insurance** typically responds;
- **Specific** time limits for claims.

## Interaction with employment law

HSWL and EJL 2003 work together:

- **Dismissal of a whistleblower** — both EJL unfair
  dismissal and HSWL detrimental treatment;
- **Specific** EJL protections for safety-related
  dismissals;
- **Tribunal** for unfair-dismissal claims; **Royal Court**
  for personal-injury / negligence claims.

## Practical recommendations

For employers:

- **Written safety policy**;
- **Risk assessments** for specific tasks and
  workplaces;
- **Training** documented;
- **PPE** provided and maintained;
- **Reporting** systems for incidents;
- **Cooperation** with Inspectorate inspections.

For workers:

- **Cooperate** with safety arrangements;
- **Raise concerns** through line management first, then
  Inspectorate;
- **Whistleblower protection** if retaliated against;
- **Specific** civil claims if injured.
