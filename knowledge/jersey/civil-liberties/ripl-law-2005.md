# Regulation of Investigatory Powers (Jersey) Law 2005 — Overview

## What the law does

The **Regulation of Investigatory Powers (Jersey) Law
2005** (RIPL 2005) is Jersey's principal statute
governing **State surveillance and investigatory powers**.
It:

- **Regulates** interception of communications (including
  postal interception);
- **Regulates** acquisition and disclosure of
  communications data (e.g. phone records, internet
  activity logs);
- **Regulates** covert surveillance (directed and
  intrusive);
- **Regulates** the use of covert human intelligence
  sources (CHIS);
- **Establishes authorisation procedures** for each
  power;
- **Creates the Investigatory Powers Commissioner** for
  oversight;
- **Provides** Code of Practice safeguards.

The framework is modelled on the **UK Regulation of
Investigatory Powers Act 2000** (RIPA 2000). UK law has
since been substantially replaced by the **Investigatory
Powers Act 2016**, but Jersey's RIPL 2005 remains in
force — a notable gap with UK law that has been the
subject of consultation and review.

## Four principal techniques regulated

### Interception of communications

**Reading or recording** the content of a private
communication while it is being transmitted:

- **Telephone calls**;
- **Email**;
- **Postal mail**;
- **Other electronic communications**.

Requires a **warrant** from the Bailiff (or other
prescribed authoriser) on specific grounds (national
security, serious crime, etc.). Unauthorised interception
is a criminal offence.

See [`interception-of-communications.md`](interception-of-communications.md).

### Acquisition of communications data

**Metadata** about communications (who, when, where, how
long) — but **not** content. Examples:

- **Call records** (which number called which, when, for
  how long);
- **Internet** subscriber records;
- **Location data** from mobile phones;
- **Email** sender / recipient / time data (not content).

Authorised internally by designated authorisers within
public authorities, subject to RIPL safeguards.

### Covert surveillance

Two types:

- **Directed surveillance** — covert monitoring of a
  specific person likely to result in obtaining private
  information (e.g. observation, photography, tracking
  movements);
- **Intrusive surveillance** — covert monitoring inside
  residential premises or private vehicles, or use of a
  surveillance device that obtains information of the
  same quality;
- **Specific** safeguards for each.

Intrusive surveillance has the highest authorisation
threshold; directed surveillance lower but still
formally regulated.

See [`covert-surveillance.md`](covert-surveillance.md).

### Covert human intelligence sources (CHIS)

Use of **informants** or **undercover officers** who form
a relationship with the subject to obtain or provide
information without the subject's knowledge:

- **Police informants**;
- **Undercover officers**;
- **Specific** safeguards in the Code of Practice;
- **Vulnerable CHIS** (minors, mentally impaired persons)
  — specific protections.

See [`covert-human-intelligence-sources.md`](covert-human-intelligence-sources.md).

## Authorising authorities

Different techniques have different authorisers:

| Technique | Authoriser |
|---|---|
| Interception warrant | Bailiff (or specified officer) |
| Communications data | Designated officer within authorised body |
| Directed surveillance | Designated senior officer of authorised body |
| Intrusive surveillance | Senior authorising officer + judicial approval |
| CHIS | Designated senior officer |

The **authorised bodies** include the States of Jersey
Police, the JFSC for regulatory investigations, Customs &
Excise, and specified other public authorities.

## Grounds for use

Powers may be authorised only for specified purposes:

- **National security**;
- **Preventing or detecting serious crime**;
- **Safeguarding economic well-being of Jersey**;
- **Public safety**;
- **Public health**;
- **Tax-collection** purposes;
- **Other specified statutory purposes**.

Powers cannot be used for **trivial or unauthorised**
purposes — any breach undermines the lawfulness of the
evidence obtained.

## Proportionality

Every RIPL authorisation must be:

- **Necessary** — for a permitted statutory purpose;
- **Proportionate** to the issue being investigated;
- **Specific** in scope (target, duration, technique);
- **Time-limited** (with renewal options);
- **Recorded** for audit.

These align with ECHR Article 8 requirements (right to
private life).

## Codes of Practice

Detailed Codes of Practice supplement RIPL:

- **Interception of Communications Code**;
- **Acquisition and Disclosure of Communications Data
  Code**;
- **Covert Surveillance Code**;
- **Covert Human Intelligence Sources Code**.

Codes are **binding** on authorised officers; non-
compliance can result in:

- **Evidence exclusion** at trial;
- **Civil claims** against the State;
- **Specific** judicial scrutiny.

## Oversight — Investigatory Powers Commissioner

Article 43 of RIPL provides for an **Investigatory Powers
Commissioner**:

- **Appointed** by the Bailiff;
- **Drawn from** the Court of Appeal judges;
- **Independent** of the executive;
- **Reviews** authorisations and use of powers;
- **Annual report** to the Bailiff;
- **Specific** investigatory powers in cases of complaint.

## Complaints and remedies

A person who believes they have been improperly
surveilled can:

- **Complain** to the Investigatory Powers Commissioner;
- **Seek redress** through ordinary civil remedies (false
  imprisonment, breach of privacy, breach of human
  rights);
- **Challenge** specific authorisations on judicial
  review.

A specific **Investigatory Powers Tribunal** equivalent
does **not** exist in Jersey at the level of detail of
the UK's IPT.

## Concerns and reform pipeline

The 2022 review by States members noted:

- **RIPL is based on UK RIPA 2000** — now superseded in
  UK by IPA 2016;
- **Technological developments** (encryption, modern
  internet, mobile devices) have outpaced the original
  framework;
- **Oversight** could be strengthened;
- **Specific** updates to Codes of Practice are
  ongoing.

Major statutory revision was under consultation but
**not yet enacted** as of May 2026.

## Civil-liberties balance

RIPL sits at the intersection of:

- **State investigatory capability** — for legitimate
  crime detection and national-security purposes;
- **Individual privacy** — protected by ECHR Article 8
  via the Human Rights (Jersey) Law 2000;
- **Procedural safeguards** — authorisation, oversight,
  remedies.

The Code of Practice framework and judicial / Commissioner
oversight are central to making the balance work in
practice.
