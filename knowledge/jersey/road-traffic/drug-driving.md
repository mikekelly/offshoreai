# Drug-Driving in Jersey

## The current legal position (May 2026)

Driving while **impaired by drugs** is an offence under the
Road Traffic (Jersey) Law 1956. The principal route to
prosecution is **impairment** — police must show the driver
was unfit to drive properly because of drug use, on
objective evidence (poor control, slurred speech, failed
field sobriety tests, blood/urine showing drugs).

What Jersey **does not have** as of May 2026:

- **Statutory drug-concentration limits** (no equivalent of
  the English specified-limit drug-driving regime);
- **Police power to conduct roadside drug tests** (other
  than impairment-based field tests);
- **Per se offences** — i.e. being over a numeric limit
  regardless of impairment.

The result is that **prosecution depends on proving
impairment** — a more difficult evidential test than
drink-driving's mechanical "over the limit" rule.

## Reform pipeline

In **February 2026**, States representatives proposed
legislation to:

- **Introduce statutory drug limits** for controlled and
  some prescription substances;
- **Give police roadside testing powers** (swab tests
  similar to UK drug-wipes);
- **Create per se offences** — being over the limit is
  itself the offence;
- **Mandatory disqualification** on conviction.

The proposal is **not yet enacted** as of May 2026. The
States Assembly's timetable suggests potential
introduction during 2026 or 2027.

Until enacted, the **impairment-based regime** continues
to apply.

## How drug-driving is currently prosecuted

Procedure:

1. **Stop** by States or Honorary Police, typically on
   observed signs of impairment;
2. **Field sobriety tests** — balance, walking, eye
   movement assessments;
3. **Arrest** if officer has reasonable suspicion;
4. **Blood / urine sample** at the police station —
   tested for controlled and prescription drugs;
5. **Charge** based on:
   - Impairment evidence (field tests, officer
     observation); plus
   - Drug presence in blood / urine;
6. **Magistrate's Court** proceedings.

## Substances covered

The impairment offence is **substance-neutral** —
impairment by any drug counts, including:

- **Controlled drugs** (cannabis, cocaine, MDMA, heroin,
  etc.);
- **Prescription medication** (sedatives, opioids,
  benzodiazepines, some antihistamines);
- **Over-the-counter** medicines with warnings;
- **Combination** of drugs and alcohol.

A driver impaired by **lawful prescription medication**
can still be prosecuted if the impairment is real. "I have
a prescription" is not a defence to impairment.

## Sentencing

Drug-impairment driving sentencing tracks drink-driving:

- **Mandatory disqualification** (typically 12+ months
  for a first offence);
- **Fine** up to £2,000;
- **Imprisonment** up to 6 months;
- **Endorsement** of licence;
- **Aggravation** for accident, presence of others,
  refusal to cooperate.

For combined drink-and-drug cases, the alcohol penalty
typically dominates but the drug element is an
aggravating factor.

## Defences

Substantive defences:

- **Identity** — were you the driver?
- **Impairment not made out** — passing field tests,
  alternative medical explanation;
- **Procedural defect** in the sample / testing;
- **Innocent ingestion** — extremely narrow;
- **Lawful prescription within label** — relevant to
  intent for some charges but not impairment.

## Recommendations

For drivers:

- **Do not drive** under the influence of any impairing
  substance, prescription or otherwise;
- **Check medication labels** — "do not drive or operate
  heavy machinery" means it;
- **Consult GP / pharmacist** if uncertain about a
  prescription's effect on driving;
- **Plan transport** if attending an event where drug use
  is likely;
- **Morning after** cannabis or sedatives can still
  impair — be cautious.

If charged:

- **Engage specialist legal advice immediately**;
- **Cooperate** with the sample procedure (refusal is its
  own offence);
- **Note** every procedural detail.
