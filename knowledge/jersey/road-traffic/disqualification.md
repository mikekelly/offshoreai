# Disqualification from Driving

## Two kinds

### Mandatory disqualification

For specified offences, disqualification is **mandatory** —
the Magistrate must impose it (subject to "special
reasons" exceptions, which are very narrow). Typical
mandatory-disqualification offences:

- **Drink-driving** (any reading over the limit);
- **Drug-impaired driving**;
- **Failing to provide a specimen** without reasonable
  excuse;
- **Dangerous driving** (Article 23);
- **Causing death by dangerous driving**;
- **Causing death by careless driving while under the
  influence**;
- **Aggravated vehicle taking**.

Minimum periods are statutory — typically **12 months for
a first offence**, longer for repeats.

### Discretionary disqualification

For other offences, the Magistrate **may** impose
disqualification based on the seriousness, the driver's
history, and the circumstances. Examples:

- **Speeding** (especially substantial excess);
- **Careless driving**;
- **Driving without insurance**;
- **Failing to stop / report** after an accident;
- **Mobile phone use** while driving (repeat).

## Periods

Disqualification periods vary widely:

- **Days** — for less serious instances;
- **Weeks** — for moderate cases;
- **Months** — for serious cases;
- **Years** — for serious cases with aggravation;
- **Lifetime** — extremely rare; for the most serious
  repeated dangerous offending.

## Re-test requirement

For some offences (typically dangerous driving and
serious drink-driving), the court orders that the driver
**re-takes the driving test** before the licence can be
restored. This is in addition to the disqualification.

## Totting up — Jersey approach

Jersey does **not** operate a UK-style automatic
"12 points = 6 month disqualification" totting-up scheme.
Instead:

- The court **considers the driver's history** at
  sentencing;
- **Multiple endorsements** weigh heavily toward
  disqualification;
- **Pattern** of offending — e.g. several speeding
  offences in a short period — typically results in a
  disqualification being imposed in the next case;
- **No automatic** trigger — it remains a matter for
  judicial discretion.

This can produce **less predictable** outcomes than the
UK system, but allows tailored sentencing.

## Special reasons

For mandatory-disqualification offences, the court can
decline to disqualify only if **special reasons** are
shown. These are:

- **Connected** with the offence (not the offender);
- **Special** in the sense of being unusual or
  particular;
- **Not amounting to a defence** (because if there's a
  defence, that's an acquittal);
- **Mitigating** sufficiently to justify the very narrow
  outcome.

Examples occasionally accepted:

- **Spiked drink** — drink consumed without knowledge;
- **Genuine emergency** — driving briefly to escape a
  threat;
- **Short distance** in a private place mistakenly
  thought non-public.

Not accepted:

- **Hardship** to the driver (work, family);
- **Inconvenience** of disqualification;
- **First offence** as such;
- **Clean record**.

## Effect of disqualification

A disqualified driver:

- **Cannot drive any motor vehicle** on a road;
- **Cannot apply** for a new licence in the meantime;
- **Faces the separate, serious offence** of driving
  while disqualified if they drive;
- **May face insurance impact** even after restoration —
  premiums rise sharply;
- **May face travel impact** — some countries restrict
  entry;
- **Employment impact** if driving is required.

## Reducing the period

A driver disqualified for a long period (typically 2+
years) may apply for **early restoration** of the licence
after a substantial portion has elapsed. The court will
consider:

- **Conduct since** disqualification;
- **Genuine remorse and rehabilitation**;
- **Circumstances** justifying restoration;
- **Public safety** considerations.

## Driving while disqualified

A separate, serious offence with:

- **Substantial fine**;
- **Possible imprisonment** (more likely for repeats);
- **Further extended disqualification**;
- **Aggravation** if combined with other offences (e.g.
  no insurance, drink-driving).
