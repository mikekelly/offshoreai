# Road Traffic (Jersey) Law 1956 — Overview

## What the law does

The **Road Traffic (Jersey) Law 1956** (RTJL 1956) is the
principal Jersey statute regulating motor vehicles, driving,
and road use. It covers:

- **Driving licences** — types, eligibility, foreign-licence
  exchange;
- **Vehicle requirements** — registration, condition, lights,
  documentation;
- **Driving offences** — careless and dangerous driving,
  drink-driving, speeding, mobile phone use, traffic-light
  contraventions;
- **Sentencing** — fines, disqualification, prison;
- **Procedure** — Parish Hall enquiries (via the Honorary
  Police framework), Magistrate's Court, appeals.

It is **almost 70 years old** but has been amended
extensively — including substantial 2020s amendments on
hazardous driving, drink-driving and (proposed) drug-driving.

The Magistrate's Court is the principal forum for traffic
offences, with the **Parish Hall Enquiry** as the
distinctive Jersey pre-court mechanism — see
[`parish-hall-enquiries.md`](parish-hall-enquiries.md).

## Structure

The RTJL 1956 is organised by:

- **Part 1** — General offences and powers (careless /
  dangerous driving, drink-driving, mobile phones,
  failing to stop)
- **Part 2** — Licensing and vehicle requirements
- **Part 3** — Speeding and traffic regulation
- **Part 4** — Insurance and registration interaction
- **Part 5** — Penalties, disqualification, endorsement
- **Part 6** — Procedure, evidence, appeals
- **Part 7** — Miscellaneous and amending provisions

Article-level coverage is a planned follow-up — this file
is the first overview tier.

## Sentencing framework

The Magistrate's Court Guidelines provide structured
sentencing tariffs. For speeding, the guidelines map fine
bands and endorsement / disqualification recommendations
against excess speed and zone limit — see
[`speeding.md`](speeding.md). For drink-driving, a
structured matrix maps reading bands to fine levels and
disqualification periods.

## Procedural distinctives

### Parish Hall Enquiry layer

Most routine motoring offences in Jersey are **first dealt
with at a Parish Hall Enquiry** by the **Centenier** of the
relevant parish, not in court. A Centenier can:

- **Caution** the offender (no further action);
- **Issue a fine** (up to £200 per offence);
- **Refer to Magistrate's Court** if the offence is more
  serious, if the offender refuses to admit, or if the
  defendant requests court.

The defendant has the **right to demand Magistrate's Court**
instead at any point. See
[`parish-hall-enquiries.md`](parish-hall-enquiries.md).

### Threshold for court

Some offences **cannot** be dealt with at Parish Hall:

- **Speeding 15 mph or more over the limit**;
- **Drink-driving**;
- **Dangerous driving**;
- **Driving while disqualified**;
- **Failing to stop / report after an accident** (in
  serious cases);
- **Any offence with mandatory disqualification**;
- **Any offence the Centenier judges too serious for the
  £200 ceiling**.

These go directly to Magistrate's Court summons.

### Honorary Police

Enforcement of road traffic on Jersey roads is shared
between:

- The **States of Jersey Police** (uniformed, salaried);
- The **Honorary Police** of each parish — Centeniers,
  Vingteniers, Constable's Officers (volunteer-elected,
  not salaried).

See [`honorary-police.md`](honorary-police.md).

## Sanctions

The RTJL 1956 provides for:

- **Fines** — set against the **standard scale of fines**
  for Jersey (level 1-4, with monetary equivalents);
- **Endorsement** of the licence;
- **Disqualification** from driving — discretionary or
  mandatory depending on the offence;
- **Prison** sentences for serious offences (dangerous
  driving, causing death by driving, repeat drink-driving);
- **Vehicle forfeiture** in some narrow contexts.

Jersey **does not** operate a UK-style "totting up" of
penalty points leading to automatic disqualification.
Instead, the sentencing court considers the totality of
the driver's history.

## Recent and forthcoming reforms

- **Causing Death or Serious Injury by Driving** —
  expanded grounds following States Assembly debate;
- **Drug-driving** — proposed February 2026 reform to
  introduce statutory drug limits and roadside testing
  powers (not yet in force as of May 2026);
- **New Highway Code** — updated rules for vulnerable road
  users (cyclists, pedestrians, e-scooters).

See [`drug-driving.md`](drug-driving.md) for the drug-
driving reform position.
