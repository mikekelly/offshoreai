# Parish Hall Enquiries — The Distinctive Jersey Forum

## What this is

A **Parish Hall Enquiry** is a pre-court forum, presided
over by a **Centenier** (an elected honorary police
officer) of the parish in which an alleged offence took
place. It is the route by which the **majority of routine
Jersey motoring offences are resolved** without ever
reaching the Magistrate's Court.

There is **no direct equivalent** in UK or other
common-law jurisdictions. Foreign lawyers should not
assume it works like a UK fixed-penalty notice, a Scottish
fiscal fine, or an English court — it has features of
each but distinctive procedure and ceiling.

## The Centenier's role

A **Centenier** is an **elected, unpaid official** of one
of Jersey's 12 parishes, with broad powers in **minor
criminal matters** including most road traffic. Each parish
has a senior **Connétable** (parish head) and several
Centeniers and Vingteniers.

At the Parish Hall the Centenier:

- **Reviews** the evidence presented by the States Police
  or by other Centeniers / Vingteniers;
- **Decides** whether there is sufficient evidence to
  justify a charge;
- **Determines** whether the public interest requires
  prosecution or whether the matter can be dealt with at
  Parish Hall level;
- **Engages** with the alleged offender;
- **Issues** a caution, fine, or referral to court.

The Centenier is **not a judge** in the conventional
sense — they have prosecutorial and limited adjudicative
roles combined, an unusual mixture justified by long
parish tradition.

## Powers at the Parish Hall

The Centenier may:

- **Caution** (no further action; recorded);
- **Issue a written fine** of up to **£200 per offence**;
- **Refer** to Magistrate's Court if the offence exceeds
  Parish-Hall scope.

The Centenier may **only** issue a fine or caution if:

- The offender **admits** the offence;
- The offender **accepts** the proposed penalty.

A defendant who **disputes** the offence — even if it is
of the level Parish Hall could handle — must be referred
to court.

## Defendant's procedural rights

A person summoned to a Parish Hall Enquiry has the right
to:

- **Be informed** of the alleged offence in advance;
- **Bring a friend, family member, or advocate** for
  support;
- **Decline to admit** the offence;
- **Demand** that the case be heard at **Magistrate's
  Court** instead — at any point, before or after the
  Parish Hall outcome;
- **Appeal** any Parish Hall outcome by requesting
  Magistrate's Court re-hearing;
- **Receive a copy** of the Centenier's record.

The "right to opt out" is critical. A defendant who fears
the Centenier may impose an unjust fine, or who wants
formal court treatment, can simply decline.

## When does a case go to Parish Hall?

Routine **motoring offences** are typical Parish Hall
matter. Examples that **can** be dealt with at Parish
Hall:

- **Minor speeding** (under 15 mph excess);
- **Mobile phone** offences;
- **Failing to display** insurance disc;
- **Traffic-light** offences (in some cases);
- **Minor careless driving** (in some cases);
- **Parking** offences.

The "schedule of offences" treatable at Parish Hall is
maintained by the Law Officers' Department and reviewed
periodically.

## When does it NOT go to Parish Hall?

The Parish Hall route is **excluded** for:

- **Speeding 15 mph or more over the limit**;
- **Drink-driving** (any reading);
- **Drug-driving**;
- **Dangerous driving** (Article 23 RTJL);
- **Causing death or serious injury** by driving;
- **Driving while disqualified**;
- **Failing to stop / report** after an accident (in
  serious cases);
- **Any offence carrying mandatory disqualification**;
- **Any offence for which the Centenier judges the £200
  ceiling inadequate**.

These offences are referred directly to **Magistrate's
Court** by summons.

## Procedural mechanics

A typical Parish Hall Enquiry runs:

1. **Honorary Police** (or States Police) report the
   alleged offence to the parish;
2. **Defendant summoned** to the Parish Hall on a
   specified date;
3. **Centenier meets** the defendant;
4. **Evidence reviewed** (police statement, witness
   evidence);
5. **Defendant invited** to admit or deny;
6. **If admit**: Centenier proposes a penalty; defendant
   accepts or asks for court;
7. **If deny**: matter referred to Magistrate's Court;
8. **Outcome recorded** by the Centenier.

The whole process is **typically 15-30 minutes** and is
**informal** — no robes, no transcripts in the formal
sense, no advocate's address.

## Attendance

- **Summons to attend** — must attend if served with a
  formal summons or bail condition;
- **Request to attend** — voluntary; the Centenier may
  proceed to summons if you don't;
- **Failure to attend** with no good reason — may result
  in summons or worse;
- **Postponement** possible on good grounds (work, health,
  travel).

## Records and consequences

A Parish Hall outcome:

- **Is recorded** by the Centenier;
- **Appears on records** that may be relevant to future
  Parish Hall or court matters;
- **Does not formally count** as a criminal conviction
  for most purposes (no court conviction);
- **May be reported** to other authorities depending on
  the offence (e.g. licensing implications);
- **Endorsement of driving licence** — yes, even Parish
  Hall outcomes can lead to endorsement for relevant
  offences.

## Strategic considerations

For a motorist deciding between Parish Hall and court:

| Factor | Parish Hall | Magistrate's Court |
|---|---|---|
| Speed | Resolved same day usually | Several weeks |
| Formality | Informal, no advocate needed | Formal procedure |
| Penalty ceiling | £200 per offence | Statutory max for offence |
| Disqualification | Cannot be imposed | Can be imposed |
| Endorsement | Can endorse | Can endorse |
| Record | Parish record | Formal court conviction |
| Right to appeal | Yes — request Magistrate's Court | Royal Court appeal |
| Cost | None | Court fees + advocate fees if engaged |
| Advice | Advocate optional | Advocate recommended for serious matters |

For **most minor offences** where the defendant accepts
guilt, Parish Hall is faster, cheaper, and less serious on
record. For **disputed matters** or where **disqualification
is possible**, Magistrate's Court is appropriate.

## Pitfalls

- **Admitting** an offence at Parish Hall without legal
  advice when court might have been better (e.g. if
  defences exist, or if endorsement is consequential for
  insurance);
- **Failing to attend** a summoned Parish Hall — court
  follows on;
- **Accepting** a £200 fine when the offence might warrant
  a court £50 fine instead — Centenier penalties are not
  always lower than court penalties;
- **Forgetting** the right to opt out to Magistrate's
  Court;
- **Assuming** Parish Hall has no consequences — it can
  endorse licences and create records.
