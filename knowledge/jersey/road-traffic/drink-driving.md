# Drink-Driving in Jersey

## The legal limit

Jersey's prescribed drink-drive limits are:

- **80 milligrammes of alcohol per 100 millilitres of
  blood** (BAC 0.08%);
- **35 microgrammes of alcohol per 100 millilitres of
  breath**;
- **107 milligrammes of alcohol per 100 millilitres of
  urine**.

These mirror the **historic UK limit** (still applicable in
England, Wales and Northern Ireland) and are **higher than
Scotland's** (50mg / 100ml blood). Drivers used to Scottish
or many European limits should not assume Jersey's lower
limit applies.

## The offences

The principal offences are:

- **Driving** a motor vehicle on a road or other public
  place with alcohol concentration above the limit;
- **Attempting to drive** with excess alcohol;
- **Being in charge** of a motor vehicle with excess
  alcohol (a lesser but still serious offence);
- **Failing to provide a specimen** of breath, blood or
  urine on request;
- **Driving / attempting to drive / in charge while
  unfit through drink** — even below the limit, if
  driving ability is impaired.

Each offence is **distinct**; a driver can be charged with
one or several depending on the facts.

## The procedure — roadside to charge

A typical drink-driving stop runs:

1. **Stop**: by States of Jersey Police or Honorary
   Police (Centenier, Vingtenier, Constable's Officer);
2. **Initial assessment**: officer observes signs of
   impairment, smells alcohol, or has reasonable
   suspicion;
3. **Roadside breath test**: a **screening breathalyser**
   ("screening test") at the roadside;
4. **Arrest** if the screening test indicates excess
   alcohol or is refused;
5. **Station evidential test**: a more accurate
   **evidential breath test** at the police station, or
   a **blood / urine sample**;
6. **Charge** if the evidential test confirms excess
   alcohol;
7. **Bail to court** or **detention** depending on
   circumstances.

The screening test is **not evidence of guilt** by itself
— it is a tool to establish reasonable suspicion. The
evidential test is what supports prosecution.

## Failing to provide a specimen

Refusing to provide a specimen **without reasonable
excuse** is a separate offence carrying penalties **as
serious as drink-driving itself** — including mandatory
disqualification and possible imprisonment. The rationale
is to prevent evasion by refusal.

"Reasonable excuse" is narrow:

- **Medical inability** (rare; needs medical evidence);
- **Religious objection** — generally not recognised;
- **Practical impossibility** at the moment in question.

## Sentencing

Drink-driving offences carry **mandatory disqualification**
(typically **minimum 12 months for a first offence**,
substantially longer for repeats). Additional penalties:

- **Fine**: up to **£2,000** for a first offence;
- **Imprisonment**: up to **6 months** for a first
  offence;
- **Mandatory disqualification**: from driving;
- **Endorsement** of the licence;
- **Re-test requirement** in some cases before licence
  restored.

Repeat offenders face escalating consequences:

- **Longer disqualifications** (often 3+ years for a
  second offence);
- **Higher fines**;
- **Imprisonment** more likely;
- **Article 23 dangerous driving** charges may
  alternatively or additionally apply.

For the most serious cases (causing death by drink-
driving), the case goes to the **Royal Court** with much
higher penalties — including substantial imprisonment.

## Sentencing matrix

The Magistrate's Court Guidelines map alcohol reading bands
to disqualification periods and fine levels. Higher
readings → longer disqualification and higher fines.
Indicative pattern (specifics vary):

| BAC reading | Indicative outcome (first offence) |
|---|---|
| Just over limit (35-50 μg/100ml breath equivalent) | 12-month disqualification, £400-£700 fine |
| Moderate (50-80) | 14-18 month disqualification, £700-£1,200 fine |
| High (80-110) | 18-22 month disqualification, £1,200-£1,800 fine |
| Very high (110-150) | 22-30 month disqualification, £1,500-£2,000 fine, possible imprisonment |
| Extreme (150+) | 30+ months disqualification, max fine, custodial considered |

Aggravating factors (accident caused, presence of
children, refusing test, evasive driving) push higher.

## Forum

Drink-driving **cannot** be dealt with at Parish Hall
Enquiry. All cases go to the **Magistrate's Court** by
summons; the most serious go to the **Royal Court**.

This means:

- **No £200 ceiling** — the full sentencing range applies;
- **Formal procedure** — admission of guilt, evidence,
  submissions;
- **Right to legal representation** — strongly
  recommended;
- **Right to remain silent** at police interview (subject
  to inferences in some cases);
- **Right to a defence advocate** at trial.

## Defences

Substantive defences:

- **Identity** — were you the driver?
- **Procedural defect** in the breath / blood test
  procedure (calibration, timing, two-specimen rule);
- **Hip flask defence** — alcohol consumed *after*
  driving but before the test (narrow; medical evidence
  needed);
- **No public place** — narrow Jersey-specific
  considerations;
- **Necessity / duress** — extremely narrow.

The breath-test procedure is heavily prescribed by statute
— any departure can be a defence. Specialist advice is
essential.

## After conviction

A driver convicted of drink-driving:

- **Loses licence** immediately for the disqualification
  period;
- **Pays fine** (and prosecution costs);
- **May serve prison** for serious cases;
- **Faces insurance impact** — premiums rise sharply,
  some insurers refuse cover;
- **Travel impact** — some countries restrict entry for
  drink-driving convictions;
- **Employment impact** — for occupations requiring a
  clean licence;
- **Re-test requirement** before licence restoration in
  some cases.

## Practical recommendations

For drivers:

- **Don't drive after any drinks** — Jersey's limit is
  not low but the risk of error / variability is
  significant;
- **Plan transport** — taxis, designated drivers, public
  transport;
- **Morning-after** caution — alcohol clears at roughly
  1 unit per hour; heavy nights can leave drivers over
  the limit into the morning.

If stopped:

- **Cooperate** with the test procedure;
- **Refusal is its own serious offence**;
- **Engage specialist legal advice** as soon as possible
  if charged;
- **Note** every procedural detail (timing, calibration
  numbers if visible, officer ID).
