# Article 17 — Appointment of Trustees Out of Court

Article 17 of the **[Trusts (Jersey) Law 1984][tjl]** is the
**statutory fallback** mechanism for appointing new or additional
trustees when the trust deed does not provide for the appointment or
when the appointment mechanism in the deed cannot be invoked.

## What Article 17 says

A new or additional trustee may be appointed out of court where:

- the **trust terms lack** a relevant appointment provision;
- the **relevant power has lapsed** (e.g., the named appointor has
  died or lost capacity); or
- the **appointor cannot act** for any other reason.

In those situations the **trustees**, the **last remaining
trustee**, or their **personal representative** may make the
appointment.

The newly-appointed trustee:

- gains **the same powers and duties** as the original trustees;
- has the **trust property vested in them** (jointly with continuing
  trustees) by operation of the Article;
- assumes the office with effect from the appointment instrument's
  effective date.

(See the consolidated text at [jerseylaw.je][tjl].)

## Why Article 17 matters

Most Jersey trust deeds contain an express appointment power —
typically vested in the settlor (lifetime), the protector, or the
continuing trustees. Article 17 is the safety net that prevents a
trust from becoming **paralysed** when:

- the settlor has died and the deed did not provide for succession of
  the appointor power;
- a sole-name appointor has lost capacity;
- the deed's appointment mechanism produces a deadlock between
  multiple appointors;
- the trust has been silent on appointment from inception (rare in
  modern practice, common in older deeds).

## Vesting on appointment

A characteristic Jersey feature: when a new trustee is appointed
under Article 17, **trust property vests in them automatically by
operation of law**, without a separate vesting deed. This contrasts
with some jurisdictions that require a positive transfer.

The automatic vesting applies to property within the trust at the
time of appointment. Property held through a separate legal vehicle
(e.g., shares of a company underlying the trust) is not affected by
the vesting itself — the company continues as it was, but the trust's
beneficial interest in the shares is now held jointly with the new
trustee.

## Order of preference among potential appointors

Article 17 names: the trustees; the last remaining trustee; their
personal representative.

Where the deed does provide for an appointor but that appointor
cannot act, the Article 17 fallback opens. Where the deed provides
for *no* appointor at all, Article 17 is the only mechanism.

In practice, where there are multiple possible Article 17 appointors
(e.g., two continuing trustees), the appointment power is exercised
**jointly** — consistent with [Article 22](co-trustees-acting-together.md).

## When Article 17 is unavailable

Article 17 is unavailable where no person in the listed categories
exists or can act. The typical scenario: a Jersey trust has only one
trustee, who has died, and the deceased trustee's personal
representative has not been appointed or cannot act in the time
required.

The fallback is then a **Royal Court application** under
[Article 51](article-51-directions.md) (for directions including
appointment) or [Article 44](court-appointed-resident-trustee.md)
 (for the appointment of a resident trustee where the
Court considers the trust requires a Jersey-resident trustee).

## Pitfalls

- **Premature appointment.** Appointing a new trustee under Article
  17 while the original appointment mechanism could still be invoked
  is irregular and may be set aside.
- **Vesting and foreign-situated property.** While Jersey-law vesting
  is automatic under Article 17, foreign-situated property (e.g.,
  real estate held directly by the trust in a foreign jurisdiction)
  may require additional local-law steps to transfer registered
  title to the new trustees.
- **PTC director changes vs trustee changes.** A PTC acting as sole
  trustee continues as trustee through changes in its directors;
  Article 17 is not engaged by a board change. The relevant
  internal-governance provisions are in
  [`corporate-trustee-resolution.md`](corporate-trustee-resolution.md).
