# Article 14 — Resignation or Removal of Enforcer

Article 14 of the **[Trusts (Jersey) Law 1984][tjl]** is the
**enforcer-lifecycle** provision: it governs how an enforcer of a
non-charitable purpose trust may resign or be removed.

## What Article 14 says

An enforcer **may resign** by giving **written notice**, **unless
doing so would facilitate a breach** of the trust.

(See the consolidated text at [jerseylaw.je][tjl].)

The Article also accommodates removal, by mechanism prescribed in
the trust instrument (typically by the settlor while alive, or by a
protector, or by the Royal Court on application).

## Resignation — the "unless...facilitate a breach" qualifier

An enforcer cannot simply resign in circumstances where the
resignation would leave the trust without an enforcer at a moment
when the trustee is about to commit a breach. The Article imposes a
substantive constraint on the enforcer's freedom to walk away.

In practice, an enforcer who wishes to resign will:

- give written notice to the trustee and any other person named in
  the deed;
- ensure **a successor enforcer is identified** and willing to act
  before the resignation takes effect;
- where the deed is silent on succession, apply to the Royal Court
  for directions or for the appointment of a successor.

## Removal

The deed will normally provide for removal of the enforcer — typically
by the settlor (while alive and competent), by a protector, or by the
Royal Court on application. Where the deed is silent, the Royal Court
has jurisdiction under [Article 51](article-51-directions.md) to
remove an enforcer on application of an interested person.

Grounds for removal vary by deed but generally include:

- failure or refusal to act;
- incapacity;
- conflict of interest;
- conduct inconsistent with the proper performance of the office.

## Continuity of the office

A non-charitable purpose trust **must** have an enforcer at all
material times (Article 12). A gap in the office is a fundamental
defect. The deed should always provide:

- a clear **succession mechanism** (named successor, mechanism for
  appointment by the protector or settlor);
- a **default mechanism** (Royal Court application) if the named
  mechanism fails;
- a **transition procedure** ensuring the new enforcer has the
  records and information needed to perform the function from day
  one.

## Royal Court applications

The Royal Court will entertain applications to:

- appoint a successor enforcer where the deed mechanism has failed;
- remove an enforcer for cause;
- give directions to a resigning enforcer on the conduct of the
  resignation.

These are friendly proceedings under Article 51; costs are typically
out of the trust fund.

## Pitfalls

- **Premature resignation.** Resigning without an identified
  successor in a context where the trustee is about to act on the
  purposes leaves the trust unprotected and may itself be a breach
  of the enforcer's duties.
- **Settlor-controlled removal.** Some deeds give the settlor (or
  a protector) wide removal powers. These are fiduciary if the role
  is fiduciary; an arbitrary removal may itself be challenged.
- **Indemnity and release on resignation.** A resigning enforcer
  should typically obtain a release and indemnity from the trustee
  and any successor enforcer; this is standard practice but is not
  mandated by the Article.
