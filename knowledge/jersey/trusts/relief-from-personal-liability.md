# Article 45 — Court Power to Relieve Trustee from Personal Liability

Article 45 of the **[Trusts (Jersey) Law 1984][tjl]** gives the
Royal Court a **discretionary safety valve**: where a trustee has
breached the trust but acted **honestly and reasonably** and
**ought fairly to be excused**, the Court may relieve the trustee
wholly or partly from personal liability.

## What Article 45 says

The Royal Court may relieve a trustee **wholly or partly** from
personal liability for a breach of trust where the trustee:

- has acted **honestly**;
- has acted **reasonably**; and
- **ought fairly to be excused** for the breach or for omitting to
  obtain Court directions.

(See the consolidated text at [jerseylaw.je][tjl].)

## The three limbs

### 1. Honesty

Honesty is the threshold. A **dishonest** trustee — one acting in
bad faith, with knowledge that the act is a breach, or with
indifference to whether it is — is **not eligible** for Article 45
relief. This is consistent with the irreducible core in
[Article 30](./breach-of-trust-liability.md) (no exoneration for
fraud).

### 2. Reasonableness

The standard is **objective**: would a reasonable trustee in the
trustee's position have acted as the trustee did? A trustee that
made an honest mistake but **fell below the standard of a reasonable
trustee** does not satisfy this limb.

The professional-trustee skill uplift in
[Article 21](./trustee-duties.md) applies: a professional trustee's
"reasonable" standard is higher than a lay trustee's.

### 3. Ought fairly to be excused

Even where honesty and reasonableness are made out, the Court asks
whether **fairness** points toward relief. Factors:

- **the conduct of the beneficiaries** (a beneficiary who instigated
  or consented to the breach has weaker claim to recovery);
- the **proportionality** of personal liability to the trustee's
  fault — is full recovery disproportionate?;
- whether the trustee took **steps to mitigate** the breach;
- whether the trustee **sought directions** under
  [Article 51](./article-51-directions.md) — and if not, why not.

The "ought fairly to be excused" limb is the most discretionary; the
Royal Court has a wide remit.

## Application — when sought

Article 45 relief is typically sought:

- **defensively** — as part of the trustee's response to a
  breach-of-trust claim by beneficiaries;
- **proactively** — by the trustee under
  [Article 51](./article-51-directions.md), after a self-discovered
  breach, asking the Court for relief alongside directions on
  remediation.

A trustee that proactively brings a self-discovered breach to the
Court typically has a stronger Article 45 case: the trustee is
demonstrating honesty and the willingness to be supervised, which
weighs toward "ought fairly to be excused".

## Scope — wholly or partly

The relief can be **partial**. The Court may:

- relieve from **all** liability;
- relieve from a **proportion** (e.g., 50%);
- relieve from **specified components** (e.g., relief from the
  lost-gains element but not the principal loss);
- impose **conditions** (e.g., trustee continues to assist with
  remediation; trustee waives part of accrued fees).

This flexibility lets the Court fashion outcomes that fit the
specific facts.

## Relationship with deed exoneration clauses

A trustee may have **two avenues** to escape liability:

1. **Deed exoneration clauses** — which may exclude liability for
   negligence (below the gross threshold) and require the trustee
   to show the clause covers the conduct;
2. **Article 45 statutory relief** — discretionary, fact-sensitive,
   and not pre-bargained.

The two are independent. A trustee with a strong deed-exoneration
defence does not need Article 45; a trustee without one has Article
45 as the residual hope. The Court has recognised that Article 45
does **not** displace the irreducible-core rules from
[Article 30](./breach-of-trust-liability.md) — gross negligence and
wilful misconduct remain unprotectable.

## "Omitting to obtain court directions"

A specific application: a trustee who **omits** to obtain Court
directions on a difficult matter, and then is later challenged for
the decision taken, may seek Article 45 relief on the basis that
the failure to apply was itself honest and reasonable in context.

The Court will be sceptical where the matter was clearly
"momentous" within the *Public Trustee v Cooper* framework — see
[`article-51-directions.md`](./article-51-directions.md). A trustee
that should have applied for blessing and did not is more
exposed than a trustee that proceeded with a genuinely borderline
decision.

## Pitfalls

- **Relying on Article 45 in advance.** Article 45 is a
  back-up; trustees should not rely on it as a planning tool.
  Decisions should be defensible without invoking the relief.
- **Failing to disclose proactively.** A trustee that discovers a
  breach and conceals it loses the "honesty" limb. Early disclosure
  is essential.
- **Professional trustee, lower-standard defence.** A professional
  trustee cannot argue Article 45 relief on the basis that an
  ordinary trustee would have acted similarly — the professional
  standard applies under Article 21.
