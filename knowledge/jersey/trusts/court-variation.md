# Article 47 — Court Variation of Terms of a Jersey Trust

Article 47 of the **[Trusts (Jersey) Law 1984][tjl]** is the
**Court variation** jurisdiction — the historical "variation of
trusts" power, distinct from the modern **Articles 47B–47H
mistake-based set-aside** regime. The Royal Court may approve
arrangements **varying or revoking** trust terms on behalf of:

- **minors**;
- **interdicts** (persons under formal incapacity);
- **unascertained persons**; and
- **unborn beneficiaries**,

provided the arrangement appears **beneficial** to that person.

## What Article 47 says

The Court may approve arrangements "**varying or revoking all or any
of the terms of the trust**" on behalf of:

- a **minor** (who cannot consent for themselves);
- an **interdict** (a person under a guardianship-equivalent
  Jersey-law incapacity regime);
- **unascertained persons** (persons not yet identifiable —
  e.g., a future spouse);
- **unborn beneficiaries**,

where the Court is satisfied that the arrangement **appears
beneficial** to the person on whose behalf approval is sought.

The Court may also **confer powers on trustees** for transactions
deemed expedient but lacking express authority.

(See the consolidated text at [jerseylaw.je][tjl].)

## The naming-warning — Article 47 vs Articles 47B–H

"**Article 47**" is **ambiguous**:

- **Article 47** simpliciter — this Article — is the **Court
  variation** jurisdiction. It permits the Court to approve a
  proposed variation of trust terms in favour of those who cannot
  consent for themselves.
- **Articles 47B–47H** — added by the **Trusts (Amendment No. 6)
  (Jersey) Law 2013** — is the **mistake-based set-aside** regime,
  covering set-aside of transfers and exercises of fiduciary
  powers made under mistake.

These are **functionally distinct** jurisdictions. Cite the
sub-Article in formal work; see also
[`article-47-set-aside.md`](./article-47-set-aside.md).

## When Article 47 is invoked

Common variation applications:

- **early termination** of a discretionary trust where the adult
  beneficiaries are unanimous but the class includes unborn issue;
- **family rearrangement** — e.g., dividing a single trust into
  branch-specific sub-trusts;
- **tax-driven variation** — e.g., introducing a new beneficiary
  class to optimise UK or US tax treatment, where the change
  affects unborn or unascertained persons;
- **commercial restructuring** — modernising old administrative
  provisions in a trust drafted decades ago;
- **conferral of powers** — adding a power (e.g., to invest in a
  category not contemplated by the deed) where the deed is silent.

## The "beneficial" test

The Court must be satisfied that the proposed arrangement is
**beneficial** to the person on whose behalf approval is sought.
The standard is **substantive**, not nominal:

- a proposed variation that **only** benefits adult consenting
  beneficiaries at the expense of unborns is not beneficial to
  the unborns and will not be approved;
- a variation that **on balance** improves the position of unborns
  (e.g., by introducing a tax-efficient structure that increases
  the long-run value of the trust) can be approved even where
  short-term cash flow is unchanged or marginally reduced;
- the Court will sometimes require **actuarial or tax analysis**
  to evaluate the proposed benefit to unborns.

The Royal Court's approach mirrors the English Court's analysis
under the Variation of Trusts Act 1958 in many respects, with
Jersey-specific adaptations.

## Procedure

The application is by **Representation** in the Royal Court (Samedi
Division), supported by:

- the **trust deed** and any amendments;
- the **proposed deed of variation** drafted ready for execution;
- **affidavit evidence** explaining the rationale and the proposed
  benefit;
- **professional analysis** of the cross-border tax and accounting
  consequences;
- evidence of **adult-beneficiary consent** (in writing, signed);
- **guardian ad litem** representation for minor and unborn
  beneficiaries — typically a senior Jersey Advocate appointed by
  the Court to make submissions on the unborns' interests;
- evidence of **service** on all interested parties.

## Powers conferral

Article 47 also permits the Court to **confer powers on trustees**
for transactions the trust deems expedient but for which the deed
provides no express authority. This is the Jersey equivalent of the
English "Re Coxen" / general powers-conferring jurisdiction.

Common applications:

- conferring an **investment power** for an asset class the deed
  does not contemplate;
- conferring a **borrowing power** for a specific transaction;
- conferring a **power to vary administrative provisions** where the
  deed is silent.

## Interaction with Article 37 (deed-based variation)

[Article 37](./article-37-variation.md) permits variation **under the
deed's terms**. Article 47 is the **Court-led variation** route for
matters that exceed the deed's variation power or that affect
non-consenting interests. The two routes are complementary:

- where the deed provides for variation **and** the affected
  parties all consent, Article 37 is used;
- where the deed does not provide for the variation **or** there
  are non-consenting interests (minors, unborns), Article 47 is
  used.

## Interaction with Article 47A

[Article 47A](./court-purpose-trust-redirection.md) is the **cy-près-
style** redirection power for charitable and non-charitable purpose
trusts whose purposes have failed. Article 47 is the broader
variation jurisdiction; Article 47A is the specialist
purpose-redirection power. The two may be invoked together where
the failing purpose trust also has beneficiary aspects.

## Pitfalls

- **Inadequate unborn-representation.** Failing to convene a
  guardian ad litem for unborns risks the resulting order being
  challenged for procedural defect.
- **Substantive benefit thin.** A variation that is patently for
  the benefit of consenting adults at the expense of unborns will
  be refused. Take advice on framing.
- **Cross-border tax-trigger variation.** Variations that
  re-engineer the trust often have foreign-tax consequences. Model
  before applying.
- **Confusing Article 47 with Articles 47B–H.** Article 47 is the
  variation power; Articles 47B–H are the mistake regime. Cite
  precisely.
