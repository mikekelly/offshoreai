# Beneficiary Information Rights — Jersey

A beneficiary's right to information about a Jersey trust is governed
by the **Royal Court's inherent supervisory jurisdiction** over the
administration of trusts, as articulated by the Privy Council in
**[*Schmidt v Rosewood Trust Ltd* [2003] UKPC 26][schmidt]**. The Jersey
Royal Court has adopted *Schmidt* as a correct statement of Jersey law
and applies it through a pair of working presumptions: **disclosure of
the deed and accounts is presumed**, **disclosure of the letter of
wishes is presumed against**, and other materials are weighed in the
balance.

For the user-facing question ("the beneficiary is asking for the deed
— what must I provide?"), see
[`use-cases/trust-officer/beneficiary-information-rights.md`](../use-cases/trust-officer/beneficiary-information-rights.md).

## The starting point — no automatic entitlement

Before *Schmidt v Rosewood*, the leading common-law authority was the
English Court of Appeal in **Re Londonderry's Settlement [1965] Ch
918**, which framed the beneficiary's right to disclosure as flowing
from an **equitable proprietary interest** in trust documents. The
*Londonderry* approach also articulated a "trustee's reasoning is
confidential" rule that has continued, in a softened form, into the
modern law.

The Privy Council in **Schmidt v Rosewood Trust Ltd [2003] UKPC 26**
(per Lord Walker) rejected the proprietary foundation:

> The Board cannot regard it as a reasoned or binding decision that a
> beneficiary's right or claim to disclosure of trust documents or
> information must always have the proprietary basis of a transmissible
> interest in trust property.

(Quoted in the [Wikipedia summary of *Schmidt*][schmidt] and in
[*Schmidt* full-text summary on casemine][casemine].)

And the headline rule that the Jersey Royal Court has repeatedly
applied:

> No beneficiary (and least of all a discretionary object) has any
> entitlement as of right to disclosure of anything which can plausibly
> be described as a trust document. Especially when there are issues
> as to personal or commercial confidentiality, the Court may have to
> balance the competing interests of different beneficiaries, the
> trustees themselves and third parties. Disclosure may have to be
> limited and safeguards may have to be put in place.

(Lord Walker in *Schmidt*; see [casemine summary][casemine])

## The supervisory framework

Because disclosure is supervisory rather than proprietary:

- **The Court (and, in advance of any application, the trustee
  exercising its judgment) carries out a balancing exercise** of the
  interests of:
  - the requesting beneficiary;
  - other beneficiaries (whose own interests in privacy and in the
    administration of the trust may be affected);
  - the trustee (in administering the trust efficiently);
  - third parties whose information may be in the documents (the
    settlor; the protector; a former trustee; banking and investment
    counterparties).
- **Disclosure may be limited** — redacted accounts; summaries
  instead of full minutes; inspection-only at the trustee's office.
- **Safeguards may be imposed** — undertakings as to onward
  disclosure, anonymisation, or restriction to legal advisers only.

(See [Dickinson Gleeson — Rights of a beneficiary][dg]; [Mondaq —
Beneficiaries' Rights][mondaq])

## Jersey presumptions by document type

In the Jersey Royal Court's application of *Schmidt*, the following
presumptions have emerged:

| Document | Presumption | Comment |
|---|---|---|
| **Trust deed** (and amendments) | *In favour of* disclosure | The deed is the foundational document of the beneficiary's interest. A trustee who refuses to provide the deed will need a strong reason and should expect to defend the refusal in Court. |
| **Trust accounts** | *In favour of* disclosure | The presumption is for disclosure, particularly to fixed-interest beneficiaries and significant discretionary objects. Selective redaction may be appropriate where third-party confidentiality is engaged. |
| **Letters of wishes** | *Against* disclosure | "On the basis that the settlor was likely to have intended the letter to be confidential." ([Mondaq][mondaq]) Disclosure may still be ordered where it is in the beneficiaries' interests as a whole. |
| **Trustee minutes / deliberations** | No firm presumption | The *Londonderry* concern (that disclosing reasoning chills decision-making) survives; the Court balances. |
| **Lawyers' advice to trustees** | Against disclosure (privilege) | Generally not disclosable to beneficiaries; trustee should not waive without thought. |
| **Third-party correspondence** | Against disclosure | Trustee owes confidentiality duties to non-trust parties (banks, investment managers, other clients of the trust company). |
| **Investment portfolio details** | Disclosable subject to redaction | Specifically, position-by-position values may need redaction where they could be weaponised in family litigation. |

([Mondaq][mondaq]; [Dickinson Gleeson][dg])

## Who is a "beneficiary" for these purposes

The *Schmidt* jurisdiction extends to all persons whose interests in
the proper administration of the trust the Court is entitled to
supervise. That includes:

- **Fixed-interest beneficiaries** — strongest case.
- **Discretionary objects** — within the jurisdiction, but the Court
  considers the strength of their interest.
- **Mere objects of a power of addition** — weaker case; *Schmidt*
  itself was on facts where the claimant was a discretionary object.
- **Future / contingent beneficiaries** — within scope where the
  interest is sufficiently real and not merely speculative.

The following are **not** beneficiaries for *Schmidt* purposes
(though they may have other avenues):

- **Settlors** (unless named as beneficiaries) — settlors who want
  information have to rely on the deed or on accepted practice, not
  on *Schmidt*.
- **Protectors** — the protector's information rights flow from the
  deed and from the proper discharge of the protector's function.
- **Creditors** — must use ordinary civil-litigation routes.

## What the trustee should do when a request lands

A skeleton response procedure that follows the case law:

1. **Acknowledge promptly.** A trustee that delays without a reason
   risks costs consequences if the matter ends up in Court.
2. **Identify the requester's status.** Fixed-interest, discretionary,
   object of power, third party.
3. **Identify the documents requested.** Match each to the table
   above.
4. **Consider the requester's purpose.** Stated purpose is relevant
   to the balancing exercise — preparing tax returns, considering
   trust performance, or pursuing related litigation each pull the
   balance differently.
5. **Consider other beneficiaries.** What would each say about the
   proposed disclosure? Convening other beneficiaries in advance is
   sometimes wise.
6. **Decide a position.** Propose a package — typically: deed
   provided; accounts provided with appropriate redaction; letter
   of wishes withheld; minutes summarised.
7. **Communicate the position in writing**, with reasons that meet
   the *Schmidt* "good reason" standard for any refusal.
8. **If the dispute escalates**, apply to the Royal Court for
   **directions** under [Article 51](./article-51-directions.md). This
   transfers the contest into the supervisory framework, protects the
   trustee on costs (usually out of the fund), and forces the
   beneficiary to articulate the request.

## Interaction with the Data Protection (Jersey) Law 2018

The **Data Protection (Jersey) Law 2018** (DPJL) gives a Jersey-resident
individual the right to make a **subject access request (SAR)** for
their personal data held by any data controller, including a trustee.
Three points matter for the trustee:

- The DPJL gives an **additional** route, parallel to *Schmidt* — not
  a substitute.
- The DPJL contains exemptions covering third-party personal data,
  legal professional privilege, and confidentiality.
- The DPJL relates to **personal data of the requester**, not to
  trust documents in their entirety. A SAR can be answered without
  disclosing the full trust file; conversely, granting *Schmidt*
  disclosure of the deed does not exhaust the SAR.

A focused topic file on the interaction is planned at
[`../aml-cft/dpjl-and-trust-disclosure.md`](../../../jersey/aml-cft/dpjl-and-trust-disclosure.md)

## Interaction with the *Schmidt* "balancing" exercise where there is
related litigation

A standard pressure point is a beneficiary requesting trust
information while running **divorce proceedings**, **inheritance
litigation in another jurisdiction**, or **regulatory proceedings**
that the trust does not wish to be drawn into. The Royal Court will
take this into account in the balancing exercise but will not
automatically refuse — refusal must be justified on the merits of the
trust-administration interest, not as a tactic to shield assets from
legitimate process.

Where the foreign court has ordered the beneficiary to produce trust
documents, the trustee should consider:

- whether the order binds the beneficiary in personam (typically yes)
  vs whether it binds the trustee (typically no, where the trustee is
  not before the foreign court);
- whether the trustee should pre-emptively seek Royal Court
  directions under [Article 51](./article-51-directions.md) on its
  proper response;
- the impact on the Jersey **firewall** (Article 9 of the Trusts Law)
  — see [`firewall.md`](./firewall.md) *(stub)*.

## What this is **not** about

- *Schmidt* is **not** about the trustee's right to obtain
  information from beneficiaries (e.g., for CDD or AEoI purposes) —
  that is a separate regulatory matter under the
  [Money Laundering (Jersey) Order 2008](../aml-cft/money-laundering-order-2008.md)
  and CRS/FATCA regulations.
- *Schmidt* is **not** the avenue to obtain information from a
  **third party** holding trust assets (e.g., a bank) — those routes
  are *Norwich Pharmacal* applications and ordinary civil
  disclosure.

## Practical practice points

- **Letters of wishes** are the document trustees most often
  mis-handle. Do not provide them by default. If you propose to
  refuse, articulate why in *Schmidt* terms.
- **Third-party redaction**. Trust accounts often contain figures and
  identities of non-trust parties (e.g., the trust's bank, the
  underlying investment manager). Redact those before disclosing.
- **Acknowledge minor/unborn beneficiaries' interest.** Where
  disclosure to one branch of a family may prejudice another, an
  Article 51 application — with a guardian ad litem for minor
  beneficiaries — is the proper protective course.
- **Document your reasoning.** A trustee that refuses disclosure
  should have a contemporaneous minute setting out the reasoning. In
  later proceedings, the trustee's reasoning will itself be examined.
