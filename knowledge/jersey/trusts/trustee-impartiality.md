# Article 23 — Impartiality of Trustee

Article 23 of the **[Trusts (Jersey) Law 1984][tjl]** is the
**impartiality** duty. It requires a trustee with multiple
beneficiaries (or multiple purposes) to act impartially between
them, unless the trust terms say otherwise.

## What Article 23 says

Where a trust has more than one beneficiary, or more than one
purpose, the trustee:

- "**shall be impartial**"; and
- "**shall not execute the trust for the advantage of one at the
  expense of another**";

— except as **the trust terms otherwise provide**.

(See the consolidated text at [jerseylaw.je][tjl].)

## The two limbs of impartiality

The Article reaches two related but distinct duties:

### 1. Impartiality between beneficiaries of the same class

A trustee with multiple beneficiaries within a single class — e.g.,
all children of the settlor as discretionary objects — must not
systematically prefer one over another. This is **even-handedness
across the class**.

It does *not* require equal distributions: the trustee retains
discretion to favour individual circumstances. What it forbids is
*systematic bias* unjustified by the merits.

### 2. Impartiality between competing interests

A trustee with **competing interests** (typically life-tenants and
remaindermen, but also discretionary objects of different generations,
or charitable and non-charitable beneficiaries of a hybrid trust)
must not favour one interest at the expense of the other in
administration decisions.

The classic example: investment policy. An income-focused
investment policy advantages the life-tenant at the expense of the
capital-base for the remaindermen, and vice versa. The trustee must
strike a **balance** consistent with the deed and the beneficiaries'
respective interests.

## "Except as the trust terms otherwise provide"

The impartiality duty is **default-displaced** by the deed. Many
modern family trusts explicitly:

- **direct the trustee** to favour certain beneficiaries (e.g., "the
  settlor's spouse shall be the primary beneficiary during their
  lifetime");
- **prioritise classes** (e.g., "current income beneficiaries shall
  be preferred to remainder beneficiaries");
- **define purposes** that effectively re-direct the impartiality
  inquiry.

A deed-authorised preference is therefore legitimate; what the
Article forbids is a trustee's *own* unauthorised preference.

## Article 23 and Article 21

Article 23 sits on top of Article 21's broader duties. A breach of
impartiality is also a breach of [Article 21](./trustee-duties.md)
duties — typically a breach of the duty to administer the trust per
its terms (because the terms imply impartiality), or a breach of the
utmost-good-faith standard.

The remedy for breach is conventional: compensation to disadvantaged
beneficiaries, or unwinding of the impugned transaction, on
application under [Article 30](./breach-of-trust-liability.md)
 or [Article 51](./article-51-directions.md).

## Letter-of-wishes guidance

A common practical tension: the settlor's **letter of wishes** may
indicate preferences (e.g., "I would prefer my eldest son to receive
the family home"). The trustee:

- is **not legally bound** by the letter of wishes (which is
  non-binding by definition);
- **may take it into account** as evidence of the settlor's intent;
- **must still satisfy the Article 23 impartiality duty** in respect
  of beneficiaries the letter does not address;
- where the letter indicates a preference that the deed does not
  authorise as a binding direction, the trustee should treat the
  preference as evidence to be weighed in the trustee's own
  discretionary decision-making.

For a momentous decision in which the letter of wishes is material,
the trustee may seek the Royal Court's blessing under
[Article 51](./article-51-directions.md) — see the use-case at
[distribution-decisions-and-court-blessing.md](../use-cases/trust-officer/distribution-decisions-and-court-blessing.md).

## Pitfalls

- **Long-standing patterns of preference** without revisiting
  whether the pattern remains justified. The impartiality duty is
  ongoing; the trustee should periodically reconsider whether
  distributions are still in line with the deed and the beneficial
  interests as they now stand.
- **Family-business trusts** where the operating-business interests
  of one beneficiary class are pitted against the income needs of
  another. The deed should usually address this tension expressly;
  silence is litigation-prone.
- **Investment policy as impartiality issue.** An over-conservative
  or over-aggressive policy can be challenged as impartial-breach by
  the disadvantaged interest. Document the investment policy with
  reasoned reference to the trust's beneficial interests.
