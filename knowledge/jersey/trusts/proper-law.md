# Article 4 — Proper Law of a Trust

Article 4 of the **[Trusts (Jersey) Law 1984][tjl]** sets out how the
**proper law** of a trust is determined. The proper law is the system
of law that governs the trust — its validity, its construction, the
rights and duties of trustees and beneficiaries, and (subject to the
[firewall](./firewall.md)) the questions Article 9 catches.

## The Article 4 hierarchy

Article 4 identifies three routes by which the proper law is
established:

1. **Express choice.** The law named in the trust instrument as the
   proper law, where the instrument contains an express choice.
2. **Implied choice.** Where there is no express choice, the law
   that the terms of the trust impliedly select.
3. **Closest connection.** Where neither express nor implied choice
   is found, the law with which the trust has its closest connection.

For the "closest connection" inquiry, Article 4 lists relevant
connecting factors:

- the place of administration of the trust **designated by the
  settlor**;
- the **situs of the trust assets**;
- the **residence or place of business of the trustee**; and
- the **places where the objects of the trust are to be fulfilled**.

(See the consolidated text at [jerseylaw.je][tjl] for the exact
formulation.)

## Why express choice matters

A Jersey-drafted trust will almost always make an express choice of
Jersey law as the proper law. This:

- **Anchors recognition under Article 3** as a Jersey trust;
- **Engages the firewall under Article 9** (which protects only
  Jersey trusts);
- **Engages the settlor-reserved-powers protection under Article
  9A**; and
- **Ensures the Royal Court is the natural forum** for
  interpretation, blessing and breach-of-trust questions (subject to
  Article 5 jurisdictional reach and any jurisdiction clause).

A trust whose proper law is *not* Jersey, but which has a Jersey
trustee or Jersey-situated assets, can come before the Royal Court
under [Article 5](./court-jurisdiction-over-trusts.md), but the Court
will apply that *foreign* law to substantive questions of validity
and interpretation, applying Jersey procedural rules.

## Implied choice — when

A modern Jersey-drafted instrument will almost never need to rely on
the implied-choice limb. It tends to be relevant in:

- older instruments without an express choice (some 1970s and 1980s
  Channel-Islands family trusts);
- trusts created by conduct or by oral declaration (see
  [Article 7](./creation-of-a-trust.md)) where the conduct or oral
  terms imply Jersey law;
- arrangements where assets and parties are all unambiguously
  Jersey-connected (e.g., a "self-settled trust" by a Jersey
  resident over Jersey assets), in which case implied choice and
  closest connection point the same way.

## Change of proper law

A trust deed may include a power to change the proper law (express
recognition is in [Article 41](./change-of-proper-law.md)).
The mechanics — what consents are required, the timing of the
change, the effect on choice-of-law for prior acts — are typically
spelt out in the deed and are subject to general principles of
conflict of laws.

A change of proper law away from Jersey will typically end the
Article 9 firewall protection for that trust prospectively; the
Royal Court may also lose jurisdiction unless the deed retains a
Jersey forum or trustee.

## Pitfalls

- **"Governed by the laws of Jersey" is enough.** The form of words
  used in the trust instrument does not need to use the term "proper
  law"; any expression amounting to a choice of Jersey law is
  effective.
- **A foreign jurisdiction clause does not equal a foreign proper
  law.** *Crociani v Crociani* [2014] UKPC 40 is the leading
  authority — see the use-case sketch at
  [`use-cases/trust-officer/index.md`](../use-cases/trust-officer/index.md)
  under "forum and conflicts of law". A trust may have Jersey as its
  proper law and yet name another forum for disputes.
- **Proper law affects firewall scope.** A non-Jersey-proper-law
  trust does not engage the [firewall](./firewall.md). Where the
  client's planning depends on the firewall, the proper-law choice
  is dispositive — drafted accordingly.
