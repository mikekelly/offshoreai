# Article 22 — Duty of Co-Trustees to Act Together

Article 22 of the **[Trusts (Jersey) Law 1984][tjl]** is the **joint
action** rule for multi-trustee trusts. It establishes the
**unanimity default** for trustee decisions, with limited carve-outs.

## What Article 22 says

Where there is more than one trustee:

- "**all the trustees shall join** in performing the trust";
- "**no power or discretion** given to the trustees **shall be
  exercised** unless **all the trustees agree** on its exercise";
- the trust terms **may authorise majority decision-making**;
- a **dissenting trustee** may require the dissent to be **recorded
  in writing**.

(See the consolidated text at [jerseylaw.je][tjl].)

## Why unanimity is the default

Unanimity is the equity-court default because:

- it protects beneficiaries against ill-considered decisions —
  every trustee has a veto;
- it ensures every trustee is engaged in every decision and
  contributes their judgment;
- it strengthens the joint-and-several responsibility of trustees
  for the outcomes.

In practice, unanimity is also operationally onerous, which is why
many modern Jersey trust deeds depart from the default and provide
for majority decisions.

## Deed-authorised majority decisions

A trust deed can:

- provide for **simple majority** decisions;
- require a **special majority** (e.g., two-thirds, or a particular
  trustee's consent within the majority);
- distinguish **classes of decision** — e.g., unanimity for
  dispositive decisions, majority for administrative;
- require the **agreement of a named trustee** within the majority
  (often a Jersey-regulated professional trustee for substance
  reasons).

Where the deed provides for majority decisions, the dissenting
trustee retains the right to **record the dissent in writing**.
This is important practically:

- it documents the dissenter's position for the future;
- it engages the **outgoing-trustee protections** in
  [Article 34](./outgoing-trustee.md) should the dissenter
  resign over the decision;
- it limits the dissenter's personal liability for any consequent
  breach by the trust.

## Dissent and Article 51

A dissenting trustee in a deadlock — particularly where the deed
requires unanimity and the trustees cannot agree on a momentous
decision — may apply to the Royal Court for **directions** under
[Article 51](./article-51-directions.md). The category-3 surrender of
discretion (where the trustees surrender to the Court because they
are deadlocked) is the framework. The Court then takes the
substantive decision.

## Corporate trustees

A **corporate trustee** is a single legal person, even though
internally it acts through its board. Article 22's joint-action rule
applies to **separate trustees**, not to the internal governance of
a corporate trustee. The corporate trustee's internal decision-
making is governed by [Article 28](./corporate-trustee-resolution.md) —
typically a board resolution or properly delegated officer action.

## Co-trustees and delegation

Article 22's unanimity rule applies to the **exercise** of powers,
not to **administrative implementation**. A unanimous trustee
decision to (e.g.) sell a trust asset can be **implemented** by one
trustee acting alone, provided the delegation is consistent with
[Article 25](./trustee-delegation.md). The unanimous decision is the
gating step; implementation can be operationally streamlined.

## Pitfalls

- **Implicit unanimity assumption in routine matters.** A trust that
  routinely "rubber-stamps" decisions without engaging all trustees
  is operating in breach of Article 22; the rubber-stamped decisions
  may be voidable.
- **Default unanimity in PTC contexts.** A PTC with a multi-director
  board has internal majority governance; but a co-trustee structure
  with the PTC and a natural-person co-trustee is subject to
  Article 22 unanimity at the trustee level even if the PTC's board
  uses majority internally.
- **Documenting the dissent.** A trustee who dissents but does not
  ensure the dissent is recorded loses the protective effect of
  Article 22's dissent-recording provision.
- **Deadlock without resolution mechanism.** A deed providing for
  unanimity without a deadlock-resolution mechanism (e.g., independent
  trustee casting vote, protector decision, Court application) is
  fragile — every standoff requires an [Article 51](./article-51-directions.md)
  application.
