# Article 8 — Property Which May Be Placed in a Trust

Article 8 of the **[Trusts (Jersey) Law 1984][tjl]** is a permissive
rule: it confirms that **any property** may be held by or vested in a
trustee upon trust, and that trustees may **accept additional
property** from any person to add to existing trust assets.

(See the consolidated text at [jerseylaw.je][tjl].)

## What "any property" covers

The breadth is deliberate. Trust property may include:

- **Cash and bank balances** in any currency;
- **Shares and securities** of any company, wherever incorporated;
- **Real property** — including Jersey realty, foreign realty, and
  beneficial interests in foreign-titled real estate (subject to the
  local-law rules on what can be held in trust);
- **Personal property** — yachts, aircraft, artworks, collectibles;
- **Choses in action** — including life-insurance policies,
  intellectual property rights, and contractual rights;
- **Beneficial interests under other trusts** ("interests under
  trust") — a common pattern in multi-tier family structures;
- **Cryptoassets and tokenised assets** — Jersey takes the practical
  view that these are property capable of being trust property,
  though the proper analysis depends on the nature of the asset and
  the deed terms.

## Additional property — additions to existing trusts

The permission to "accept additional property from any person to add
to existing trust assets" is the legal basis for the standard family-
trust practice of progressive funding:

- the trust is **initially settled** with a nominal sum (e.g., £10)
  by the named settlor;
- **subsequent transfers** are made into the trust as the family's
  estate-planning programme rolls out — typically by gifts from
  the settlor or other family members.

Where a non-original-settlor contributes property, that person may
become a "settlor" for the purposes of the
[Article 1](./interpretation-and-definitions.md) definition (the
definition is wide: "a person who provides trust property"). The
practical consequence is in foreign tax analyses (e.g., UK or US
"grantor" analyses) where the identity of contributors matters more
than in pure Jersey law.

## What Article 8 does not do

- It does **not** override foreign-law rules on what may be vested
  in a trustee. Some civilian jurisdictions do not recognise the
  trust at all; placing local real estate in a Jersey trust may not
  achieve effective transfer of the local property without parallel
  local-law steps (e.g., interposing a holding company).
- It does **not** override Article 9 firewall analysis on settlor
  *capacity*. A settlor who lacked capacity under their personal
  law (e.g., a minor under foreign domicile rules) is captured by
  Article 9 rules on capacity — see [`firewall.md`](./firewall.md).
- It does **not** answer the question of legitimate provenance. The
  trustee's AML/CFT obligations require source-of-funds and
  source-of-wealth analysis on each contribution to the trust;
  Article 8 permission is necessary but not sufficient.
