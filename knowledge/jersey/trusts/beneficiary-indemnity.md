# Article 46 — Court Power to Impound Beneficiary's Interest for Breach

Article 46 of the **[Trusts (Jersey) Law 1984][tjl]** is the
**beneficiary-instigation** rule. Where a trustee commits a breach
of trust **at the instigation or request or with the consent of a
beneficiary**, the Royal Court may **impound that beneficiary's
interest** to indemnify the trustee.

## What Article 46 says

Where a trustee commits a breach of trust:

- **at the instigation** of a beneficiary;
- **at the request** of a beneficiary; or
- **with the consent** of a beneficiary,

the Royal Court may **impound** all or part of that beneficiary's
interest as **indemnity** to the trustee.

(See the consolidated text at [jerseylaw.je][tjl].)

## Why this rule exists

The rule reflects the **equitable principle** that a beneficiary who
procured or consented to a breach should not be permitted to recover
against the trustee for losses the beneficiary themselves caused.

Article 46 codifies the equity rule in *Re Pauling's Settlement
Trusts* [1964] Ch 303 and earlier authorities: the trustee's
personal liability to a beneficiary is reduced to the extent of the
beneficiary's complicity.

## The three triggers — instigation, request, consent

The triggers form a spectrum of beneficiary involvement:

### Instigation

The beneficiary **initiated** the breach — e.g., pressured the
trustee to make an unauthorised distribution, or proposed an
imprudent investment that the trustee adopted.

### Request

The beneficiary **asked** for the breach — short of instigation but
more than mere consent. A beneficiary's repeated request for a
distribution that the trustee should have refused is a typical
example.

### Consent

The beneficiary **agreed** to the breach when notified. The consent
must be **informed** — the beneficiary must have understood that
the act was a breach (or at least that there was a serious risk it
was). Mere passive acceptance without understanding is not enough.

## How impounding works

The Court may **impound** the beneficiary's interest:

- in **whole** or in **part**;
- as **indemnity** to the trustee — i.e., the impounded interest is
  applied against the trustee's liability;
- with **flexibility** on timing — the impounding may apply to the
  beneficiary's current entitlement, future entitlement, or both.

The Court's power is **discretionary** and tailored to the facts.
A beneficiary who instigated a major breach may have their entire
interest impounded; a beneficiary who merely consented to a minor
breach may have only a proportionate part impounded.

## Practical limits

- **Only the complicit beneficiary's interest** can be impounded.
  Other beneficiaries who were not party to the breach retain their
  full claims against the trustee.
- **The trustee's liability does not disappear** — it is reduced to
  the extent of the impounding. The trustee may still face claims
  from non-complicit beneficiaries.
- **Capacity-and-knowledge** matters — a minor or otherwise
  incapacitated beneficiary cannot validly instigate, request or
  consent in a way that engages Article 46.

## Interaction with beneficiary release under Article 30

[Article 30](./breach-of-trust-liability.md) provides that
beneficiaries may **release** the trustee from liability if they
have capacity and full knowledge. Article 46 deals with a different
scenario:

- **Article 30 release** — a clean release by all-beneficiary
  consent. The trustee is **fully released** as against the
  releasing beneficiaries.
- **Article 46 impounding** — the trustee's liability remains but is
  **set off** against the complicit beneficiary's interest. The
  trustee may still face claims from other beneficiaries.

## Pitfalls

- **Documenting beneficiary involvement.** A trustee that wants to
  invoke Article 46 needs **contemporaneous evidence** of the
  beneficiary's instigation, request, or consent — written
  correspondence, signed acknowledgements, recorded conversations.
  A claim based on the trustee's later recollection is weak.
- **Beneficiary capacity questions.** Where the beneficiary's
  capacity to consent is doubtful (mental capacity, age, undue
  influence), Article 46 protection is uncertain. Engage carefully
  in such cases.
- **Non-complicit beneficiaries' claims survive.** Don't assume
  Article 46 closes off the trustee's full exposure — only the
  complicit beneficiary's interest is impounded.
