# Article 41 — Power to Provide for Change of Proper Law

Article 41 of the **[Trusts (Jersey) Law 1984][tjl]** expressly
recognises that a trust deed may authorise **changing the proper law**
of the trust from Jersey law to the law of another jurisdiction (or
vice versa, from a foreign proper law to Jersey law).

## What Article 41 says

The trust terms may authorise changing the trust's governing law
"**from the law of Jersey to the law of another jurisdiction**".

(See the consolidated text at [jerseylaw.je][tjl].)

## Why this matters

A change of proper law — sometimes called **"redomiciliation"** in
trust contexts — is a significant transformation of the trust. It
changes:

- which jurisdiction's **substantive law** governs the trust's
  validity, interpretation, and trustee duties;
- the **forum** that will most naturally hear trust matters;
- the application of the **firewall** under
  [Article 9](./firewall.md) (which protects only Jersey trusts).

Article 41 confirms that the deed can authorise such a change; the
mechanics depend on the deed's terms.

## Common change-of-proper-law scenarios

- **Jersey-to-Cayman or Jersey-to-BVI** redomiciliation — typically
  driven by family-office relocation, regulatory considerations, or
  cost arbitrage between offshore trust regimes.
- **Foreign-to-Jersey** redomiciliation — to engage the Article 9
  firewall, the Article 9A reserved-powers regime, or to bring the
  trust under Royal Court supervision.
- **Within-CD** redomiciliation between Jersey and Guernsey — usually
  for family or operational reasons.

## What changes on a proper-law change

A change of proper law is **prospective** in effect, but the
implications are wide:

| Subject | Before | After |
|---|---|---|
| Validity questions for new acts | Jersey law | New proper law |
| Trustee duties | Jersey law (including Article 21) | New proper law's trustee duties |
| Firewall protection | Article 9 of Jersey law | Equivalent provision (if any) in new jurisdiction |
| Forum | Royal Court has Article 5 jurisdiction | Foreign court has jurisdiction under foreign rules |
| Reserved-powers regime | Article 9A | New jurisdiction's equivalent |
| Court directions jurisdiction | Article 51 | New jurisdiction's directions jurisdiction |

The **historical acts** of the trustees up to the change date remain
governed by the old proper law. A breach of trust committed under
Jersey law remains a Jersey-law breach claim, even after the proper
law has changed.

## Mechanics of change

The deed will normally specify:

- **who exercises** the power (settlor during lifetime, trustee,
  protector, or by collective beneficiary consent);
- **the form** — typically a written deed of variation;
- **any consents** required (trustee consent is normal, as the
  change affects the trustee's regulatory and operational
  framework);
- **the effective date** — usually the date of the deed, sometimes
  with a delay to allow for operational transition;
- whether **the trustee changes** at the same time (often: a
  Jersey-resident trustee is replaced with a trustee resident in the
  new jurisdiction).

## Operational transitions

Beyond the legal change, a change of proper law typically involves:

- **trustee change** to a trustee in the new jurisdiction (and
  surrender of the Jersey trustee under
  [Article 19](./trustee-resignation-and-removal.md));
- **vesting** of trust property in the new trustee — automatic for
  Jersey-situated property under Article 17 mechanics, but local-law
  transfer is needed for foreign property;
- **bank, custodian, and counterparty updates** — re-titling
  accounts, updating mandates;
- **regulatory notifications** in the old and new jurisdictions;
- **AML/CFT file transfer** under the receiving jurisdiction's
  rules.

## Tax consequences

A change of proper law may itself be a **tax event** in some
jurisdictions:

- **UK** — the change can trigger an inheritance-tax exit charge
  for a UK relevant-property trust (treated as the trust property
  ceasing to be subject to the relevant-property regime if the new
  jurisdiction's rules differ);
- **US** — limited US-tax consequences from a change of governing
  law alone, but accompanied trustee changes can affect grantor-
  trust status and reporting;
- **OECD Pillar Two** — for trusts holding shares in in-scope
  multinational groups, a change of governing law may affect
  characterisation.

Model tax consequences before exercising.

## Loss of the Article 9 firewall

The most significant Jersey-law consequence of a change away from
Jersey proper law is **loss of the Article 9 firewall** for the
trust going forward. The firewall protects:

- against foreign-law forced-heirship and matrimonial claims;
- against foreign judgments inconsistent with Article 9.

After a change to a foreign proper law, the **new jurisdiction's
firewall** (if any) applies — and the Jersey-law protection drops
away. Where firewall protection is the core of the planning rationale,
a change of proper law away from Jersey should be undertaken with
care.

## Pitfalls

- **Inadequate succession planning.** A change of proper law that
  does not contemplate the trustee change leaves operational gaps.
- **Loss of firewall without replacement.** Moving from Jersey to a
  jurisdiction without a comparable firewall provision can be a
  regression in protection.
- **Tax-trigger changes.** Cross-border tax consequences are
  often overlooked. The change can produce a charge that the
  planning was supposed to avoid.
- **Old-trustee continuing liability.** A Jersey trustee that
  resigns on the change of proper law remains liable for breaches
  during its tenure — see [`outgoing-trustee.md`](./outgoing-trustee.md).
  Properly secured indemnity and Article 43A security are essential.
