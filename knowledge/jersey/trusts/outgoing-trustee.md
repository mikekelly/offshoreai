# Article 34 — Position of Outgoing Trustee

Article 34 of the **[Trusts (Jersey) Law 1984][tjl]** sets out the
**legal position of a trustee who has left office** — by resignation,
removal, or other termination of trusteeship. It deals with the
trustee's continuing obligations, its release from future claims, and
its right to security.

## What Article 34 says

On resignation or removal:

- the outgoing trustee must **surrender trust property** in its
  possession to the continuing or successor trustees;
- the outgoing trustee may exercise the **security right** under
  [Article 43A](./trustee-security.md) — i.e., require reasonable
  security for liabilities before surrender;
- the outgoing trustee gains **release from most beneficiary
  liability**, with **two principal exceptions**:
  - **breaches the outgoing trustee participated in**;
  - **actions to recover trust property** the trustee possessed or
    converted to personal use.

(See the consolidated text at [jerseylaw.je][tjl].)

## Surrender of property

The first duty: the outgoing trustee must **hand over** the trust
property to the continuing or successor trustees. The mechanics:

- **Jersey-situated trust property** vests automatically in the
  successor trustees by operation of
  [Article 17](./trustee-appointment-out-of-court.md);
- **foreign-situated property** requires positive transfer under the
  foreign jurisdiction's rules (e.g., a separate transfer deed for
  foreign real estate, a stock-transfer form for foreign-registered
  shares);
- **bank accounts and custody accounts** are typically re-titled by
  the bank or custodian on receipt of resolution and authorised
  signatures;
- **documents and records** — the outgoing trustee should hand over
  the deed, amendments, minutes, accounts, and AML/CFT records;
  retain copies for the limitation period.

## Security right under Article 43A

The outgoing trustee can require **reasonable security** for
continuing exposure before surrender. The exposure includes:

- liability for breaches committed during trusteeship;
- contractual liability under contracts entered as trustee that
  remain in force (subject to Article 32 protections);
- regulatory and tax exposures arising from the trusteeship
  period.

The security typically takes the form of:

- a **specific indemnity** from the trust fund (often with reservation
  of a fund-side lien);
- an **indemnity from beneficiaries** (where appropriate);
- a **retention of assets** by the outgoing trustee equal to the
  reasonable estimate of continuing exposure;
- in heavy-exposure cases, **insurance** maintained for a period
  post-exit.

See [`trustee-security.md`](./trustee-security.md).

## Release from future liability

The Article gives the outgoing trustee a **release from future
beneficiary claims**, **except**:

### 1. Breaches the trustee participated in

A trustee who participated in a breach during trusteeship remains
liable for it after exit. The release does not wash away past
breaches — it merely confirms the trustee's exit position for
matters not implicating the trustee.

### 2. Recovery of trust property

If the outgoing trustee **possessed or converted** trust property
beyond what the trustee was entitled to, the beneficiaries retain a
right to recover that property. A trustee who pays itself excessive
fees, retains property after exit, or otherwise holds value not
properly released cannot rely on the Article 34 release.

## The Article 34 release vs an express deed of release

The Article 34 release is **automatic** by operation of law on exit.
In practice, retiring trustees usually **also** obtain an **express
deed of release and indemnity** from:

- the continuing or successor trustees;
- the beneficiaries (those of full age and capacity).

The express deed:

- documents the **scope** of the release (often broader than the
  Article 34 default);
- documents the **scope** of the indemnity (typically against any
  liability arising from trusteeship that the outgoing trustee did
  not cause through fraud, wilful misconduct, or gross negligence);
- provides **evidence** of the parties' positions for the future.

## Documents and records — retention

The outgoing trustee should retain copies of:

- the **deed and all amendments**;
- **trustee minutes** and supporting papers for decisions taken
  during trusteeship;
- **accounts** and supporting documentation;
- **AML/CFT records** for the statutory retention period (typically
  5 years post-end of relationship under the Money Laundering Order
  2008);
- **correspondence** with beneficiaries, settlors, protectors,
  service providers and advisers.

Retention is for the trustee's defence against future claims and is
not waived by the Article 34 release.

## Limitation

A claim against an outgoing trustee for a breach is subject to the
Article 57 limitation regime — see
[`limitation-and-prescription.md`](./limitation-and-prescription.md)
 The limitation period typically starts from the date of
the breach (or from discovery for concealed breaches), not from the
date of trustee exit.

## Pitfalls

- **Premature release.** An outgoing trustee that releases trust
  property without first securing reasonable indemnity may be
  exposed for continuing liabilities with no fund-side recourse.
- **Inadequate record retention.** A trustee that loses records
  cannot defend itself against future claims; the Article 34
  release is not an evidential substitute.
- **Foreign-property transfer gaps.** Trust property that requires
  local-law transfer steps in a foreign jurisdiction may remain
  formally titled in the outgoing trustee's name even after exit —
  exposing the trustee on foreign-jurisdiction matters until the
  title is properly transferred.
- **Beneficiary release scope.** A beneficiary release covers what
  the beneficiary releases; unborn or unascertained beneficiaries
  cannot release. A clean release is rarely available for trusts
  with such beneficiaries; the prudent trustee then relies on a
  combination of fund-indemnity and continuing security under
  Article 43A.
