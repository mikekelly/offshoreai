# Trusts — Jersey

> **Comparing Jersey to other trust regimes?**
> [Jersey trust vs Cayman STAR vs BVI VISTA — when to use which](./wormhole-trust-comparison-jersey-star-vista.md)
> distils the cross-border vehicle choice (Article 9 firewall + 9A reserved
> powers vs STAR purpose trusts vs VISTA share-ownership) into one node.

## What this section is about

This section covers **the Jersey-law trust** — what it is, how it is
created, who can be a trustee, what duties trustees owe, when the
Royal Court will intervene, and how a Jersey-law trust interacts
with foreign legal regimes (forced-heirship rules, matrimonial
property, foreign court orders).

A **trust** in this corpus means a relationship in which one person
(the **trustee**) holds property not for their own benefit but for
the benefit of others (the **beneficiaries**) or for a stated
purpose. A **Jersey trust** is one whose **proper law** — the system
of law that governs its validity, interpretation, and the rights and
duties of the parties — is the law of Jersey.

The governing statute is the **[Trusts (Jersey) Law 1984][tjl]**,
amended several times. The most important amendments were:

- **2006** — the Article 9A reserved-powers provision and the
  abolition of the rule against perpetuities for new trusts;
- **2013** — the Articles 47B–H statutory mistake regime.

The Trusts Law is **expressly not a codification**
([Article 1](./interpretation-and-definitions.md)) — pre-existing
equitable doctrine, Royal Court case law, and customary-law concepts
fill in where the statute is silent. The **Privy Council** — sitting
as the final court of appeal from Jersey — has produced several
trust-law decisions that are treated as binding in Jersey
(*Schmidt v Rosewood*, *Crociani v Crociani*).

## Conceptual map — themes in Jersey trust law

The Trusts Law's 60+ Articles cluster into a small number of
recurring themes. Group your reading by theme rather than by Article
number.

### Theme 1 — Existence, validity, recognition

When does a Jersey trust exist? When is it valid? Will Jersey
recognise it? These foundational questions are answered by
**Articles 2, 3, 7, 8, 10, 10A, 11**.

- [`existence-of-a-trust.md`](./existence-of-a-trust.md) — the
  Article 2 existence test: separation of legal and beneficial
  ownership.
- [`recognition-of-a-trust.md`](./recognition-of-a-trust.md) —
  Article 3 default recognition rule.
- [`creation-of-a-trust.md`](./creation-of-a-trust.md) — Article 7:
  oral, written, by conduct (unit trusts excepted).
- [`property-in-a-trust.md`](./property-in-a-trust.md) — Article 8:
  what property can be settled.
- [`beneficiaries.md`](./beneficiaries.md) — Article 10: certainty
  of objects; classes; conditions.
- [`disclaimer-of-interest.md`](./disclaimer-of-interest.md) —
  Article 10A: a beneficiary's right to disclaim.
- [`validity-of-a-trust.md`](./validity-of-a-trust.md) — Article 11:
  vitiating factors (illegality, duress, fraud, impossibility).

### Theme 2 — The Jersey firewall and cross-border protection

The most distinctive feature of Jersey trust law. Foreign forced-
heirship rules, matrimonial-property regimes, and foreign judgments
that would defeat the trust are disapplied by Jersey courts.

- **[`firewall.md`](./firewall.md)** — Article 9, the firewall.
  Validity, interpretation, settlor capacity, administration,
  trustee powers, and beneficial rights are all determined by
  Jersey law; no foreign rule defeats this; foreign judgments
  inconsistent with Article 9 are unenforceable in Jersey.
- [`proper-law.md`](./proper-law.md) — Article 4: when a trust is a
  Jersey trust at all (the gateway to the firewall).
- [`change-of-proper-law.md`](./change-of-proper-law.md) —
  Article 41: changing proper law in or out of Jersey, with the
  firewall consequences.

### Theme 3 — Settlor control via reserved powers

Settlors often want to retain control while still having a valid
trust. Article 9A enumerates a wide menu of powers the settlor may
reserve; **reservation does not by itself make the settlor a
trustee** and does not invalidate the trust.

- **[`reserved-powers.md`](./reserved-powers.md)** — Article 9A.
- [`power-of-revocation.md`](./power-of-revocation.md) — Article 40:
  the consequences of a reserved revocation power being exercised.

### Theme 4 — Purpose trusts (non-charitable)

Jersey permits a trust to exist **without beneficiaries** — for a
non-charitable purpose, with an enforcer to police it. Used heavily
in commercial "orphan" structures and family-governance vehicles.

- **[`purpose-trusts.md`](./purpose-trusts.md)** — Article 12.
- [`enforcers.md`](./enforcers.md) — Article 13: the mandatory enforcer.
- [`enforcer-resignation-removal.md`](./enforcer-resignation-removal.md)
  — Article 14: enforcer lifecycle.
- [`court-purpose-trust-redirection.md`](./court-purpose-trust-redirection.md)
  — Article 47A: cy-près-style redirection on failure.

### Theme 5 — Trusteeship — appointment, retirement, removal

Mechanics of being a trustee — taking the office, leaving it,
co-existing with co-trustees, being replaced.

- [`number-of-trustees.md`](./number-of-trustees.md) — Article 16.
- [`trustee-appointment-out-of-court.md`](./trustee-appointment-out-of-court.md)
  — Article 17.
- [`prohibition-of-renunciation.md`](./prohibition-of-renunciation.md)
  — Article 18: acceptance is committal.
- [`trustee-resignation-and-removal.md`](./trustee-resignation-and-removal.md)
  — Article 19.
- [`court-appointed-resident-trustee.md`](./court-appointed-resident-trustee.md)
  — Article 44: Royal Court can install a Jersey-resident trustee on

### Theme 6 — Trustee duties

The statutory core of what a trustee must do. Article 21 is the
default standard; later Articles refine specific duty areas.

- **[`trustee-duties.md`](./trustee-duties.md)** — Article 21: prudent
  person, utmost good faith, no profit, accounts, segregation.
- [`co-trustees-acting-together.md`](./co-trustees-acting-together.md)
  — Article 22: unanimity default for co-trustees.
- [`trustee-impartiality.md`](./trustee-impartiality.md) — Article 23.

### Theme 7 — Trustee powers and operational tools

What the trustee can do under default law.

- [`trustee-powers.md`](./trustee-powers.md) — Article 24: powers of a

- [`trustee-delegation.md`](./trustee-delegation.md) — Article 25:
  delegation to professional agents.
- [`trustee-remuneration.md`](./trustee-remuneration.md) — Article 26.
- [`power-to-appropriate.md`](./power-to-appropriate.md) — Article 27:

- [`corporate-trustee-resolution.md`](./corporate-trustee-resolution.md)

### Theme 8 — Information and disclosure

When and how the trustee must give information to beneficiaries and
others. Two doctrines run in parallel: the statutory Article 29
regime and the *Schmidt v Rosewood* supervisory jurisdiction.

- **[`beneficiary-rights.md`](./beneficiary-rights.md)** —
  *Schmidt v Rosewood* and the Jersey presumptions for/against
  disclosure by document type.
- [`article-29-disclosure.md`](./article-29-disclosure.md) —
  Article 29: statutory disclosure; what the trustee need not
  disclose (deliberations, reasoning, proposed exercises).

### Theme 9 — Breach of trust and trustee liability

When the trustee falls short, what happens.

- **[`breach-of-trust-liability.md`](./breach-of-trust-liability.md)**
  — Article 30: compensation, no offset, irreducible core (fraud /
  wilful misconduct / gross negligence cannot be excluded by deed).
- [`relief-from-personal-liability.md`](./relief-from-personal-liability.md)
  — Article 45: Court discretion to relieve honest, reasonable
  trustees.
- [`beneficiary-indemnity.md`](./beneficiary-indemnity.md) — Article 46:
  impounding the complicit beneficiary's interest.
- [`constructive-trustee.md`](./constructive-trustee.md) — Article 33:
  knowing receipt and dishonest assistance.
- [`outgoing-trustee.md`](./outgoing-trustee.md) — Article 34:
  position after exit.
- [`limitation-and-prescription.md`](./limitation-and-prescription.md)
  — Article 57: the distinctive **3-year / 21-year** Jersey
  regime, with no limitation for fraud.

### Theme 10 — Mistake and set-aside (modern jurisdiction)

The 2013-introduced statutory mistake regime — a vehicle for undoing
distributions, appointments and transfers that were made under
qualifying mistake (commonly an unforeseen tax consequence).

- **[`article-47-set-aside.md`](./article-47-set-aside.md)** —
  Articles 47B–47H, including the *S & T Trusts* three-limb test
  and Jersey's divergence from English *Pitt v Holt*.

### Theme 11 — Royal Court supervision

The Royal Court has wide supervisory jurisdiction over trusts. Most
trust applications are "friendly" proceedings, not hostile
litigation.

- **[`article-51-directions.md`](./article-51-directions.md)** —
  the directions jurisdiction; the four *Public Trustee v Cooper*
  categories; the *Re S Settlement* three-limb test for blessing
  momentous decisions; Beddoe procedure.
- [`court-jurisdiction-over-trusts.md`](./court-jurisdiction-over-trusts.md)
  — Article 5: when the Court has jurisdiction over a trust at all.
- [`court-variation.md`](./court-variation.md) — Article 47: variation
  on behalf of minors / unborns (distinct from the mistake regime).
- [`payment-of-costs.md`](./payment-of-costs.md) — Article 53: costs
  out of the trust fund in friendly proceedings.

### Theme 12 — Termination and failure

How a trust ends.

- [`duration.md`](./duration.md) — Article 15: perpetual duration
  permitted (since 2006).
- [`termination-of-trust.md`](./termination-of-trust.md) — Article 43:
  the Jersey *Saunders v Vautier* analogue.
- [`failure-or-lapse.md`](./failure-or-lapse.md) — Article 42:
  resulting trust to settlor on failure.
- [`trustee-security.md`](./trustee-security.md) — Article 43A:
  outgoing trustee's right to security.

### Theme 13 — Foreign trusts in Jersey

Trusts whose proper law is not Jersey, when they come before the

- [`foreign-trust-enforceability.md`](./foreign-trust-enforceability.md)
