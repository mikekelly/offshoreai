# Article 35 — Spendthrift or Protective Trust

Article 35 of the **[Trusts (Jersey) Law 1984][tjl]** is the
**spendthrift / protective trust** provision. It permits a trust to
include terms that make a beneficiary's interest **liable to
termination** or **subject to diminution** on specified events —
typically the beneficiary's bankruptcy or attempted alienation of the
interest.

## What Article 35 says

The terms of a trust may:

- make a beneficiary's interest **liable to termination**;
- **restrict alienation** of the interest by the beneficiary;
- subject the interest to **diminution** if the beneficiary becomes
  **bankrupt**.

(See the consolidated text at [jerseylaw.je][tjl].)

## What a "protective" or "spendthrift" trust looks like

The classic protective-trust structure:

- the beneficiary has a **defined interest** (often a life interest
  in income, or a discretionary entitlement);
- the interest **automatically terminates** on a trigger event —
  e.g., the beneficiary's bankruptcy, attempted assignment of the
  interest, or attempt by creditors to attach the interest;
- on termination, the interest **converts** into a **discretionary
  trust** for a class including the beneficiary, the beneficiary's
  family, and other named persons;
- the trustee then exercises discretion to make distributions —
  typically continuing to support the beneficiary, but without the
  beneficiary having a "vested" interest that creditors can reach.

The mechanism dates back to English equity (the *Re Brandon's
Trusts* line and the modern English statutory equivalent at section
33 of the Trustee Act 1925). Jersey codifies the substance at
Article 35.

## Why use a protective trust

The principal use cases:

1. **Asset protection from beneficiary's creditors.** A beneficiary
   with creditor exposure (entrepreneur, professional in
   high-liability fields, family member with personal financial
   difficulties) holds an interest that is invisible to creditors
   in any substantive way — creditors cannot attach a discretionary
   entitlement that does not exist until the trustee exercises
   discretion.
2. **Asset protection from divorce.** Some jurisdictions treat a
   spouse's interest in a trust as matrimonial property in
   divorce. A protective structure may reduce that exposure — but
   foreign divorce courts have considerable freedom and the
   protection is not absolute. See
   [`firewall.md`](./firewall.md) on Article 9.
3. **Protection from imprudent spending.** A "spendthrift" structure
   limits the beneficiary's ability to dissipate the interest in
   advance — the beneficiary cannot assign or pledge the future
   entitlement.
4. **Inheritance planning.** A protective interest can preserve the
   capital for next-generation beneficiaries while supporting the
   current generation.

## Restrictions on alienation

The Article expressly permits **restrictions on alienation**:

- the beneficiary's interest **cannot be assigned** ahead of
  vesting;
- the beneficiary's interest **cannot be pledged** as security;
- attempted alienation triggers termination of the interest under
  the spendthrift mechanism.

The Royal Court will give effect to these restrictions consistent
with the deed.

## Bankruptcy

Where the beneficiary becomes **bankrupt**, the Article permits the
interest to be **diminished or terminated**. The practical effect:

- the trust property does not pass to the bankruptcy estate (because
  the beneficiary's interest has terminated);
- the trustee continues to administer the trust;
- distributions to the bankrupt beneficiary are within the trustee's
  discretion under the protective-class structure, subject to the
  bankruptcy court's reach over received-in-bankruptcy property.

The cross-border position is complex: a foreign bankruptcy court may
not respect the Jersey trust's protective mechanism, particularly
where the beneficiary's assets in the foreign jurisdiction include
distributions received from the trust. The firewall under
[Article 9](./firewall.md) protects the trust against the foreign-
judgment-enforcement route in Jersey, but distributions made into
the foreign jurisdiction are at the trustee's risk.

## Foreign rules attacking spendthrift provisions

Some jurisdictions have anti-spendthrift rules that void terms
attempting to put assets beyond creditor reach (the US has variable
state-by-state rules, with some states accepting and some refusing
spendthrift clauses). The Jersey trust's spendthrift provisions are
respected by Jersey law and the Royal Court; whether a foreign court
will respect them is a separate question that turns on the foreign
jurisdiction's rules.

## Pitfalls

- **Cliff-edge termination.** A spendthrift trigger that terminates
  the interest on (e.g.) "any creditor claim" may be over-broad and
  may catch routine credit checks. Drafting should be careful.
- **Foreign creditor reach.** The protection is strongest within
  Jersey. Distributions received by a beneficiary in a foreign
  jurisdiction are exposed to foreign creditor reach.
- **Family-business interests.** Where the beneficiary has a
  meaningful role in operating a family business held by the trust,
  the protective structure may be incompatible with operational
  reality. Consider whether the protection-of-interest goal is best
  served by spendthrift terms or by holding the operational role
  outside the trust.
