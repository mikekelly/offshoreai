# Article 54 — Trustee's Estate and Insolvency

Article 54 of the **[Trusts (Jersey) Law 1984][tjl]** is the
**ring-fencing** provision. It confirms that trust property is
**not part of the trustee's own estate** for insolvency or general
creditor purposes — the trustee's interest is limited to what is
needed to perform the trust.

## What Article 54 says

A trustee's "**interest in the trust property is limited to that
which is necessary for the proper performance of the trust**" and
the trust property does **not form part of the trustee's** assets,
**except** regarding beneficiary interests.

(See the consolidated text at [jerseylaw.je][tjl].)

## Why this matters

A trustee — natural person or corporate — has its own balance sheet:
its own assets, its own creditors, its own potential for insolvency.
Article 54 ensures that:

- the **trust property is not available** to the trustee's general
  creditors on insolvency;
- the **beneficiaries' interests are preserved** through the
  trustee's insolvency;
- a **bankruptcy of the trustee** does not bankrupt the trust.

This is the **structural foundation** of trust insolvency-remoteness
in Jersey. Combined with [Article 32](./trustee-third-party-liability.md)
(third-party liability limited to trust property when capacity is
known), Article 54 produces the operational segregation that makes
the trust workable as a wealth vehicle.

## Operational implications

### Trustee insolvency

If a Jersey trustee (whether a TCB, a PTC, or a natural person)
becomes insolvent:

- the **trustee's general assets** are subject to bankruptcy /
  *désastre* proceedings;
- the **trust property** is **not part** of those proceedings — it
  remains held on trust;
- the trustee's office may be **terminated** under the deed (typical
  deeds include automatic termination on trustee insolvency);
- a **successor trustee** is appointed to take over the trust
  property and continue administration.

### Trustee's own creditors

A general creditor of the trustee — e.g., a bank lending to the
trustee in the trustee's personal capacity — has no claim against
the trust property under Article 54. The creditor's claim is
against the trustee's own balance sheet.

A creditor whose claim **arises out of the trust** (e.g., a
counterparty to a trust-related contract) has the protections of
[Article 32](./trustee-third-party-liability.md) and the trustee's
right of indemnity, but the trust property is still protected from
the trustee's *general* creditors.

### Beneficiary claims

The exception "**except regarding beneficiary interests**" preserves
the obvious: beneficiaries' equitable interests in the trust
property are precisely what the trust is for. Article 54's
ring-fencing does not insulate the trust from the beneficiaries'
own claims — the beneficiaries are the beneficial owners (in equity)
of the trust property.

## "Necessary for proper performance"

The Article's limitation of the trustee's interest to "what is
necessary for proper performance" includes:

- **legal title** to trust property;
- **operational authority** to deal with the property;
- **right of indemnity** from the trust fund for properly-incurred
  liabilities;
- **right to remuneration** under
  [Article 26](./trustee-remuneration.md);

but **excludes**:

- any **proprietary benefit** beyond the indemnity and remuneration;
- any **right of use** of trust property except as trustee;
- any **right against the trust** for claims unrelated to
  trusteeship.

## Cross-border insolvency

Where a Jersey trustee is **foreign-incorporated** (or a Jersey
trustee has foreign-located property), cross-border insolvency rules
apply:

- the **insolvency court** in the trustee's home jurisdiction
  handles the trustee's own insolvency;
- the **Jersey court** retains jurisdiction over the trust property
  under [Article 5](./court-jurisdiction-over-trusts.md);
- the **trust's interests** are typically recognised by foreign
  insolvency courts as proprietary interests of the beneficiaries,
  not of the trustee — i.e., the trust property is not in the
  trustee's bankruptcy estate.

The principle of trust-property segregation is well-established
internationally; Article 54 codifies the Jersey position.

## Pitfalls

- **Commingling of trust and personal property.** A trustee that
  fails to maintain segregation (in breach of
  [Article 21](./trustee-duties.md) and Article 54's implicit
  requirements) creates evidential difficulties — creditors may
  attack the segregation. The remedy is rigorous
  account-segregation in practice.
- **Bank account titling.** Trust bank accounts should be titled
  "[Trustee Name] as trustee of [Trust Name]" or similar — not just
  in the trustee's own name — to evidence the trust capacity for
  Article 54 purposes.
- **PTC capital and trust property.** A PTC's own paid-up share
  capital is the PTC's own property; the PTC's trust holdings are
  trust property. The two must be kept distinct in the PTC's books.
