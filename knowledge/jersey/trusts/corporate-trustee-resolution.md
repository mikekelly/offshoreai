# Article 28 — Corporate Trustee Acting by Resolution

Article 28 of the **[Trusts (Jersey) Law 1984][tjl]** governs how a
**corporate trustee** takes decisions. It bridges trust law and
corporate law: the corporate trustee's internal governance produces
binding trustee acts when the procedures in the Article (or in the
corporate trustee's own constitutional documents) are followed.

## What Article 28 says

A corporate trustee may act:

- by **board resolution**; or
- through **officers or employees** appointed to act on its behalf.

For **non-company** corporate trustees (e.g., a foundation acting as
trustee), an "**equivalent decision-making process**" substitutes for
a board resolution.

(See the consolidated text at [jerseylaw.je][tjl].)

## Why this matters

A natural-person trustee acts by the trustee's own decision.
A corporate trustee — typically a Jersey-licensed trust-company
business or a private trust company — has no single will; it acts
through human officers. Article 28 is the statutory framework that
makes the human officers' acts binding as the corporate trustee's
acts.

The Article is structurally important for:

- **Jersey TCBs** acting as professional trustees, taking
  hundreds of decisions a week through their boards or delegated
  officers;
- **Private trust companies (PTCs)** acting as trustees of family
  trusts, with family-member directors making decisions through
  board minutes;
- **Foundations acting as trustees** in certain bespoke
  structures, using their council resolutions.

## Board resolution route

The standard route. A board resolution:

- is passed by the **directors** of the corporate trustee acting as
  a board;
- is **recorded in minutes** signed (or otherwise authenticated) on
  behalf of the board;
- is **binding on the corporate trustee** as a trustee act.

The internal governance — quorum, majority requirements, conflict
management — comes from the corporate trustee's own articles of
association or equivalent constitutional document. The Trusts Law
does not impose specific corporate governance rules on the corporate
trustee; that is the role of [Companies (Jersey) Law
1991](../companies/companies-law-1991.md) or the equivalent for
foreign-incorporated corporate trustees.

## Officer / employee route

A corporate trustee may also act through **officers or employees**
who hold authority to act on its behalf. This is the
**delegated-authority** route within the corporate trustee:

- The board passes a **standing resolution** delegating certain
  decisions to named officers or to a committee.
- The officers act within the delegated scope and the corporate
  trustee is bound.
- The delegating board retains supervisory responsibility.

This is **internal delegation** within the corporate trustee and is
distinct from external delegation under
[Article 25](./trustee-delegation.md). The Article 25 framework
applies when the corporate trustee delegates to a **third party**
(e.g., an investment manager); internal officer delegation is
governed by Article 28.

## "Equivalent decision-making process" for non-company corporates

The Article expressly accommodates non-company corporate trustees by
allowing an "equivalent decision-making process":

- a **foundation** as trustee acts through its **council**
  resolutions;
- a **statutory body** as trustee acts through its statutorily-
  prescribed mechanism;
- a **chartered body** acts through its charter procedures.

The substantive requirement is that the corporate trustee has a
**defined, formal decision-making mechanism** that produces binding
acts.

## Conflict of interest at the corporate level

A common practical issue: a director of the corporate trustee is
also a beneficiary of the trust. The conflict is at the
**individual-director level**, not at the corporate-trustee level.
The Royal Court approach:

- the conflicted director should normally **recuse** from the trust
  decision;
- the remaining directors take the decision;
- if all directors are conflicted, the corporate trustee is **disabled**
  from acting and should consider applying to the Royal Court for
  directions under [Article 51](./article-51-directions.md) (a
  category-3 surrender of discretion).

## PTC director-change continuity

A PTC acting as trustee continues as **the same trustee** through
changes in its directors. There is no Article 17 / 19 event when the
PTC's board changes. From the trust's perspective, the trustee
remains the PTC.

This is operationally significant: a family that wants to change the
substance of trustee decision-making (e.g., bring in a new family
member as decision-maker) can do so by changing the PTC's board,
without invoking the trust-law machinery for trustee changes.

## Pitfalls

- **Inadequate documentation of decisions.** A corporate trustee
  that takes decisions without proper minutes is exposed: the
  validity of trustee acts may be challenged for want of evidence
  that the decision was properly taken.
- **Officer action outside delegation scope.** An officer who acts
  without a clear delegation chain may bind the corporate trustee
  (under apparent authority principles) but the corporate trustee
  may have an internal liability claim against the officer.
- **Mixing corporate-conflict and trust-conflict analysis.** A
  conflict at the individual-director level engages the corporate
  trustee's internal conflict-management rules; a conflict at the
  trust-acts level engages the equitable jurisdiction. The two
  layers must both be cleared for the act to be valid.
- **Foreign-incorporated corporate trustees.** A corporate trustee
  incorporated outside Jersey acts through its home-law governance
  mechanism. Article 28's "equivalent decision-making process"
  accommodates this, but the trustee should ensure its acts comply
  with both its home-law corporate rules and Jersey trust-law
  requirements.
