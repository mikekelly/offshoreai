# Article 25 — Delegation by Trustee

Article 25 of the **[Trusts (Jersey) Law 1984][tjl]** is the
**delegation** provision. It permits a Jersey trustee to delegate the
execution of trusts and powers — both administrative and dispositive
— to agents, and to engage professional service providers.

## What Article 25 says

A trustee may:

- **delegate the execution of trusts and powers** (administrative and
  dispositive);
- **further delegate** delegated authority;
- **employ professional agents** including investment managers,
  accountants, advocates, bankers, brokers, custodians, advisers,
  nominees, property agents, and solicitors;
- **pay** the delegated agents from the trust fund.

A trustee is **not liable for losses** from delegations made:

- **in good faith**; and
- **without neglect**.

Delegated agents may retain **usual commissions**.

(See the consolidated text at [jerseylaw.je][tjl].)

## The shift from common-law non-delegation to statutory permission

At general equity, a trustee historically could not delegate
discretionary functions — *delegatus non potest delegare*. The Royal
Court has applied a modernised version that, in line with Article 25,
permits broad delegation provided the trustee chooses and supervises
the delegate prudently.

The Article 25 framework:

- **Permits** delegation, including of dispositive functions;
- **Conditions** the trustee's protection on the delegation being
  in good faith and without neglect — i.e., the trustee must exercise
  proper care in **selection**, **instruction**, and **supervision**
  of the agent;
- **Allows further delegation** (e.g., the trustee can delegate to a
  manager who in turn engages sub-advisers).

## Selection, instruction, supervision

The Royal Court has been clear that the "without neglect"
qualification is meaningful. The trustee must:

### Select prudently

- choose an agent with appropriate qualifications, expertise, and
  reputation;
- conduct adequate due diligence (regulatory standing, financial
  stability, references);
- document the selection rationale;
- for regulated services, ensure the agent holds the necessary
  regulatory permissions.

### Instruct clearly

- give the agent a clear mandate consistent with the trust's
  objectives and the deed's constraints;
- communicate restrictions and priorities (impartiality, conflict-
  management, distribution authority);
- record the mandate in writing.

### Supervise actively

- monitor performance on a regular schedule;
- review reports and accounts;
- escalate issues that emerge;
- terminate the delegation where the agent's performance falls
  below expectations.

A trustee that fails on any of these may be **liable for losses**
caused by the delegate's default — Article 25's protection does not
extend to careless delegation.

## "Usual commissions"

The Article expressly permits delegated agents to retain **usual
commissions** for their services. This is significant for:

- **investment-management agents** charging customary fee structures
  including management fees and performance fees;
- **brokerage agents** retaining customary commissions on trades;
- **banking agents** retaining customary spreads on FX or banking
  services.

The trustee's duty is to ensure the **fees are usual** — not
unreasonable, and properly disclosed. A delegate charging
above-market fees that have not been negotiated is a flag.

## Common delegations in practice

| Delegation | Typical delegate |
|---|---|
| Investment management | Discretionary fund manager (DFM), private bank, family-office CIO |
| Custody | Custodian bank, prime broker |
| Administration / book-keeping | Outsourced trust administrator (where the trustee is itself a Jersey TCB this is rarely outsourced; for PTC structures it's common) |
| Tax compliance | Tax-services firm in each relevant jurisdiction |
| Legal advice | Jersey Advocates / English solicitors / foreign counsel as needed |
| Specific transaction execution | Counsel, conveyancers, agents |

## Interaction with corporate trustees

A corporate trustee acting through its directors and officers is not
"delegating" within Article 25 when it acts via internal officers —
that is **internal governance** under
[Article 28](./corporate-trustee-resolution.md). Article 25 deals with
**external delegation** to third-party agents.

## Pitfalls

- **Rubber-stamping delegate decisions.** A trustee that signs off
  on a delegate's recommendations without reviewing them is not
  exercising supervision; the trustee remains liable for the
  delegate's mistakes despite the Article 25 protection.
- **Affiliate delegations and conflict.** Delegating to an entity
  in the same group as the trustee creates a conflict of interest
  that needs deed authorisation or beneficiary consent or Court
  approval. Don't assume a related-party delegation is okay just
  because it is operationally convenient.
- **Sub-delegation without authority.** The trustee should ensure
  that any further delegation by the primary agent is within the
  trustee's permission; uncontrolled sub-delegation can lead to
  unauthorised exposure.
- **Documenting "in good faith and without neglect".** A trustee
  defending a delegation-loss claim needs contemporaneous evidence
  of the selection, instruction, and supervision process. Build
  the file at the time, not in retrospect.
