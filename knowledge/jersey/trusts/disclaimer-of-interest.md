# Article 10A — Disclaimer of Interest

Article 10A of the **[Trusts (Jersey) Law 1984][tjl]** allows a
beneficiary to **disclaim** the whole or any part of their interest
under a trust.

## What Article 10A says

A beneficiary may disclaim **the whole or any part** of his or her
interest under a trust **in writing**, **whether or not the
beneficiary has received any benefit** under the trust. The trust
terms may provide for the **revocability** of the disclaimer.

(See the consolidated text at [jerseylaw.je][tjl].)

## Why disclaimer matters

The principal reasons a beneficiary disclaims:

- **US-person and other tax concerns.** A beneficiary who becomes a US
  person, or a beneficiary in a jurisdiction where the trust interest
  triggers immediate adverse tax consequences, may disclaim to avoid
  bringing the trust within scope of the foreign tax regime.
- **Family-dynamics protection.** A beneficiary who is fragile or in
  difficult personal circumstances (e.g., facing creditor pressure,
  going through divorce) may disclaim to protect the trust from
  attack via their personal-law claims.
- **Estate-planning re-engineering.** A beneficiary may disclaim in
  favour of the next class (typically children or remaindermen),
  effectively skipping a generation for tax or planning purposes.

## Writing requirement

Disclaimer must be **in writing**. Oral disclaimers are not effective
under Article 10A. The writing should be clear, identify the
disclaiming beneficiary, identify the interest being disclaimed (in
whole or in specified part), and be delivered to the trustee. A
witnessed signature is good practice.

## Effect of disclaimer

A disclaimed interest is treated as never having vested in the
disclaiming beneficiary — the disclaimer operates **ab initio** in the
ordinary case. The interest passes according to the terms of the
trust as if the disclaiming beneficiary had never existed (or had
predeceased the relevant event), or as the deed otherwise provides.

A partial disclaimer (e.g., "I disclaim my interest in respect of
distributions from a specified sub-fund") is permissible under the
"whole or any part" wording. The boundaries of the disclaimed
interest must be clear in the writing.

## Revocability

Article 10A permits the trust to provide for the **revocability** of
a disclaimer. The default position — in the absence of express
revocability — is that a disclaimer once made is **irrevocable**. A
beneficiary who anticipates wanting to reverse should consider:

- having the deed include an express revocability clause; or
- couching the disclaimer as conditional on facts subsisting at a
  given date.

## Interaction with foreign tax law

A disclaimer is a unilateral act of the disclaimer beneficiary, but
its tax characterisation in foreign jurisdictions varies. The US
"qualified disclaimer" rules (under §2518 of the Internal Revenue
Code) impose specific timing and form requirements; a Jersey
disclaimer that does not satisfy §2518 may be treated by the IRS as
a gift by the disclaimer to the next-taker, with attendant US gift
tax consequences. UK inheritance-tax practice is more flexible but
still imposes time limits on effective disclaimers for IHT purposes.

A disclaimer is therefore typically pre-modelled with both Jersey-law
and home-country tax advice before being signed.

## Pitfalls

- **"After receiving any benefit"**. The Article allows disclaimer
  "whether or not the beneficiary has received any benefit" — but a
  beneficiary who has accepted benefit may face evidential
  difficulties asserting the disclaimer operates *ab initio*. Time
  matters; disclaim early.
- **Hidden conditions in the deed.** Some deeds make benefit
  conditional on non-disclaimer; check before disclaiming a
  conditional interest.
- **Effect on settlor's tax position.** A disclaimer may shift
  beneficial ownership in a way that re-engages settlor-based tax
  rules in the settlor's home jurisdiction.
