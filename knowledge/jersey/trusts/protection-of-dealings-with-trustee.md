# Article 55 — Protection to Persons Dealing with Trustee

Article 55 of the **[Trusts (Jersey) Law 1984][tjl]** is the
**bona fide purchaser** rule. A person dealing with a trustee in
good faith and for value, **without notice** of any breach of
trust, may deal with the trustee **as if** the trustee were the
beneficial owner.

## What Article 55 says

A **bona fide purchaser for value without notice** of any breach of
trust may deal with the trustee "**as if the trustee was the
beneficial owner**".

(See the consolidated text at [jerseylaw.je][tjl].)

## Why this matters

Without this protection, every counterparty of a trustee would have
to investigate the trust's internal affairs before transacting —
were the proposed steps within the trustee's powers? Was beneficiary
consent obtained where needed? Are there pending breach-of-trust
disputes? This would make trust property practically untransferable.

Article 55 lets a third party transact in confidence: as long as
the third party is **bona fide**, **for value**, and **without
notice** of any breach, the transaction is good — even if it turns
out the trustee was committing a breach by entering it.

## The three elements

### 1. Bona fide

The third party must act in **good faith** — no dishonesty, no
collusion with the trustee, no acts inconsistent with reasonable
commercial behaviour.

### 2. For value

The third party must provide **consideration** — not necessarily
market value, but real value. A donee or recipient of a gift cannot
rely on Article 55 because the without-consideration element
defeats the protection.

### 3. Without notice

The third party must have **no notice** — actual or constructive —
of the breach. Notice includes:

- **actual knowledge** of the breach;
- **constructive notice** — knowledge that a reasonable person in
  the position would have acquired by reasonable inquiry;
- **imputed notice** — knowledge of the third party's agents, in
  the course of the agency.

Constructive notice is significant: a third party that **should
have asked questions** but did not may lose the Article 55 protection.

## What "as if the trustee was the beneficial owner" means

The third party can treat the trustee as having **full authority**
to deal with the property:

- the trustee's transfer **passes good title** even if the trustee
  had no internal authority;
- the trustee's representations are **binding** as if from a
  beneficial owner;
- the third party's dealings are **not undone** by subsequent
  beneficiary claims of breach.

The protection runs **as against the beneficiaries**. The
beneficiaries' recourse for the breach is **against the trustee**
(under [Article 30](breach-of-trust-liability.md)) and against any
**non-protected recipient** (under
[Article 33](constructive-trustee.md) constructive trust). The
Article 55 BFPV is in the clear.

## Operational practice

Counterparties dealing with Jersey trustees typically rely on
Article 55 implicitly:

- **banks** lending to or holding deposits for trustees;
- **investment managers** receiving instructions from trustees;
- **buyers** of trust property in arm's-length transactions;
- **professional service providers** (lawyers, accountants,
  custodians) engaged by trustees.

In practice the counterparty typically:

- confirms the **identity** of the trust and the trustee;
- obtains **trustee resolutions** evidencing internal authority;
- relies on **representations** in the engagement documents that
  the trustee has the necessary authority;
- but does **not** investigate the trust's beneficial-ownership
  structure or its internal compliance with deed terms.

This is consistent with Article 55 — the counterparty's protection
does not require independent diligence on the trust's affairs.

## The "no notice" limit

Article 55 falls away where the counterparty **has notice**. Common
notice scenarios:

- the trustee tells the counterparty about a beneficiary dispute;
- the counterparty receives a notice of beneficiary claim;
- the counterparty is part of the trustee's connected group and
  has actual knowledge of the trust's affairs;
- the **circumstances are so unusual** that a reasonable
  counterparty would have asked questions.

Where notice arises, Article 55 protection drops away and the
counterparty may become liable to the beneficiaries as a knowing
recipient or assister of the breach.

## Pitfalls

- **Over-relying on Article 55 in high-risk transactions.** A
  counterparty that proceeds with a transaction where the
  circumstances suggest something is wrong may face constructive-
  notice arguments later.
- **Counterparty due-diligence files.** Counterparties should
  document what they were told (and not told) about the trust's
  affairs — to evidence the absence of notice.
- **Affiliated-party transactions.** A counterparty that is part
  of the trustee's group cannot easily claim no-notice — the
  imputed-notice rule attributes group knowledge.
