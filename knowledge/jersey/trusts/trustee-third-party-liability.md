# Article 32 — Trustee's Liability to Third Parties

Article 32 of the **[Trusts (Jersey) Law 1984][tjl]** governs the
trustee's **liability to third parties** with whom the trustee
contracts on behalf of the trust. The rule turns on whether the
third party **knew** of the trustee's capacity at the time of the
transaction.

## What Article 32 says

When a trustee transacts with a third party:

- if the third party is **aware that the trustee is acting in a
  trustee capacity**, the third party's claim is limited to **trust
  property** — the trustee is not personally liable;
- if the third party is **unaware** of the trustee's capacity, the
  third party may **claim against the trustee personally**;
- but the trustee retains a **right of recourse to the trust
  property by way of indemnity** for liabilities properly incurred.

The Article **does not** affect breach-of-trust liability between
the trustee and the beneficiaries — see
[`breach-of-trust-liability.md`](./breach-of-trust-liability.md).

(See the consolidated text at [jerseylaw.je][tjl].)

## Why this rule matters

At common law, a trustee historically contracted in their **personal
capacity** with full personal liability. The trustee's only relief
was a right of indemnity from the trust fund. Article 32 modernises
this position for Jersey: where the counterparty knows it is dealing
with a trustee, the trustee's exposure is **limited to the trust
property**.

This is operationally important for Jersey TCBs and PTCs:

- **counterparty banks**, **investment managers**, and **professional
  service providers** routinely sign engagement documents acknowledging
  the trustee's status;
- the trustee can transact at high volumes without each transaction
  putting the trustee's general balance sheet at risk;
- it aligns Jersey practice with modern offshore market expectations.

## Drafting practice — "as trustee, not personally"

The standard mechanism for invoking Article 32's protection is to
sign contracts and engagement letters explicitly **"in its capacity
as trustee of [the named trust], and not personally"** (or "in its
trustee capacity only, with recourse limited to trust property").
Phrases such as:

- "the obligations of the trustee under this agreement are limited
  to the trust property and the trustee shall not be personally
  liable";
- "any liability of the trustee shall be discharged out of the
  assets of the trust";

are routine in Jersey trust transactions and lock in Article 32's
protection.

## The "unaware" limb

Where the counterparty is **unaware** of the trustee's capacity —
e.g., where the trustee signs in its own name without identifying
the trust — the trustee is **personally liable**. The trustee then
relies on the **right of indemnity** from the trust property.

The right of indemnity is **subject to**:

- the liability being **properly incurred** (not a breach of trust);
- the trust property being **sufficient** to meet the liability;
- the trustee's own conduct — a trustee that has breached cannot
  indemnify itself for the consequences of the breach.

The "unaware counterparty" scenario typically arises with:

- transactions documented in the trustee's name alone (without
  reference to the trust);
- emergency transactions where there was no time to document the
  trustee capacity;
- transactions with counterparties who did not appreciate the
  significance of the trustee designation.

## Indemnity priority

The trustee's right of indemnity ranks **ahead of beneficial
interests** — see also
[`trustee-remuneration.md`](./trustee-remuneration.md) on the trustee's
lien. A trustee with a valid indemnity claim is paid before the
beneficiaries.

## Pitfalls

- **Failure to identify trustee capacity.** A trustee who signs in
  its own name without reference to the trust gives the
  counterparty an unaware-party claim under Article 32. Always
  document the capacity.
- **Counterparty unfamiliarity.** Some non-trust-familiar
  counterparties (e.g., service providers in jurisdictions where
  trusts are unusual) may not appreciate the significance of the
  trustee designation even when stated. Make it clear in the
  contract.
- **Indemnity exhausted by breach.** A trustee that has breached
  cannot indemnify itself for the consequences of the breach. The
  Article 32 indemnity is only for properly-incurred liabilities.
- **Cross-border enforcement.** Article 32 limits Jersey-law
  liability; a foreign court may apply its own rules to a contract
  signed in its jurisdiction. Where the counterparty is foreign and
  the contract is governed by foreign law, the trustee's
  protections may be different.
