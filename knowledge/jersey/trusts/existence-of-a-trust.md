# Article 2 — Existence of a Trust

Article 2 is the substantive existence-of-trust provision in the
**[Trusts (Jersey) Law 1984][tjl]**. It tells you when, as a matter of
Jersey law, a trust **exists** — distinct from Article 7 which tells
you how a trust is **created**, Article 3 which tells you when
Jersey **recognises** a trust, and Article 11 which tells you when a
trust is **valid**.

## What Article 2 says

A trust exists when:

> A person (known as a trustee) holds or has vested in the person
> property (of which the person is not the owner in the person's own
> right) [for]:
> (a) the benefit of any person (known as a beneficiary), whether or
> not yet ascertained or in existence;
> (b) any purpose which is not for the benefit only of the trustee; or
> (c) such benefit as is mentioned in paragraph (a) and also for any
> such purpose as is mentioned in paragraph (b).

— [Article 2, Trusts (Jersey) Law 1984][tjl]

## What this definition does

Three elements are required:

1. **A trustee who holds or has vested in them property.** There must
   be an identifiable person (natural or corporate) in whose name or
   to whose order the trust property is held.
2. **Property of which the trustee is not the owner in their own
   right.** The trustee's interest is fiduciary, not beneficial. This
   is the *separation of legal and beneficial ownership* that is the
   defining feature of a trust at common law and which Article 2
   imports into Jersey statute.
3. **A trust for the benefit of beneficiaries, for a purpose, or
   both.** Article 2(a) accommodates discretionary objects ("whether
   or not yet ascertained or in existence") — i.e., yet-unborn or
   unascertained beneficiaries do not defeat existence. Article 2(b)
   accommodates **purpose trusts**, both charitable and (subject to
   Article 12 conditions) non-charitable.

## Existence vs validity vs recognition vs creation

The four propositions are conceptually distinct, and the Trusts Law
treats them at different Articles:

| Question | Provision |
|---|---|
| **Does a trust exist** on these facts? | [Article 2](existence-of-a-trust.md) — this file |
| Is the trust **created** in a permissible manner? | [Article 7](creation-of-a-trust.md) |
| Does Jersey **recognise** this trust as valid and enforceable? | [Article 3](recognition-of-a-trust.md) |
| Is the trust **valid** (free of vitiating factors)? | [Article 11](validity-of-a-trust.md) |

A trust that exists under Article 2 will normally be valid under
Article 11 and recognised under Article 3, but a vitiated trust (e.g.,
for an illegal purpose) can exist in fact while being unenforceable
in law. Article 2 sets the existence threshold; Articles 11 and 3 set
the enforceability threshold.

## Practical implications

- **Sham-trust analysis.** Where a settlor purports to settle property
  but in substance retains beneficial ownership, the Royal Court may
  find the trust does not exist under Article 2 — element 2 fails. The
  leading Jersey "sham" authority is *In Re Esteem Settlement* [2003]
  JLR 188; see [`sham-trusts.md`](sham-trusts.md).
- **Reserved-powers trusts.** A settlor who reserves powers under
  [Article 9A](reserved-powers.md) does not, by reservation alone,
  cease to satisfy element 2: Article 9A(3A) provides that a
  reservation "shall not of itself constitute the settlor... a
  trustee". A reserved-powers trust **exists**.
- **Bare trusts and nominee arrangements** satisfy Article 2 even
  though the beneficial owner is a single named person; the legal/
  beneficial split is the essence of the trust.
- **Purpose trusts.** Article 2(b) is the gateway for non-beneficiary
  purpose trusts. For non-charitable purposes the additional
  conditions in [Article 12](purpose-trusts.md) (an enforcer, a
  permissible purpose, written instrument) must also be met.

## Pitfalls

- **The "trustee not owner in own right" element** is sometimes
  glossed in routine documents. Where a settlor and trustee are the
  same person initially (a "declaration of trust by the settlor
  over his own assets"), the analysis is that the settlor has
  declared themselves to hold the property in a different capacity;
  the dual capacity is sufficient to satisfy Article 2 if properly
  documented and observed in practice.
- **Article 2 does not require writing.** Existence does not depend
  on a written instrument — see [Article 7](creation-of-a-trust.md)
  ("oral declaration, or by an instrument in writing... or arise by
  conduct"). Unit trusts are an exception requiring writing.
- **Article 2 does not require beneficiaries.** Purpose trusts under
  Article 2(b) exist without any beneficiary. The settlor's
  intention must be sufficiently clear; the role of the enforcer
  (Article 13) is to police the purpose in the absence of
  beneficiaries.
