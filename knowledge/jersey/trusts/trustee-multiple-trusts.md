# Article 31 — Trustee Acting in Respect of More Than One Trust

Article 31 of the **[Trusts (Jersey) Law 1984][tjl]** addresses the
position of a trustee — typically a professional trust company — that
acts as trustee for **multiple trusts** simultaneously. It does two
things: it disapplies cross-trust **notice imputation**, and it
permits **arm's-length transactions** between trusts under the same
trustee.

## What Article 31 says

### No cross-trust notice

A trustee administering more than one trust **shall not, in the
absence of fraud, be affected by notice** of any instrument, matter,
fact or thing **acquired through the administration of another
trust**.

### Conflict disclosure

Co-trustees must **disclose** conflicting interests **before**
transactions.

### Transactions between trusts under common trusteeship

A trustee **may enter into a contract or other arrangement with
itself** in its capacity as trustee of one or more other trusts —
provided fiduciary duties are respected.

(See the consolidated text at [jerseylaw.je][tjl].)

## Why this matters for Jersey TCBs

A typical Jersey-licensed trust-company business administers
hundreds or thousands of trusts. Without Article 31:

- **knowledge** acquired in one trust's affairs would, by
  common-law notice imputation, attach to every other trust under
  the same trustee — a practical impossibility and a serious
  conflict generator;
- **transactions between two trusts** (e.g., one trust selling an
  asset to another) would be inherently conflicted because the same
  trustee sits on both sides.

Article 31 solves both problems pragmatically.

## No cross-trust notice — the protection

The protection is **strong** but **not absolute**:

- it applies "**in the absence of fraud**" — a trustee that uses
  knowledge from one trust to advantage another in a way that
  amounts to fraud loses the protection;
- it applies to **matters acquired through administration**, not to
  matters known generally;
- it does not displace **regulatory** obligations — JFSC-supervised
  trustees have separate firm-level know-your-customer and
  conflict-management duties under the TCB Code of Practice; see
  [`../financial-regulation/codes-of-practice.md`](../financial-regulation/codes-of-practice.md)
  *(stub)*.

In effect: the trustee's individual officers acting on a specific
trust matter are not deemed to know what other officers of the same
trustee know about other trusts. Knowledge ringfencing is preserved.

## Transactions between trusts — the permission

Article 31 permits cross-trust transactions, subject to fiduciary
duties. In practice this means:

- the trustee must act in the **best interests of each trust
  separately** in the transaction;
- **independent valuation** is normally required for the transaction
  price;
- **conflict disclosure** to each trust's beneficiaries (or to a
  protector / Court) is appropriate where the transaction is
  significant;
- the transaction must be **on arm's-length terms** — the same
  terms a third party would have accepted.

Where the transaction is **momentous**, the trustee will commonly
seek **Royal Court blessing** under
[Article 51](./article-51-directions.md) for each trust's side of the
transaction. The blessing protects the trustee's decision against
later challenge by beneficiaries asserting that the transaction
favoured the other trust.

## Conflict disclosure to co-trustees

Where the trust has **co-trustees** and the trustee acting for
multiple trusts has knowledge of a conflicting interest, **disclosure
to the co-trustees is required before** the transaction. The
disclosure should be:

- **prompt** — before the transaction is concluded;
- **specific** — identifying the conflict, not just acknowledging
  it generally;
- **documented** in the trustee minute.

## Pitfalls

- **Treating Article 31 as a blanket dispensation.** It is not.
  Fiduciary duties continue to apply; what Article 31 changes is the
  notice rule and the strict no-self-dealing rule.
- **Knowledge segregation in practice.** A TCB should not rely on
  Article 31 to excuse poor information barriers; the TCB Code of
  Practice expects formal conflict-management systems regardless
  of the statutory notice rule.
- **Cross-trust transactions in family groups.** A family with
  multiple trusts under the same trustee may want to move assets
  between trusts (e.g., to consolidate underlying companies). The
  Article 31 permission is helpful but the trustee should still
  obtain valuation and consider blessing — a "convenience"
  transaction at the wrong price exposes the trustee on both sides.
