# Article 43A — Trustee Security

Article 43A of the **[Trusts (Jersey) Law 1984][tjl]** is the
**security right** of an outgoing or distributing trustee. It permits
the trustee to require **reasonable security** for liabilities —
existing, future or contingent — before surrendering or distributing
trust property.

## What Article 43A says

A trustee that is **departing** (resigning, being removed, or
otherwise ceasing to act) or that is **distributing** trust property
(on termination or by way of significant capital distribution) may
require:

- **reasonable security**;
- for **liabilities** — **existing**, **future**, **contingent**, or
  **otherwise**;
- **before** surrendering or distributing the property.

(See the consolidated text at [jerseylaw.je][tjl].)

## Why this matters

A trustee that **distributes everything** and then faces a
retrospective claim has no fund-side recourse to meet the claim and
no easy route to recover from beneficiaries who have spent the
distribution. Article 43A is the statutory hook for the practical
solution: the trustee retains some value as security against
continuing exposure.

## What "liabilities" covers

The breadth — "existing, future, contingent, or otherwise" — captures
the full range of trustee exposure:

- **existing** — current invoices, current tax liabilities, current
  litigation costs;
- **future** — anticipated liabilities under continuing contracts
  (lease obligations, service-provider arrangements, ongoing
  trustee fees);
- **contingent** — possible liabilities that may crystallise (tax
  reassessments, beneficiary claims, regulatory investigations);
- **otherwise** — catch-all for anything not fitting the above
  categories.

## Forms of security

The Article does not prescribe the form; "reasonable security" is
the standard. Common forms:

- **retention of assets** by the trustee equal to a reasonable
  estimate of continuing exposure;
- **indemnity** from beneficiaries (where the beneficiaries are of
  full age and capacity) or from the receiving trustee;
- **bank guarantee** from the receiving trustee or a beneficiary;
- **insurance** maintained for a period post-exit;
- **escrow** of a specified amount with a third party.

For substantial trusts, a **combination** is common — e.g., a
retained reserve plus beneficiary indemnity plus run-off insurance.

## "Reasonable" — the limit

The right is qualified by **reasonableness**. A trustee cannot
require security wildly out of proportion to the realistic exposure.
Factors the Royal Court will consider in a dispute:

- the **size** of the realistic exposure;
- the **likelihood** of crystallisation;
- the **availability** of recovery routes against beneficiaries;
- the **trustee's conduct** during the trusteeship — a trustee that
  has breached cannot use security to wash itself clean;
- the **beneficiaries' position** — particularly hardship arguments
  where the security demand is disproportionate.

Where the trustee and beneficiaries cannot agree on the security
amount or form, **application to the Royal Court** for directions
under [Article 51](./article-51-directions.md) is the route.

## Interaction with Article 34

[Article 34](./outgoing-trustee.md) confirms the outgoing trustee's
release from future beneficiary claims subject to specified
exceptions. **Article 43A is the security-side complement**: the
trustee's release is more durable when backed by realistic security
for the residual exposures.

## Practical pattern on resignation

A typical resigning-trustee package:

1. **Schedule of exposures** — listing existing, future and
   contingent liabilities with estimated quantum;
2. **Security schedule** — listing the proposed security and its
   form (retained reserve / indemnity / guarantee / insurance);
3. **Indemnity deed** with the continuing trustees and (where
   possible) the beneficiaries, with the security mechanism
   referenced;
4. **Release deed** from beneficiaries (of full age and capacity);
5. **Royal Court directions** if any party disputes or if minor /
   unborn interests need representation.

## Pitfalls

- **Inadequate exposure assessment.** A trustee that under-estimates
  exposure may be left short. Build the schedule carefully, with
  input from advisers (tax, regulatory, contract counsel).
- **Over-aggressive security demand.** A trustee demanding
  unreasonable security risks the Royal Court intervening on the
  beneficiaries' side and impacting the trustee's exit costs.
- **Beneficiary inability to indemnify.** Minor and unborn
  beneficiaries cannot indemnify; charitable / purpose-trust
  beneficiaries may not. Where these are the only beneficiaries,
  the security must come from fund-retention or insurance, not
  beneficiary indemnity.
- **Foreign-asset security.** Security held by way of retention of
  foreign-situated assets may be hard to enforce in practice; cash
  retention in Jersey is operationally simpler.
