# Article 24 — Powers of Trustee

Article 24 of the **[Trusts (Jersey) Law 1984][tjl]** is the
**default-powers** provision. It grants Jersey trustees the same
breadth of operational powers as a natural beneficial owner of the
trust property — a strong default that means most Jersey trust deeds
do not need to spell out granular powers.

## What Article 24 says

A trustee has "**all the same powers as a natural person acting as
the beneficial owner**" of trust property, subject to:

- the **trust terms**;
- the **trustee's duties** (notably under
  [Article 21](./trustee-duties.md) and the equitable jurisdiction);
- any requirement in the trust terms that **third-party consent** be
  obtained before exercising particular powers.

A person whose consent is required by the deed does **not** become a
trustee by virtue of holding the consent power.

(See the consolidated text at [jerseylaw.je][tjl].)

## The "natural person" benchmark

The "powers of a natural person beneficial owner" formula imports
the widest possible default scope. A natural beneficial owner of
property can:

- **buy, sell, lease, mortgage** real and personal property;
- **invest** in any category of asset;
- **enter contracts** with any counterparty;
- **lend, borrow, guarantee** in their own name;
- **sue and be sued** in respect of the property;
- **employ agents** and professionals;
- **delegate** discretionary functions to the extent the law allows;
- take any other step a beneficial owner could take.

A Jersey trustee inherits all of these as default statutory powers,
subject to the duty-and-deed constraints.

## Why this matters in drafting

Practitioners drafting deeds in jurisdictions with **narrow default
powers** (some US common-law states; many civil-law settings) must
list every operational power expressly. In Jersey, Article 24 means
the deed can be **silent on operational powers** without restricting
the trustee.

Modern Jersey deeds therefore:

- **don't repeat** the natural-person powers;
- **focus on dispositive powers** (additions, exclusions,
  advancements, distributions) where deed control is desired;
- **carve out** specific powers where the settlor wants a
  restriction or a consent requirement.

## Third-party consent powers

The deed can require a trustee to obtain consent before exercising
specified powers — typically:

- **investment consent** from an appointed investment committee or
  family-office investment manager;
- **distribution consent** from a protector or settlor (especially
  in reserved-powers trusts);
- **fundamental decision consent** (e.g., sale of a primary asset,
  termination of the trust) from a protector or named consent-
  holder.

The consent-holder is **not a trustee** by virtue of holding the
consent power. They may, however, owe **fiduciary duties** in the
exercise of the consent — the Royal Court takes a contextual view
of whether a consent role is fiduciary, depending on the deed's
language and context. A "protector" with extensive consent powers
is normally treated as a fiduciary; a more administrative
consent-holder may not be.

## Duty constraints on the exercise of power

Having a power is not the same as being free to use it. Article 24
makes explicit that all powers are exercised "**in beneficiaries'
interests**" and "**per trust terms**". The exercise is therefore
constrained by:

- the **proper-purpose doctrine** — a power must be exercised for
  the purpose for which it was conferred, not for some collateral
  purpose. Misuse for collateral purposes opens the door to
  [Article 47G/H](./article-47-set-aside.md) set-aside.
- the **impartiality** duty under [Article 23](./trustee-impartiality.md);
- the **prudent-person standard** under
  [Article 21](./trustee-duties.md);
- the **deed's express limitations** (e.g., a power "exercisable
  only with consent of X" requires X's consent every time).

## Investment powers — practical patterns

The default natural-person powers give the trustee broad investment
freedom. In practice, deeds often constrain this:

- **investment policy statements** referenced in the deed;
- **prohibited categories** (e.g., no derivatives, no investments in
  competitor businesses);
- **consent of an investment committee** required for material
  changes;
- **delegation to a professional investment manager** under
  [Article 25](./trustee-delegation.md).

The trustee's overarching duty is to **invest prudently** with a view
to the beneficiaries' interests — not merely to act within the
Article 24 default.

## Pitfalls

- **Treating Article 24 as a permission slip.** The default powers
  are subject to duties. A trustee that uses a wide power
  imprudently or for an improper purpose is in breach despite the
  Article 24 default.
- **Catch-all consent clauses.** A deed clause "any decision the
  settlor wishes to oversee" creates uncertainty about scope. Deeds
  should specify the consent-required transactions.
- **Inferring borrower / guarantor authority.** Although Article 24
  gives the trustee power to borrow or guarantee on behalf of the
  trust, this is rarely done in family-trust practice without
  express deed authority — banks and counterparties prefer explicit
  authority in the deed.
