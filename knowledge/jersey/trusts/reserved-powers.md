# Article 9A — Settlor Reserved Powers

**Article 9A** of the **[Trusts (Jersey) Law 1984][tjl]** is the
**reserved-powers** provision. It confirms that a Jersey trust is not
invalid by reason only of the settlor reserving any of a long, defined
menu of powers — and it shifts the legal analysis from "do the
retained powers make this a sham?" to a structured set of statutory
rules about the effect of retention.

Article 9A was inserted by the **Trusts (Amendment No. 4) (Jersey) Law
2006**, alongside the modernisation of the [Article 9
firewall](firewall.md). The two provisions are commercially
inseparable.

## What Article 9A says

### Paragraph (1) — the headline

A settlor's reservation of beneficial interests or specified powers
**shall not affect the validity of the trust**, and the trust **shall
take immediate effect**, except as otherwise expressed in the trust.
([Article 9A(1)][tjl])

So a Jersey trust that gives the settlor (for example) the power to
revoke does not, for that reason alone, fail to come into existence
on signing. The trust takes immediate effect; the settlor's continuing
power is overlaid on a properly-existing trust.

### Paragraph (2) — the list of powers that may be reserved

A non-exhaustive list of powers a settlor may reserve includes:

- a **power to revoke** the trust in whole or in part;
- a **power of variation** of the terms of the trust;
- a **power of advancement** of trust property;
- a **power to direct the trustee** as to investment, distribution,
  or other administrative matters;
- a **power to appoint or remove** beneficiaries, trustees,
  enforcers, protectors, or other office-holders of the trust;
- a **power to change the investment manager**;
- a **power to change the proper law** of the trust (cross-refer
  [Article 41](change-of-proper-law.md));
- a **power to restrict** the trustee's discretion (e.g., a consent
  power);
- and other related powers.

([Article 9A(2)][tjl] — for the verbatim list see the consolidated
text)

The list is open-ended in practice. The drafter is not constrained to
the listed powers; the list operates as a statutory confirmation that
the listed powers do not invalidate the trust, with similar powers
treated analogously.

### Paragraph (3) — trustee protection

A trustee acting in accordance with a settlor's exercise of a
reserved power **does not commit a breach of trust** by so acting.
([Article 9A(3)][tjl])

This is the bridge that makes a reserved-powers trust workable: the
trustee can defer to the settlor's exercise of a reserved power
without itself being in breach of trust duties owed to beneficiaries.

### Paragraph (3A) — settlor not a trustee

The reservation of powers by a settlor **shall not of itself
constitute the settlor a trustee**. ([Article 9A(3A)][tjl])

This is decisive against the line of argument that a heavily-
reserved-powers trust is in substance a trust where the settlor is
themselves a co-trustee with fiduciary duties.

### Paragraph (4) — States' power to amend the list

The States Assembly may amend the list of permitted reserved powers
by Order. ([Article 9A(4)][tjl])

## Why this matters

Reserved-powers planning is central to modern offshore-trust
practice. Settlors — especially first-generation entrepreneurs and
patriarchs of family enterprises — often want to retain control over
investment policy, distribution decisions, and the identity of
beneficiaries while still benefiting from the legal characterisation
of the structure as a trust. Article 9A makes that combination
possible under Jersey law in a way that, in many other common-law
jurisdictions, would have to be done by complex deed drafting around
unhelpful background rules.

The principal commercial uses:

- **US-grantor-trust planning.** A US-person settlor who wants the
  trust to be a grantor trust for US income-tax purposes may
  deliberately reserve revocation or directive powers; Article 9A
  makes this feasible without invalidity.
- **Family-office structures.** A settlor who is the patriarch of an
  operating-business family may reserve directive powers over
  investment and distribution while passing legal title to a
  professional trustee.
- **Living-will-style succession planning.** A settlor may reserve a
  revocation power to be exercised on diagnosis of incapacity, with
  the deed automatically locking down on the trigger event.

## Limits

Article 9A's permissive list is not unlimited:

- **Sham-trust limits.** Article 9A does not save a trust that fails
  the [Article 2](existence-of-a-trust.md) existence test because the
  settlor in substance retains beneficial ownership rather than
  retaining only the listed powers. The line between *reserved
  power* (Article 9A protects) and *retained beneficial ownership*
  (Article 2 fails) is fact-specific.
- **Foreign tax characterisation.** Reserved-powers Jersey trusts
  are often characterised by foreign tax authorities (UK HMRC, US
  IRS, etc.) under the foreign jurisdiction's substance-over-form
  rules. The Jersey-law validity is one question; the foreign tax
  result is a separate question that Article 9A cannot answer.
- **Insolvency and creditor claims.** Reserved revocation powers in
  particular may expose the trust to creditor attack under
  bankruptcy or fraud-on-creditors rules (Jersey *Pauline action*
  or foreign equivalents) — Article 9A protects the trust against
  *trust-law* invalidity, not against insolvency-law clawback.
- **Trustee duties not displaced.** A trustee acting under a
  reserved-power direction is protected by Article 9A(3); a trustee
  acting outside such direction remains subject to ordinary fiduciary
  duties — see [trustee-duties.md](trustee-duties.md).

## Drafting practice

A modern Jersey reserved-powers deed will:

- expressly invoke Article 9A;
- list the reserved powers exactly (rather than relying on
  catch-alls), so the trustee's protection under Article 9A(3) is
  clear on a given exercise;
- spell out the **mechanism of exercise** — written direction;
  witnessing; revocation procedure;
- spell out the **succession of reserved powers** on the settlor's
  death or incapacity (otherwise the powers lapse and the trustee
  reverts to full discretion);
- include a coordinated **letter of wishes** addressing how the
  trustee should act when reserved powers are not being exercised;
- be tested against the foreign tax position of the settlor and
  intended beneficiaries before signing.

## Pitfalls

- **Catch-all reservations.** A clause reserving "all such powers as
  the settlor may from time to time wish to exercise" is unhelpful;
  the trustee cannot confidently rely on Article 9A(3) protection on
  a specific direction.
- **No mechanism for incapacity.** A reserved-powers trust that does
  not address the settlor's incapacity often produces awkward
  practical disputes when the settlor becomes unable to direct and
  family members try to step into the role.
- **Reserved consent powers.** Granting a third party (e.g., a
  protector) a consent power over distributions can re-engage
  Article 9A drafting questions because the consent-holder's role
  may look fiduciary; the Royal Court takes a contextual view of
  whether protector consent is a Article 9A reserved-power-style
  veto or a fiduciary office.
- **Foreign settlor-attribution rules.** US "grantor trust" rules,
  UK "settlor-interested trust" rules and similar regimes may catch
  reserved-powers Jersey trusts and produce undesirable tax
  outcomes. Always model the foreign tax position before relying on
  Article 9A.
