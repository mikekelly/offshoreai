# Article 40 — Power of Revocation

Article 40 of the **[Trusts (Jersey) Law 1984][tjl]** confirms that a
trust may be **revocable** in whole or in part, and sets out the
consequences when a revocation is exercised.

## What Article 40 says

- A trust may be expressed as **revocable in whole or in part**.
- Once revoked, the trustee **holds the property for the settlor
  absolutely** (or for the settlor's **personal representative** if
  the settlor has died).
- "**No such revocation or variation shall prejudice anything
  lawfully done by a trustee**" before the trustee receives notice of
  the revocation.

(See the consolidated text at [jerseylaw.je][tjl].)

## The default is irrevocable

A Jersey trust is, by default, **irrevocable** — the trustee cannot
unilaterally undo the trust, and the settlor cannot revoke unless
the deed expressly reserves a power of revocation. Article 40
operates only where the deed includes such a power.

## When revocable trusts are used

Common scenarios for a revocable Jersey trust:

- **US-grantor-trust planning.** A US-person settlor often
  deliberately includes a power of revocation so that the trust is
  a "revocable trust" for US tax purposes, with grantor-trust
  treatment. The settlor's revocation power makes the trust
  transparent for US income tax during the settlor's life and avoids
  some throwback complications.
- **Living-trust-style estate planning.** A settlor uses a revocable
  trust as a probate-avoidance vehicle in conjunction with
  cross-border estate planning, planning to make the trust
  irrevocable on the settlor's death or incapacity.
- **Tentative or conditional planning.** Where the settlor is not
  yet committed to permanent settlement, a revocable structure
  allows them to retain ultimate control while testing the
  arrangement.

## Effect of revocation

On valid revocation:

- the trustee holds the **revoked property** for the **settlor
  absolutely** (or the settlor's personal representative);
- the trustee must **transfer the property** to the settlor (or
  representative) in accordance with the deed's revocation
  procedure;
- **acts done before notice** of the revocation are **protected** —
  the trustee is not in breach for distributions made or
  transactions entered into before the revocation took effect.

The settlor's right to call for the property is **absolute** — the
revocation produces a bare-trust position pending transfer.

## Procedural requirements

The deed will specify how revocation is exercised:

- **written notice** to the trustee (the standard);
- **delivery** of the notice in a specified manner;
- any **conditions** on revocation (consents, age requirements,
  etc.).

The Article's protection of "anything lawfully done before notice"
emphasises that **notice** is the critical event. A revocation that
has not been notified to the trustee is not yet effective against
the trustee, and trustee acts pending notice are protected.

## Partial revocation

The Article permits **partial revocation** — revocation of part of
the trust property or part of the trust's terms. A common pattern:

- the settlor reserves the power to revoke specific assets while the
  bulk of the trust remains in place;
- the settlor revokes a portion to fund a specific purpose (e.g., a
  charitable contribution, a real-estate purchase).

## Interaction with Article 9A reserved powers

A power of revocation is one of the powers expressly listed in
[Article 9A](./reserved-powers.md) as a power the settlor may reserve
without invalidating the trust. The interaction:

- **Article 9A** confirms reservation does not invalidate the trust;
- **Article 40** sets out the operational consequences when the
  reserved revocation power is exercised;
- **Article 9A(3)** protects a trustee that acts on the settlor's
  revocation direction.

## Tax consequences

A revocable trust is typically treated by foreign tax authorities as
**transparent to the settlor**:

- **UK** — a settlor-interested trust regime applies to revocable
  trusts settled by a UK-resident settlor; income and gains may be
  attributed to the settlor;
- **US** — a revocable trust is a "grantor trust" under §671 of the
  Internal Revenue Code; all items of income, gain, loss, and credit
  are attributed to the grantor;
- **Civil-law jurisdictions** — many treat a revocable structure as
  unaffected by the settlement, attributing the property and its
  income to the settlor.

The tax characterisation may be **desirable** (as in US grantor-trust
planning) or **undesirable** (in some UK contexts) — model the
position before drafting.

## Cross-border revocation risks

Where the trust holds **foreign-located assets**, revocation may
trigger:

- **transfer taxes** in the foreign jurisdiction on the property
  reverting to the settlor;
- **forced-heirship** claims in the settlor's home jurisdiction
  becoming live once the property is back in the settlor's estate;
- **creditor exposure** — the firewall under Article 9 protected the
  Jersey trust, but the unwound assets revert to the settlor's
  general estate.

A revocable trust is therefore less protective than an irrevocable
one in many cross-border scenarios.

## Pitfalls

- **Notice mechanics.** Failing to deliver the revocation notice in
  the manner the deed prescribes can leave the revocation
  ineffective.
- **Tax-trigger revocation.** Revocation may itself be a taxable
  event in the settlor's home jurisdiction. Model first.
- **Inadvertent partial revocation.** A settlor instructing a
  general "wind back" of the trust without specifying scope can
  produce ambiguous results. Be explicit.
- **Settlor incapacity.** A revocation power held by a settlor
  who has lost capacity cannot be exercised by attorneys or
  representatives unless the deed expressly provides for it. Plan
  the contingency in advance.
