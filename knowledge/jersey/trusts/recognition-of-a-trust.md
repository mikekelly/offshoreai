# Article 3 — Recognition of a Trust by the Law of Jersey

Article 3 of the **[Trusts (Jersey) Law 1984][tjl]** is one sentence
long but does a lot of work. It expresses, as a default rule, that
Jersey will recognise trusts:

> Subject to this Law, a trust shall be recognized by the law of
> Jersey as valid and enforceable.

— [Article 3, Trusts (Jersey) Law 1984][tjl]

## What Article 3 does

Three propositions are packed into the single sentence:

1. **Default recognition.** Jersey recognises a trust as a legal
   institution. This is not a self-evident proposition in civilian
   jurisdictions, many of which do not recognise the
   common-law-style trust at all. Article 3 cements Jersey's
   adherence to the trust concept.
2. **"Valid and enforceable".** Recognition is not nominal — a
   recognised trust is enforceable by Jersey law mechanisms (Article
   51 directions, Articles 47B–H set-aside, breach-of-trust claims
   under Article 30, etc.).
3. **"Subject to this Law".** Recognition is qualified by the rest
   of the Law. So a trust that fails [Article 11
   validity](./validity-of-a-trust.md), that is for an unlawful
   purpose, or that falls outside the conditions for non-charitable
   purpose trusts (Article 12), is not recognised under Article 3.

## Interaction with Article 9 (the firewall)

Article 3 is the **recognition** rule for Jersey trusts (i.e., trusts
whose proper law is Jersey law — see [`proper-law.md`](./proper-law.md)).
Article 9 — the **[firewall](./firewall.md)** — does separate work: it
protects a Jersey trust against attack on the basis of foreign rules
(forced heirship, matrimonial regimes, etc.) that would otherwise
constrain the settlor's freedom to settle.

Recognition under Article 3 is the precondition; the firewall under
Article 9 is the protection. Together they make the Jersey trust a
durable wealth-planning vehicle in the face of foreign legal regimes
that would prefer the property to flow differently.

## Foreign trusts

Article 3 is about recognition of a **Jersey** trust by the law of
Jersey. For the recognition of a **foreign** trust by Jersey, see
Part 3 of the Law — in particular [Article 49 —
Enforceability of a foreign trust](./foreign-trust-enforceability.md)
 The high-level rule is that Jersey will give effect to
a foreign trust according to its own proper law, subject to public
policy.

## Implications for cross-border structures

- A Jersey-law trust with foreign-situated assets is, as a matter of
  Jersey law, recognised under Article 3 and protected by the
  Article 9 firewall. **Whether the foreign jurisdiction respects
  that recognition** is a question of the foreign jurisdiction's
  conflict-of-laws rules and (where applicable) its accession to
  the Hague Convention on the Law Applicable to Trusts and on their
  Recognition.
- Jersey has not ratified the Hague Trusts Convention itself
  (although the UK has, with extension issues for Jersey). Article 3
  operates as Jersey's domestic recognition rule independently of
  the Convention.

## Pitfalls

- **Treating Article 3 as a self-standing private-international-law
  rule.** Article 3 is a Jersey-domestic rule about how Jersey
  treats Jersey trusts; it is **not** an export rule that requires
  foreign courts to accept the recognition. The work of getting
  foreign courts to respect a Jersey trust is done by the firewall
  (Article 9), by jurisdiction clauses in the deed, and by tactical
  litigation choices about where to sue and be sued. See
  [`firewall.md`](./firewall.md).
