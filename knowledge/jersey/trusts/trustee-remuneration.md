# Article 26 — Remuneration and Expenses of Trustee

Article 26 of the **[Trusts (Jersey) Law 1984][tjl]** sets out when
a trustee may be **remunerated** for their services and the trustee's
**right to recover expenses** from the trust fund.

## What Article 26 says

### Remuneration — default rule and exceptions

Unless authorised by:

- the **terms of the trust**;
- the **consent in writing of all of the beneficiaries**; or
- an **order of the court**,

a trustee **shall not be entitled to remuneration** for their
services.

### Statutory exception — professional trustees

A **professional trustee** is **entitled to reasonable remuneration**
for services provided after the Article's commencement, even where
the trust terms are silent on remuneration.

### Expenses

A trustee may **reimburse themselves** and **pay out of the trust**
all expenses and liabilities **reasonably incurred** in the
administration of the trust.

(See the consolidated text at [jerseylaw.je][tjl].)

## The default rule — no remuneration

The starting point inherited from equity is that a trustee acts
**gratuitously**. The duty is to the beneficiaries; taking a profit
from the position would breach the no-profit rule.

Three authorised routes around the default:

1. **Express deed authorisation.** Modern Jersey trust deeds almost
   always include a fee clause permitting the trustee to charge.
   Where the trustee is a Jersey TCB, the clause typically
   incorporates the TCB's published fee schedule and a margin for
   special work.
2. **Beneficiary consent.** All beneficiaries (of full age and
   capacity) in writing may consent. Note "all" — a contingent or
   unborn beneficiary's consent cannot be obtained, so this route
   is rarely available for family trusts with future-generation
   beneficiaries.
3. **Court order.** The Royal Court can authorise remuneration under
   [Article 51](./article-51-directions.md). Used where the deed is
   silent and beneficiary consent is impractical.

## The professional-trustee statutory exception

Article 26 specifically provides that a **professional trustee** is
**entitled to reasonable remuneration** notwithstanding silence in
the deed. This reflects:

- the reality that Jersey TCBs are licensed financial businesses
  operating on a fees basis;
- the impracticality of requiring every deed to spell out the fee
  arrangement;
- the policy of distinguishing fees-charging professional trustees
  (who are paid for skill) from amateur trustees (who are not).

"Professional trustee" in this context tracks the
[Article 1 definition](./interpretation-and-definitions.md) — a
trustee registered with the JFSC for trust company business.

The reasonableness standard is **market-derived**: the trustee's
published or customary fee schedule will normally be evidence of
reasonableness, but a fee above market for the work involved can be
challenged.

## Expenses — separate from remuneration

The trustee's right to recover **expenses** is broader and applies
regardless of remuneration authorisation. Recoverable expenses
include:

- **professional fees** of agents engaged under
  [Article 25](./trustee-delegation.md) (investment managers,
  accountants, lawyers, etc.);
- **administrative costs** (bank fees, custodian fees, regulatory
  filings);
- **insurance** premiums;
- **litigation costs** incurred for the trust (subject to costs
  rules and any Royal Court direction);
- **tax and similar charges** payable by the trust.

The standard is "**reasonably incurred**". Excessive or wasteful
expenses are not recoverable.

The trustee's **right to reimbursement** is a **right of indemnity**
out of the trust fund, with priority over beneficial interests. A
trustee who advances funds personally for legitimate trust expenses
ranks ahead of beneficiaries for recovery.

## Practical fee structures

Jersey TCBs typically use one or more of:

- **Time-cost fees** at agreed hourly rates;
- **Fixed annual fees** for routine administration;
- **Asset-based fees** (a small percentage of trust value);
- **Transaction fees** for specific work (e.g., distribution
  processing, large transactions);
- **Court-application fees** for non-routine work.

Fee schedules are usually annexed to or referenced in the deed.

## Indemnity and lien

A trustee has a **lien** over the trust property for unpaid fees and
expenses. The lien:

- **survives the trustee's exit** (subject to deed terms);
- **takes priority** over beneficial interests; and
- can be **enforced** by application to the Royal Court if needed.

This is the legal basis for the retiring trustee's right to a
**reasonable indemnity** before transferring property to a successor
— a continuing exposure to past acts justifies a continuing claim
against the trust fund.

## Pitfalls

- **Charging without authority.** A trustee that charges fees
  without deed authority, all-beneficiary consent, or Court order
  is in breach — even if the trustee is a professional trustee, the
  fee must be "reasonable" and properly documented.
- **Excessive disbursements.** Recovering above-market disbursements
  (e.g., legal fees substantially above prevailing rates) without
  justification is challengeable.
- **Forgetting the lien on exit.** A retiring trustee should
  document and reserve the lien position in the indemnity and
  release; not doing so may leave the trustee exposed.
