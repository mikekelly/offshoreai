# Article 49 — Enforceability of a Foreign Trust

Article 49 of the **[Trusts (Jersey) Law 1984][tjl]** is the
**foreign-trust recognition** rule. Jersey law recognises a foreign
trust as **governed by its proper law**, subject to two
**public-policy carve-outs**.

## What Article 49 says

A foreign trust is "**governed by, and shall be interpreted in
accordance with its proper law**", **except** where:

- the trust **contravenes Jersey law**; or
- the trust **applies to Jersey immovable property**.

(See the consolidated text at [jerseylaw.je][tjl].)

## The default — deference to the proper law

Jersey takes a **liberal recognition stance** for foreign trusts.
Where a trust is properly constituted under its own proper law:

- the trust's **validity** is judged by the proper law;
- its **interpretation** is by the proper law;
- the **trustee's duties** to beneficiaries are by the proper law;
- the **beneficial interests** are determined by the proper law.

This deference reflects Jersey's role as a finance centre where
foreign trusts frequently appear in operational contexts (Jersey
trustee of a Bermuda trust; Jersey bank account of a Cayman trust;
Jersey real-estate holding by a New York trust).

## Carve-out 1 — contravention of Jersey law

A foreign trust that **contravenes Jersey law** is not enforced in
Jersey. Examples:

- a trust for an **illegal purpose** under Jersey criminal law —
  e.g., a trust to launder proceeds of crime;
- a trust violating Jersey **sanctions** law;
- a trust whose **operation in Jersey** breaches a Jersey regulatory
  rule (e.g., requires the trustee to carry on regulated business
  without licence).

The carve-out is a **public-policy** mechanism. It is narrower than
a "Jersey doesn't like the result" objection — Jersey courts will
not refuse to enforce a foreign trust merely because Jersey law
would have produced a different outcome. Only a **substantive
contravention** engages this carve-out.

## Carve-out 2 — Jersey immovable property

Where a foreign trust **applies to Jersey immovable property** (real
estate located in Jersey), Jersey law applies. The carve-out
reflects:

- the **lex situs** principle — the law of the place where land is
  located governs its disposition;
- the **Jersey land-law context** — Jersey real estate is governed
  by Jersey customary land law (with its specific rules on
  conveyancing, hypothecs, *partage*, etc.) that cannot be
  displaced by a foreign trust instrument.

In practice, a foreign trust seeking to hold Jersey real estate
usually does so via an **intermediate vehicle** — a Jersey company
or partnership — to avoid the carve-out. The intermediate vehicle
holds the real estate; the foreign trust holds the vehicle.

## Article 9 firewall does not apply

A critical distinction: the [**Article 9 firewall**](./firewall.md) is
in Part 2 and applies only to **Jersey trusts**. A **foreign trust**
does not get Article 9 protection — Jersey will not disapply foreign
forced-heirship or matrimonial rules in support of a foreign trust.
The recognition under Article 49 is comparatively thin: Jersey will
enforce the foreign trust **as governed by its proper law**, but
will not actively protect it against foreign attacks the way
Article 9 protects Jersey trusts.

This is one of the principal **planning reasons** for choosing
Jersey law as the proper law: the firewall is meaningful only for
Jersey trusts.

## Article 49 in court — typical applications

Common scenarios in which Article 49 is invoked before the Royal
Court:

- a **Jersey-resident trustee** of a foreign trust seeking
  directions under [Article 51](./article-51-directions.md) — the
  Court will apply the foreign proper law to substantive questions
  while applying Jersey procedure;
- a **beneficiary** seeking to enforce a foreign trust against
  Jersey-located property of the trust;
- a **creditor** seeking to attack a foreign trust on Jersey-policy
  grounds (rarely successful under the narrow public-policy
  carve-out);
- a **family-litigation party** seeking Royal Court recognition of
  a foreign-trust interest in matrimonial or succession proceedings.

## Pitfalls

- **Assuming firewall protection for foreign trusts.** Foreign
  trusts do not benefit from Article 9. Where firewall protection
  matters, the trust should be a Jersey trust.
- **Direct Jersey-real-estate trust holdings.** A foreign trust
  cannot effectively hold Jersey land — use a Jersey corporate or
  partnership intermediary.
- **Public-policy overreach.** The Article 49 carve-out for
  contravention of Jersey law is narrower than many advisers
  assume. Jersey will not refuse to enforce a foreign trust just
  because it would have been drafted differently under Jersey law.
- **Proper law uncertainty.** If the trust's proper law is unclear,
  Article 49 cannot be confidently applied. The Court may have to
  determine the proper law first under
  [Article 4](./proper-law.md) principles before reaching the
  Article 49 question.
