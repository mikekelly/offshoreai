# Article 5 — Jurisdiction of the Court

Article 5 of the **[Trusts (Jersey) Law 1984][tjl]** sets out the
**jurisdictional reach** of the Royal Court over trust matters. The
Royal Court does not need a Jersey-proper-law trust to be seised: it
has jurisdiction over any trust on a defined list of connecting
factors.

## What Article 5 covers

The Royal Court has jurisdiction over a trust where any of the
following is satisfied:

1. The trust is a **Jersey trust** (proper law is Jersey law — see
   [`proper-law.md`](proper-law.md)); **or**
2. The trustee of a **foreign trust** is resident in Jersey; **or**
3. **Trust property** (in the case of a foreign trust) is situate in
   Jersey; **or**
4. The trust is **administered in Jersey** (even where its proper law
   is foreign).

(See the consolidated text at [jerseylaw.je][tjl].)

## The four heads in practice

| Head | Typical case |
|---|---|
| 1. Jersey trust | The standard Jersey-law family trust; every Article-51 and Articles-47B–H application falls here. |
| 2. Jersey-resident trustee | A foreign-law trust whose trustee is a Jersey-regulated trust-company business; the trust comes before the Court for trustee-supervisory matters even though substantive validity questions are decided by the foreign law. |
| 3. Jersey-situated assets | A foreign-law trust holding Jersey real property (rare) or Jersey-registered company shares. The Court has jurisdiction over the Jersey assets specifically. |
| 4. Jersey administration | A foreign-law trust administered by a Jersey administrator. Less common since the typical Jersey TCB is itself the trustee, not a separate administrator. |

In practice, the bulk of Royal Court trust work is on **head 1** —
Jersey-law trusts. Head 2 is the next most common, often arising
when foreign-law settlement instruments name a Jersey trustee for
substance or family-office reasons.

## What jurisdiction means

Holding that the Court has jurisdiction under Article 5 does not
prejudge **which law applies** to substantive questions. A foreign-law
trust before the Royal Court is still governed in matters of validity
and interpretation by its **proper law**; the Royal Court applies
Jersey procedural rules and supervisory standards.

So a Jersey-resident trustee of a Bermuda-law trust can come before
the Royal Court for an [Article 51](article-51-directions.md)
directions application; the Court will apply Bermuda law to the
substantive question (the proper construction of a power in the
deed, for example) but Jersey procedure to the application itself.

## Forum non conveniens and competing jurisdictions

The Royal Court may decline jurisdiction in favour of a more
appropriate forum where:

- the proper law of the trust is foreign;
- the foreign forum is more closely connected with the parties and
  the matters in dispute; and
- the foreign forum is willing and able to hear the matter.

This is the standard *forum non conveniens* analysis adapted to
trusts; the case law of the Royal Court and the Court of Appeal has
applied it both to retain and decline jurisdiction. Where the deed
contains a jurisdiction clause, the weight to be given to it is
governed by **Crociani v Crociani [2014] UKPC 40** — see
[`use-cases/trust-officer/index.md`](../use-cases/trust-officer/index.md)
(under "forum and conflicts of law") and the discussion in
[`proper-law.md`](proper-law.md).

## Article 5 and the firewall

Article 5 is a jurisdictional rule — *when* the Royal Court will
hear a trust matter. The **firewall** in Article 9 is a substantive
choice-of-law rule — *which law* the Court will apply to validity,
heirship-rights challenges, and similar matters, in the case of a
Jersey trust. The two work together: Article 5 gets the case in the
door; Article 9 ensures that foreign rules cannot defeat a Jersey
trust's validity once it is in.

## Pitfalls

- **Confusing jurisdiction with proper law.** A Jersey-resident
  trustee of a New York trust is **subject to Royal Court
  jurisdiction** under Article 5(2) but the trust is **not** a Jersey
  trust and is **not protected by the firewall**.
- **Forum-shopping in cross-border family matters.** Where there is
  related family litigation in another jurisdiction, the choice of
  whether to invoke Article 5 (or to acquiesce in a foreign forum)
  is tactical. Take advice early.
