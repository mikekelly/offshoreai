# Article 53 — Payment of Costs

Article 53 of the **[Trusts (Jersey) Law 1984][tjl]** is the
**costs-out-of-the-trust** provision. The Royal Court may order
that the costs and expenses of an application under the Trusts Law
be paid **out of the trust property**.

## What Article 53 says

> The court may order the costs and expenses of and incidental to an
> application to the court under this Law to be raised and paid out
> of the trust property.

(See the consolidated text at [jerseylaw.je][tjl].)

## Why this matters

Trust applications — particularly **friendly** applications under
[Article 51](./article-51-directions.md) for directions, blessings, or
variation — are **costly**:

- counsel for the trustee;
- counsel for the convened beneficiaries (often multiple,
  representing different interests);
- a **guardian ad litem** for minor and unborn beneficiaries (with
  their own counsel);
- the **Attorney General** in charitable / public-interest matters;
- valuations, expert evidence, accounting reconciliations.

If each party had to bear their own costs, beneficiaries would be
discouraged from participating and the Court's supervisory function
would be impeded. Article 53 lets the Court order costs out of the
trust fund — making the friendly-application framework workable.

## Costs in friendly proceedings — typical pattern

In a standard Article 51 friendly proceeding:

- **Trustee costs** — out of the fund on an **indemnity basis**
  (the trustee is acting for the trust and is entitled to be
  indemnified for properly-incurred costs);
- **Convened beneficiary costs** — out of the fund on a **standard
  basis** (typically), reflecting their role as representing
  interests in the friendly proceeding;
- **Guardian ad litem costs** — out of the fund (the guardian is
  appointed by the Court to represent unborns/minors who cannot
  represent themselves);
- **AG costs** — out of the fund where the AG's involvement was
  necessary for the matter.

## Hostile proceedings — different rules

Article 53 does **not** mandate fund-borne costs. In **hostile**
litigation — typically breach-of-trust claims against the trustee —
the ordinary civil-litigation costs rules apply:

- **costs follow the event** — the loser pays the winner's costs;
- a trustee that has **breached** the trust may face personal costs
  liability rather than fund indemnity;
- a trustee defending a frivolous beneficiary claim may recover its
  costs from the unsuccessful beneficiary (subject to the
  beneficiary's ability to pay).

The distinction between **friendly** and **hostile** proceedings is
therefore critical to the costs outcome.

## Beddoe applications

A **Beddoe application** — where the trustee asks the Court for
directions on whether to bring or defend trust-funded litigation —
is itself a friendly proceeding to which Article 53 applies. The
Beddoe order then governs costs of the substantive proceeding the
trustee is contemplating. See [`article-51-directions.md`](./article-51-directions.md)
and the use-case at
[`use-cases/trust-officer/distribution-decisions-and-court-blessing.md`](../use-cases/trust-officer/distribution-decisions-and-court-blessing.md).

## Pitfalls

- **Assuming costs always come out of the fund.** A trustee that
  brings an unnecessary or improperly-framed application may be
  ordered to bear costs personally.
- **Failing to apply for costs orders in advance.** A Beddoe-style
  pre-application provides costs certainty; absence of one leaves
  the trustee at risk on costs.
