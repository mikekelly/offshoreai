# Article 38 — Power of Accumulation and Advancement

Article 38 of the **[Trusts (Jersey) Law 1984][tjl]** confers
**statutory powers of accumulation and advancement**. It permits the
trustee to **accumulate income**, **apply income for the maintenance
and education** of minor beneficiaries, and **advance trust property**
to a beneficiary before the beneficiary's interest vests.

## What Article 38 says

The trust terms may **direct accumulation** of income or **retention
as income**.

Without prejudice to the deed, the trustee may:

- **accumulate income** for minor beneficiaries;
- apply income for the **maintenance and education** of minor
  beneficiaries;
- **advance trust property** to a beneficiary before vesting.

Advanced property **counts toward** the beneficiary's eventual share.
Advancements **cannot exceed** the beneficiary's presumptive share.

**No time limit** applies to the exercise of accumulation or
distribution powers (i.e., Jersey has no accumulation period
analogous to the English statutory limits before 2010).

(See the consolidated text at [jerseylaw.je][tjl].)

## Accumulation — the statutory power

The trustee may **accumulate income** for the benefit of minor
beneficiaries. Accumulation:

- adds income to **capital** rather than distributing it currently;
- builds the capital base for the beneficiary's eventual taking;
- requires **trustee discretion** in deciding whether to accumulate
  or pay out, exercised consistent with the duties of impartiality
  and good administration.

Jersey's removal of the **accumulation period** (which historically
constrained how long income could be accumulated under English
common law and the English statutory regimes) is one of the policy
distinctives of the Jersey trust regime. Long-horizon multi-
generational accumulation is feasible.

## Maintenance and education

The trustee may apply income for the **maintenance and education**
of a minor beneficiary — i.e., make distributions while the
beneficiary is still under age. Common applications:

- **school and university fees**;
- **medical care**;
- **subsistence support** to the parent or guardian for the minor's
  benefit;
- **specialist support** (therapy, tutoring) consistent with
  upbringing.

The amount should be **reasonable** in the context of the trust's
size and the beneficiary's needs; excessive maintenance distributions
risk being treated as advance distributions of capital (engaging the
"counts toward share" rule) or as breach of impartiality (if
disproportionate among siblings).

## Advancement — capital before vesting

The trustee may **advance capital** to a beneficiary before the
beneficiary's interest vests in possession. Common uses:

- **education funding** (a degree, professional qualification);
- **first-home purchase**;
- **business start-up**;
- **medical emergency**;
- **marriage settlement** (less common in modern practice).

Article 38's two important constraints:

### "Counts toward share"

An advancement is **treated as part** of the beneficiary's eventual
share. When the trust ultimately distributes capital to the
beneficiary, the previously advanced sum is **deducted**. This
prevents the advancement from being a "double-dip" — receiving
capital twice.

### "Cannot exceed presumptive share"

The advancement is **limited** to what the beneficiary would
presumptively take if the trust distributed now. The trustee cannot
advance more than the beneficiary's expected entitlement, because
doing so would prejudice other beneficiaries.

## Interaction with the deed

The Article 38 powers are **default** powers — they apply unless the
deed:

- **disapplies** them (rare);
- **modifies** them (common — deeds typically expand on the
  statutory base, e.g., extending the advancement power to **adult**
  beneficiaries, or removing the "counts toward share" rule for
  specified categories of advancement);
- **conditions** them (e.g., requiring protector consent).

Modern Jersey trust deeds almost always include detailed
maintenance-and-advancement clauses that customise the Article 38
defaults.

## No time limit

The Article expressly confirms **no time limit** on the exercise of
accumulation or distribution powers. This is consistent with
Jersey's **abolition of the rule against perpetuities** for trusts
created after 2006 — see [`duration.md`](./duration.md).

## Practical considerations

### Tax of advancements and maintenance

An advancement or maintenance distribution may be a **taxable event**
in the beneficiary's home jurisdiction:

- **UK**: a relevant-property trust distribution can trigger an exit
  charge; the receipt may be income or capital for UK income-tax
  purposes;
- **US**: maintenance distributions for a minor may be income
  attributable to the beneficiary; advancement of capital may have
  grantor-trust or gift-tax consequences;
- **Civil-law jurisdictions**: may treat advancements as gifts with
  gift-tax exposure.

Pre-model the tax consequences before distributing.

### Documentation

A trustee making an advancement should:

- minute the **decision** with reasons;
- record the **amount** and the **deduction** to be made from the
  beneficiary's eventual share;
- communicate the advancement to the beneficiary in writing,
  including the "counts toward share" effect;
- update the **trust accounts** to reflect the advancement.

## Pitfalls

- **Inadequate impartiality analysis.** Advancing capital to one
  child but not to another within a discretionary family trust
  invites an impartiality challenge from the unadvanced child or
  their issue. Document the reasons.
- **Excess advancement.** An advancement exceeding the presumptive
  share is voidable as ultra vires under Article 38; the trustee
  may have to recover the excess.
- **Implicit advancement by conduct.** A trustee that habitually
  pays for an adult beneficiary's living expenses without
  documenting it as advancement creates evidential difficulties on
  the eventual final distribution.
- **Tax-loss optimisation.** A trustee that times advancements
  without considering the beneficiary's home-jurisdiction tax
  position can create avoidable tax cost.
