# Article 7 — Creation of a Trust

Article 7 of the **[Trusts (Jersey) Law 1984][tjl]** sets the
permissible **modes of creation** of a Jersey trust. Existence (Article
2) and creation (Article 7) are distinct: Article 2 says what a trust
*is*; Article 7 says how a trust *comes into being*.

## What Article 7 says

A trust may be created:

- by **oral declaration**;
- by an **instrument in writing** (including a will or codicil);
- by **conduct**;
- or, by clear implication, by any combination of the above.

A **unit trust** is the express exception — its creation must be
documented in writing.

(See the consolidated text at [jerseylaw.je][tjl].)

## Why Article 7 matters

- **No statute of frauds.** Unlike English equity, Jersey trust law
  has no general writing requirement for the creation of an *inter
  vivos* trust. A settlor can settle assets by oral declaration or
  even by conduct. This makes informal "trust arrangements"
  evidentially fraught but legally viable.
- **Testamentary trusts.** A trust created by will or codicil is
  expressly within Article 7. The Probate (Jersey) Law 1998 governs
  the underlying testamentary instrument; the Trusts Law governs
  the trust that comes into being on grant of representation.
- **Conduct-created trusts.** Where a person deals with property in
  a manner inconsistent with retaining beneficial ownership, the
  Royal Court may infer the creation of a trust by conduct.
  Practical examples are family arrangements where the legal owner
  holds for someone else without a formal declaration.

## Modern practice

In commercial and family-office practice almost every Jersey trust is
created by **a written trust instrument**, executed by the settlor
(or settlors) and the trustee(s). The advantages of writing are
evidential and operational:

- a contemporaneous record of the terms;
- the ability for third parties (banks, brokers, registries) to
  confirm trustee authority;
- the ability to make express choice of Jersey as the proper law
  (engaging Article 3 recognition and Article 9 firewall protection);
- the ability to populate Article 9A reserved powers expressly.

A practitioner who departs from a written instrument in modern
practice will normally have a specific reason — for example, a
verbal "nominee" arrangement intended to be replaced with a written
declaration of trust later.

## Property required

Article 7 does not address what property the settlor must put into
the trust on creation. That is the function of
[Article 8](./property-in-a-trust.md). A trust can be created with a
nominal sum (£10 is a common "settlement sum"), with the principal
property added later under Article 8's general permission to accept
additional property.

## Pitfalls

- **The three certainties.** Jersey adopts the common-law "three
  certainties" requirement for a valid trust — certainty of
  intention, certainty of subject matter, certainty of objects
  (beneficiaries or purposes). Article 7 governs *how* a trust is
  created; the three certainties govern *whether* the creation is
  effective.
- **Unit trusts must be in writing.** The express exception in
  Article 7 closes the door on oral unit trusts. A unit-trust deed
  is therefore essential.
- **Testamentary trusts and validity of the will.** A will-trust
  fails if the will itself fails (e.g., invalid execution under the
  Wills and Successions (Jersey) Law 1993). The Trusts Law does not
  cure underlying testamentary defects.
