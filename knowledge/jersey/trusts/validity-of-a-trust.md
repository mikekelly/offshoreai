# Article 11 — Validity of a Jersey Trust

Article 11 of the **[Trusts (Jersey) Law 1984][tjl]** is the
**validity** provision. It works in conjunction with
[Article 3](./recognition-of-a-trust.md) (recognition) and
[Article 2](./existence-of-a-trust.md) (existence) to establish that a
Jersey trust is binding unless an identified vitiating factor applies.

## What Article 11 says

A Jersey trust is **valid and enforceable in accordance with its
terms** unless:

- the trust is **contrary to Jersey law**;
- the trust is for a **non-charitable purpose** but lacks a
  beneficiary or enforcer satisfying Article 12 conditions;
- the trust was procured by **duress** or **fraud**; or
- the Court finds the trust **impossible of performance**.

(See the consolidated text at [jerseylaw.je][tjl].)

## Limbs of invalidity

### Contrary to Jersey law

A trust that requires the trustee to do something illegal under Jersey
law — e.g., distribute proceeds of crime, hold property for a purpose
that breaches AML/CFT obligations — is invalid under Article 11. The
relevant body of "Jersey law" includes the substantive criminal law,
the [Proceeds of Crime (Jersey) Law 1999][pocl], sanctions laws, and
any other rule that prohibits the trustee's intended action.

### No beneficiary and no qualifying enforcer (purpose trusts)

A trust must have **someone or something** to enforce it. The
ordinary route is **beneficiaries** (Articles 2 and 10). The
purpose-trust route is open under [Article 12](./purpose-trusts.md),
but only if the deed provides for an **enforcer** under
[Article 13](./enforcers.md) and the purpose is one Article 12 permits.

A non-charitable purpose trust without an enforcer fails Article 11.

### Duress or fraud

A trust created under duress (the settlor was compelled to settle) or
procured by fraud (e.g., the settlor was misrepresented to about
material facts) is voidable on application by the affected person.
The Article 11 ground sits alongside the Articles 47C–H
**mistake-based set-aside** regime — see [`article-47-set-aside.md`](./article-47-set-aside.md)
— and the choice between Article 11 invalidity and Articles 47B–H
set-aside depends on the nature of the vitiating factor.

| Vitiating factor | Likely avenue |
|---|---|
| Duress | Article 11 invalidity |
| Fraud (settlor deceived as to trust terms) | Article 11 invalidity |
| Mistake (settlor acted under wrong belief about effect / consequences / fact) | Articles 47B–H set-aside |
| Mistake going to the very intention to settle | Article 11 (with Articles 47B–H as alternative) |

### Impossibility of performance

A trust whose terms are impossible to perform — for example, a trust
to distribute trust property to a class of beneficiaries none of whom
have ever existed — may be declared invalid by the Court. In practice
the Royal Court will more commonly use its variation jurisdiction
(Article 47 and the older trustee-cy-près-style powers) to save the
trust rather than declare it invalid.

## Partial invalidity

The Article 11 grounds can in principle apply to a part of a trust
rather than the whole. A trust that contains both lawful and unlawful
purposes may be enforced as to the lawful purposes only, depending on
severability and the settlor's intention.

## Application — who can ask the Court

An application to declare a trust invalid (or partly invalid) may be
made by:

- the **settlor** (or the settlor's personal representative);
- a **trustee** (where the trustee is concerned that the trust is
  invalid and seeks Court guidance — typically via an
  [Article 51 directions](./article-51-directions.md) application);
- a **beneficiary**;
- in appropriate cases, the **Attorney General** (for charitable
  trusts and public-interest matters).

## Pitfalls

- **Conflating invalidity with set-aside.** Invalidity under Article
  11 attacks the trust *as a whole* on listed grounds; set-aside
  under Articles 47B–H attacks a specific *disposition* or
  *power-exercise* for mistake. Practitioners sometimes plead
  Article 11 invalidity where the proper remedy is a 47B–H
  set-aside, or vice versa.
- **Public policy and impossibility are residual grounds.** They
  exist but are not the first ports of call. Where the underlying
  problem is statutory illegality (AML, sanctions), the analysis
  is direct under Article 11(a).
- **Article 9 firewall protects against foreign-law invalidity
  challenges.** A foreign-law rule of forced heirship cannot ground
  Article 11 invalidity in Jersey, because Article 9 disapplies the
  foreign rule — see [`firewall.md`](./firewall.md).
