# Article 27 — Power to Appropriate

Article 27 of the **[Trusts (Jersey) Law 1984][tjl]** is the
**appropriation power**. A trustee may set off specific trust assets
against a beneficiary's interest without that beneficiary's consent,
subject to the deed.

## What Article 27 says

A trustee may **appropriate** trust property toward satisfaction of
a beneficiary's interest:

- "**in such manner and in accordance with such valuation as the
  trustee thinks fit**";
- **subject to the trust terms**;
- **without the beneficiary's consent**.

(See the consolidated text at [jerseylaw.je][tjl].)

## Why this power matters

Appropriation is the engine of **in-specie distributions** — the
trustee transferring an asset (rather than cash) to a beneficiary
in satisfaction of an interest. Common use cases:

- the family home transferred to the surviving spouse on the
  settlor's death;
- artwork or jewellery transferred to a beneficiary as a personal
  gift in satisfaction of an income or capital entitlement;
- shares of an underlying family company transferred to the next
  generation of beneficiaries;
- a portfolio transferred to a beneficiary's personal account on
  the termination of an interest.

The alternative — selling the asset, distributing the cash, and
having the beneficiary repurchase — generates transaction costs and
sometimes tax events. Article 27 makes the direct route legal.

## Valuation discretion

The trustee's discretion as to valuation is **"such valuation as the
trustee thinks fit"** — wide. In practice the trustee will:

- obtain a **professional valuation** (especially for unique or
  illiquid assets — art, real estate, private company shares);
- value the asset at **fair market value** unless the deed
  otherwise directs;
- document the valuation methodology in the trustee minute approving
  the appropriation;
- consider whether the asset should be valued at a **discount** for
  lack of marketability or minority interest (typical for private
  company share appropriations).

The wide discretion is not a licence to value arbitrarily. The
**impartiality** duty ([Article 23](trustee-impartiality.md)) and
the **prudent-person** duty ([Article 21](trustee-duties.md))
constrain valuation: a low valuation that advantages the recipient
disadvantages other beneficiaries.

## "Without the beneficiary's consent"

The Article does not require the receiving beneficiary's consent —
the trustee can make the appropriation unilaterally. In practice:

- the beneficiary's **co-operation** is almost always needed
  (signing transfer documents, accepting registered title to
  property);
- a beneficiary may refuse to take an in-specie asset and may
  prefer cash. The trustee can in principle force the issue by
  appropriating and then transferring; but the practical reality
  is to **discuss** before appropriating.

## Impartiality and appropriation

Appropriation engages the impartiality duty acutely. If the trustee
appropriates the family home to one beneficiary at "fair value", and
the value subsequently turns out to be too low or too high, the
**other beneficiaries** of the same class are affected. Best
practice:

- obtain **independent valuation**;
- circulate the **proposed appropriation** to the other beneficiaries
  before completion (with sensitivity to confidentiality);
- where the appropriation is **momentous**, seek the Royal Court's
  blessing under
  [Article 51](article-51-directions.md) — see
  [`use-cases/trust-officer/distribution-decisions-and-court-blessing.md`](../use-cases/trust-officer/distribution-decisions-and-court-blessing.md).

## Tax implications

Appropriation can be a **taxable event** in the receiving
beneficiary's home jurisdiction — even though no money has changed
hands. Pre-modelled tax advice is essential:

- **UK** — capital gains on the trust's notional disposal; possible
  inheritance-tax exit charge for relevant property trusts.
- **US** — the receipt may be a distribution of distributable net
  income with consequences for the beneficiary.
- **Civil-law jurisdictions** — may treat the appropriation as a
  gift.

## Pitfalls

- **Unvalued appropriations.** A trustee that appropriates without
  a contemporaneous valuation is exposed: the appropriation value
  is open to challenge by other beneficiaries.
- **Conflict with deed terms.** Some deeds restrict appropriation —
  e.g., requiring beneficiary consent, or requiring a specific
  valuation method. Check the deed.
- **Appropriation of unique assets in a multi-beneficiary trust.**
  Where only one example of the asset exists (the family painting,
  the only flat in London), appropriation in favour of one
  beneficiary is necessarily a denial to the others. The
  impartiality analysis is acute; document carefully.
