# DL 2013 Article 4 — Direct and Indirect Discrimination

Article 4 establishes the **two primary forms** of
discrimination.

## Direct discrimination

A person discriminates **directly** against another when:

- They treat that other person **less favourably** than they
  treat or would treat others;
- **Because of** a protected characteristic.

### Key elements

- **Less favourable treatment** — comparing to actual or
  hypothetical comparator;
- **Because of** — causation by the characteristic;
- **Conscious or unconscious** — the person discriminating
  may not have intended discrimination.

### Direct examples

- "Women aren't promoted" — direct sex discrimination;
- "He's too old for this role" — direct age discrimination;
- "We don't hire Muslims" — direct race discrimination (where
  race protection extends to religion-as-proxy);
- "She's just become a mother, can't be promoted" — direct
  pregnancy / maternity discrimination.

### Justification

Direct discrimination is **generally not justifiable** —
even with a legitimate aim:

- **Exception: age** — direct age discrimination can be
  justified if proportionate means of achieving a legitimate
  aim;
- **Exception: occupational requirement** — specific
  characteristic genuinely required for the role (e.g., model
  to portray specific demographic).

## Indirect discrimination

A person discriminates **indirectly** against another when:

- They apply a **provision, criterion, or practice** that is
  apparently **neutral**;
- But that **puts** or **would put** persons sharing a
  protected characteristic at a **particular disadvantage**
  when compared with persons not sharing it;
- And the other person is put at that disadvantage;
- **Unless** the person can show the provision is **a
  proportionate means of achieving a legitimate aim**.

### Key elements

- **Neutral provision** — facially neutral;
- **Particular disadvantage** — statistically or in fact
  affecting protected group disproportionately;
- **Justification** — proportionate means / legitimate aim
  defence.

### Indirect examples

- "Must work 60 hours per week" — may indirectly discriminate
  against women, disabled, parents;
- "Must have a 5-year residency in Jersey" — may indirectly
  discriminate against migrants from specific groups;
- "Must speak English to specific level" — may indirectly
  discriminate against specific national groups (subject to
  job-relevant justification).

### Justification defence

- **Legitimate aim** — genuine business / regulatory /
  practical need;
- **Proportionate means** — least restrictive way of
  achieving the aim;
- **Balancing** legitimate aim vs disadvantage caused;
- **Documented** business reasons help defence.

## Direct vs indirect — practical distinction

| Feature | Direct | Indirect |
|---|---|---|
| Treatment | Less favourable to individual | Neutral rule applied to all |
| Causation | Because of characteristic | Particular disadvantage |
| Group | Specific individual | Protected group |
| Justification | Generally none (except age) | Proportionate / legitimate aim |
| Examples | "We don't hire X" | "Need 10 years' experience" |

## Burden of proof

Initial burden on the **claimant** to establish a **prima
facie** case:

- Some evidence of less-favourable treatment / particular
  disadvantage;
- Connection to protected characteristic.

Then burden shifts to **employer / respondent** to provide a
non-discriminatory explanation.

## Remedies

If discrimination proven:

- **Compensation** (uncapped);
- **Injury to feelings** awards (Vento bands);
- **Declarations**;
- **Recommendations**;
- **Specific** orders to prevent future discrimination.
