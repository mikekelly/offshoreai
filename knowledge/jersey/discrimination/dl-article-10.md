# DL 2013 Article 10 — Reasonable Adjustments (Disability)

Article 10 imposes a **duty to make reasonable adjustments**
for **disabled persons** — a unique form of discrimination
protection beyond direct / indirect discrimination.

## The provision

A person has a duty to make **reasonable adjustments** where:

- A **provision, criterion, or practice** puts a disabled
  person at a **substantial disadvantage**;
- A **physical feature** of premises puts a disabled person
  at a substantial disadvantage;
- A disabled person would, but for the absence of an
  **auxiliary aid**, be at a substantial disadvantage.

Failure to make reasonable adjustments is **unlawful
discrimination**.

## Three duties

### Duty 1: Provision, criterion, or practice

Where a workplace / service practice disadvantages disabled
persons, the duty-holder must:

- **Modify** the practice to alleviate the disadvantage;
- **Vary** application of the practice for specific cases;
- **Provide** alternative arrangements;
- **Subject to** reasonableness.

### Duty 2: Physical features

Where physical features (steps, narrow doors, layout)
disadvantage disabled persons:

- **Remove** the feature where possible;
- **Alter** it to make it accessible;
- **Avoid** it by alternative means;
- **Subject to** reasonableness.

### Duty 3: Auxiliary aids

Where auxiliary aids would alleviate disadvantage:

- **Provide** the aid (e.g., wheelchair access, hearing
  loop, software);
- **Specific to** the individual's needs;
- **Subject to** reasonableness.

## What is "reasonable"

The reasonableness test considers:

- **Effectiveness** of the adjustment;
- **Cost** of the adjustment;
- **Resources** of the duty-holder;
- **Disruption** to operations;
- **Availability** of financial assistance;
- **Nature** of the activity (business / service / educational);
- **Practicability**.

A **major business** has more resources and so more
adjustments are reasonable than for a small business.

## What is "disability"

Under the DL, **disability** means:

- **Physical, mental, sensory, or intellectual** impairment;
- That has a **substantial and long-term adverse effect** on
  ability to carry out normal day-to-day activities;
- "**Long-term**" typically 12+ months;
- "**Substantial**" more than minor / trivial.

**Specific** conditions are deemed disabilities:

- **Cancer** (from diagnosis);
- **HIV**;
- **Multiple sclerosis**;
- **Severe disfigurement**;
- **Specific** mental health conditions.

## Practical examples

### Workplace

- **Adjustable desk** for back conditions;
- **Modified hours** for fatigue conditions;
- **Quieter workspace** for sensory conditions;
- **Mentor / buddy** for autism;
- **Wheelchair ramp** for premises access;
- **Specific software** for visual / hearing impairments.

### Services

- **Wheelchair access** to premises;
- **Hearing loop** in customer-facing areas;
- **Large-print** documents;
- **Specialist** staff training;
- **Alternative communication** methods.

### Education

- **Extra time** in exams;
- **Special accommodation** during studies;
- **Note-takers** in lectures;
- **Specialist equipment**.

## Who has the duty

The duty applies to:

- **Employers**;
- **Service providers**;
- **Educational establishments**;
- **Premises** landlords / managers (some);
- **Public** bodies in their public functions.

## When duty arises

The duty arises:

- Where the duty-holder **knows or should know** that the
  person is disabled;
- And **knows or should know** of the disadvantage;
- **Anticipatory** duty in some contexts (services should
  anticipate disabled visitors).

## Failure to comply

Failure constitutes **discrimination**:

- **Compensation** claim;
- **Specific** orders to make adjustments;
- **Recommendations** to prevent future failures.
