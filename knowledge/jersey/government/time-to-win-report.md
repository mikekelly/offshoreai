# Time to Win — FSCP Final Report

## What this is

**"Time to Win"** is the **final consolidated output of the
[Financial Services Competitiveness Programme (FSCP)](./financial-services-competitiveness-programme.md)**,
published by the Government of Jersey in 2026. It is the
single document that draws together the Programme's four
workstreams into one diagnosis-and-action statement for
Jersey's **financial and related professional services
(FRPS)** sector.

The report is **deliberately direct**: its thesis is that
Jersey's FRPS sector is **strong but losing ground in
relative terms**, and that the fix is **execution
discipline** — not a new vision, not deregulation, and not
new institutions. The headline framing is that *"the
privilege has shifted"*: it is no longer a privilege for
clients to operate in Jersey; it is now Jersey's privilege
to host and serve global business. ([gov.je — Time to Win][ttw])

The figures and competitive claims below are **as stated in
the report** (published 2026). Treat the industry-size
numbers as point-in-time official figures of that
publication, not stable doctrine — for the live,
decay-managed industry picture see
[`../frontier/jersey-finance-industry-2026.md`](../frontier/jersey-finance-industry-2026.md).

## Who produced it

Forewords are signed by:

- **Deputy Lyndon Farnham** — Chief Minister;
- **Deputy Ian Gorst** — Minister for External Relations and
  the minister with responsibility for financial services.

The Programme was built around **four workstreams** and an
enhanced industry-engagement effort. Stated engagement
scale: **200+ stakeholder interviews**, **1,000+ event
attendees**, **100+ industry experts**, and **50+
businesses consulted**. ([gov.je — Time to Win][ttw])

### The four workstreams

1. **International Tax Strategy** — led by **Revenue
   Jersey** with a panel of senior tax experts; a
   forward-looking tax strategy founded on simplicity and
   certainty (a tax system that is both well-designed and
   competitively administered).
2. **Business Environment Improvements ("Quick Wins")** —
   jointly led by the **Department for the Economy** and the
   **[JFSC](../financial-regulation/jfsc.md)**, delivered
   with **Mourant Consulting**; built on interviews with
   over 200 stakeholders to find quick, high-impact reforms.
3. **External Growth Strategy** — jointly delivered by the
   **Ministry of External Relations** and **Jersey Finance
   Ltd**; a global comparative analysis of markets, value
   pools, and Jersey's "right to win", informed by 100+
   contacts representing 50+ brands (over one-third based
   outside Jersey). Summarised in **Annex 3** below.
4. **Independent Expert Panel** — chaired by **Sir Howard
   Davies**, synthesising workstreams 1–3 into independent
   advice on strategy, positioning, and prioritisation.
   Reproduced as **Annex 2** below.

## Why FRPS matters (as stated)

The report restates the sector's weight in the economy:

- FRPS generates **more than half of Jersey's economic
  output** (the report uses ~**60%**);
- it provides **2 in every 5 jobs**;
- it generates **£6 in every £10 of tax revenue** —
  described as equivalent to funding the entire health and
  education budgets plus most of social security;
- **output per full-time equivalent** in FRPS is **nearly
  three times** the average across the rest of the economy;
- Jersey is the **3rd largest source of FDI into the UK**.

It frames the long arc: since the sector's emergence in the
**early 1960s**, real income per person rose from ~**£7,800**
(today's prices) to **£65,800**, against a UK move from
~**£9,400** to **£40,157** over the same period. ([gov.je — Time to Win][ttw])

### The four pillars (figures as stated in the report)

| Pillar | Figure stated |
|---|---|
| **Banking** | **£182.7bn** in deposits across **19 institutions**, including one-third of the world's largest banks |
| **Funds** | **£465.9bn** in regulated fund assets, **88%** in alternative asset classes; the [JPF](../funds/jersey-private-fund.md) regime has attracted **nearly 1,500 structures** since launch, adding **£86.7bn** in administered funds |
| **Private Wealth** | an estimated **£1.4 trillion** in assets across **66,000 client relationships** spanning **171 jurisdictions** |
| **Professional Services** | accounting, law, tax, and consulting serving the other pillars and the wider Island |

These are the report's own figures; they are more precise
than (and supersede, for the report's publication date) the
rougher point-in-time ranges in the frontier file.

## What has changed (the report's diagnosis of pressure)

The report argues Jersey's **core advantages remain real**
— legal certainty, political stability, strong courts, tax
neutrality, and FATF/OECD compliance "among the strongest of
any assessed jurisdiction" — but the competitive landscape
has shifted:

- **Proliferation of IFCs.** In 1970 there were ~**14**
  major international financial centres; by **2025** there
  were ~**135 IFCs**. Singapore, Dubai, and Abu Dhabi have
  invested heavily and, on customer experience, speed of
  authorisation, digital onboarding, and regulatory
  transparency, are in some areas "measurably stronger".
- **The great wealth transfer.** Over **$12 trillion** is
  expected to transfer to younger generations **by 2030**;
  strongest wealth-creation growth is expected in markets
  such as **India and Saudi Arabia**, outside Jersey's
  traditional geography. **91%** of high-net-worth
  individuals under 40 hold some digital-asset investments,
  versus **70%** overall.
- **Changed client expectations.** Clients and advisers now
  expect digital-first processes, predictable timelines, and
  single points of contact; tolerance for repeated
  information requests and unpredictable timescales has
  fallen sharply.

The conclusion: Jersey is **not** losing momentum on
quality, credibility, or standards — it is losing it to
**friction, complexity, and institutional overlap**. Other
jurisdictions are catching up on standards while
outperforming Jersey on **speed, certainty, and service**.

## The structural problems named

- **Institutional overlap / no single front door.** The
  FRPS ecosystem spans the **JFSC** (regulator + registry),
  **Revenue Jersey** (tax), **Jersey Finance Ltd**
  (promotion/advocacy), and **Government** (policy +
  infrastructure). A firm must engage each independently;
  documentation given to one body is not visible to or
  accepted by another. The failure is described as
  **structural** — "no one has been mandated to own the
  whole system."
- **Cautious regulatory posture.** Authorisation/consent
  can take longer than comparable jurisdictions without
  better outcomes. In Private Wealth, **60%** of all
  customer relationships are subject to **enhanced due
  diligence**, yet only **2%** use the existing tools to
  reduce duplication.
- **Super-equivalence.** Requirements that exceed minimum
  international standards have accumulated without assessing
  proportionate benefit — the original **Sound Business
  Practice Policy** (now the **Sound Business Policy**) is
  cited as a case in point, as is the role of the **MLCO**.
- **Digital and administrative friction.** Core regulatory,
  tax, and registry processes remain substantially manual;
  Government, JFSC, and Revenue Jersey built systems
  independently with no interoperability mandate, agreed data
  standards, or single owner of the digital experience.

## The reset — one foundation plus five imperatives

The report groups its programme under **one advantage to
maintain** plus **five imperatives for competitiveness and
growth**. (Note: these "imperatives" — *what to change* —
are distinct from the **test sectors** below, *where change
will show*.)

**The foundation to maintain — tax neutrality and
simplicity.** What will stay, explicitly: a simple tax
regime with tax neutrality at its core; a competitive
personal income tax regime; **no capital gains, wealth, or
inheritance tax**; and an agile approach to
[Pillar Two](../tax/pillar-two.md).

**The five imperatives:**

1. **Reducing regulatory cost and complexity** — maintain
   relevant standards while minimising super-equivalence;
   simplify Codes of Practice, the Handbook, and Guidance;
   reform the civil financial penalty regime; flexibility to
   MLCO requirements; streamline KYC; publish customer-service
   targets.
2. **Renewing appetite for growth** — embed a growth mindset
   across Government and the JFSC; scan for opportunities and
   make "targeted bets" (e.g. considering a legislative/
   regulatory framework for **insurance**); a **concierge
   service** for new-to-Island business; increased targeted
   external marketing by Jersey Finance; a possible **JFSC
   regulatory sandbox**.
3. **Leading in tokenisation** — build the right ecosystem
   for institutional adopters via the **Digital Assets
   Innovation Council**; modernise frameworks so tokenised
   assets, digital registers, and digital corporate actions
   are recognised; invest in skills.
4. **Embracing digitalisation** — incentives for faster tech
   adoption; **accredited e-KYC**; a digital platform for
   Pillar Two and business taxpayers; modernised interfaces;
   greater cyber-security investment.
5. **Investing in Jersey's attractiveness as an IFC** —
   investment in **IFC2** (office space); improved
   **connectivity with London** (including business flight
   schedules); a cross-Government pro-growth agenda (housing
   supply, education/skills); active international diplomacy
   and new bilateral agreements.

## Where the reset will be tested

The report names sectors that will show "sooner and more
visibly than any others" whether the reset is working:

- **Funds** — recent [JPF](../funds/jersey-private-fund.md)
  reforms must translate into sustained growth;
- **Banking** — a refreshed bank-licensing policy to signal
  Jersey is "open for business";
- **Digital** — where pace of innovation and adoption will
  determine whether Jersey "leads or observes";
- **Innovation** — proportionate frameworks for tokenised
  products; possible insurance framework;
- **Private wealth and family offices** — the sector where
  Jersey's reputation is deepest and client behaviour the
  most reliable indicator of progress.

(The executive summary frames four early-evidence areas —
Funds, Banking, Digital, Private wealth/family offices — and
the "Where the Reset will be Tested" section adds Innovation
as a fifth.)

## Momentum already established (since mid-2025)

The report lists reforms the Programme had already delivered
or begun:

- **modernisation of the Jersey Private Fund regime** — JPF
  reforms effective **August 2025**: removal of the
  **50-investor cap**, **24-hour turnaround** for compliant
  applications, and an expanded professional-investor
  definition;
- simplification of the **Sound Business Practice Policy**
  (now the **Sound Business Policy**);
- **partial repeal of the Control of Borrowing (COBO)**
  framework (with ultimate repeal underway);
- launch of the **Digital Assets Innovation Council**;
- a review of the **Memorandum of Understanding** between
  Government and the JFSC;
- a **concierge service** and a **core monitoring function**
  announced in **November 2025**.

## Annex 1 — Delivery Roadmap (selected, verified)

The roadmap is split into **already completed/underway**,
**now until September**, and **September onwards**, across
Government, the JFSC, and Jersey Finance. Selected
verifiable items:

- **Government / tax:** ratified a **Double Taxation
  Agreement (DTA) between Jersey and Bahrain**; a dedicated
  **business tax portal** (initial phase, focused on Pillar 2
  taxpayers); a **new personal income tax residence
  framework** under review; a new **Pillar 2 tax incentive**
  designed to take effect **1 January 2026**; published
  guidance on the tax treatment of digital assets; plans to
  **expand treaty relationships with Gulf Cooperation Council
  (GCC) partners** and secure participation in UK Free Trade
  Agreements.
- **Government / structural:** review of the **JFSC enabling
  law**; review of how Government is structured/resourced for
  FRPS; review of the **Consent Regime** and of
  **intermeddling**; review of the remit/membership of the
  **Financial Services Advisory Board (FSAB)**; enhancements
  to the **Depositor Compensation Scheme**; a simplified
  **Schedule 2** approach (changes to the Money Laundering
  Order, the JFSC AML Handbook and Guidance); a framework for
  **e-KYC provider accreditation**.
- **JFSC:** reduced frequency of funds reporting; **review
  and refresh of the Bank Licensing Policy** (with a Phase 2
  for different business models); a published revised
  strategy and business plan; explore a **regulatory
  sandbox**; a **Registry transformation programme**;
  enhanced risk-based supervision.
- **Jersey Finance:** develop and deliver a **concierge
  service**; a business case for additional external
  marketing in priority markets.
- **External:** **Ministerial-led trade visits** to priority
  markets including the **USA and the GCC**; further
  investment in **London connectivity**.

## Annex 2 — Independent Expert Panel report

The Panel was chaired by **Sir Howard Davies** (former
Chairman of NatWest Group 2015–2024; former Chairman of the
UK Financial Services Authority; former Deputy Governor of
the Bank of England; former Director of the LSE). Members:
**Tracy Garrad** (former CEO of HSBC Channel Islands & Isle
of Man; former JFSC Commissioner and Risk Chair), **Nigel
Williams** (Chairman and co-founder of Royalton Partners),
**Alan Houmann** (former MD / Head of Government Affairs
EMEA at Citi), and **Michael Moore** (Chief Executive of the
British Private Equity and Venture Capital Association
(BVCA); former MP and Cabinet minister).

Key Panel findings and recommendations (as stated):

- **Scale of the challenge.** FRPS generates **40% of tax
  revenues** and **44% of gross value added**; the sector
  faces "existential threats" from new competition,
  accelerating innovation, and more market-sensitive
  regulation elsewhere. **"Doing nothing is not an option."**
- **Competitiveness slipping.** The Panel cites the latest
  **Z/Yen Global Financial Centres Index** ranking Jersey
  **31st** (down from **25th in 2024** and **13th in 2009**),
  not among the 15 IFCs likely to become more significant,
  and **81st on fintech** (behind Tianjin and Kigali). *(Note:
  other corpus references cite GFCI 39 of March 2026 ranking
  Jersey 38th — see
  [`../history/finance/trajectory.md`](../history/finance/trajectory.md);
  GFCI editions and dates differ.)*
- **Funds.** AIFMD has driven an exodus of funds onshore
  (Luxembourg, Ireland); NPPR access remains for now but is
  not guaranteed. Reposition Jersey as a **specialist
  jurisdiction for non-EU-marketed funds**, complementing
  onshore centres and competing with **Cayman** for
  London-managed offshore fund business.
- **Banking.** Licence count has fallen from **42 to under
  20** through consolidation; UK **ring-fencing** has been
  "particularly damaging" (ending deposit "upstreaming"), and
  much of the GVA decline since the late 1990s reflects
  falling bank profits. Attract **smaller specialist
  institutions / private banks** (e.g. the recent Bank
  Valorai example) and consider tiered licensing on the Isle
  of Man model.
- **Tax.** From **1 January 2025** Jersey introduced a **15%
  Multinational Corporate Income Tax (MCIT)** and income
  inclusion rule for the largest groups; the Panel recommends
  **earmarking a portion of the one-off "upside" revenue**
  (largely from banks) for reinvestment in the sector rather
  than general expenditure.
- **Institutional reform.** Restructure the three overseeing
  bodies to remove overlap: refocus **Jersey Finance**
  exclusively on external promotion and a new **concierge
  service**; establish a **"nerve centre"** in Government
  reporting to the Minister (delivery + horizon-scanning);
  strengthen, rename, and give the **FSAB** a KPI-monitoring
  role; keep the **registry within the JFSC**; recruit
  experienced supervisors ("grey panthers") and recalibrate
  risk appetite.
- **Sector strategies.** Digital assets — focus on
  **institutional tokenisation**, not retail crypto; trusts —
  pursue **Gulf-based (incl. Indian) families**; insurance —
  introduce a comprehensive framework but **do not actively
  market** it (allow organic development).
- **Operational.** Reduce gold-plating / super-equivalence
  (MoneyVal has become a "whole-of-island mission"); apply
  global rules more intelligently (the German BAFIN online
  registration example); reform the penalty regime with SLAs;
  streamline KYC (a shared non-proprietary tool such as
  Fenergo, "submit once, share with many"); build a
  dedicated FRPS tax service in Revenue Jersey.
- **Enabling infrastructure.** Reform the **Control of
  Housing and Work (Jersey) Law 2012** (attach housing
  licences to the person, not the business; address supply,
  including land reclamation); improve the education and
  cultural offer; restore an **8pm London flight** (the last
  winter return currently departs 4:40pm) ideally to City or
  Heathrow. Public spending has risen **33% since 2018**,
  deepening dependence on the sector.
- **Implementation.** Publish an action plan ahead of the
  2026 election; establish a cross-ministerial group chaired
  by the Minister for Financial Services; create a public
  monitoring dashboard; run a campaign explaining the
  sector's importance to Islanders. *"The window for action
  is narrow."*

## Annex 3 — Workstream 3: External Growth Strategy (Oct 2025)

The Workstream 3 summary ("Future competitiveness of the
FRPS sector in Jersey", October 2025) frames a forward-
looking **growth strategy** and "North Star". Key content:

- **Where Jersey is today.** FRPS contributes **over 40% of
  GVA**, **22% of all jobs**, and an estimated **80% of
  corporate tax revenues** (collectively **>40% of total tax
  revenue**). Trust company businesses employ the largest
  workforce; fund administration is the fastest-growing; and
  banking is the highest tax contributor.
- **The pressure.** Emerging IFCs in Asia and the GCC are
  growing faster — e.g. **Singapore and Dubai expected to
  grow 8–9% annually** in private-financial-asset (PFA)
  bookings vs **~5% in the Channel Islands**.
- **Five global trends to navigate:** geopolitical
  volatility; changing wealth pools (US ~30% of global PFA in
  2030; **India ~12% p.a.**, **GCC ~7% p.a.**); the rise of
  private markets (~15% CAGR); the growing prominence of
  digital assets; and **AI disruption** (up to **30% of work
  hours automated by 2030**, with fund administration
  exposed).
- **North Star vision:** "A beautiful and stable island IFC
  that strives for customer excellence, acts with clarity and
  agility, and is intent on growth", supported by two new
  roles — a **Nerve Centre** and a **Concierge**.
- **Five strategic plays:** (1) position Jersey as a
  **specialist jurisdiction for funds** (and a disruptor in
  AI-enabled fund administration); (2) **expand and enrich
  the banking landscape**; (3) **targeted geographic bets**
  (USA for funds, GCC trusts, and — Horizon 2 — HNW
  non-resident Indians for private wealth); (4) become a
  **competitive digital-assets jurisdiction**; and (5)
  **streamline KYC / onboarding**.
- **Three enabling pillars:** a compelling talent strategy;
  advancing digitalisation; and strengthening connectivity.
  Execution unfolds across **three horizons (H0/H1/H2)**.
- **Sector data points:** TCB customer inflows are mainly UK
  (~45%) and Europe (~16%), with the Middle East fastest-
  growing; global TCB ~2% CAGR (2024–30). Jersey has **~100
  family offices** (~5% of the European pool). Banking GVA
  rose **31%** at the 2022–23 rate-cycle peak. Jersey fund
  **NAV has stagnated at ~£0.5tn since 2021** while
  Luxembourg grew from **€4.0tn to €5.3tn**; over **40% of
  Jersey funds under administration are offshore funds**.

> **Note on the report's own numbers.** Different sections of
> the report quote the sector's GVA share differently — the
> body uses "more than half (~60%)" of economic output, while
> the Expert Panel and Workstream 3 annexes use **44% of
> GVA** / **over 40% of GVA**. The "~60%" figure is of
> *economic output / activity*; the ~40–44% figures are the
> narrower *GVA* share consistent with Statistics Jersey (see
> [`../economy/index.md`](../economy/index.md)). Cite the
> measure, not just the percentage.

## Funding (as stated)

**Budget 2026–2029** provides a funding envelope of **almost
£31 million** for the Programme's delivery — about **£8m per
year** for a sector the report says contributes **~£4bn per
year** in economic value. The report says it "will make the
case for increasing the funding if required." ([gov.je — Time to Win][ttw])
