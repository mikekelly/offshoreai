# Crown Dependency — Jersey's Constitutional Status

Jersey is one of the three **British Crown Dependencies**, alongside
Guernsey and the Isle of Man. The Crown-Dependency status is sometimes
misunderstood; this page sets out what it means and what it does not mean.

## What "Crown Dependency" means

The Government of Jersey states it on its own pages in plain terms:
Jersey "is a Crown Dependency, since the Island's relationship stems
from the sovereignty of the British Crown, as opposed to the UK Parliament
or HM Government." This status gives Jersey "constitutional rights of
self government and judicial independence". ([gov.je — relationship with
the UK and EU][gov-rel])

The London Office of the Government of Jersey puts the corollary equally
plainly: Jersey "is a self-governing dependency of the Crown" that
"is not and has never been part of the United Kingdom".
([londonoffice.gov.je][london])

In practical constitutional terms:

- The **King is head of state**. Locally he is represented by the
  [Lieutenant-Governor](./lieutenant-governor.md), who holds a ceremonial
  office of the Crown rather than an executive office of the Jersey
  government.
- Jersey has its own **legislature** ([the States Assembly](./states-assembly.md)),
  its own **executive** ([the Council of Ministers](./council-of-ministers.md)),
  its own **courts** ([the Royal Court](../legal-system/royal-court.md)
  and on appeal the [Court of Appeal](../legal-system/court-of-appeal.md)),
  its own **tax system** ([Revenue Jersey](../tax/revenue-jersey.md)) and
  its own **financial regulator** ([the JFSC](../financial-regulation/jfsc.md)).
- The **final court of appeal** is the Judicial Committee of the Privy
  Council (JCPC), sitting in London. This is the Crown's court, not
  Parliament's. See [legal-system/privy-council](../legal-system/privy-council.md).

## What it does not mean

- It does **not** mean Jersey is part of the United Kingdom. Jersey is
  not represented in the UK Parliament at Westminster.
  ([londonoffice.gov.je][london])
- It does **not** mean the UK Parliament can freely legislate for Jersey.
  By "charter and convention, the UK Parliament does not legislate for
  the Island without consent." ([gov.je][gov-rel]) The mechanism by which
  primary UK legislation is extended to Jersey is the **Order in Council**,
  and the Council of Ministers ordinarily seeks the States Assembly's
  view first.
- It does **not** mean Jersey is, or ever was, part of the European
  Union. Jersey was outside the EU, but covered for goods purposes by
  Protocol 3 to the UK's accession treaty (see [Relationship with the
  EU](../../../jersey/international/eu-relationship.md) below).
- It is **not** the same as "British Overseas Territory" — Bermuda, the
  Cayman Islands, the BVI, Gibraltar etc. are Overseas Territories,
  with a different (post-imperial) constitutional link to the UK.

## Where the relationship is documented

There is no single written constitution of Jersey. The constitutional
relationship is a composite of:

- The **Royal Charters** of the Crown to the people of Jersey, beginning
  with the charters of the late mediaeval and early modern period that
  confirm the privileges of the Island following the loss of mainland
  Normandy to the King of France in **1204**. ([gov.je][gov-rel])
- The **Norman customary law** preserved as part of Jersey's domestic
  legal system, supplemented by Laws of the States and modern statute.
  See [sources-of-law.md](../legal-system/sources-of-law.md).
- The **International Identity Framework** agreed between Jersey and
  the UK Government in 2007, which records the UK's recognition that
  "Jersey has an international identity which is distinct from that
  of the UK." ([gov.je][gov-rel])
- **Convention** — including the convention against UK legislative
  intervention without consent, and the convention that the UK consults
  Jersey on international agreements that would extend to it.

## Defence and foreign affairs

The UK Government carries **formal responsibility for the Island's
defence and, to some extent, its foreign affairs** ([gov.je][gov-rel]),
but the practical position is more nuanced:

- **Defence.** Jersey has no armed forces of its own; its external
  defence is the UK's responsibility. The Lieutenant-Governor is the
  ceremonial link to UK defence chains of command.
- **Foreign affairs.** Jersey now negotiates and signs a wide range of
  agreements in its own name — most relevantly **double taxation
  agreements** ([international/dtas.md](../international/dtas.md)) and
  **tax information exchange agreements**
  ([international/tieas.md](../international/tieas.md)) — and is
  recognised by the UK as having a distinct international identity. The
  UK still typically signs the underlying multilateral instruments on
  Jersey's behalf, with Jersey's territorial extension explicit.

## Relationship with the UK

The UK is Jersey's "closest international partner" with centuries of
deep social, cultural, economic, and constitutional ties.
([gov.je][gov-rel]) The relationship has formal and informal layers:

- **Formal.** The Crown link; Privy Council assent to Jersey Laws by
  Order in Council; UK responsibility for defence and (partly) foreign
  affairs.
- **Informal.** A long-standing customs and free-movement-of-people
  arrangement, currency union (the Jersey pound is at par with sterling
  and Bank of England notes circulate freely in Jersey), close legal
  and professional links (Jersey Advocates often hold dual qualification
  with English or Scottish bars).

For the customs and trade arrangement see
[international/uk-relationship.md](../uk-relationship.md).

## Relationship with the EU — before and after Brexit

**Before Brexit.** Jersey's formal relationship with the European Union
was governed by **Protocol 3 to the UK's 1972 Accession Treaty**. Jersey
was part of the **EU Customs Union and Single Market for the purposes
of trade in goods**, but was otherwise treated as a "third country" for
EU purposes — in particular, Jersey was outside the scope of free movement
of persons, services and capital, and outside the EU's tax directives.
([gov.je][gov-rel])

**After Brexit.** "Jersey's formal relationship with the EU under
Protocol 3 also fell away." ([gov.je][gov-rel]) The replacement framework is:

- The **UK–EU Trade and Cooperation Agreement (TCA)** — Jersey is
  covered by the goods-trade and fisheries provisions of the TCA.
- A **Memorandum of Understanding with the UK signed on 1 March 2021**
  that establishes dispute-resolution mechanisms under the TCA.
  ([gov.je][gov-rel])

For the consequences for Jersey-based businesses (no free movement of
services into the EU; non-EU-AIF marketing into the EU via national
private-placement regimes; etc.), see
[international/eu-relationship.md](../../../jersey/international/eu-relationship.md).

## Why this matters for everything else in this corpus

The constitutional status is the single most important fact about Jersey
for the purposes of this knowledge base, because it underpins almost
everything else:

- Jersey having **its own tax system** is a consequence of being a
  self-governing Crown Dependency, not a tax-policy concession from the
  UK. See [tax/index.md](../tax/index.md).
- Jersey having **its own financial-services regulator and regulatory
  laws** is the basis for the offshore-finance industry's existence.
  See [financial-regulation/index.md](../financial-regulation/index.md).
- Jersey having **its own legal system** (Norman-customary in origin,
  modernised by statute) is why Jersey-law trusts, foundations,
  companies and partnerships exist as distinct vehicles. See
  [legal-system/sources-of-law.md](../legal-system/sources-of-law.md).
- Jersey being **outside the EU** is why CRS implementation, FATCA,
  AIFMD marketing into Europe, and the absence of EU tax directives all
  work the way they do for Jersey-based structures. See
  [international/index.md](../international/index.md).
