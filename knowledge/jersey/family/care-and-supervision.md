# Care and Supervision — Public-Law Child Protection

## When this regime applies

The **public-law** part of the **Children (Jersey) Law
2002** (Part 4) covers cases where the **State** —
through the **Minister for Children** — intervenes to
protect a child at risk. This is distinct from the
**private-law** child arrangements regime (parent vs
parent disputes).

The applicant is typically the **Minister for Children**;
the respondents are the parents (and any other PR
holders); the child is represented through a **guardian
ad litem**, usually a JFCAS officer.

## The threshold — significant harm

Before any care or supervision order can be made, the
court must be satisfied of the **threshold criteria**:

- The child is **suffering**, or is **likely to suffer**,
  **significant harm**; **and**
- The harm is **attributable** to:
  - The **care given** to the child, not being what
    would be reasonable to expect a parent to give;
    **or**
  - The **child being beyond parental control**.

"Significant harm" is a high threshold. It is not
ordinary parenting difficulties or even some forms of
neglect — it requires a level of harm or risk that
justifies State intervention with a family.

## Forms of harm

Harm includes:

- **Ill-treatment** — physical, sexual, emotional abuse;
- **Impairment** of health (physical or mental);
- **Impairment** of development;
- **Witnessing** ill-treatment of another (a recognised
  form of significant harm to children).

Harm is "significant" if it is not trivial and meaningfully
affects the child compared to what could be expected.

## The orders available

### Care order

A **care order** places the child in the **care of the
Minister for Children**. Key features:

- **PR shared** — the Minister gains PR; birth parents
  retain theirs;
- **Minister decides** day-to-day living arrangements
  (typically with foster carers, kinship carers, or in a
  residential setting);
- **Care plan** approved by the court at the time of the
  order;
- **Continues** until discharged or until the child is 18.

### Supervision order

A **supervision order** puts the child under
**supervision** by the Minister while the child remains
at home (or with kinship carers):

- **No PR** transferred;
- **Minister monitors** the child's welfare;
- **Conditions** may be attached (requiring parents to
  engage with services, drug testing, etc.);
- **Time-limited** — typically 1 year, renewable up to 3
  years.

### Emergency protection order (EPO)

A short-term, urgent order:

- Where there is **imminent risk** of significant harm;
- **PR conferred** on the applicant temporarily;
- Typically **lasts up to 8 days**, extendable to 15;
- Used where a child must be **removed immediately** or
  **kept** somewhere safe.

### Child assessment order

Where there are concerns but the standard threshold for
care/supervision isn't met, an **assessment order**
requires the parents to permit the child's
**examination / assessment** — often a precursor to a
fuller decision.

### Police protection (PPP)

A **police protection power** (PPP) — not a court order,
but a **statutory police power**:

- Where a constable has reasonable cause to believe a
  child would suffer significant harm;
- The child is removed and kept in police protection;
- For **up to 72 hours**;
- Triggers immediate Minister / Family Division
  involvement.

## The court process

Public-law children cases follow a structured timeline:

1. **Letter before proceedings** — Minister gives parents
   notice of concerns; opportunity to engage;
2. **Application** filed if matters cannot be resolved;
3. **First hearing** — directions, interim orders if
   needed;
4. **Case management** — JFCAS appointed; expert
   assessments commissioned (psychological,
   parenting-capacity);
5. **Issue resolution hearing** — narrowing the issues;
6. **Final hearing** — evidence, findings on threshold,
   welfare assessment, order;
7. **Care plan** approved.

Cases are typically resolved within **26 weeks** —
aligned with English good-practice — but extensions are
common in complex cases.

## Threshold and welfare — two-stage

The court's analysis is **two-stage**:

1. **Threshold** — is the significant-harm threshold
   met? (Fact-finding.)
2. **Welfare** — given the threshold is met, what order
   best serves the child's welfare?

A case may fail at threshold and end. Or it may pass
threshold but the court may decide that **no order** is
necessary (with services in place).

## Care planning

The **care plan** sets out:

- **Where** the child will live (foster care, kinship
  care, residential);
- **Contact** with parents and siblings;
- **Education**, healthcare, religion;
- **Cultural** considerations;
- **Permanence plan** — return to parents, adoption,
  long-term foster care.

The court must approve the care plan. Care plans for
**adoption** are heavily scrutinised.

## Rights of parents

Parents in public-law children proceedings have:

- **Right to legal representation** — typically
  **publicly funded** (legal aid);
- **Right to be heard** at all hearings;
- **Right to challenge** evidence and assessments;
- **Right to propose** alternative care arrangements
  (kinship carers);
- **Right to maintain contact** unless welfare requires
  otherwise.

Parents who feel concerns are unfounded should engage
specialist family-law advocates **immediately** on
receiving any pre-proceedings letter.

## Adoption pathway

In serious cases, the eventual order may be a **placement
order** — authorising the Minister to place the child for
adoption. This is the most drastic order possible and is
made only where:

- **Nothing else will do** (per *Re B-S* / similar
  authorities adopted in Jersey);
- The court is satisfied that the welfare of the child
  requires it;
- The parents' consent is dispensed with where they
  unreasonably withhold it or other grounds apply.

## Discharge and variation

Care and supervision orders can be **discharged** on
application:

- Parents may apply;
- Minister may apply;
- Child (if old enough) may apply;
- Variation possible for terms within an order.

## Pitfalls

For parents:

- **Failing to engage** with services pre-proceedings —
  evidence of refusal supports threshold and removal;
- **Adversarial posture** without specialist advice —
  case-management decisions can go against parents
  early;
- **Coaching** other adults / children — readily
  detected;
- **Withdrawing** from the process — court proceeds in
  absence and rules adversely.

For the Minister:

- **Insufficient evidence** on threshold;
- **Inadequate care plan** at final hearing;
- **Procedural irregularities** in pre-proceedings;
- **Failure to consider** kinship alternatives properly.
