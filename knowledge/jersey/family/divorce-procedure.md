# Divorce Procedure — Petition to Decree Absolute

## Step-by-step

A typical undefended Jersey divorce runs:

1. **Petition filed** by the petitioner
2. **Statement of Arrangements for Children** filed (if
   minor children)
3. **Service** on the respondent (4-week minimum before
   hearing)
4. **Respondent's Acknowledgement of Service** within 8
   days (longer if outside Jersey)
5. **Setting down for hearing** — by the petitioner
6. **Affidavit** in support of the petition
7. **Decree nisi** pronounced
8. **Wait at least 6 weeks and 1 day**
9. **Application for decree absolute** by the petitioner
10. **Decree absolute** issued — marriage formally ends

Total **minimum timeline**: ~12 weeks. Real-world
timelines (including ancillary relief) commonly extend to
**6-18 months**.

## Stage 1 — Filing the petition

The petitioner prepares a **Petition** in Form 3 (sole
ground) or Form 4 (multiple grounds), setting out:

- **Parties** — names, addresses, dates of birth;
- **Marriage** — date, place, marriage certificate
  (attached);
- **Jurisdiction** — basis (domicile or 1-year habitual
  residence);
- **Children** — names, dates of birth (minor children);
- **Ground** for divorce (Article 7);
- **Facts** supporting the ground;
- **Prayer** — what relief is sought (decree, costs,
  ancillary relief).

If there are minor children, a **Statement of
Arrangements for Children** (Form 4A) is also filed
setting out residence, contact, schooling, healthcare,
and financial arrangements.

The petition is filed with the **Greffier** of the Royal
Court and a court fee is paid.

## Stage 2 — Service

The petition must be **served on the respondent** at
least **4 weeks before the hearing**. Methods:

- **Personal service** by the Viscount (or an agent);
- **Postal service** (recorded post);
- **Service by solicitors' agreement** — common where the
  respondent has instructed solicitors.

Service **outside Jersey** is possible but requires longer
notice (typically 6-8 weeks) and may require translation
in non-English jurisdictions.

A respondent who cannot be located may face **substituted
service** (e.g. by advertisement) or **service dispensed**
in extreme cases.

## Stage 3 — Acknowledgement of Service

The respondent must file an **Acknowledgement of Service**
within:

- **8 days** of service in Jersey;
- Longer (typically 21-28 days) if served outside Jersey.

The Acknowledgement indicates:

- Whether the respondent **admits** the ground;
- Whether the respondent **defends**;
- Whether the respondent **consents** (relevant for
  1-year-separation grounds);
- Whether the respondent has **claims** of their own.

A respondent who fails to acknowledge can proceed in
default — the divorce continues without them.

## Stage 4 — Setting down

The petitioner applies to **set down** the case for
hearing. At least **10 days** before the proposed
hearing date, the petitioner files:

- An **Affidavit** verifying the truth of the petition;
- Confirmation of the grounds;
- Any **Statement of Arrangements** addressing the
  children;
- A request for the case to be listed for the next
  available **undefended session**.

## Stage 5 — Decree nisi

The Family Judge considers the petition at the
**undefended session** (a regular court sitting for these
matters). The judge:

- Reviews the petition, affidavit, acknowledgement, and
  arrangements;
- Confirms procedural compliance;
- **Pronounces decree nisi** if satisfied;
- **Adjourns** for clarification if needed;
- **Refuses** if the ground is not made out (rare in
  undefended cases).

A pronouncement of decree nisi does **not** end the
marriage — it is a provisional order.

## Stage 6 — The 6-week wait

After decree nisi, **at least 6 weeks and 1 day** must
elapse before decree absolute can be applied for. This
period:

- Allows for any reconciliation;
- Permits the parties to **finalise ancillary relief**;
- Gives the respondent time to challenge if grounds
  emerge.

In practice, parties often **wait substantially longer**
than the minimum — sometimes years — to:

- **Settle ancillary relief** first (decree absolute
  before financial settlement can be risky, e.g.
  losing entitlement on death);
- **Time the divorce** with tax or pension
  considerations;
- **Negotiate** custody / contact arrangements.

## Stage 7 — Decree absolute

The petitioner applies for decree absolute by filing the
relevant form and any supporting evidence (e.g.
ancillary-relief order). The court issues the **decree
absolute** — typically administratively, without a
hearing.

The marriage ends on the date of decree absolute. Key
effects:

- **Free** to remarry;
- **Loss of spousal succession rights** under Jersey
  forced-heirship — légitime / douaire end on divorce
  (will-update essential);
- **Loss of mutual-claim status** for some purposes;
- **Triggers** any contingent provisions in trusts /
  wills.

If the petitioner does not apply, the respondent can apply
**after a further period** (typically 3 months after the
6-weeks-and-a-day).

## Costs

Court fees and own legal fees:

- **Petition fee** — set by court tariff;
- **Service costs** — Viscount or recorded post;
- **Advocate / solicitor fees** — typically several
  thousand pounds for undefended; substantially more for
  contested;
- **Ancillary relief** can add tens of thousands in
  contested cases.

**Costs orders**: in undefended cases, costs typically lie
where they fall (each party pays their own). In contested
cases, costs may be awarded against the losing party.

## Defended divorces

Where the respondent **defends**:

- A **Defence** (sometimes called an "Answer") is filed;
- The case proceeds to a **contested trial** in the
  Family Division;
- Evidence is heard (often with witness evidence);
- Costs implications are substantial;
- Outcomes are rare to differ from undefended (the
  petitioner usually has alternative grounds available).

## Service abroad and recognition

Foreign-resident respondents:

- Service via diplomatic channels in some jurisdictions;
- **Hague Service Convention** considerations apply
  (Jersey acceded through UK extension);
- Translation requirements;
- Reciprocal recognition of Jersey decrees abroad —
  varies by jurisdiction; obtain local advice.
