# Child Relocation — Moving a Child Out of Jersey

## The starting position

A parent **cannot remove a child from Jersey** for
permanent relocation without:

- **Consent of all PR holders**; or
- A **court order** permitting the relocation.

Holiday travel is different (see
[`parental-responsibility.md`](parental-responsibility.md))
— this file is about **permanent moves**.

## The court's approach

Following *GT v RJ* [2018] EWFC 26 (Mostyn J), adopted in
Jersey decisions including *A v B* [2019] JRC 001A, *C v
D* [2019] JRC 090A, and *LL v MM* [2020] JRC 137:

- **Welfare paramountcy** under Article 2(1) CJL 2002 —
  the only test;
- **No presumption** in favour of the relocating parent;
- **Welfare checklist** applies;
- The court does a **holistic / "360-degree" evaluation**;
- The court's function is **evaluation, not pure
  discretion**.

The older *Payne v Payne* [2001] 1 FLR 1052 "discipline"
(which favoured a primary-carer's reasonable proposal) is
**guidance only**, not mandatory. Jersey courts have moved
beyond Payne.

## Factors the court considers

The court evaluates:

- The **applicant's case** — is it well thought out and
  evidenced? Concrete plans for housing, schooling,
  employment, support network?
- The **motivation** — genuine welfare-driven or
  ulterior?
- The **impact on the left-behind parent** — will
  meaningful contact survive?
- The **child's wishes** (weighted by age and
  understanding);
- The **practical viability** — can the resident parent
  cope abroad?
- The **child's existing relationships** in Jersey;
- The **education** comparison;
- **Cultural / language** considerations;
- **Connection** to the proposed destination.

## What makes an application succeed

- **Well-evidenced plans** — letter from employer, school
  place, family in destination, housing arranged;
- **Genuine motivation** — moving to be near family,
  better career prospects, returning to country of origin;
- **Realistic contact plan** — preserved contact for the
  left-behind parent (long stays in holidays, video
  contact, generous mid-year visits);
- **Child's wishes** aligning (where age-appropriate);
- **Continuity of education and culture** in the new
  location;
- **Support network** in the new location.

## What makes an application fail

- **Vague plans**;
- **Motive driven by spite** or punishment of the
  ex-partner;
- **Destroying contact** without alternative;
- **Disruption** without proportionate benefit;
- **Child** opposed (where age makes that view weighty);
- **Stability** in Jersey being uprooted for marginal
  improvement;
- **Inadequate evidence** of viability.

## Procedure

1. **Specific issue / leave to remove** application;
2. **JFCAS welfare report** — typically ordered;
3. **Evidence** of plans (housing, school, employment);
4. **Disclosure** of finances;
5. **Hearing** — usually substantial (1-3 days for
   contested);
6. **Order** — granted, refused, or conditional (e.g.
   subject to specific contact arrangements).

These applications are **expensive and stressful**.
Mediation is encouraged but resolution is rare without
court.

## Conditional orders

Courts often grant relocation subject to conditions:

- **Specific contact** schedule (holidays, video);
- **Cost-sharing** for the child's travel for contact;
- **Continuing engagement** with Jersey schools / family;
- **Reporting** on the child's adjustment;
- **Mirror orders** in the destination jurisdiction
  (Jersey not being Hague 1996 makes this important).

## The Hague 1996 gap

Jersey is **not party** to the **1996 Hague Convention on
Parental Responsibility and Protection of Children**.
Consequences:

- **Foreign PR orders** may not be recognised in Jersey;
- **Jersey orders** may not be straightforwardly
  recognised abroad;
- **Cross-border** disputes are more complex than in
  Hague jurisdictions;
- **Mirror orders** in the destination state become
  important to ensure ongoing enforceability.

## Abduction — the 1980 Convention

If a parent **unilaterally relocates** a child, the
left-behind parent may invoke the **1980 Hague Convention
on Civil Aspects of International Child Abduction**:

- Jersey **is party** (through UK extension);
- **Return** ordered if removal was wrongful and child is
  under 16;
- **Limited defences** (consent, settled, grave risk of
  harm, child's objection);
- **Speed** is the essence — applications are
  expedited.

Unilateral relocation is a high-risk strategy and exposes
the relocating parent to costs, return orders, and
criminal proceedings in some jurisdictions.

## Pitfalls

- **Unilateral** removal — abduction risk;
- **Inadequate** evidence of plans;
- **Failing** to engage the left-behind parent in
  discussions;
- **Destroying** the left-behind parent's contact in the
  proposed plan;
- **Underestimating** the holistic evaluation — the court
  weighs many factors.
