# Matrimonial Causes (Jersey) Law 1949 — Overview

## What the law does

The **Matrimonial Causes (Jersey) Law 1949** (MCJL) is the
principal Jersey statute on:

- **Divorce** and **judicial separation**;
- **Nullity** of marriage;
- **Ancillary relief** — financial orders on divorce
  (maintenance, lump sums, property transfer, sale, secured
  provision);
- **Jurisdiction** — when Jersey is the proper forum;
- **Decree nisi / decree absolute** — the two-stage
  divorce procedure.

It is **77 years old** as of 2026. Despite that age, it
remains the operative regime — including the **fault
grounds** under Article 7 and the **3-year bar** on
petitioning. A no-fault reform is drafted but **not yet in
force**.

The procedural rules are the **Matrimonial Causes Rules
2005**.

## Jurisdictional gateway

The Royal Court Family Division has jurisdiction over a
divorce petition if **either**:

- **Both parties** are **domiciled in Jersey** when the
  proceedings begin; **or**
- **Either party** is **habitually resident in Jersey
  throughout the year** ending with the petition.

**Nationality is irrelevant.** Jersey is **not** subject
to EU Brussels II — there is no "lis pendens"-based
jurisdiction race; instead, *forum non conveniens*
principles apply, weighing connecting factors (witnesses,
applicable law, parties' habitual residence).

## Structure of the law

| Part / Articles | Topic |
|---|---|
| Articles 1-6 | Definitions, jurisdiction, judicial separation |
| Article 7 | Grounds for divorce (fault and non-fault) |
| Articles 8-15 | Divorce procedure; decree nisi and absolute |
| Articles 16-24 | Nullity, judicial separation |
| Articles 25-31 | Ancillary relief — maintenance, lump sums, property |
| Articles 32-40 | Costs, evidence, miscellaneous |

## Key concepts

### Decree nisi / decree absolute

A Jersey divorce is a **two-stage** procedure:

- **Decree nisi** — the court's provisional decision that
  the divorce should be granted;
- A **wait of at least 6 weeks and 1 day** follows;
- **Decree absolute** — the formal end of the marriage;
  applied for by the petitioner.

A spouse who fails to apply for decree absolute may have
the divorce stalled indefinitely. The respondent can
apply after a further period.

### 3-year bar

A divorce **petition cannot be presented** within **3
years** of the marriage, save in exceptional circumstances
(typically extreme hardship). The bar is intended to
discourage hasty divorces. **Reform pending**.

### Joint applications

**Not currently available.** Jersey law requires one
spouse to be the petitioner and the other the respondent —
an adversarial structure that survives even in
non-contested cases. **Reform pending**.

### Ancillary relief

Financial orders made in conjunction with (or after) the
divorce. The court has broad discretion under Articles
25-31; see [`ancillary-relief.md`](./ancillary-relief.md).

## Available orders

Beyond the decree itself, the court may order:

- **Periodical payments** — child or spousal maintenance,
  including interim;
- **Secured provision** — maintenance secured against
  assets;
- **Lump sum** payments;
- **Transfer or settlement** of property;
- **Sale** of property;
- **Variation** of post-nuptial settlements;
- **Costs** orders;
- **Injunctions** ancillary to financial relief.

The court **cannot** make:

- **Pension sharing orders** — the MCJL does not provide
  for them; offsetting is used instead. **Reform pending**
  to introduce lump-sum payments from pensions.

## Pension treatment

Jersey ancillary relief addresses pensions by **offsetting**
only:

- The pension's actuarial value is determined;
- That value is treated as an asset of the pension-holding
  spouse;
- The non-pension-holding spouse receives compensating
  value from other matrimonial assets.

This produces less satisfactory outcomes than English-
style pension sharing in cases where the pension is the
principal asset (typical of long marriages with one
non-working spouse). The pending reform is designed to
address this.

## Confidentiality

Family proceedings are **almost always in private**:

- **All children proceedings** are in private (Article
  34(2)-related practice);
- **Most ancillary relief** is in private;
- **Decree nisi pronouncements** are typically in public
  but undefended cases are administrative;
- **Judgments** are usually published in **anonymised**
  form;
- **The press has no access** to the Family Division.

## How this compares to English law

The MCJL has its roots in mid-20th-century English
divorce law and many concepts run parallel. But:

- Jersey **retains fault grounds** (England abolished
  them in 2022);
- Jersey **retains the 3-year bar** (England's was at one
  point in s.3 of the MCA 1973);
- Jersey **does not have pension sharing** (a substantial
  divergence);
- Jersey **is not bound by Brussels II / Lugano**;
- Jersey **applies discretion** broadly along
  English / *White v White* / *Miller; McFarlane* lines —
  the *sharing*, *needs*, *compensation* principles are
  recognisable.

Foreign lawyers should not assume English procedure or
relief apply automatically — see
[`../use-cases/international-lawyer/`](../use-cases/international-lawyer/index.md).
