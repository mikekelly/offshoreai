# Parental Responsibility (PR) under the CJL 2002

## What PR is

**Parental responsibility** is the bundle of rights,
duties, powers, and responsibilities a parent has in
relation to a child. It covers:

- **Where the child lives**;
- **Schooling** decisions;
- **Healthcare** decisions (consent to treatment);
- **Religion** and upbringing;
- **Name** changes;
- **Travel** abroad;
- **Discipline** (within lawful limits);
- **Financial provision** for the child;
- **Access** to records (school, medical).

A person with PR may exercise it without consulting
others holding PR — except for certain "major" decisions
where consultation or consent is expected (long-term
relocation, religion change, change of school in some
cases, name change).

## Who has automatic PR

| Status | PR? |
|---|---|
| **Mother** (any) | ✅ Yes — automatic |
| **Father married to mother** at birth | ✅ Yes — automatic |
| **Father unmarried** to mother | Depends — see below |
| **Step-parent** | ❌ No — unless acquired |
| **Civil partner / spouse** of biological mother | ❌ No — unless acquired |
| **Adoptive parents** | ✅ Yes — on adoption order |
| **Special guardian** | ✅ Yes — limited |
| **Local authority / Minister** under care order | ✅ Shared — limited |

## Unmarried fathers

This is the area of most practical difficulty. An unmarried
father has PR if:

- He was **married to the mother** at the child's birth
  (paradoxically — "unmarried at birth" means not married
  *to the mother* at that time, e.g. married to someone
  else); **or**
- The child was **born in Jersey after 2 December 2016**
  **and** the father is **registered on the birth
  certificate**; **or**
- He has entered a **formal Parental Responsibility
  Agreement** with the mother (Jersey-prescribed form,
  filed with the Family Division); **or**
- He has obtained a **court order** conferring PR.

An unmarried father of a **pre-December 2016** child who
did not enter an agreement and has no order has **no PR**
— even if he is on the birth certificate and even if he is
significantly involved in the child's life. This is a
high-risk situation; specialist advice should be sought.

## Acquiring PR — formal agreement

A mother and unmarried father may enter a **Parental
Responsibility Agreement**:

- **Prescribed form** under the CJL Rules;
- **Both parties** sign;
- **Witnessed** as prescribed;
- **Filed** with the Greffier of the Family Division;
- **Effective on filing**.

Once granted, PR is essentially equivalent to PR by birth.
The agreement cannot be revoked unilaterally — only by
court order.

## Acquiring PR — court order

Where the mother will not agree, the father may apply for
a **Parental Responsibility Order**. The court considers:

- The **degree of commitment** the father has shown;
- The **degree of attachment** between father and child;
- The **father's reasons** for applying;
- The **welfare of the child** (paramount).

The court typically grants PR where the father has shown
commitment, even if the mother opposes. The order
confers PR but does not by itself regulate residence or
contact (those need separate orders).

## Acquiring PR — step-parents and partners

A step-parent or civil partner of a child's mother may
acquire PR by:

- **Parental Responsibility Agreement** with all PR
  holders (typically the mother, plus any father with PR);
- **Court order** (similar process to unmarried fathers);
- **Adoption** (in narrow cases).

## Acquiring PR — same-sex parents

For same-sex couples:

- **Civil partners / spouses** of the biological mother
  do **not automatically** have PR;
- Must acquire by **agreement** or **order**;
- **Joint adoption** of a child gives both PR;
- **Surrogacy** — pending Children and Civil Status
  (Amendments) (Jersey) Law 2024.

## Loss of PR

PR is **not lost** by:

- Divorce;
- Separation;
- Imprisonment;
- Relocation;
- Disagreement with the other PR holder.

PR **can be ended** by:

- **Adoption** of the child by others (terminates
  birth-parent PR);
- **Court order** revoking PR (rare — typically only for
  unmarried fathers whose grant was court-ordered);
- **Care order** — local authority gains shared PR but
  birth parents retain theirs.

## Exercise of PR

A PR holder may **exercise PR independently** — they need
not consult others. But:

- **Major decisions** typically require **consultation** —
  relocation abroad, name change, school change,
  religion, serious medical treatment;
- **Disputes** between PR holders go to court for
  **specific issue** or **prohibited steps** orders.

## Practical issues

### Travel abroad with a child

A parent with PR may take the child on holiday, but:

- **Other PR holders** should be informed and ideally
  consent;
- **Permanent relocation** requires consent of all PR
  holders or a court order;
- A parent who removes a child against another PR
  holder's wishes may face **abduction proceedings** (1980
  Hague Convention).

### Schooling decisions

Both PR holders should be consulted on:

- **School choice**;
- **Removal** from a school;
- **Special educational needs** decisions.

Schools should not act unilaterally on the say-so of one
parent against the known wishes of the other.

### Medical decisions

Either PR holder can consent to treatment. But:

- **Major non-emergency** treatment is typically not
  proceeded with against the known objection of another
  PR holder;
- **Specific issue** orders are available for disputes;
- **Emergency** treatment proceeds without consent under
  necessity.

### Disagreements

Where PR holders disagree:

- **Specific issue order** to determine a particular
  question;
- **Prohibited steps order** to restrain;
- **Mediation** as a first step (often required);
- **Court** as last resort.

## Pitfalls

- **Unmarried fathers** assuming they have PR when they
  do not (especially pre-2016 births);
- **Step-parents** acting as parents without formal PR;
- **Civil partners / same-sex spouses** assuming PR
  follows the relationship;
- **Unilateral relocation** of children — major risk.
