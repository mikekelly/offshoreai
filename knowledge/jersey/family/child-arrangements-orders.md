# Child Arrangements Orders under Article 10 CJL 2002

## The four orders

Article 10 of the **Children (Jersey) Law 2002** empowers
the Family Division to make four types of order regulating
a child's care:

1. **Residence order** — with whom the child lives;
2. **Contact order** — when and how the child sees the
   non-resident parent (or other person);
3. **Specific issue order** — a particular disputed
   question (school, religion, name change);
4. **Prohibited steps order** — restraining a step that
   could be taken with parental responsibility (e.g.
   removal from jurisdiction, change of school).

These can be combined. A typical post-separation order
might include residence with one parent, contact for the
other, and a prohibited steps order against unilateral
relocation.

## Who can apply

**Without leave**:

- Parents (married or unmarried, with or without PR);
- Guardians;
- Anyone in whose favour a residence order is already in
  force;
- Anyone the child has lived with for a substantial
  period.

**With leave**:

- Grandparents (typically);
- Other relatives;
- The child themselves in some narrow cases.

## Welfare paramountcy

Every Article 10 order is subject to:

- **Welfare paramountcy** (Article 2(1)) — the child's
  welfare overrides all other considerations;
- **Welfare checklist** (Article 2(3)) — wishes,
  feelings, needs, change effects, harm, capability;
- **No delay** principle (Article 2(2));
- **No order** principle — the court should not make an
  order unless doing so is better than not.

## Residence orders

### What they do

A **residence order** settles with whom the child is to
live. The order:

- **Confers PR** on the person granted residence (if they
  didn't already have it);
- **Prevents** removal from Jersey without consent or
  further order (statutory prohibition);
- **Prevents** change of surname without consent;
- **Does not** exclude the other parent from PR.

### Shared residence

Increasingly common. **Shared residence orders** state
that the child lives with both parents on a defined
pattern (alternating weeks, school days/weekends, etc.).
Practical implications:

- Both parents are **equal residence holders**;
- **Schools and authorities** treat both as residents;
- **State benefits** may follow the actual time split.

### What it doesn't do

A residence order does **not**:

- Give the resident parent **exclusive** rights over
  upbringing — the other parent retains PR;
- Permit **relocation abroad** without consent or order;
- Override **public-law** care orders.

## Contact orders

### What they do

A **contact order** regulates the time the child spends
with a person (usually the non-resident parent, but may
also be grandparents or others).

Forms of contact:

- **Direct** — face-to-face;
- **Staying** — overnight or longer;
- **Visiting** — for the day;
- **Indirect** — phone, video, letters;
- **Supervised** — at a contact centre or with a third
  party present (where there are safety concerns).

### The presumption

There is **no automatic presumption** of any particular
contact pattern. But the court typically expects:

- **A continuing relationship** with both parents wherever
  safe and appropriate;
- **Regular, predictable contact** in line with the
  child's age and routines;
- **Adjustments** as the child grows.

### Where there are safety concerns

Where there is alleged domestic abuse, substance abuse,
or other safety risk:

- **Fact-finding hearing** to determine what occurred;
- **Risk assessment** — typically JFCAS-led;
- **Supervised contact** as an interim measure;
- **Indirect contact** in severe cases;
- **No contact** in extreme cases.

The court treats safety as the priority and is now
sceptical of "automatic contact" arguments where there
are documented risks.

## Specific issue orders

A specific issue order resolves a **particular disputed
question**. Examples:

- **School choice** — when parents disagree;
- **Religion** — particular faith, particular
  observances;
- **Medical treatment** — non-emergency where parents
  disagree;
- **Vaccination** — particularly contentious in some
  cases;
- **Name change** — surname after divorce;
- **Travel abroad** — a specific trip;
- **Therapy / counselling** — particularly with
  separated parents.

## Prohibited steps orders

A prohibited steps order **restrains** a step that could
be taken with PR. Examples:

- **Removal from Jersey** — most common;
- **Change of school** without consent;
- **Surname change**;
- **Specific contacts** (e.g. a particular relative or
  associate considered harmful);
- **Religious indoctrination** in extreme cases;
- **Surgery / cosmetic procedures** on the child.

## Procedure

Typical procedure:

1. **Application** filed (Form prescribed under the CJL
   Rules);
2. **First hearing** — often a directions hearing;
3. **JFCAS welfare report** ordered if facts contested;
4. **Fact-finding hearing** if necessary (e.g. domestic-
   abuse allegations);
5. **Substantive hearing** — evidence, submissions,
   judgment;
6. **Order** sealed.

**Contested orders** can take **6-18 months** depending on
complexity, especially where JFCAS reports and fact-
finding are needed.

## The role of JFCAS

In contested children matters:

- **JFCAS officer** typically appointed to prepare a
  welfare report;
- **Child interviewed** age-appropriately;
- **Both parents** interviewed;
- **Other relevant adults** (grandparents, schools)
  consulted;
- **Recommendations** to the court;
- **Guardian** appointment in complex cases.

JFCAS officers are usually social workers with family-
proceedings training. Their recommendations carry
weight.

## Variation and termination

Orders may be **varied** on application:

- **Change of circumstances** — significant change;
- **Children growing up** — older children's preferences
  weighted more heavily;
- **Welfare** continues to be paramount.

Orders typically **end** when the child reaches **18**, or
earlier in narrow cases (older teenagers' preferences may
make orders practically unenforceable).

## Pitfalls

- **Unilateral relocation** — risks abduction proceedings;
- **Withholding contact** to punish the other parent —
  courts react adversely;
- **Coaching children** — readily detected by JFCAS;
- **Ignoring** orders — contempt;
- **Failing** to engage in mediation before litigating;
- **Underestimating** legal costs in contested cases.
