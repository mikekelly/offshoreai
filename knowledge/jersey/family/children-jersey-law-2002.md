# Children (Jersey) Law 2002 — Overview

## What the law does

The **Children (Jersey) Law 2002** (CJL 2002) is the
principal Jersey statute on:

- **Parental responsibility** — who has it, how it's
  acquired;
- **Child arrangements** — residence, contact, specific
  issue, prohibited steps orders;
- **Care and supervision** — public-law protection of
  children at risk;
- **The welfare paramountcy principle**;
- **The welfare checklist** the court must consider;
- **Adoption-related** provisions;
- **Schedule 1** — financial provision for children
  outside divorce.

It is closely modelled on the **English Children Act 1989**
and shares much of its terminology — but Jersey case law
applies its own development.

## The paramountcy principle

**Article 2(1)**: the **child's welfare** is the court's
**paramount consideration** in any decision under the
Law.

This is the single most important principle in Jersey
family law touching children. It overrides:

- The parents' wishes;
- Adult convenience;
- Procedural neatness;
- Any presumption in favour of either parent.

## The welfare checklist

**Article 2(3)** requires the court to have particular
regard to:

- The **child's ascertainable wishes and feelings** —
  weighted by age and understanding;
- The **child's physical, emotional, and educational
  needs**;
- The **likely effect** on the child of any change in
  circumstances;
- The child's **age, sex, background**, and any relevant
  characteristics;
- Any **harm** the child has suffered or is at risk of
  suffering;
- The **capability** of each parent (and any other
  relevant person) to meet the child's needs;
- The **range of powers available** to the court.

The checklist applies to **all** Law-based decisions
touching the child.

## The "no delay" principle

Article 2(2): **delay** in determining questions about a
child's upbringing is **likely to prejudice** the child's
welfare.

The court therefore aims for prompt resolution, with
timetabling discipline.

| Schedule 2 | Care plan requirements |

## Key concepts

### Parental responsibility (PR)

The bundle of **rights, duties, powers, and
responsibilities** that a parent has in relation to a
child. Includes the right to be consulted on major
decisions (schooling, healthcare, religion, residence) and
the responsibility to provide for the child. See
[`parental-responsibility.md`](./parental-responsibility.md).

### Child arrangements orders (Article 10)

The court orders that regulate where a child lives, who
they see, what they do (or do not do), and specific
issues. Four types:

- **Residence** order — who the child lives with;
- **Contact** order — when and how the child sees the
  non-resident parent;
- **Specific issue** order — a particular disputed
  question (school choice, religion, name change);
- **Prohibited steps** order — restraining a parent from
  doing something.

See [`child-arrangements-orders.md`](./child-arrangements-orders.md).

### Care and supervision (Part 4)

When a child is at risk of significant harm, the local
authority (or, in Jersey, the Minister for Children) may
seek:

- A **care order** — child taken into the care of the
  Minister;
- A **supervision order** — child remains at home but
  under supervision;
- An **emergency protection order** — urgent short-term
  protection.

The threshold test is **significant harm**, attributable
either to inadequate parenting or beyond parental
control. See [`care-and-supervision.md`](./care-and-supervision.md).

## Jurisdiction

The Family Division has jurisdiction in children matters
under the CJL 2002 if:

- The child is **habitually resident in Jersey**; or
- The child is **present in Jersey, not habitually
  resident elsewhere or in the UK**, and immediate
  exercise of power is required for welfare; or
- The parents' **matrimonial proceedings are continuing**
  in Jersey.

These rules are set out separately in the **Child Custody
(Jurisdiction) (Jersey) Law 2005**.

## The role of JFCAS

The **Jersey Family Court Advisory Service** (JFCAS):

- Prepares **welfare reports** in contested children
  cases;
- Captures the **child's wishes and feelings** through
  age-appropriate interviewing;
- May be appointed **guardian ad litem** in complex cases;
- Assists with **mediation referrals**.

Family Division judges may also meet with the child
directly in appropriate cases.

## Hague Convention and recognition

A crucial gap: **Jersey is not party to the 1996 Hague
Convention on Parental Responsibility and Protection of
Children**. The consequence:

- **Foreign parental-responsibility orders** may not be
  recognised in Jersey without further proceedings;
- **Cross-border** children disputes are more complex than
  in jurisdictions that are Hague-party;
- **Practical effect** — international families need to
  consider Jersey orders if the children spend
  significant time here.

Jersey **is** party (through UK extension) to:

- **1980 Hague Convention** on the Civil Aspects of
  International Child Abduction;
- **Hague Service Convention**;
- **Hague Evidence Convention**.

## Confidentiality

Children proceedings are conducted in **private**:

- **Closed court** — no public, no press;
- **Anonymised judgments** for any published decisions;
- **Strict** restrictions on disclosure outside the
  proceedings;
- Contempt sanctions for breaches.

## Reform pipeline

The **Children and Civil Status (Amendments) (Jersey) Law
2024** is drafted but not yet in force. It will:

- Provide for **parental orders** in surrogacy
  arrangements;
- Clarify legal status of children born via fertility
  treatment;
- Other modernisations.
