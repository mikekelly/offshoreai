# Ancillary Relief — Splitting Assets on Divorce

## What "ancillary relief" means

Ancillary relief is the set of **financial orders** the
Royal Court may make alongside (or after) a divorce
decree:

- **Maintenance** — interim, periodic, secured;
- **Lump sum** payments;
- **Transfer or settlement** of property;
- **Sale** of property;
- **Variation** of post-nuptial settlements (including
  trusts that are nuptial in character);
- **Cost orders** ancillary to the above.

The court does **not** make:

- **Pension sharing orders** — not in the MCJL;
- **Pension lump-sum orders** — reform pending;
- **Property orders** affecting third-party assets except
  through nuptial-settlement variation.

## Statutory powers

Articles 25-31 of the MCJL 1949 set out the powers. The
court's discretion is broad — it considers **all the
circumstances** of the case.

## The discretionary factors

Following the English authorities adopted by the Royal
Court (*White v White*, *Miller; McFarlane*, *Charman*),
the court considers:

- **Income, earning capacity, property, and financial
  resources** — present and foreseeable;
- **Financial needs, obligations, and responsibilities**;
- **Standard of living** before the breakdown;
- **Age** of the parties and **duration** of the marriage;
- **Physical and mental disability** of either party or
  any child;
- **Contributions to family welfare** — including
  childcare and homemaking (treated equally with
  breadwinning);
- **Conduct** — only if conduct is so extreme it would be
  inequitable to disregard it;
- **Loss of benefit** — e.g. pension rights forfeited on
  divorce.

**The welfare of any minor child is paramount.**

## The sharing principle

Following *White v White* and *Miller; McFarlane*,
applied in Jersey:

- **Matrimonial acquest** (assets acquired during the
  marriage from joint efforts) starts with a presumption
  of **50:50 sharing**;
- **Non-matrimonial assets** (pre-marital wealth,
  inherited assets, gifts to one spouse) are not in
  principle subject to sharing;
- **Departures from equal** are justified by **needs**
  (one party's needs exceed half-share);
- **Non-matrimonial assets may be drawn upon** to meet
  needs.

The sharing principle does **not extend to income** —
post-divorce earnings of a spouse are not shared.

## Pensions — the Jersey gap

Pension sharing is **not available** in Jersey. The court
addresses pensions by **offsetting**:

- The pension's **actuarial / CETV value** is determined;
- That value is treated as an asset of the pension-
  holding spouse;
- The **non-pension-holding spouse** receives
  compensating value from **other matrimonial assets**.

This produces less satisfactory outcomes than English-
style pension sharing where:

- The pension is the **principal asset** of a long
  marriage;
- There is **no liquid matrimonial wealth** to offset;
- A **clean break** is desired but pension is locked.

**Reform pending**: a draft law would introduce **lump-
sum orders from pensions**, but full pension-sharing
orders are not in prospect.

## Needs

Where matrimonial assets are insufficient for both
parties' needs at a 50:50 split, the court considers
**needs** — broadly:

- **Housing** — including dependent children;
- **Income** — sufficient for reasonable standard of
  living;
- **Childcare** — direct costs and lost earning capacity;
- **Future security** — particularly for the older or
  health-affected spouse.

**Need-based departures** typically favour the financially
weaker spouse and can take their share above 50%.

## Compensation

Where one spouse has suffered **economic disadvantage**
because of decisions made during the marriage (typically
a career sacrifice for childcare), the court may make a
**compensation-based** award. This is conceptually
distinct from sharing — though in practice they often
overlap.

## Clean break

The court has a duty to consider whether a **clean break**
is appropriate:

- **Single capital adjustment** ending all financial ties;
- **Time-limited maintenance** terminating on a specified
  date;
- **Deferred clean break** — capital adjustment now,
  maintenance for a limited period.

Clean breaks are encouraged where feasible. Lifetime
maintenance orders are now rare.

## Worldwide assets

The court considers **assets worldwide**:

- **Jersey assets** — direct jurisdiction;
- **English / Welsh / Scottish / NI assets** — typically
  amenable to mirror orders;
- **EU and other foreign assets** — enforcement requires
  cooperation; **mirror orders** in the foreign court
  often needed;
- **Trust assets** — depends on the nature of the trust
  (nuptial settlement may be variable; bare trust
  treated as property; firewall protections for
  irrevocable third-party trusts).

## Trusts in ancillary relief

A critical Jersey issue:

- **Nuptial settlements** — trusts created during or in
  contemplation of the marriage for the parties' benefit
  — can be **varied** by the Family Division;
- **Non-nuptial trusts** — third-party trusts not
  primarily for the parties' benefit — **firewall**
  protections under Article 9 TJL apply; the trust assets
  may not be reachable;
- **Sham trusts** — courts may pierce the structure;
- **Asset of either party** — if a spouse has substantial
  expectation under a discretionary trust, the court may
  treat that as a financial resource.

See [`../trusts/firewall.md`](../trusts/firewall.md).

## Interim relief

Pending the substantive order, the court may grant:

- **Interim maintenance** — for living costs;
- **Interim legal costs** — under Article 31, where
  alternative funding has been explored;
- **Injunctions** — preserving assets pending resolution.

## Procedure

Standard procedure (compressed):

1. **Application** for ancillary relief in the petition
   or by separate notice;
2. **Form E** financial disclosure by both parties;
3. **First Appointment** — case management;
4. **Financial Dispute Resolution (FDR)** hearing —
   non-binding judicial indication encouraging settlement;
5. **Final hearing** if not settled;
6. **Court order** — sealed and binding.

Many cases settle at or after FDR. A contested final
hearing is the **last resort** and the most expensive
option.

## Costs

- **Each party** typically bears own costs in financial
  proceedings;
- **Costs orders** rare absent litigation misconduct;
- **Total costs** can be substantial — six figures in
  contested high-value cases.

## Pitfalls

- **Failing** to value pensions fairly given the
  offsetting regime;
- **Ignoring** trust interests in the disclosure stage —
  the court takes a dim view of incomplete disclosure;
- **Misjudging** non-matrimonial vs matrimonial assets;
- **Pre-empting** decree absolute before financial
  settlement — risks losing succession rights;
- **Underestimating** legal costs.
