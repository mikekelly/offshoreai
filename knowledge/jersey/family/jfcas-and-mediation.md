# JFCAS and Family Mediation in Jersey

## JFCAS — the Jersey Family Court Advisory Service

JFCAS provides **independent welfare assessment** in
children proceedings. JFCAS officers are typically
qualified social workers with family-proceedings
training.

### Functions

- **Welfare reports** for contested children matters;
- **Child's wishes and feelings** captured through
  age-appropriate interviewing;
- **Guardian ad litem** appointments in public-law cases
  (care and supervision);
- **Direct meetings** with children where the judge
  considers it appropriate;
- **Liaison** between the court and the child / family;
- **Recommendations** to the court (typically respected
  but not binding).

### When the court orders a JFCAS report

Typically in:

- **Contested child arrangements** orders;
- **Relocation** applications;
- **Allegations of harm** that need investigation;
- **Public-law** cases (always — JFCAS officer is the
  guardian);
- **Specific issue** orders touching child welfare.

### What the report covers

A JFCAS report typically includes:

- Interviews with **each parent**;
- Interview with the **child** (age-appropriate);
- Observation of the child with each parent where
  practicable;
- Contact with **schools** and other relevant adults;
- An **assessment** against the welfare checklist;
- **Recommendations** for the order to be made.

## Mediation and ADR

Jersey has well-developed family mediation. Forms
available:

### Family Foundation mediation

A **free** mediation service:

- Mediators include **judges**, **Family Proceedings
  Officers**, and **JFCAS officers**;
- **Voluntary** but encouraged by the court;
- **Non-binding** — agreements reached are not strictly
  binding but typically respected;
- **Effective** for many cases — particularly children
  matters and lower-value financial matters.

### Private mediation

- **Private family mediators** offering paid services;
- Often **specialist** (financial issues, complex
  children matters);
- **Faster** than court proceedings.

### Financial Dispute Resolution (FDR)

A **judge-led** non-binding settlement hearing:

- **Court-led FDR** — free, with a Family Judge giving
  non-binding indication;
- **Private FDR** — paid; often with English family
  barristers acting as FDR judge;
- **Without prejudice** — discussions cannot be used in
  evidence;
- **Many cases settle** at FDR.

### Direct negotiation

Solicitor-led negotiation through correspondence.
Effective for cooperative cases.

## Mandatory NCDR — reform pending

A pending draft law would require parties to **engage in
non-court dispute resolution** before applying to court.
Until enacted, ADR remains **voluntary** but heavily
encouraged.

The court can already:

- **Refer** parties to mediation;
- **Stay** proceedings to allow mediation;
- **Penalise** parties who refuse reasonable ADR
  through costs orders.

## Effect of mediation on litigation

- Agreements at mediation can be **incorporated into a
  court order** by consent;
- Discussions are typically **without prejudice** —
  cannot be referred to in court;
- **Lack of agreement** at mediation does not prevent
  subsequent litigation;
- A party who **refuses** mediation may face costs
  consequences.

## Practical recommendations

For separating families:

- **Engage early** with mediation — the longer parties
  litigate, the more entrenched positions become;
- **Consider** the cost — mediation is typically much
  cheaper than litigation;
- **Specialist mediators** for specific issues
  (financial, children);
- **Don't expect** mediation to be appropriate where
  there is domestic abuse, severe power imbalance, or
  safety concerns.

For litigators:

- **Refer clients** to ADR routinely;
- **Document** mediation attempts (relevant for costs);
- **Use FDR** strategically — often the most efficient
  settlement forum.
