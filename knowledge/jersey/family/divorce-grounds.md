# Divorce Grounds under Article 7 MCJL 1949

## The seven grounds

Article 7 sets out **fault-based** and **non-fault-based**
grounds. Until the pending no-fault reform comes into
force, a petitioner must rely on one of these.

### Fault-based grounds

1. **Adultery** (with intolerability) — committed by the
   respondent; the petitioner must find it intolerable to
   live with the respondent. **Opposite-sex only** under
   the statute (a gap reflecting the law's age, now
   anomalous since same-sex marriage was introduced in
   2018).
2. **Desertion** — by the respondent for **at least 2
   years** immediately preceding the petition. Desertion
   requires absence + intent + without consent + without
   reasonable cause.
3. **Unreasonable behaviour** — the respondent has
   behaved in such a way that the petitioner cannot
   reasonably be expected to live with them. The most
   commonly relied-on ground in practice; covers a wide
   spectrum from violence and verbal abuse to coldness,
   neglect, financial irresponsibility, addiction.
4. **Incurable unsound mind** — the respondent has been
   continuously under care for incurable mental illness
   for **5 years** before the petition.
5. **Imprisonment** — the respondent is serving **life
   imprisonment** or a sentence of **15 years or more**.

### Non-fault grounds (require separation)

6. **One year's separation with consent** — the parties
   have lived apart for at least **1 year** and the
   respondent consents to the divorce.
7. **Two years' separation without consent** — the parties
   have lived apart for at least **2 years**, regardless
   of consent.

## Article 7 in practice

The vast majority of Jersey divorces proceed on:

- **Unreasonable behaviour** (most common — used in
  arguably mild cases);
- **2 years' separation** (consent-based) — typical for
  cooperative separations;
- **5 years' separation** — would apply in old-style
  reform but Jersey currently has 2 years not 5.

Adultery is rarely pleaded except where central. The
unsound mind and imprisonment grounds are rare.

## The 3-year bar

A divorce petition **cannot be presented within 3 years**
of the marriage, save in **exceptional circumstances** —
typically:

- **Extreme hardship** to the petitioner;
- **Exceptional depravity** of the respondent;
- **Court's discretion** on application.

The bar applies to **petitioning**, not to the events
relied on. A petitioner waiting out the 3 years may rely
on conduct that occurred earlier.

## Living apart — what counts?

For non-fault grounds, "living apart" is required:

- Physical separation — typically two households;
- May exist within one household in some narrow cases
  (separate rooms, separate finances, no shared life);
- **Brief reconciliations** of up to 6 months don't break
  the period but extend it by their duration;
- Documented through bank records, council/parish
  records, separate addresses, evidence of separate
  lives.

## Unreasonable behaviour — what works

The test is **objective**: can a reasonable person be
expected to live with this respondent given the
petitioner's character and circumstances?

Common pleadings:

- **Violence** — physical, sexual, emotional;
- **Verbal abuse** — sustained, demeaning;
- **Financial control** — limiting access to money,
  coercive financial conduct;
- **Coldness** — withholding affection, refusing
  communication;
- **Substance abuse**;
- **Gambling addiction** with family-financial impact;
- **Lack of intimacy** for a substantial period;
- **Workaholic behaviour** to family-detriment;
- **Mental cruelty**.

The pleading is typically a paragraph or two of specific
examples. The respondent may admit, deny, or simply not
defend.

## Adultery — what's required

Three elements:

- **Sexual intercourse** between the respondent and a
  person of the opposite sex (current statutory wording);
- **The petitioner's intolerability** to continue;
- **Within the 6 months** preceding petition (typically
  — the petitioner cannot "wait out" adultery and then
  petition years later).

The third party need not be named in the petition.

## Desertion — what's required

- **Physical separation** — the respondent has left;
- **Intent** to desert permanently;
- **No consent** from the petitioner;
- **No reasonable cause** for leaving (a respondent who
  fled domestic violence is not in desertion);
- **At least 2 years** unbroken.

Rare in practice because the alternative grounds
(unreasonable behaviour, 2-year separation) are easier.

## Defended divorces

A respondent who disputes the ground may file an
**Answer** defending the petition. This is uncommon
because:

- It is expensive;
- It risks worse findings at trial;
- It rarely changes the outcome (separation periods
  become available); and
- Costs typically follow the unsuccessful defence.

Defended divorces are essentially limited to nullity
disputes, jurisdiction disputes, and contested
unreasonable-behaviour cases where the respondent fears
adverse findings affecting other matters (e.g. a parallel
employment matter, criminal proceedings).

## No-fault divorce — reform status

A no-fault divorce regime is **drafted** and would:

- **Remove** all fault grounds (adultery, desertion,
  unreasonable behaviour, unsound mind, imprisonment);
- **Remove** the 3-year bar;
- **Permit** joint applications;
- **Align** Jersey with England and Wales (where no-fault
  divorce has been in force since 2022).

The reform is **not yet enacted**. Until it is, the
fault-based regime applies.
