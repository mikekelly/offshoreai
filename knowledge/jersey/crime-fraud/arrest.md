# Arrest under the PPCE 2003

## Powers of arrest

Police (both States and Honorary) may arrest a person:

- With **reasonable grounds for suspecting** the person
  is committing, has committed, or is about to commit an
  offence;
- Under **statutory** arrest powers attached to specific
  offences;
- Under **common-law** powers in narrow contexts (e.g.
  breach of the peace);
- Under a **warrant of arrest** issued by the Magistrate's
  Court.

## "Reasonable grounds"

The reasonable-grounds test:

- **Objective** — the officer must have actual grounds, not
  mere suspicion;
- **Specific to the person** — not general suspicion of
  an area;
- **Reviewable** — by the custody officer at the station,
  and by a court if challenged.

Common factual bases:

- **Eye-witness** identification;
- **Direct observation** of conduct;
- **Information from a credible source**;
- **Possession** of items linking to an offence;
- **Behaviour** consistent with committing an offence.

## What the officer must do at arrest

On arrest, the officer must:

- **Tell the person they are under arrest**;
- **State the offence** for which they are arrested;
- **Caution** the person:
  > "You are not obliged to say anything unless you wish
  > to do so, but what you say may be put into writing
  > and given in evidence";
- **Identify themselves** (uniform / warrant card);
- **Use no more force than necessary**.

Failure to follow these requirements may render the
arrest **unlawful** and provide grounds for:

- Exclusion of subsequent evidence;
- Civil claims (wrongful arrest, false imprisonment);
- Internal disciplinary action.

## Citizen's arrest

A **private citizen** may also arrest:

- Where they **witness** an indictable offence in
  progress;
- Where they have **reasonable grounds** to suspect a
  person of having committed an indictable offence;
- **Limited to** specific circumstances — not a general
  arrest power equivalent to the police.

A citizen who exceeds this power may face civil and
criminal consequences.

## After arrest

The arrested person must be:

- **Taken to a police station** as soon as practicable
  (typically the nearest designated station);
- **Presented to a custody officer**;
- **Booked in** with full record of arrest, time, reasons.

A **custody officer** (an independent officer) then
decides whether detention is lawful.

## Search incident to arrest

On arrest, the officer may search the person for:

- **Weapons** that could assist escape or harm;
- **Evidence** relating to the suspected offence;
- **Items** that could assist commission of further
  offences.

The search must be:

- **Proportionate** to the suspected offence;
- **Recorded** in the officer's notes;
- **Conducted appropriately** (strip searches by same-sex
  officer, in private).

## Use of force

Force used in arrest must be:

- **Reasonable** in all the circumstances;
- **Proportionate** to the resistance encountered;
- **Necessary** to effect the arrest.

Excessive force is unlawful and may result in:

- **Civil claims** (battery, false imprisonment);
- **Criminal proceedings** against the officer (assault,
  causing actual or grievous bodily harm in extreme
  cases);
- **Disciplinary** action;
- **Exclusion** of resulting evidence at trial.

## Arrest by Honorary Police

The Honorary Police (Centeniers, Vingteniers, Constable's
Officers) have **full arrest powers** under the PPCE
2003. An arrest by an honorary officer is legally
identical to one by a States officer.

In practice:

- Honorary Police more commonly handle parish-level
  matters;
- States Police handle serious investigations;
- Both forces work jointly on many cases.

See [`../road-traffic/honorary-police.md`](../road-traffic/honorary-police.md).

## Arrest of juveniles

A juvenile (under 18):

- Can be arrested under the same powers;
- **Parent / guardian** must be notified at the earliest
  opportunity;
- **Appropriate adult** required before interview;
- **Detention** in specific juvenile facilities where
  practicable;
- See [`youth-justice.md`](youth-justice.md).

## Arrest of vulnerable adults

For adults with learning difficulties, mental-health
issues, or other vulnerabilities:

- **Appropriate adult** required for interview;
- **Medical** examination if needed;
- **Specific** safeguards in detention.

## Voluntary attendance

A person **not under arrest** may attend the police
station voluntarily for interview. Key points:

- **Free to leave** at any time;
- **Not in custody** (no PPCE detention rules apply in
  the same way);
- **Still entitled** to legal advice;
- **Still cautioned** if questioned about an offence;
- If detention becomes appropriate, **must be arrested**
  formally — cannot simply continue interview without
  arrest.

A common protocol where the State wants information but
not custody.

## What to do if arrested

Practical advice:

1. **Stay calm**;
2. **Identify yourself** truthfully (refusal to identify
   may be its own offence in some contexts);
3. **Don't resist physically** — challenge unlawful
   arrest through proper channels later;
4. **Don't answer questions** beyond your name and basic
   identifying information;
5. **Ask for a solicitor immediately**;
6. **Note times and names** if you can;
7. **Use your phone call** to inform someone of your
   arrest;
8. **Don't sign anything** without legal advice.
