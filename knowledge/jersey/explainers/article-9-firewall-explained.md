# The Article 9 Firewall — What It Is and Why It Matters

## The short version

The "Article 9 firewall" is a rule in Jersey trust law that
says: **Jersey law decides whether a Jersey trust is valid
and what it can do — even if a court somewhere else tries to
apply that other place's law.**

It's the single most important reason internationally-mobile
families use Jersey trusts.

## What problem it solves

Imagine you're an Italian businessman, tax-resident in
Dubai, with children in London and São Paulo, and a
substantial business you want to leave to specific children
in specific ways. Italian law says **forced heirship** — your
children automatically inherit defined fractions of your
estate, regardless of what you want. UAE Sharia rules say
fixed-share inheritance. Brazilian law has its own rules.
English law has different rules again.

You'd like to put your wealth in a trust so it gets
distributed your way, not according to any one country's
default inheritance rules. **Which country's law decides
whether your trust is valid?**

Without a firewall, an Italian court could decide that
Italian forced-heirship applies to your Jersey trust and
that the trust is invalid in respect of the part that
violates forced-heirship. Same for a Sharia court in the
UAE, or any other forced-heirship jurisdiction.

The Article 9 firewall is Jersey's answer: **Jersey law
applies to a Jersey trust. Foreign forced-heirship rules
don't override it.**

## What Article 9 actually says

The detail lives in
[Article 9 of the Trusts (Jersey) Law 1984](../trusts/firewall.md).
Key elements:

- A Jersey trust's **validity** is governed by Jersey law;
- The **rights and powers** of the parties to a Jersey
  trust are governed by Jersey law;
- The trust **cannot be set aside** because of foreign-law
  rights of forced heirship;
- Foreign judgments against a Jersey trust on these grounds
  are **not recognised** in Jersey.

Anti-forced-heirship is the headline use, but the firewall
covers more than that — it also protects against:

- Matrimonial-property claims under foreign law that would
  treat trust assets as marital property;
- Sham-trust attacks under foreign-law standards;
- Foreign-court orders purporting to direct trustee action.

## What the firewall does NOT do

The firewall protects against **specific kinds of foreign
attack on trust validity**. It does **not**:

- **Hide assets from tax authorities** — CRS reporting still
  works through Jersey trusts to the beneficiary's home
  jurisdiction;
- **Protect against everything** — fraudulent-conveyance
  claims, claims by creditors of the settlor at the time
  of settlement, and direct claims against assets situated
  in the claimant's jurisdiction are different issues;
- **Stop foreign-court judgments existing** — a foreign
  court can still rule against the trust under its own law;
  the firewall means Jersey won't enforce that judgment
  against the trust assets in Jersey;
- **Work for non-Jersey-law trusts** — only Jersey trusts
  governed by Jersey law have Article 9 protection.

## In practice — how the firewall is used

Three operational realities:

### 1. Trust assets are typically held in Jersey

Trust assets typically sit in Jersey-incorporated companies
held by the trust, with custody / bank accounts in Jersey
or other jurisdictions that recognise Jersey-law trust
positions. The firewall is strongest where the assets are
**reachable only through Jersey legal process**.

### 2. The trustee is Jersey-resident

A Jersey trust company is the trustee. Any attempt to compel
trustee action goes through Jersey's Royal Court — which
applies Article 9.

### 3. Foreign judgments need Jersey-court enforcement

If a foreign court rules against the trust, the claimant
typically needs Jersey-court enforcement to get to the
assets. Jersey's courts won't enforce judgments inconsistent
with Article 9.

In practice, **most foreign claimants don't even start the
litigation** once their lawyers advise them about the
firewall — the structural certainty is its deterrent value.

## Common challenges to the firewall

The firewall is **fact-sensitive**. Common challenges:

- **The settlor's domicile / residence** at the time of the
  trust — strong cases have the settlor in a jurisdiction
  where the trust is acceptable; weak cases have a settlor
  whose home jurisdiction would say the structure was a
  sham.
- **Genuine settlement** — a real transfer of legal
  ownership at the time of settlement, not a paper-only
  arrangement.
- **Family-circumstance timing** — settling assets to avoid
  imminent divorce, imminent creditor claim, or imminent
  forced-heirship event is weaker than settling them
  pre-emptively.

A well-structured Jersey trust addresses these at the design
stage. A poorly-structured one is more vulnerable than the
firewall might suggest.

## What to ask your adviser

Three good questions:

1. **Is my home jurisdiction's forced-heirship regime one
   the firewall has been tested against?** (Some
   jurisdictions have generated case-law in Jersey; others
   have not.)
2. **What evidence are we preserving to demonstrate the
   genuineness of the settlement?**
3. **What's the position if I move countries before / after
   the trust is established?**
