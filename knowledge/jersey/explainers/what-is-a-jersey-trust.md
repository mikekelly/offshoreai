# What Is a Jersey Trust?

## The short answer

A trust is a legal arrangement where someone (the **settlor**)
gives assets to a **trustee** to look after for the benefit
of other people (the **beneficiaries**) or for a defined
purpose. A **Jersey trust** is a trust governed by Jersey
law — under the **[Trusts (Jersey) Law 1984](../trusts/trusts-law-1984.md)**
— with three features that make it useful for international
wealth-planning:

1. **A "firewall" that makes Jersey law decide who's right** —
   even if someone in another country tries to challenge the
   trust under their own country's rules.
2. **The settlor can keep some control** without breaking the
   trust — through carefully-defined "reserved powers".
3. **A specialist court** (the Royal Court) that has built up
   30+ years of trust case-law and understands the structure
   in depth.

## The three roles

Every trust has three roles. In a Jersey trust:

- **Settlor** — the person putting assets into the trust.
  Often the wealthy individual or family principal.
- **Trustee** — the person who holds and manages the assets,
  with legal ownership of them. Almost always a regulated
  Jersey trust company (a "TCB" — Trust Company Business).
- **Beneficiaries** — the people who benefit from the
  trust. Often the settlor's family, sometimes including
  the settlor themselves.

Two further roles are common but optional:

- **Protector** — someone with a defined role overseeing the
  trustee (often a family member or trusted adviser);
- **Letter of wishes** — a non-binding document the settlor
  writes to guide the trustee's decisions.

## How the assets are held

The trustee is the **legal owner** of the trust assets. The
beneficiaries are the **beneficial owners**. This separation
is the core of the trust — it's why a trust can do things a
direct ownership arrangement can't, like protecting wealth
from a single point of failure (divorce, bankruptcy, foreign
inheritance claims).

In practice, the trustee often holds the assets through
**Jersey companies the trust owns**. So the asset chain
looks like:

`asset → Jersey company → Jersey trust → beneficiaries`

This layering makes administration cleaner and lets different
asset types be held in different companies.

## What types of Jersey trust exist

Several flavours:

- **Discretionary trust** — the most common. The trustee
  decides which beneficiaries get what, and when.
- **Fixed-interest trust** — beneficiaries have defined
  entitlements (e.g. "income to A for life, capital to B on
  A's death").
- **Reserved-powers trust** — the settlor retains specific
  powers (investment direction, beneficiary changes,
  trustee removal). These powers are defined by Jersey law
  in
  [Article 9A](../trusts/reserved-powers.md), so retaining
  them doesn't break the trust.
- **Purpose trust** — a trust for a defined purpose, not for
  named beneficiaries.

For most international wealth-planning, the **discretionary
trust with reserved powers** is the typical choice.

## What makes the Jersey trust distinctive

### The firewall — Article 9

When a Jersey trust is challenged under another country's
law (a foreign court claims someone's forced-heirship right
should override the trust, for example), Jersey law has a
defined rule. Briefly: **Jersey law decides whether the
trust is valid**, not the foreign law. Foreign forced-
heirship rules don't override the trust.

This is the **[Article 9 firewall](../trusts/firewall.md)**.
It's the single most-cited feature of Jersey trust law in
international wealth-planning.

### Reserved powers — Article 9A

Settlors often want to keep some control — over investments,
over beneficiary distributions, over the choice of trustee.
Some legal systems treat such retained powers as breaking
the trust ("if you still control it, it isn't really a
trust"). Jersey law takes a different view: specific powers,
defined in
[Article 9A](../trusts/reserved-powers.md), can be retained
without breaking the trust.

This makes Jersey particularly suitable for clients who want
trust-style succession protection without giving up all
control.

### A specialist court — the Royal Court

The Royal Court of Jersey has a dedicated trust-law
jurisdiction. Trustees can ask the Court for **directions**
on important decisions
([Article 51](../trusts/article-51-directions.md)) — a kind
of pre-clearance for tricky decisions. The Court can also
**set aside** a decision a trustee made under a mistake
([Article 47](../trusts/article-47-set-aside.md)) — a remedy
that's wider in Jersey than under English law.

These mechanisms make Jersey trusts more robust in practice
than trusts in jurisdictions without comparable court depth.

## What Jersey trusts are not

- **Not** a way to hide ownership from tax authorities. The
  trust's beneficial owners are reported under
  [CRS](../international/crs.md) and (for US persons)
  [FATCA](../international/fatca.md).
- **Not** automatically tax-efficient. Whether a trust
  saves tax depends on the home jurisdiction of the settlor
  and beneficiaries — Jersey itself imposes no income tax
  on properly-structured trusts (no IHT, no CGT either), but
  the home jurisdiction's rules dominate the tax outcome.
- **Not** a guarantee against every claim. Specific facts
  matter — a trust set up in fraud of creditors, or to
  defeat a known liability, is challengeable. The firewall
  protects against foreign-law forced-heirship and
  matrimonial-property claims; it does not protect against
  every conceivable challenge.

## When a Jersey trust is the right choice

Common use cases:

- **Succession protection** where forced-heirship rules
  abroad would otherwise control inheritance;
- **Family-governance vehicle** across generations;
- **Asset protection** against specific risks;
- **Pre-realisation planning** (founder equity, pre-IPO);
- **Cross-border wealth-holding** where multiple
  jurisdictions are involved.

Common situations where a Jersey trust is **not** the
right tool:

- For tax avoidance in the home jurisdiction (CRS reports
  through the structure);
- For very small estates where the running costs of a trust
  outweigh the benefits;
- Where the client wants direct control (a company may be
  a better tool than a trust).

## What to ask your adviser

Five good questions:

1. **What's my settlor / beneficiary position under my home
   country's tax rules?** (This drives the structure.)
2. **What reserved powers am I retaining, and how do they
   interact with my home country's anti-avoidance rules?**
3. **Who will be the trustee, and what's their fee
   structure?**
4. **What reporting obligations do I have personally?**
   (CRS, FATCA, home-country trust-reporting forms.)
5. **What happens if I move countries?**
