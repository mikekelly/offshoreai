# Consumer Safety (Jersey) Law 2006 — Overview

## What the law does

The **Consumer Safety (Jersey) Law 2006** (CSL 2006)
establishes the **public-law product-safety regime** in
Jersey:

- Requires goods placed on the market to be **reasonably
  safe**;
- Empowers the Minister to issue **safety regulations**
  for specific product categories;
- Empowers **enforcement** — recall, notices, prosecution;
- Provides **criminal sanctions** for non-compliant
  suppliers.

It is the public-law counterpart to the **Supply of Goods
and Services (Jersey) Law 2009** (contractual quality
obligations) — both apply, with the CSL 2006 the
regulatory enforcement route and the SGSL 2009 the
contractual route between buyer and seller.

## The general safety requirement

Goods placed on the Jersey market must be **reasonably
safe** having regard to:

- **The nature** of the goods;
- **The way** they are marketed and labelled;
- **Use** for which they are intended (or reasonably
  foreseeable);
- **Any warnings** and instructions provided;
- **The standards** applicable (Jersey, UK, EU, ISO);
- **Reasonable consumer expectations**.

Suppliers (manufacturers, importers, distributors,
retailers) have shared responsibility — not just the
maker.

## Specific regulations

The Minister may make regulations for specific products
or hazards:

- **Toys** — safety standards;
- **Electrical products** — wiring, plugs, insulation;
- **Children's furniture** — bunk beds, cots, prams;
- **Cosmetics and personal-care products**;
- **Food and beverage** containers (in some respects);
- **Pyrotechnics**;
- **Specific hazardous chemicals**;
- **Sports and leisure** equipment.

Many regulations adopt UK or EU technical standards by
reference, providing alignment with major markets.

## Enforcement

Enforcement is carried out by **Trading Standards** (a
department / function within Customer and Local Services
or successor body):

- **Inspection** of premises and goods;
- **Sampling** for testing;
- **Suspension notices** preventing sale pending
  investigation;
- **Forfeiture orders** for unsafe goods;
- **Prosecution** for non-compliant suppliers;
- **Recall** powers for products already sold.

## Offences

The CSL 2006 creates offences for:

- **Supplying** unsafe goods;
- **Failing to comply** with safety regulations;
- **Failing to comply** with notices;
- **Obstructing** trading standards officers;
- **Specific** mislabelling / misrepresentation offences.

Penalties include **fines** (substantial for serious
breaches) and **imprisonment** for the most serious cases.

## Recall

Where unsafe goods have been sold:

- **Suppliers** required to recall;
- **Information** to consumers and the public;
- **Refund / replacement** typically arranged;
- **Coordination** with manufacturers / overseas
  suppliers for product traced to its origin.

## Interaction with SGSL 2009

The two statutes work together:

| Issue | CSL 2006 (public-law) | SGSL 2009 (contractual) |
|---|---|---|
| Who enforces | Trading Standards | The consumer |
| Forum | Magistrate's / Royal Court | Petty Debts / Royal Court |
| Remedy | Recall, notices, fines | Refund, replacement, damages |
| Standard | Reasonable safety + specific regulations | Implied terms (satisfactory quality, fitness for purpose, correspondence with description) |

A consumer harmed by an unsafe product typically has:

- **Civil claim** under SGSL 2009 + tort (negligence);
- **Public-law complaint** to Trading Standards under
  CSL 2006;
- Both can run in parallel.

## Practical recommendations

For consumers:

- **Report** unsafe products to Trading Standards
  promptly;
- **Keep evidence** — packaging, receipts, photographs;
- **Pursue civil remedies** in parallel where loss
  suffered;
- **Engage Citizens Advice Jersey** for guidance.

For suppliers:

- **Comply** with applicable safety regulations;
- **Test** before placing on the market;
- **Document** compliance — useful in any subsequent
  investigation;
- **Cooperate** with Trading Standards;
- **Recall** promptly where unsafe goods identified.
