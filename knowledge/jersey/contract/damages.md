# Damages in Jersey Contract Law

## The compensatory principle

The fundamental principle is **compensation**:

- **Put the innocent party** in the position they would have
  been in **had the contract been performed**;
- **Subject to** practical limitations (causation, remoteness,
  mitigation);
- **Broadly aligned** with English contract law approach.

## Categories of damages

### Expectation loss

The **principal measure**:

- **Net** value of performance to the innocent party;
- **Lost profits** (in commercial contexts);
- **Difference** between contract price and market price (for
  goods);
- **Lost benefit** generally.

### Reliance loss

- **Expenditure** incurred in reliance on the contract;
- **Used** as alternative measure where expectation loss
  difficult to quantify;
- **Subject to** caps and adjustments.

### Restitution

- **Return** of benefits conferred;
- **Used** where contract avoided / rescinded;
- **Specific** Jersey rules.

### Mental distress damages

Generally **not recoverable** for breach of contract, **except**:

- **Specific** contracts where mental enjoyment is central
  (holiday contracts, ceremonial events);
- **Specific** Jersey case law allowing such damages in
  defined circumstances.

### Aggravated damages

- **Rare** in contract;
- **Specific** circumstances of malicious / wilful breach;
- **Specific** Jersey case law.

### Exemplary damages

- **Generally not** available for breach of contract in
  Jersey;
- **Specific** narrow categories.

## Causation

The breach must **cause** the loss:

- **'But for'** test;
- **Subject to** intervening factors (novus actus
  interveniens);
- **Multiple causation** issues addressed case-by-case.

## Remoteness

Loss must be **not too remote**:

### *Hadley v Baxendale* (1854) test

- Loss arising **naturally** from the breach;
- **Or** loss which the parties **reasonably contemplated** at
  the time of contract as a probable result of breach.

### Jersey application

- **Persuasive** authority on Jersey courts;
- **Same** test applied in practice;
- **Specific** Jersey case law extending / modifying in
  specific contexts.

## Mitigation

The innocent party must **mitigate** their loss:

- **Reasonable steps** to avoid further loss;
- **Cannot recover** loss that could reasonably have been
  avoided;
- **Subject to** factual analysis of what was reasonable;
- **Burden** on the breaching party to prove failure to
  mitigate.

## Liquidated damages and penalty clauses

### Liquidated damages

- **Pre-agreed** measure of damages;
- **Enforceable** if a **genuine pre-estimate** of loss;
- **Common** in commercial contracts;
- **Specific** Jersey rules.

### Penalty clauses

- **Punitive** rather than compensatory;
- **Unenforceable** under traditional Jersey approach
  (similar to English law);
- **Modern English** position (*Cavendish v El Makdessi*)
  may be **persuasive** in Jersey but not authoritative;
- **Specific** Jersey case law on this.

## Specific contexts

### Sale of goods

- **Specific** statutory rules under Sale of Goods (Jersey)
  Law 2010;
- **Difference** between contract and market price;
- **Specific** consequential loss rules.

### Construction / services

- **Cost of remedying** defects typically;
- **Diminution in value** as alternative;
- **Specific** Jersey case law on construction.

### Real property

- **Specific** Jersey rules;
- **Specific performance** often preferred to damages for real
  property;
- **Specific** customary considerations.

### Financial / commercial

- **Lost profits** typically;
- **Specific** Jersey rules on currency-conversion losses;
- **Specific** rules on interest on damages.

## Interest on damages

- **Interest** typically awarded;
- **From** specified date (typically date of cause of action
  accruing or date of judgment);
- **Statutory rate** or commercial rate as determined;
- **Specific** Jersey rules.

## Quantification

For commercial damages:

- **Expert evidence** for complex quantification;
- **Specific** Jersey rules on valuation methodology;
- **Specific** treatment of contingencies and uncertainty.
