# Force Majeure under Jersey Law

## The question

Your client has a Jersey-law contract. An unforeseen event
(pandemic, war, sanctions, supply-chain failure) makes
performance impossible or commercially ruinous. Can the
client suspend or terminate? Two doctrines are in play —
**force majeure** (contractual) and **frustration /
impossibility** (default rule).

## Force majeure as a contractual term

Force majeure in Jersey is **principally a creature of
contract**:

- **No general implied** force-majeure regime;
- **Express clause** required to invoke contractual relief;
- **Drafting** governs scope and effect;
- **Interpretation** by Jersey courts follows
  Pothier-aligned construction rules with English persuasive
  authority.

## Typical clause structure

A well-drafted Jersey force-majeure clause specifies:

- **Trigger events** (defined list and / or general
  catch-all);
- **Causation requirement** — event must *prevent*, *hinder*
  or *delay* performance;
- **Notice obligation** within a specified period;
- **Mitigation obligation**;
- **Consequence** — suspension, extension, or termination;
- **Long-stop date** — termination right after sustained
  force-majeure period;
- **Allocation** of accrued obligations and pre-existing
  liabilities.

## Construction principles

Jersey courts construe force-majeure clauses:

- **Strictly** — the party seeking relief bears the burden;
- **Against the drafter** in case of ambiguity (*contra
  proferentem*);
- **By reference to commercial purpose** (Pothier and
  modern interpretation rules);
- **In light of the parties' allocation of risk**.

## Frustration / impossibility (default rule)

Where the contract has **no force-majeure clause**, Jersey
recognises a **doctrine of frustration / impossibility**
rooted in:

- **Pothier** — performance becoming impossible by no fault
  of the obligor;
- **Modern** Jersey case law converging with English
  frustration doctrine but anchored in customary
  obligations;
- **Specific** outcome — contract discharged, future
  obligations released.

## Frustration — high threshold

Frustration requires:

- **Supervening event** (after contract formation);
- **Without fault** of the party invoking;
- **Performance** rendered impossible, illegal, or radically
  different;
- **Not** mere hardship, expense, or commercial inconvenience;
- **Not** a foreseen risk allocated by the contract.

## Effect of frustration

When frustration applies:

- **Future obligations** automatically discharged;
- **Accrued obligations** generally remain;
- **Restitution** for benefits conferred — unjust-enrichment
  principles apply;
- **No statutory** equivalent of the UK Law Reform
  (Frustrated Contracts) Act 1943 — Jersey relies on common
  law / customary principles.

## Specific Jersey contexts

### Sanctions

- **Sanctions** imposed after contract formation may trigger
  force majeure or frustration;
- **Specific** clauses now common (sanctions clauses);
- **Sanctions and Asset-Freezing (Jersey) Law 2019** —
  illegality consequences.

### Sale of Jersey real property

- **Royal Court passing** required (Friday afternoon
  hearings);
- **Court closure** historically triggered force-majeure
  arguments;
- **Specific** Royal Court practice on passing delays.

### Banking / finance documents

- **LMA-style** force-majeure clauses common;
- **Market disruption** as related concept (interest
  determination);
- **Specific** Jersey-law variations.

## Drafting recommendations

- **Define** trigger events specifically and include catch-all;
- **Clarify** causation standard (prevent / hinder / delay);
- **Specify** notice mechanics;
- **Address** mitigation;
- **Include** termination right after long-stop period;
- **Allocate** accrued obligations and ongoing payments;
- **Consider** sanctions / pandemic / cyber clauses
  expressly.

## Pitfalls

- **Assuming** there is an implied force-majeure regime —
  there is not;
- **Drafting** generic "act of God" clauses without modern
  detail;
- **Missing** notice deadlines (often strictly construed);
- **Ignoring** mitigation obligations;
- **Misunderstanding** frustration as a substitute for a
  well-drafted clause.
