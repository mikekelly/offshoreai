# Bermuda Corporate Income Tax Act 2023

## What it is

The **Corporate Income Tax Act 2023** is the statute by
which Bermuda introduced its first ever corporate income
tax. The Act was enacted **27 December 2023**, became
operative for fiscal years beginning **on or after 1
January 2025**, and implements a **15% Corporate Income
Tax (CIT)** on Bermuda-constituent entities of in-scope
multinational enterprise (MNE) groups.

The CIT is designed to operate as a **Qualified Domestic
Minimum Top-up Tax (QDMTT)** within the OECD/G20
[Pillar Two](../jersey/tax/pillar-two.md) GloBE (Global
Anti-Base Erosion) framework — meaning Pillar Two top-up
tax that would otherwise be collected by other
jurisdictions (under the IIR or UTPR) is captured by
Bermuda first.

## Why it's strategically significant

The political and structural significance is substantial.
Bermuda is the **first traditionally no-tax major
offshore jurisdiction** to formally introduce a corporate
income tax. The decision was a direct response to:

1. The OECD/G20 Pillar Two architecture (the GloBE Rules)
   becoming operational across major jurisdictions from
   1 January 2024;
2. The risk that, absent a Bermuda QDMTT, top-up tax on
   Bermuda-constituent entities would be collected by
   **other jurisdictions** (parent or sub jurisdictions)
   under the **Income Inclusion Rule** or the
   **Undertaxed Profits Rule**;
3. A strategic decision that **Bermuda should collect
   that revenue** rather than let it flow to other
   jurisdictions.

Bermuda has thereby retained tax-revenue sovereignty for
its in-scope MNEs while moving from a 0% to 15%
effective-rate posture on those specific entities.

## Scope — who's caught

The CIT applies only to **Bermuda Constituent Entities of
In-Scope MNE Groups**. An MNE Group is in scope where its
**consolidated annual revenue exceeds EUR 750 million in
at least two of the four preceding fiscal years** — the
standard Pillar Two threshold.

Therefore the CIT is **not** a tax on:

- Smaller groups (sub-EUR 750m revenue);
- Stand-alone Bermuda companies;
- Most insurance, fund, and family-office structures
  unless they sit inside a larger qualifying MNE group;
- Captive insurance vehicles where the captive itself
  isn't part of a larger qualifying MNE group.

For **95%+ of Bermuda's corporate population** — captive
insurance vehicles, ILS structures, exempted companies
holding family wealth, smaller PE structures — the CIT
does **not** apply. The historical Bermuda position of
**no corporate income tax** continues to apply outside the
in-scope MNE perimeter.

## How the tax operates

### Tax base

The CIT applies to the **Bermuda Constituent Entity's
GloBE income** — broadly, financial-accounting profit
with Pillar Two adjustments (covered taxes, certain
exclusions for shipping, investment-fund definitions,
etc.) as set out in the OECD Model Rules.

### Rate

**15%** on GloBE income.

### Effective date

Tax periods **beginning on or after 1 January 2025**.

### Filing and payment

Bermuda Constituent Entities of in-scope MNE Groups must
file CIT returns with the **Bermuda CIT Agency** (a new
administrative body established under the Act) and pay
the tax. Returns are due **15 months after the end of the
fiscal year** (with an 18-month deadline for the first
filing period).

### Tax credits

The Act provides for a **Qualifying Refundable Tax Credit
(QRTC)** regime to incentivise economic activity in
Bermuda — including investment in qualifying tangible
assets, qualifying employment, and innovation. The credits
must be designed to qualify under the Pillar Two QRTC
rules so they don't reduce the effective tax rate for
GloBE purposes.

## Bermuda CIT 2023 vs Jersey MCIT 2025

The two regimes are structurally similar but with
operational differences.

| Feature | Bermuda CIT 2023 | Jersey MCIT 2025 |
|---|---|---|
| Statute | Corporate Income Tax Act 2023 | Multinational Corporate Income Tax (Jersey) Law 2025 |
| Effective date | Tax periods starting on or after 1 Jan 2025 | Tax periods starting on or after 1 Jan 2025 |
| Rate | 15% | 15% |
| Scope | In-scope MNE groups (EUR 750m revenue threshold) | In-scope MNE groups (EUR 750m revenue threshold) |
| Underlying corporate-tax regime | Previously 0% across the board | Zero-Ten (0%/10%/20% — see [`../jersey/tax/zero-ten.md`](../jersey/tax/zero-ten.md)) |
| Administrative body | Bermuda CIT Agency | Jersey Comptroller of Taxes |
| Filing deadline | 15 months post year-end | 15 months post year-end (typical Pillar Two timeline) |
| QDMTT-qualifying | Yes | Yes |

For the Jersey detail see
[`../jersey/international/pillar-two-mcit.md`](../jersey/international/pillar-two-mcit.md).

## Industries affected

### Reinsurance

Bermuda's substantial **commercial reinsurance industry**
(Class 4 reinsurers, Class 3A and 3B insurers — see
[`./insurance-classes.md`](./insurance-classes.md)) is
where the CIT bites hardest. Major global
reinsurers with Bermuda parent structures are in scope.

Practical responses include:

- **QRTC planning** — using the qualifying refundable tax
  credits for substantive Bermuda activity;
- **GloBE income optimisation** — within the Pillar Two
  parameters;
- **Group structure review** — for some MNEs, the CIT may
  shift the calculus on where to hold particular
  functions.

### ILS (Insurance-Linked Securities)

[ILS structures](./ils.md) where the SPV is part of a
larger qualifying MNE group fall in scope. Stand-alone
ILS structures (the typical model) do not.

### Other industries

Most other Bermuda-resident activities (captives owned by
non-MNE-group enterprises, family-office trusts, mid-size
PE, etc.) do not fall in scope.

## What it means for structuring decisions

For sponsors considering Bermuda vs other Crown
Dependencies / Caribbean jurisdictions for in-scope
multinational structures:

- **In-scope MNEs**: the CIT brings Bermuda into parity
  with [Jersey's MCIT 2025](../jersey/international/pillar-two-mcit.md),
  Guernsey's equivalent, and IoM's equivalent. The choice
  is now substance-led rather than tax-led for in-scope
  groups;
- **Below-threshold groups**: Bermuda's no-corporate-tax
  posture remains intact and continues to be a meaningful
  differentiator against onshore alternatives;
- **Reinsurance**: the CIT does not undermine Bermuda's
  reinsurance industry competitiveness on substance,
  Solvency II equivalence, and market depth — but the
  pure-tax differential vs other re-domiciles has
  narrowed.

## Practical timeline

| Date | Event |
|---|---|
| 27 December 2023 | CIT Act 2023 enacted |
| 2024 | Bermuda CIT Agency established; QRTC consultations |
| 1 January 2025 | Effective for tax periods starting then |
| April 2026 | First quarterly estimated payments (depending on entity) |
| ~Q1 2027 | First annual returns due (15-month rule) |

The **first full CIT return cycle** is therefore still
ahead at the time of writing (May 2026); outcomes
including industry responses, return-population sizes,
and revenue collected are on the watch-list.

## Pitfalls

- **Assuming the CIT is a general corporate income tax**
  — it isn't; it applies only to in-scope MNE groups;
- **Underestimating scope** — even moderately-sized
  groups can be in scope once consolidated revenue is
  considered;
- **Ignoring QRTC opportunities** — the credits regime
  rewards substantive Bermuda activity and can
  materially reduce the effective tax;
- **Treating Bermuda CIT and Jersey MCIT as identical**
  — the underlying corporate-tax base differs (Bermuda
  previously 0%; Jersey Zero-Ten);
- **Substance assumptions** — Bermuda's
  [Economic Substance Act 2018](./bma.md) (predates the
  CIT) continues to apply on a parallel track.
