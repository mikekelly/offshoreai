# Bermuda Segregated Accounts Company (SAC)

## What it is

A **Bermuda Segregated Accounts Company (SAC)** is an
exempted company that has elected to operate under the
**Segregated Accounts Companies Act 2000** ("SACA"). The
key feature: the company maintains **statutorily
segregated accounts**, each with its own assets and
liabilities, **insulated from one another** and from the
SAC's general account.

The SAC sits in the **cell-company family** alongside:

- **Guernsey PCC** (the original, 1997);
- **Cayman SPC** (Segregated Portfolio Company, 1998);
- **BVI SPC** (statute mirrors Cayman);
- **Jersey PCC** (Loi (2006) sur les sociétés de cellules
  protégées).

SACs are extensively used in **Bermuda's insurance
sector**, particularly for **captive insurance**, **ILS
(insurance-linked securities)**, and **rent-a-captive**
structures.

## Why SACs exist

The same problem as other cell companies: a sponsor wants
**multiple distinct risk pools** within a single legal
entity, with statutory segregation between them. SACA
provides:

- **Single legal entity** with one board, one registered
  office, one regulatory licence;
- **Multiple segregated accounts**, each ringfenced;
- **Statutory protection** preventing creditors of one
  account reaching another;
- **Streamlined** governance with multi-cell flexibility.

## Bermuda's insurance focus

While Cayman SPCs are general-purpose, Bermuda SACs are
**heavily insurance-oriented**:

- **Captive insurance** programmes — a single SAC houses
  multiple corporate captives in segregated accounts;
- **ILS structures** — each catastrophe-bond series sits
  in its own account;
- **Rent-a-captive** — third-party captive services
  delivered through SAC accounts;
- **Reinsurance** — collateralised reinsurance arrangements.

The Bermuda Monetary Authority (BMA) supervises SACs
operating in insurance — see [`insurance.md`](insurance.md).

## Formation

A SAC is formed by:

1. **Incorporating** as a Bermuda exempted company (or
   converting an existing company);
2. **Registering** under the SACA — typically applied
   for at the same time as incorporation;
3. **BMA consent** where the SAC's purposes are
   regulated (insurance most commonly);
4. **Setting up** initial segregated accounts.

Each segregated account is created by board resolution
in accordance with the SAC's governing documents.

## Operating a SAC

### Account creation and identification

New accounts are created by board resolution. Each has:

- A **unique designation** within the SAC;
- **Specific** assets and liabilities;
- **Account-specific** rules (in the SAC's governing
  instrument);
- **Account participants** with rights to that account's
  economic interests.

When contracting in respect of a specific account, the
SAC must:

- **Identify itself** as the SAC acting in respect of a
  particular account;
- **Maintain** segregated records;
- **Issue** account-specific shares where investor
  segregation is wanted.

### Account participants and account owners

Different roles:

- **Account participant** — a person with rights / interest
  in a specific account;
- **Account owner** — a person entitled to the residual
  value of an account.

The terminology differs from Cayman SPC (which uses
"portfolio shares") but the substance is similar — each
investor pool has its own economic interests linked to
its account's performance.

### Statutory segregation

Under SACA:

- **Assets** of a segregated account are not available to
  satisfy liabilities of any other account;
- **Liabilities** of an account are payable only from the
  account's assets;
- **General account** assets are available for general
  liabilities;
- **Bermuda courts** are required to give effect to the
  segregation;
- **Foreign-court** recognition is generally well-
  established for Bermuda SACs in insurance contexts.

## Use cases

### Captive insurance

A corporate captive parent runs multiple insurance lines
(D&O, environmental, property, business interruption)
through different accounts of one SAC:

- **Single licensed entity**;
- **Account-level** economics tracked separately;
- **Cost-efficient** vs running separate captive
  companies.

### Insurance-Linked Securities (ILS)

A repeat ILS sponsor (a catastrophe-bond issuer) uses one
SAC to issue multiple bond series:

- Each series sits in its own account;
- **Bankruptcy-remote** at the series level;
- **Common** sponsor infrastructure (board, custodian,
  administrator);
- **Investor** capital ring-fenced to the specific bond
  series.

Bermuda's ILS market is the world's largest, and SACs
are central to the structure.

### Rent-a-captive

Specialty captive service providers offer third-party
clients access to captive insurance through accounts of
the provider's SAC. The client gets captive-insurance
economics without forming their own captive entity.

### Multi-strategy funds

Fund sponsors running multiple strategies use SACs to
segregate strategy-specific assets and liabilities
within a single licensed fund entity — broadly parallel
to Cayman SPC usage.

## Account-specific economics

A SAC can issue different classes of shares linked to
specific accounts:

- **Holders of Class A shares** participate in Account 1's
  performance;
- **Holders of Class B shares** participate in Account 2;
- **Cross-class** voting and governance rules per
  constitutional documents.

This provides multi-strategy / multi-investor flexibility
within one corporate entity.

## Comparison

| Feature | Bermuda SAC | Cayman SPC | Guernsey PCC | Guernsey ICC |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Vintage | 2000 | 1998 | 1997 (original) | Later |
| Single legal entity | Yes | Yes | Yes | **No — each cell is its own entity** |
| Statutory segregation | ✅ | ✅ | ✅ | ✅ |
| Insurance focus | **Primary use** | Used | Used | Used |
| Funds focus | Used | Primary use | Used | Used |
| BMA / cell regulator pre-vetting | Yes | None equivalent | GFSC oversight | GFSC oversight |

The Bermuda regulatory pre-vetting and insurance focus
are the distinguishing features. For pure fund use,
Cayman SPC is typically the closer analogue. For
insurance / ILS, Bermuda SAC is the standard.

## Pitfalls

- **Sloppy account identification** in contracting can
  break segregation;
- **Foreign-court** recognition risk for non-insurance
  international contracts;
- **Common counterparty** exposure across multiple
  accounts can complicate enforcement;
- **Regulatory** approvals for the underlying activity
  (insurance, fund) are separate from the SACA
  registration;
- **Capital allocations** across accounts must be
  managed carefully — under-capitalised accounts can
  produce regulatory issues.
