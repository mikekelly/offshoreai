# Guernsey Trust Law — Differences from Jersey

## Overview

Guernsey's trust law and Jersey's are siblings, both
descended from a shared Norman customary heritage and
both modernised by mid-20th-century statutes. The two
regimes now overlap heavily but **differ in important
respects** that matter when choosing between them.

The principal Guernsey statute is the **Trusts
(Guernsey) Law 2007** (TGL 2007), which replaced an
older 1989 Law and modernised the framework.

## High-level similarities

Both Guernsey and Jersey:

- Recognise **discretionary, fixed-interest, accumulation,
  and purpose trusts**;
- Allow **settlor-reserved powers** without invalidating
  the trust;
- Provide a **firewall** disapplying foreign forced-
  heirship etc.;
- Permit **non-charitable purpose** trusts subject to
  enforcer requirements;
- Have **trust company regulation** for professional
  trustees;
- Maintain a **Royal Court** supervisory jurisdiction
  over trust matters.

## Principal differences

### Article 9 firewall vs Guernsey s.14

**Jersey Article 9** is the gold standard firewall — it
explicitly disapplies foreign forced-heirship,
matrimonial-property regimes, and contrary foreign law to
Jersey trusts, plus the 2006 Article 9(4) amendment
disapplies foreign judgments based on inconsistent law.

**Guernsey s.14** of TGL 2007 provides a similar but
**less explicit** statutory firewall. Specifically:

- Guernsey law applies to validity, capacity,
  administration, and rights of the trust;
- Foreign law is disapplied where inconsistent;
- Specific provisions on foreign judgments are narrower
  in detail than Jersey's Article 9(4).

**In practice**, the protection is comparable but
Jersey's Article 9(4) language is more explicit on
foreign-judgment non-recognition. Sophisticated forced-
heirship-defensive structures often still prefer
**Jersey** for that explicit statutory language.

### Reserved powers — Article 9A (Jersey) vs Part II (Guernsey)

**Jersey Article 9A** lists a wide, specific menu of
reserved powers (revocation, advancement, amendment,
trustee direction, investment, beneficiary appointment,
etc.) and confirms that reserving them does not
invalidate the trust.

**Guernsey** has similar provisions but the menu and
specifics differ in detail. Both regimes are broadly
equivalent in outcome; sponsors can structure equivalent
reserved-powers arrangements in either jurisdiction.

### Trustee duties and the *Hastings-Bass* / Article 47 equivalence

**Jersey Article 47B-J** provides a statutory regime for
setting aside trustee decisions on grounds of mistake or
inadequate deliberation, wider than the English *Pitt v
Holt* approach. The three-limb *Re S&T Trusts* test
governs.

**Guernsey** has **codified a parallel mistake regime**
in TGL 2007, similarly wider than the English approach.
The detailed statutory wording differs from Jersey's but
the outcomes are broadly comparable.

Both jurisdictions are **wider than England post-Pitt v
Holt**, but **Jersey's case law** under Article 47B-J is
more developed.

### Purpose trusts — enforcer requirements

Both jurisdictions allow non-charitable purpose trusts.
The specific enforcer mechanics and duration rules
differ in detail but produce broadly comparable
structures.

### Duration

- **Jersey**: trusts can be perpetual since 2006
  amendments (the previous 100-year rule abolished);
- **Guernsey**: trusts can be perpetual under TGL 2007.

Both jurisdictions permit unlimited duration. This is
a major contrast with English law, where the rule
against perpetuities continues to constrain duration
even after the Perpetuities and Accumulations Act 2009.

### Trust company regulation

- **Jersey**: TCBs (Trust Company Businesses) regulated
  under the Financial Services (Jersey) Law 1998 — see
  the Jersey corpus.
- **Guernsey**: equivalent regulation under the Regulation
  of Fiduciaries, Administration Businesses and Company
  Directors, etc. (Bailiwick of Guernsey) Law 2000.

Both regulators (JFSC and GFSC) maintain Codes of
Practice for trustees and supervise the industry. The
specific licensing categories and compliance
expectations differ in detail but are broadly equivalent.

### Confidentiality

- **Jersey** trust documents are private;
- **Guernsey** trust documents are private;
- **Both** require trustees to maintain registers of
  beneficial ownership accessible to specified
  authorities.

### Beneficiary information rights

- **Both** jurisdictions apply *Schmidt v Rosewood*
  principles — beneficiaries have rights but trustees
  have discretion;
- **Specific** statutory provisions in each jurisdiction
  refine the scope.

### Court jurisdiction

- **Jersey**: Royal Court of Jersey supervises trusts;
- **Guernsey**: Royal Court of Guernsey supervises
  trusts;
- **Both**: appeals to respective Courts of Appeal, then
  Privy Council.

The **Privy Council** jurisprudence on trusts is
relevant to both, given final-appeal commonality. JCPC
decisions in either jurisdiction influence trust
practice in the other.

## Choosing between Jersey and Guernsey

In practice:

| Factor | Lean Jersey | Lean Guernsey |
|---|---|---|
| **Maximum forced-heirship defence** | Article 9(4) explicit | s.14 less explicit |
| **Case-law depth on Article 47B mistake** | Re S&T Trusts and progeny | Less developed |
| **EU-marketing for fund/services context** | Both equivalent | Both equivalent |
| **Trust-company industry depth** | Larger industry | Mature but smaller |
| **PCC / ICC for trustee structures** | PCC available | PCC + ICC available |
| **PIF vs JPF for fund-trust combinations** | JPF | PIF |
| **Customary-law nuances** | Jersey customary law | Guernsey customary law |

For most cross-border family-wealth structures, the two
regimes are competitive substitutes. The **specific
choice** often depends on:

- **Sponsor familiarity** with one regime vs the other;
- **Existing service-provider relationships**;
- **Group infrastructure** (administrator location);
- **Specific** asset / family considerations.

For **maximum-defence** structures against aggressive
forced-heirship or matrimonial claims, **Jersey's
Article 9(4)** continues to be the marginally preferred
choice.
