# Guernsey Incorporated Cell Company (ICC)

## What it is

The **Guernsey Incorporated Cell Company (ICC)** is the
second-generation Guernsey cell vehicle. It addresses a
specific limitation of the PCC: under a PCC, all cells
share a **single legal personality**, which means foreign
courts that don't fully recognise statutory segregation
might (in theory) pool cellular liabilities.

The ICC solves this by giving **each cell its own
separate legal personality**:

- The ICC itself is a company;
- Each cell of the ICC is **separately incorporated** as
  a distinct legal entity;
- Each cell can be **dealt with as a company** in its
  own right;
- The ICC and its cells share **governance infrastructure**
  (board, registered office, administration);
- **Cells migrate** in and out of the ICC structure as
  separate companies.

## Why ICC

The PCC is sufficient for most uses. The ICC matters
when:

- **Foreign-court** recognition is acute — each cell as
  its own incorporated company is unambiguously
  separate;
- **Counterparties** require separate corporate
  personality (some regulated counterparties don't deal
  with cells, only companies);
- **Cell migration** is contemplated — an ICC cell can
  more cleanly leave the ICC and continue as a
  standalone company (or join another ICC) because it
  has its own corporate personality already;
- **Specific regulatory** regimes treat cells differently
  from companies, and the sponsor wants the cell
  treated as a company.

## How an ICC differs from a PCC

| Feature | PCC | ICC |
|---|---|---|
| Legal personality | Single (whole PCC) | Multiple (ICC + each cell) |
| Cell as a separate "company" for foreign-court purposes | No (statutory ringfence) | Yes (each cell is a company) |
| Cell migration in/out | Specific procedure | Simpler — cell is already a company |
| Governance infrastructure | Shared core + cells | Shared (ICC + cells under common board) |
| Insurance use | Common | Less common |
| Funds use | Common | Common |
| Cost / complexity | Lower | Slightly higher (more registrations) |

A useful framing: **PCC = one company with ringfenced
internal accounts**; **ICC = a group of companies under
shared governance with statutory cell-ring-fencing**.

## Formation

An ICC is formed by:

1. **Incorporating** the **ICC core** as a Guernsey
   company;
2. **Incorporating** **each cell** as a separate
   incorporated cell — they are registered companies
   with their own constitutional documents and
   shareholders;
3. **GFSC** approval where the activities are regulated;
4. **Beneficial-ownership** disclosure for the ICC and
   each cell;
5. **Shared registered office** typical;
6. **Common board** typical (though cells can have their
   own directors).

The administrative overhead is slightly higher than for a
PCC because each cell is a separate company on the
register.

## Cell operation in an ICC

### Each cell is a company

When acting, a cell:

- **Contracts** in its own name (as a separate company);
- **Holds** its own assets;
- **Incurs** its own liabilities;
- **Files** its own annual returns (consolidated as part
  of the ICC group filing in some cases);
- **Has its own** shareholders.

This makes the cell's separate-entity status **clear
beyond doubt** to foreign courts and counterparties.

### Shared services

The cells of an ICC typically share:

- **Board** (same directors for all cells);
- **Registered office**;
- **Company secretary**;
- **Auditor**;
- **Administrator**;
- **Sub-management arrangements**.

This delivers the **cost efficiency** of a single-entity
cell company while preserving the **legal-personality
clarity** of separate companies.

## Use cases

### Investment funds with international counterparties

ICCs are common where the cell is a fund interacting
with **international prime brokers, custodians, and
counterparties** who insist on dealing with a clearly
separate corporate counterparty. The ICC structure
satisfies them while still delivering economies of scale
across the ICC family.

### Captive insurance with multi-jurisdictional reach

For captives with global reinsurance arrangements where
specific foreign jurisdictions might not fully recognise
PCC segregation, an ICC provides a more robust separate-
entity story for each cell.

### Sponsor platforms

Asset managers running multiple fund products as
separate corporate entities under shared governance
infrastructure use ICCs to deliver clear corporate
separation alongside operational efficiency.

## Tax

Both PCCs and ICCs are subject to Guernsey's standard
corporate tax regime (mostly 0%, with specific
exceptions for finance, regulated businesses, and
property income). The cell-by-cell vs entity-wide
treatment depends on the regulatory regime and the
cell's activity.

## Comparison with other jurisdictions

Guernsey is **alone** in offering both PCC and ICC
forms. Bermuda has only SAC; Cayman has only SPC; BVI
has only SPC; Jersey has only PCC. For sponsors needing
the ICC's separate-personality robustness, Guernsey is
the choice.

## Pitfalls

- **Confusing** PCC and ICC — they look similar but
  operate fundamentally differently;
- **Over-engineering** — most use cases don't need ICC
  separation; PCC is simpler;
- **Cell migration** complexity — moving a cell between
  ICCs requires careful planning;
- **Regulatory** approvals must cover each cell
  separately in some regimes;
- **Administrative** burden of separate company
  filings.
