# IoM Companies Acts — 1931 vs 2006

## The dual-regime structure

The Isle of Man is unusual in operating **two parallel
companies regimes**:

- **Companies Act 1931** — the long-established Companies
  Act broadly modelled on the UK Companies Act 1929 (and
  subsequent UK reforms);
- **Companies Act 2006** — a modern, lighter-touch
  companies regime introduced to compete with BVI BC
  Act / Cayman Companies Act flexibility.

Both are **fully in force**. A sponsor incorporating an
IoM company **chooses** which regime to use. Existing
1931-Act companies can re-register under the 2006 Act
(or vice versa, in limited cases). This dual-regime
flexibility is distinctive to the Isle of Man.

## Why two regimes

The 1931 Act was modelled on early 20th-century UK
corporate law — substantial procedural formalities
(statutory meetings, share-capital maintenance,
directors' minimum residence requirements in some
contexts, etc.). For some business categories — long-
established IoM businesses, regulated entities expecting
familiar UK-style company law — the 1931 Act remains
appropriate.

But for **international business** — offshore SPVs,
holding companies, light-touch structures — the 1931
Act's formality was uncompetitive against BVI BCs and
Cayman Exempteds. The 2006 Act was introduced to compete
directly: **fewer formalities, more flexibility, faster
turnaround**.

Rather than replace one with the other, the IoM
**retained both**, giving sponsors the choice.

## Companies Act 1931 features

- **Statutory authorised capital** concept;
- **Memorandum and Articles** of association;
- **Minimum two directors** historically (now reducible);
- **Annual return** and AGM;
- **Statutory financial-statement** filing in some cases;
- **Specific** capital maintenance rules;
- **Older case-law** inheritance from UK pre-1985.

Used for:

- **Trust companies** and other long-established
  regulated entities;
- **Locally-trading businesses**;
- **Some** insurance / pension trustees;
- **Specific** legacy structures.

## Companies Act 2006 features

- **No authorised capital** requirement (issued capital
  only);
- **Memorandum** only (no Articles required, though
  typically used);
- **One director** sufficient;
- **No statutory** AGM;
- **No filing** of accounts in most cases;
- **No capital maintenance** in the 1931-Act sense;
- **Modern** corporate-law flexibility (mergers,
  consolidations, redomicile);
- **Foreign-company migration** in / out permitted.

Used for:

- **Offshore holding** companies;
- **SPVs** for structured finance;
- **International** business with no IoM trading;
- **Fund** structures (alongside the IoM funds regime);
- **Pre-IPO** structures.

## Comparison

| Feature | CA 1931 | CA 2006 |
|---|---|---|
| Authorised capital | Required | Not required |
| Minimum directors | One (since reform) | One |
| Articles required | Recommended | Optional |
| Accounts filing | Often required | Not for most |
| Capital maintenance | Yes (modified) | No |
| Mergers / consolidations | Specific procedure | Streamlined |
| Re-registration in / out | Permitted | Permitted |
| Typical use | Regulated / local | International / offshore |

## Choosing the regime

For a typical offshore use case (SPV, holding, fund
structure), the **2006 Act** is the more flexible and
cost-efficient choice. The 1931 Act remains relevant for:

- **Locally-trading** companies;
- **Regulated** entities preferring traditional UK-style
  governance;
- **Insurance** companies (separate regulatory regime
  but typically 1931-Act vehicles);
- **Specific** legacy or institutional preferences.

## Re-registration

An existing 1931-Act company can re-register under the
2006 Act:

- **Special resolution** of shareholders;
- **Filing** with the Companies Registry;
- **Effective** from date of registration;
- **Continuity** of legal personality and existing
  contracts.

Re-registration the other way (2006 → 1931) is more
restrictive and less common.

## Migration in / out

Both 1931 and 2006 Act companies can:

- **Re-domicile in** from foreign jurisdictions;
- **Re-domicile out** to compatible jurisdictions;
- **Merge** with foreign or domestic companies (specific
  procedures).

The migration regime makes the IoM a viable
restructuring jurisdiction in addition to a destination
for new incorporations.

## Use cases

### Offshore holding company

A typical IoM holdco for international business:

- **CA 2006 Act** vehicle;
- **One director** (often a corporate director provided
  by an IoM corporate services firm);
- **One shareholder** (often an EU or UK group parent);
- **No accounts filing**;
- **Light annual** return.

Competes with BVI BCs and Cayman Exempteds for this use.

### eGaming licensee

The IoM is a major eGaming jurisdiction:

- Licensees can use either Act;
- **CA 2006** is increasingly common for new licensees;
- **Specific** eGaming Supervision Commission
  regulation applies separately.

See [`egaming.md`](egaming.md).

### Insurance vehicle

Insurance vehicles (life assurance especially) often use
**CA 1931**, in part because the regulatory regime
historically aligned with the 1931 Act's procedural
patterns. Newer insurance structures may use the 2006
Act.

## Pitfalls

- **Choosing 1931** for a simple offshore SPV (over-
  formality);
- **Choosing 2006** for a regulated entity where the
  regulator expects 1931-Act familiarity;
- **Re-registration** complexity if circumstances change;
- **Counterparty** familiarity — UK and EU counterparties
  may be more comfortable with 1931-Act vehicles for
  governance / KYC purposes.
