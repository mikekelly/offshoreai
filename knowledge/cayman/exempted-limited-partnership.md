# Cayman Exempted Limited Partnership (ELP)

## What it is

The **Cayman Exempted Limited Partnership (ELP)** is the
dominant **closed-ended fund vehicle** for private equity,
venture capital, real estate, credit and infrastructure
fund structures globally. It is the partnership-form
counterpart to the exempted company:

- **One or more general partners (GPs)** with management
  authority and unlimited liability;
- **One or more limited partners (LPs)** with limited
  liability (limited to capital contribution and amounts
  contractually committed);
- **Operates under the Exempted Limited Partnership Act**
  (the "ELP Act") — a statute purpose-built for fund
  use.

The ELP form is a **contractual vehicle** more than a
corporate one — the **Limited Partnership Agreement
(LPA)** is the master document, and the ELP Act largely
provides default rules that can be modified by the LPA.

## Why ELPs dominate PE/VC

ELPs are preferred over corporate vehicles for closed-
ended funds because:

- **Tax transparency** in most major investor
  jurisdictions (USA, UK, EU) — LPs taxed as if they
  invested directly in underlying assets, avoiding entity-
  level tax;
- **Flexibility** of LPA — sponsors can shape governance,
  economics, and waterfall terms without statutory
  constraint;
- **Limited-liability** protection for LPs;
- **No statutory capital maintenance** — capital is
  contributed, distributed, and recycled per the LPA;
- **Investor familiarity** — global PE/VC LPs expect
  this form;
- **Compatibility** with parallel-fund structures
  (Delaware LP for US LPs; Cayman ELP for non-US LPs).

## Formation

Forming a Cayman ELP requires:

1. **Statement of Registration** filed with the Registrar
   of Exempted Limited Partnerships;
2. **General partner** — Cayman exempted company, foreign
   corporation, individual, or another ELP;
3. **At least one limited partner** at formation;
4. **Limited Partnership Agreement** (contractual, not
   filed publicly);
5. **Registered office** in Cayman (provided by a
   licensed corporate services provider);
6. **Initial fee** paid.

Typical timeline: **1-2 business days** for the
registration itself. LPA negotiation typically takes
weeks to months.

## The General Partner

The GP carries **unlimited liability** for the
partnership's debts and obligations. To manage this risk:

- The GP is typically a **separate Cayman exempted
  company** (or Delaware LLC) acting as nominee GP;
- The GP company has **minimal assets** other than its
  GP role;
- The actual management is delegated by the GP to an
  **Investment Manager** (often a separate Cayman or
  onshore entity that is the operating company of the
  sponsor).

The GP must be a "qualifying" person under the ELP Act —
typically requires Cayman registration if it is itself a
Cayman entity, or registration as a foreign-LP-GP if non-
Cayman.

## Limited Partners

LP characteristics:

- **Limited liability** to the amount of capital
  contributions and any amounts owed under the LPA;
- **No management role** in the partnership — exercising
  management would risk losing limited-liability
  protection (the "safe harbour" provisions of the ELP
  Act define what LPs can do without crossing the line);
- **Statutory safe harbours** include serving on advisory
  committees, voting on specified matters, and making
  capital contributions;
- **LPA can grant** additional rights and obligations.

## Standard fund-economics terms

A typical LPA includes:

- **Capital commitments** by LPs;
- **Management fee** payable to the GP / Investment
  Manager (typically 1.5-2% of committed or invested
  capital);
- **Carried interest** to the GP (typically 20% of
  profits above a preferred return);
- **Preferred return** to LPs (typically 8%);
- **GP catch-up** (typically 50% or 100%);
- **Investment period** (typically 5 years);
- **Fund term** (typically 10 years, extendable);
- **Distribution waterfall** (deal-by-deal or whole-fund);
- **GP commitment** (typically 1-2% of fund size);
- **Specific LPA-defined** investor protections.

## Cayman regulatory perimeter

ELPs that are **closed-ended funds** typically register
as **private funds** under the **Private Funds Act 2020**:

- **Registration** with CIMA;
- **Annual filings**;
- **Audited financial statements**;
- **CIMA oversight** over the fund.

ELPs that are **not investment vehicles** (e.g., joint-
venture partnerships, holding partnerships) typically
avoid registration as funds. The distinction turns on
whether the ELP is an "investment fund" for the purposes
of the Private Funds Act 2020 — see [`private-funds-act.md`](private-funds-act.md).

## Tax treatment

- **Cayman**: no direct tax on the ELP or partners;
- **LPs taxed in their home jurisdiction** as if they
  held the underlying assets directly (in most major
  jurisdictions — there are nuances);
- **GP taxed** on management fees and carried interest in
  its home jurisdiction;
- **Economic substance** requirements may apply to the
  GP and Investment Manager (under the Economic
  Substance Act 2018).

## Withdrawal and dissolution

- **LP withdrawal** typically restricted to LPA-permitted
  events (e.g., specific defaults, LP misconduct, GP
  buyout);
- **Closed-ended** structure means LPs cannot demand
  redemption (unlike open-ended funds);
- **Dissolution** at end of fund term, sale of final
  investment, or other LPA-triggered events;
- **Wind-up** procedure follows the ELP Act and LPA.

## Comparison with parallel structures

| Feature | Cayman ELP | Delaware LP | Jersey LP (LPL 1994) |
|---|---|---|---|
| Statutory regime | ELP Act | Revised Uniform LP Act | LP Law 1994 |
| Tax transparency | Yes | Yes | Yes |
| GP liability | Unlimited | Unlimited | Unlimited |
| LP liability | Limited | Limited | Limited |
| Typical use | Non-US-LP fund | US-LP fund | EU-LP fund (with NPPR) |
| Audit / disclosure | Private Funds Act 2020 | Limited | LPL 1994 + JFSC if regulated |

Parallel structures with **Cayman ELP** for non-US LPs
and **Delaware LP** for US LPs are routine in mid- and
large-cap PE funds.

## Recent reforms

The ELP Act has been amended multiple times since the
mid-1990s, modernising:

- **Migration** of foreign LPs to Cayman;
- **GP and LP** statutory safe harbours;
- **Economic substance** integration;
- **Beneficial-ownership** disclosure requirements;
- **AML/CFT** integration.

## Pitfalls

- **GP solvency** — using an under-capitalised GP can be
  problematic in distressed scenarios;
- **LP management participation** — crossing the safe-
  harbour line risks limited-liability loss;
- **Side-letter** management — bespoke LP arrangements
  create administrative complexity;
- **Substance** — paper-only Cayman presence may not
  satisfy Economic Substance Act requirements;
- **Open-ended use** — the ELP form is theoretically
  available for open-ended funds but is suboptimal vs
  exempted company for that use.
