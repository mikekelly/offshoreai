# Guernsey Protected Cell Company (PCC) — The Original

## What it is

The **Guernsey Protected Cell Company (PCC)** is the
**original cell-company concept** — Guernsey invented it
through the **Protected Cell Companies Ordinance 1997**
(now consolidated in the Companies (Guernsey) Law 2008).

A PCC is a **single legal entity** divided into:

- A **core**, with its own assets and liabilities
  (typically minimal);
- One or more **cells**, each with its own assets and
  liabilities **statutorily ringfenced** from the core
  and from each other.

The 1997 Ordinance was the global first — Bermuda
followed with SAC in 2000, Cayman with SPC in 1998, BVI
with SPC subsequently, Jersey with PCC in 2006. All
these are evolutions of the Guernsey original.

## The original invention

Before 1997, **no jurisdiction** offered a statutorily
segregated single-entity vehicle. The options were:

- **Separate companies** for each pool of business
  (expensive, slow);
- **Contractual** segregation within one company (no
  statutory protection against insolvency clawback).

Guernsey solved this by creating a **new corporate
form** — a company "containing" sub-units that the
statute treats as separately ringfenced for asset /
liability purposes while remaining a single legal entity.

## Why Guernsey

The driver was Guernsey's **captive insurance industry**.
Captive sponsors wanted multiple insurance programmes
through one regulated entity, with risk segregation
between programmes. The PCC delivered this. Insurance
became — and remains — the principal use case.

Subsequently funds and structured finance adopted the
PCC form. Today PCCs are used across multiple sectors.

## Formation

A Guernsey PCC is formed by:

1. **Incorporating** under the Companies (Guernsey) Law
   2008 as a PCC (or converting an existing company);
2. **GFSC** (Guernsey Financial Services Commission)
   approval where the activity is regulated (insurance,
   funds);
3. **Beneficial-ownership** disclosure;
4. **Registered office** in Guernsey;
5. **Initial cells** created at incorporation or by
   subsequent board resolution.

## Operating a PCC

### Cell creation and identification

Cells can be created and dissolved by board resolution
in accordance with the PCC's governing documents. Each
cell:

- Has a **unique designation**;
- Holds its own **assets and liabilities**;
- Is **statutorily protected** from the cells and from
  the core;
- Has its own **records and accounting**.

### Contracting

When acting in respect of a cell:

- The PCC must **identify itself** as the PCC acting in
  respect of a specific cell;
- **Cellular assets** of that cell are available to
  cellular creditors of that cell;
- **Core assets** are available to core creditors but
  **not** to cellular creditors except via their cell;
- **Inter-cellular dealings** are recorded as contracts
  between the PCC-as-cell-A and the PCC-as-cell-B.

### Statutory segregation

The segregation is **strong**:

- **Guernsey courts** must respect it;
- **Foreign courts** generally do, particularly in
  insurance contexts where Guernsey PCCs are well-known;
- **Improper cell identification** is the primary risk
  to segregation.

## PCC vs ICC

Guernsey has **two cell-company forms**:

- **PCC** (1997) — single legal entity with statutorily
  segregated cells;
- **ICC** (Incorporated Cell Company, later) — multiple
  legal entities, each cell is its own incorporated
  body.

See [`incorporated-cell-company.md`](incorporated-cell-company.md).

The PCC remains the more common form. The ICC is used
where:

- **Foreign-court** recognition risk is acute (each ICC
  cell is unambiguously a separate company);
- **Counterparties** insist on a separate legal
  personality;
- **Specific** regulatory regimes require separate
  entities.

## Use cases

### Captive insurance (the original use)

A captive parent runs multiple insurance programmes via
PCC cells:

- **Each cell** = one insurance programme;
- **Single licence** under the Guernsey insurance regime;
- **Single board** with cell-level economics tracked;
- **Cost-efficient** vs separate captives.

This is the genesis use case and remains dominant.

### Investment funds

Multi-class / multi-strategy funds use PCCs to segregate
investment strategies within one licensed fund vehicle.
The Guernsey funds regime accommodates PCC structures.

### Structured finance

Securitisation conduits with multiple bond series can
use a PCC, with each series in its own cell.

### Multi-strategy hedge / PE platforms

Sponsors running multiple strategies use PCCs for
single-entity multi-strategy operation.

## Regulatory perimeter

A PCC is **not regulated by virtue of form alone**.
Regulation attaches to the **activity** of the PCC and
its cells:

- **Insurance** — Insurance Business (Bailiwick of
  Guernsey) Law;
- **Funds** — POI Law / specific fund-regime regulations;
- **Banking** — Banking Supervision Law.

See [`gfsc.md`](gfsc.md) for the regulator.

## Comparison

| Feature | Guernsey PCC | Bermuda SAC | Cayman SPC | Guernsey ICC |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Vintage | **1997 (original)** | 2000 | 1998 | Later |
| Single legal entity | Yes | Yes | Yes | **No** |
| Statutory segregation | ✅ | ✅ | ✅ | ✅ (separate legal personality) |
| Insurance focus | Heavy use | Heavy use | Used | Used |
| Funds focus | Heavy use | Used | Heavy use | Used |
| Most foreign-court robust | Standard | Standard | Standard | Best (each cell is its own company) |

## Recent developments

The PCC regime has been refined through subsequent
amendments to the Companies Law and specific
regulations. The 1997 Ordinance has been superseded by
integrated provisions in the Companies (Guernsey) Law
2008.

## Pitfalls

- **Sloppy** cell identification in contracts;
- **Inter-cellular** dealings without proper
  documentation;
- **Common counterparty** exposure across cells;
- **Foreign-court** non-recognition in specific
  jurisdictions;
- **Cell allocation** of common expenses must be
  defensible.
