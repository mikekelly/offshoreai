# Non-Payment of Rent — Procedure for the Landlord

## The question

This is the question that triggered the coverage audit:

> "My tenant isn't paying rent. Can I just evict them?
> What protections do they have?"

The short answer is **no, you cannot just evict** — and
yes, the tenant has substantial protections. Non-payment is
a breach of the tenancy agreement under the **Residential
Tenancy (Jersey) Law 2011**, which means it triggers the
**statutory breach procedure**: written notice to remedy
within 7 days, followed (if unremedied) by application to
the Petty Debts Court for a termination and eviction order.
Self-help — changing the locks, removing the tenant, or
disposing of their belongings — is unlawful and may itself
be a criminal offence.

## The short answer

| Step | Who | What | When |
|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | Landlord | Serve written notice identifying the breach (unpaid rent and amount) and requiring the tenant to remedy it | Day 0 |
| 2 | Tenant | Has **7 days** to pay the arrears in full | By day 7 |
| 3 | Landlord | If unpaid, apply to **Petty Debts Court** for termination and eviction order | Day 7+ |
| 4 | Court | Hears the application; may grant order with possible **stay of execution** | Court-scheduled |
| 5 | Viscount | Enforces the eviction order — attends, removes tenant and possessions, restores possession | After order, subject to stay |

At no point may the landlord effect possession personally.

## The proper answer

### Step 1 — Identify the breach

Non-payment is a **breach of the agreement** within the
RTJL's breach regime. The landlord's right to terminate is
not automatic on missing a rent date — it is conditional
on serving statutory notice and the tenant failing to
remedy.

The notice should:

- Be **in writing** and signed by or on behalf of the
  landlord;
- **Identify the breach** specifically (rent due for
  which period(s), in what amount);
- **Require remedy** — payment of the arrears — within **7
  days** of service;
- **Be delivered** to the tenant (the agreement may
  specify a method; otherwise personal delivery or
  recorded post to the unit address).

The 7-day window is a statutory minimum. The landlord may
give longer, but cannot give less.

### Step 2 — Wait the 7 days

The tenant has the period stated in the notice (minimum 7
days) to pay the arrears. If the tenant pays within the
period, the breach is remedied and the tenancy continues
— the landlord cannot then proceed to termination on the
same arrears.

A partial payment is **not** remedy unless the agreement
or the notice specifies that the partial amount is
acceptable. The safer course is to require payment in full.

### Step 3 — Apply to the Petty Debts Court

If the arrears remain unpaid after the notice period, the
landlord applies to the **Petty Debts Court** for an order:

- **Terminating** the residential tenancy agreement;
- **Evicting** the tenant from the unit;
- (Optionally) a **money judgment** for the arrears and
  any subsequent rent that accrues.

The court has **exclusive original jurisdiction** under the
RTJL. The Royal Court does not handle residential-tenancy
disputes at first instance.

The application typically requires:

- The tenancy agreement;
- The condition report;
- Evidence of arrears (rent ledger, bank statements);
- A copy of the breach notice and proof of service;
- A schedule of attempts to engage with the tenant.

### Step 4 — The hearing

The Petty Debts Court hearing is relatively informal. The
court will consider:

- **Whether the breach is made out** — was rent due, was it
  unpaid, was statutory notice given, did 7 days elapse;
- **Whether the tenant has any defence** — disputed
  liability, set-off (e.g. landlord's failure to repair),
  procedural defects in notice;
- **Whether to grant a stay of execution** — typical for
  vulnerable tenants, families with children, those
  awaiting Income Support / rehousing.

The court **may**:

- **Refuse** the order if the breach is not made out or
  the procedure was defective;
- **Grant** the order, with **immediate effect** in clear
  cases;
- **Grant with stay of execution** — commonly 14-28 days,
  occasionally longer, to allow the tenant to find
  alternative accommodation or pay arrears.

### Step 5 — Enforcement by the Viscount

Once the order is granted (and any stay has expired) the
landlord applies for the Viscount to execute the eviction.
The Viscount:

- Attends the unit at a scheduled time;
- Gives the tenant the opportunity to leave with their
  possessions;
- **Removes** the tenant (with force if necessary) and
  their possessions if they refuse to leave;
- **Stores** removable possessions; **sells** items of
  realisable value; **disposes** of others unless claimed
  within 15 days;
- **Accounts** to the tenant for net proceeds (Viscount's
  fees deducted), or pays into the consolidated fund if
  the tenant cannot be located.

The Viscount's fees may be recovered from the tenant (or
the landlord under the lease terms). This is a real cost
of enforcement that landlords often underestimate.

## Tenant protections — what makes this "not easy"

A landlord seeking to evict for non-payment must navigate
several tenant-protective doctrines:

### Procedural

- **7-day notice** is statutory minimum; defective notice
  is fatal to the application;
- **Service requirements** — must be the correct address,
  correct addressee, correct mode under the agreement or
  by personal service;
- **Court application** required — no self-help.

### Substantive

- **Tenant set-off** for landlord breach (e.g. unfit
  premises — see [`repairs-and-standards.md`](repairs-and-standards.md)) may
  reduce or extinguish the arrears claim;
- **Discrimination defence** — if the eviction is in
  reality discriminatory under the Discrimination (Jersey)
  Law 2013 (race, sex, disability, etc.), the tenant may
  cross-claim;
- **Harassment** — landlords engaging in pressure tactics
  during the notice period (cutting off services, entering
  without consent, intimidation) commit a separate offence
  and risk damages claims.

### Vulnerability

- **Stay of execution** routinely granted for families with
  children, tenants on Income Support, or those awaiting
  social-housing placement;
- **Children (Jersey) Law 2002** considerations apply where
  minor children would be made homeless;
- **Mental Health (Jersey) Law 2016** considerations apply
  where a tenant has a capacity issue.

### Time

- **Total elapsed time** from missed payment to actual
  possession is typically **2-4 months** in a straightforward
  case, longer if disputed or stayed. Self-help would be
  faster but is unlawful.

## What a landlord should **not** do

- **Change the locks** — unlawful eviction; criminal
  offence under RTJL; tort liable in damages;
- **Cut off utilities** — harassment offence;
- **Remove the tenant's possessions** — conversion;
  criminal offence;
- **Enter the property without consent** — except per
  agreement (typically with 24-48 hours' written notice and
  at reasonable times);
- **Refuse a partial payment** intending to manufacture
  the arrears — courts dislike this and it may undermine
  the breach claim;
- **Sue for arrears in the Royal Court** at first
  instance — exclusive jurisdiction sits with Petty Debts
  Court.

## What a landlord **should** do

- **Document everything** from day 1 of arrears — emails,
  letters, payment records;
- **Engage early** — many arrears cases are resolved by
  payment plan once the tenant understands the
  consequences;
- **Serve the 7-day notice promptly** once it is clear the
  tenant will not pay voluntarily;
- **Check the agreement** — does it cover Viscount fees,
  costs, interest on arrears?
- **Consider** parallel claim for the money judgment if
  the tenant has assets;
- **Engage a Jersey advocate** if the matter is contested
  or the tenant is represented;
- **Document attempts to mitigate** — Citizens Advice
  referrals, payment-plan offers — courts look favourably
  on landlords who try.

## What a tenant **should** do

- **Engage** immediately on receiving any rent-arrears
  notice — do not ignore it;
- **Pay** within the 7-day window if at all possible;
- **Negotiate** a payment plan if not — many landlords
  prefer this to court;
- **Contact** Citizens Advice Jersey or JACS for support;
- **Consider** Income Support eligibility — see
  [`../use-cases/jersey-resident/index.md`](../use-cases/jersey-resident/index.md);
- **Raise** any set-off (landlord's repair failures, etc.)
  in writing and document;
- **Attend** any Petty Debts Court hearing — non-attendance
  typically results in default order against the tenant.
