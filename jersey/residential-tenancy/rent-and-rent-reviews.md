# Rent and Rent Reviews — Post-2025 Regime

## The post-15 April 2026 rules

Following the 2025 amendment, **periodic** residential
tenancies are subject to:

- **One increase per year** — no more than once in any
  12-month period;
- **RPI cap** — increase must not exceed the **Jersey
  Retail Prices Index** for the relevant period;
- **2 months' written notice** — minimum;
- **"Significantly below market rent" carve-out** — narrow,
  Tribunal-testable;
- **Rent Tribunal** — for disputes over the above.

These rules apply to **all periodic tenancies in
existence on or entered after 15 April 2026** —
including those grandfathered from before the amendment.

## Once per year

A landlord may increase rent on a periodic tenancy at most
**once in any rolling 12-month period**. The 12 months runs
from the **previous increase**, not from the start of the
tenancy.

A second increase within 12 months is unenforceable.

## RPI cap

The increase amount may not exceed the **Jersey RPI**
for the relevant period (typically the 12 months ending
at the most recent published figure before notice). The
landlord must:

- **Identify** the RPI figure relied upon;
- **Show** the calculation in the notice;
- **Round** consistently (typically to the nearest pound).

## 2 months' written notice

The increase takes effect no earlier than **2 months**
after written notice is delivered to the tenant. The notice
must:

- Be **written** and **signed**;
- **Identify** the property and the parties;
- **State** the new rent;
- **Show** the RPI figure relied upon;
- **Specify** the effective date.

## The "significantly below market rent" carve-out

A landlord may seek to increase rent **above RPI** where
the existing rent is **significantly below market rent**
for comparable units. This is a narrow exception:

- "Significantly" implies more than marginal;
- Comparable evidence is required (recent local lettings);
- The Rent Tribunal is the forum for testing the
  landlord's case;
- The Tribunal may permit the increase, refuse it, or
  set a lower figure.

In practice, this carve-out is used sparingly and is the
principal trigger for Rent Tribunal proceedings.

## Fixed-term rent reviews

For **fixed-term** tenancies (≤ 3 years post-2025):

- The agreement may **specify** a rent review at a stated
  date;
- Reviews must still observe the once-yearly and RPI rules
  in substance unless the increase relies on the "below
  market" carve-out;
- A landlord cannot use the fixed-term form to evade the
  RPI cap.

## Pre-2025 agreements with above-RPI rent-review clauses

A pre-existing agreement with, e.g., an "open market rent
review" clause:

- The clause is **unenforceable** to the extent it permits
  above-RPI increases without the carve-out;
- The Tribunal may strike out or modify the clause;
- The amended regime **supersedes** contractual
  provisions in conflict.

## Rent increases above RPI without Tribunal approval

A landlord who imposes an increase above RPI without
Tribunal approval:

- **Tenant is not required to pay** the excess;
- **Refusal to pay the excess is not arrears** of rent —
  i.e. the landlord cannot use non-payment of the excess
  as a ground for termination;
- **Tribunal application** by the tenant typically
  resolves the issue;
- **Repeated unlawful increases** may be an offence.

## Initial rent at commencement

The RTJL does **not** cap **initial rent** at commencement
of a new tenancy. The market sets the entry rent. The
caps apply to **subsequent increases** during the
tenancy.

This is the main route around the cap: a landlord could
end a tenancy and re-let at market rent, subject to the
notice and ground rules. The 2025 amendment makes this
harder by:

- Requiring 12 months' notice for no-fault termination;
- Subjecting suspected retaliatory / circumventing
  terminations to scrutiny.

## Social housing and exclusions

The cap rules do **not** apply to:

- **Social housing** (Andium, housing trusts) — separate
  regime;
- **Employment-linked accommodation** tied to a contract
  of employment;
- **Lodging-house** arrangements (outside the RTJL
  generally).

## Practical recommendations

For landlords:

- **Calendar** the once-yearly review date and don't try
  to add a second;
- **Apply RPI** as the default cap;
- **Document** the "below market" case carefully if
  relying on the carve-out;
- **Use the prescribed notice form** when issued by
  ministerial order;
- **Don't** issue retaliatory increases following a tenant
  complaint — Tribunal will scrutinise.

For tenants:

- **Check** any proposed increase against published Jersey
  RPI;
- **Demand** the RPI calculation in writing;
- **Apply to the Rent Tribunal** if the increase exceeds
  RPI without a clear "below market" case;
- **Do not** withhold the **pre-increase** rent — that is
  arrears and triggers eviction risk;
- **Document** the proposed increase, response, and any
  related communications.
