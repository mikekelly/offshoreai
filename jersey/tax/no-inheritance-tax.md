# No Inheritance Tax — Jersey

Jersey **does not have an inheritance tax** in the UK or US
sense. Wealth transferred at death, or by lifetime gift, is **not**
subject to a Jersey wealth-transfer tax.

## What Jersey does have

A residual **probate fee** / **droit de succession** applies on
formal probate of estates in Jersey. This is:

- A **registration fee** for administering the estate, not a
  wealth tax;
- **Modest** in amount, scaled to estate value;
- Levied to fund the administration of the **judicial greffe**'s
  probate function.

It is not comparable to UK Inheritance Tax (currently 40% on
estates above the nil-rate band) or US Federal Estate Tax.

## What this means

- **Death**: Jersey residents do not pay a Jersey wealth-transfer
  tax on death. Assets pass to heirs (subject to customary-law
  succession rules and any will) without Jersey IHT.
- **Lifetime gifts**: Jersey does not impose gift tax. A
  Jersey-resident grandparent giving shares to a grandchild
  incurs no Jersey gift tax.
- **Trust funding**: Settling assets on a Jersey trust does not
  trigger a Jersey IHT-equivalent.

## Why this matters internationally

Jersey is **attractive for succession planning** precisely
because:

- **Jersey-domiciled wealthy individuals** can transfer assets
  generationally without Jersey wealth-transfer tax;
- **Trust planning** in Jersey is not burdened by IHT-equivalent
  charges;
- **Family-office structures** can manage multi-generational
  wealth without IHT drag.

But, **as always**, foreign tax may apply:

- **UK IHT** applies to UK-domiciled individuals **on their
  worldwide assets** — Jersey residence does not shield from UK
  IHT;
- **US Federal Estate Tax** applies to US citizens worldwide and
  to non-US persons on US-situs assets;
- **Various civil-law jurisdictions** impose succession taxes on
  worldwide assets or local-situs assets.

A Jersey trust set up by a UK-domiciled settlor remains within
UK IHT scope under UK rules, despite the absence of Jersey IHT.

## Customary-law succession

Jersey **customary law** has historical rules about
"légitime" — fixed shares for spouse and children. **The Wills
and Successions (Jersey) Law 1993** reformed succession to
allow more testamentary freedom, but some customary-law shares
remain for movable estate.

Movable and immovable estate are governed by different rules
in Jersey customary law — a complexity beyond the tax framework
but relevant when planning Jersey wills.
