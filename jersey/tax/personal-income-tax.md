# Personal Income Tax — Jersey

Jersey charges personal income tax under the **[Income Tax (Jersey) Law
1961][itl]**. The headline rate is **20%**, but the effective rate for
many taxpayers is lower because of **marginal relief** (an exemption
threshold tapered against income). Revenue Jersey publishes the
allowances and thresholds for each Year of Assessment on
[gov.je][rj-allowances]; the numbers in this file are for **YA 2025**.

## Standard rate — 20%

> The standard rate of tax in Jersey is 20%, which is the maximum
> personal income tax you will pay in a year.
>
> — [Revenue Jersey — Marginal income deduction explained][mr]

Two important corollaries:

- Jersey does **not** apply progressive higher-rate bands. There is no
  40% or 45% band as in the UK. A Jersey-resident with very high
  income pays **20%** of total taxable income (after personal
  allowances and reliefs) and not a penny more in income tax.
- Jersey does **not** apply a separate higher rate to dividends or
  investment income. The single rate of 20% applies to all income that
  is brought into charge.

This is sometimes summarised as the "20 means 20" policy: 20% is both
the floor (after marginal relief is exhausted) and the ceiling.

## Marginal relief — how the effective rate is computed

For taxpayers below a certain income level, Jersey applies a
**marginal income deduction** that reduces the tax bill below the
20% standard. The deduction is "the deduction you get from the
standard tax rate to reduce your tax bill. The lower your income the
bigger the deduction." ([Revenue Jersey][mr])

The mechanism, in stylised form:

1. Tax is provisionally computed at the **standard rate of 20% on
   taxable income** after deducting personal allowances.
2. Tax is *also* computed under the **marginal-relief method**: the
   excess of total income (less specified marginal-relief deductions)
   over the **exemption threshold**, taxed at a higher percentage
   (currently 26% in published worked examples — confirm against the
   current YA rate before relying).
3. The taxpayer pays the **lower** of the two computations.

For low-income households, computation (2) gives a smaller bill; as
income rises, computation (1) eventually wins and the effective rate
converges on 20% from below. A Jersey resident with high income
therefore pays a flat 20%, and a Jersey resident with low income
pays much less in effective terms, but no Jersey resident ever pays
**more than 20%** in personal income tax on their total income.
([Revenue Jersey][mr])

## YA 2025 exemption thresholds

The personal-tax **exemption thresholds** for Year of Assessment 2025
published by Revenue Jersey are:

| Item | YA 2025 |
|---|---|
| Single person exemption threshold | **£20,700** |
| Married / civil-partnership exemption threshold | **£33,200** |
| Second-earner's allowance | **£8,200** |
| Child allowance (per child) | **£3,850** |
| Additional allowance for children (further uplift to the exemption threshold) | **£5,750** |
| Standard childcare tax relief | **£7,850** |
| Higher childcare tax relief (pre-school) | **£20,400** |

Source: [Revenue Jersey — 2025 tax allowances and reliefs][rj-allowances].

The thresholds are revised by States Assembly resolution as part of
the annual Government Plan and should be re-verified for each new YA.
For the immediately preceding YA, see Revenue Jersey's [2024
allowances page](https://www.gov.je/TaxesMoney/IncomeTax/Individuals/AllowancesReliefs/pages/2024taxallowances.aspx).

## Residence

Personal residence for Jersey income-tax purposes turns on physical
presence in the Island and the surrounding facts; the principal status
categories used by Revenue Jersey are **resident**, **ordinarily
resident** and **non-resident**. The criteria, day-counting rules and
implications for foreign-source income are dealt with separately in
[residence-individuals.md](residence-individuals.md). *(That file is a
stub at this revision.)*

## Foreign-source income

A Jersey **resident and ordinarily resident** individual is taxable on
worldwide income, with relief for foreign tax suffered under the
relevant double-taxation agreement where one exists (see
[international-tax.md](international-tax.md)) or by unilateral relief
where it does not. A Jersey **resident but not ordinarily resident**
individual is taxable on Jersey-source income and on foreign income
brought into Jersey (a remittance-type regime). A **non-resident** is
taxable in Jersey only on Jersey-source income — most importantly
Jersey-employment income and Jersey-property income.

## Interaction with social security and long-term care

Personal income tax is separate from:

- **Social-security contributions**, payable on earnings up to an
  annual ceiling; see [social-security.md](social-security.md).
- The **Long-Term Care contribution**, collected through the
  income-tax mechanism; see [long-term-care.md](long-term-care.md).

For a Jersey-resident employee, the income tax, social-security
contribution, and LTC contribution are typically deducted at source
through the **ITIS** (Income Tax Instalment System) employer scheme.

## High-Value Residency

A specialised tax regime applies to individuals granted housing consent
under the 2(1)(e) **High-Value Residency** route. Those individuals
have a separate tax computation with a **minimum tax contribution**
each year, intended to ensure that the wealthy newcomer makes a
material contribution to Jersey revenues. See
[high-value-residency-tax.md](high-value-residency-tax.md) and the
[housing-and-work side](../immigration-residency/high-value-residency.md).
*(Both files are stubs at this revision.)*
