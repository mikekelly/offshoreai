# Planning Enforcement

## The framework

The PBL 2002 gives the Minister and Department broad
enforcement powers against:

- **Development without planning permission**;
- **Breach of conditions** attached to an approved
  application;
- **Breach of listed-building** controls;
- **Breach of conservation-area** restrictions;
- **Failure to comply** with the Building Bye-Laws.

## Powers available

### Enforcement notice

The principal tool. An enforcement notice:

- **Identifies** the breach;
- **Specifies the steps** required to remedy it
  (typically: cease work, reverse changes, restore);
- **Sets a time** for compliance;
- **Becomes effective** after a specified date (unless
  appealed);
- **Failure to comply** is a criminal offence with
  unlimited fines.

### Stop notice

For ongoing unauthorised works:

- **Immediate cessation** ordered;
- **Effective immediately** on service;
- **Failure to comply** is a serious offence.

### Prosecution

For unauthorised development:

- **Criminal offence** under the PBL 2002;
- **Unlimited fines** on indictment;
- **Possible imprisonment** for serious cases (typically
  cases involving listed buildings, conservation areas,
  or commercial development without permission).

### Direct action

The Minister can:

- **Carry out remedial works** at the owner's cost;
- **Recover costs** as a debt;
- **Apply** for a Royal Court order if needed.

### Listed-building offences

Unauthorised works to a listed building:

- **Serious criminal offence** in their own right;
- **Unlimited fines**;
- **Imprisonment** for the most serious cases (active
  destruction of historic features);
- **Restoration orders** may require reinstatement at
  the owner's cost — even if the original feature was
  destroyed.

## How enforcement starts

Typical triggers:

- **Public complaint** — neighbours frequently report
  unauthorised works;
- **Aerial / satellite imagery** — annual comparisons
  catch additions;
- **Planning portal monitoring** — Department compares
  approved vs as-built;
- **Sale of property** — buyer's solicitor investigations
  surface unauthorised works;
- **Other regulatory contact** — building inspector
  visits, environmental health inspections.

## What the Department does

When alerted to a potential breach:

1. **Investigation** — site visit, photographs,
   examination of records;
2. **Determination** — is there a breach?
3. **Engagement** — typically writing to the owner /
   occupier first, asking for explanation;
4. **Options assessment**:
   - **No further action** (work is permitted /
     authorised after all);
   - **Retrospective application** invited;
   - **Enforcement notice** issued;
   - **Prosecution** for serious cases;
5. **Notice / proceedings** if no resolution.

## Retrospective applications

You can apply for permission **after** carrying out
unauthorised works:

- **Same process** as a normal application;
- **No automatic acceptance** — the application is judged
  on its merits;
- **Removal** still possible if the application is
  refused;
- **Higher scrutiny** sometimes — particularly for
  listed-building / conservation-area cases.

A retrospective application **doesn't reverse** the
underlying breach — it can only authorise the works
going forward. The owner may still face enforcement for
the period of unauthorised development.

## Defences

In enforcement proceedings:

- **Permission was granted** (with evidence);
- **Permitted development** under the GD Order 2011
  (with detailed measurements);
- **Not development** at all (within the statutory
  definition);
- **Four-year / ten-year rule** — limitation periods
  may apply in specific Jersey contexts;
- **Procedural defects** in the enforcement notice.

## Appeals against enforcement

An enforcement notice can be appealed on grounds
including:

- **Permission should be granted** (effectively a
  retrospective application);
- **The matters alleged are not breaches**;
- **The required steps are excessive**;
- **The time for compliance is unreasonable**;
- **Procedural defects** in the notice.

Appeals follow the standard planning appeal route — see
[`appeals.md`](appeals.md).

## Cost recovery

If the Department carries out remedial works:

- **Recoverable as a debt** against the property owner;
- **Charge on the property** in some cases;
- **Costs can be substantial** for major restoration.

## Impact on sale and lending

Unauthorised works affect:

- **Buyer due diligence** — solicitors check planning
  history;
- **Mortgage availability** — some lenders refuse;
- **Insurance** — affected for unauthorised structures;
- **Sale price** — discounted for unresolved issues;
- **Indemnity insurance** — sometimes available but
  conditional.

## Practical recommendations

For owners:

- **Don't build without permission** — the cost of
  enforcement vastly exceeds the cost of a proper
  application;
- **Keep records** — approved drawings, decision
  notices, building approval certificates;
- **If you bought a property with unauthorised works**,
  investigate immediately — you inherit the
  enforcement risk;
- **Engage promptly** with the Department if a breach is
  alleged — cooperative resolution is much cheaper than
  litigation.

For neighbours / objectors:

- **Report** suspected breaches promptly;
- **Documentary evidence** (photographs, dates) is
  valuable;
- **Specific** complaints carry more weight than generic
  ones;
- **Anonymity** — complaints are typically handled
  confidentially, though source may be inferable.
