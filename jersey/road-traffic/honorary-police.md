# The Honorary Police

## What this is

Jersey is policed by **two parallel forces**:

- The **States of Jersey Police** — uniformed, salaried,
  full-time professional force;
- The **Honorary Police** of each parish — elected,
  unpaid, part-time volunteers who serve their parish.

This dual structure is **distinctive to the Channel
Islands** and has no UK equivalent. It is rooted in the
parishes' historic constitutional position as the
fundamental unit of Jersey civic government.

## The roles

Each parish has three ranks of honorary police:

### Connétable

The **Connétable** is the elected head of the parish. They
are also **ex officio** a member of the **States
Assembly** (Jersey's parliament) and head of the parish
honorary police force. The Connétable has broad civic and
ceremonial functions in addition to policing oversight.

### Centenier

A **Centenier** is the second-most-senior honorary police
officer in the parish. Centeniers:

- Conduct **Parish Hall Enquiries** for offences arising
  in their parish — see
  [`parish-hall-enquiries.md`](parish-hall-enquiries.md);
- Have powers of **arrest and charge** under prescribed
  conditions;
- **Present cases** at the Magistrate's Court for matters
  the parish prosecutes;
- **Conduct civil ceremonies** (marriages, in some cases);
- **Serve summonses** and other formal documents;
- **Perform parish-administrative** functions.

There are typically several Centeniers per parish, elected
for three-year terms.

### Vingtenier

A **Vingtenier** is the most junior honorary police rank,
historically responsible for one of the **vingtaines**
(parish sub-divisions). Vingteniers:

- **Patrol** and respond to incidents;
- **Issue** preliminary breath tests and other tests
  under Centenier direction;
- **Report** offences to Centeniers for Parish Hall
  proceedings;
- **Assist** at parish events and emergencies.

### Constable's Officers

In some parishes there are also **Constable's Officers** —
helpers under the Connétable's direction.

## How the dual policing works

The two forces operate **in parallel** with overlapping
jurisdiction:

- **States Police** lead on **serious crime**, **major
  incidents**, **drug enforcement**, and **detailed
  investigations**;
- **Honorary Police** lead on **parish-level
  enforcement**, **routine motoring offences**,
  **community policing**, and **Parish Hall matters**;
- Both can attend the same incident; coordination is
  routine.

In road-traffic terms specifically:

- A speeding stop may be initiated by **either force**;
- A **breath test** may be administered by either;
- An arrest may be made by either;
- Subsequent **Parish Hall referral** is to the
  **Honorary Police of the parish where the offence
  occurred**, regardless of which force made the stop.

## Why this matters for road traffic

For a Jersey driver involved in a routine traffic incident:

- **Most enforcement encounters** at parish level involve
  the Honorary Police;
- **Parish Hall** disposition is the typical resolution
  for minor matters;
- **Centeniers** decide whether to caution, fine, or refer
  to court;
- The system reflects the **parishes' historic role** as
  the basic unit of civic government.

Foreign drivers — and even some new Jersey residents —
sometimes mistake the volunteer-elected Honorary Police
for "less serious" or "informal" police. They are
**fully empowered** under Jersey law and their enforcement
actions are legally effective in the same way as those
of the States Police.

## Recent reforms

Periodically there are proposals to reform the dual-force
structure — combining or rationalising. So far the
parishes have successfully argued for retaining the
Honorary Police as constitutionally and operationally
distinct.
