# Cohabitation — The Jersey Gap in Financial Protection

## The gap

Jersey **does not formally recognise cohabitation** for
the purposes of financial relief on relationship
breakdown. Unmarried couples — however long-term — do
**not** have access to:

- **Ancillary relief** equivalent to divorce;
- **Property adjustment** orders against jointly-used
  assets;
- **Spousal maintenance**;
- **Pension offset** considerations;
- **Forced-heirship** spousal rights on death.

This is a significant divergence from many comparable
jurisdictions and a substantial practical risk.

## What's available

A separating cohabitant has limited remedies:

### Property ownership

- **Beneficial ownership** disputes — proprietary
  estoppel, constructive trust, resulting trust — under
  ordinary property law principles;
- **Contributions** to a property in another's name may
  generate a proprietary claim;
- **Documentary evidence** essential;
- **Litigation** is expensive and uncertain.

### Unjust enrichment

A claim that the other party has been **unjustly
enriched** at the claimant's expense — Jersey applies the
doctrine following Pothier and modern Royal Court
jurisprudence. Available where:

- The claimant has **conferred a benefit** on the
  defendant;
- The defendant has been **enriched**;
- The enrichment is **unjust** (e.g. no consideration,
  mistaken expectation);
- **No legal basis** justifies the enrichment.

This is **more onerous** than matrimonial claims and
typically produces less generous outcomes.

### Schedule 1 claims (for children)

Where there are **children**, Schedule 1 of the
**Children (Jersey) Law 2002** provides for:

- **Child maintenance**;
- **Lump sums** for child-related expenses;
- **Property transfers** for the child's housing;
- **Settlements** of property for the child's benefit.

These are claims for the **child** (mediated through the
resident parent), not financial relief for the parent
themselves. The orders end when the child no longer
requires support.

### Contractual arrangements

Cohabiting couples can mitigate risk by:

- **Cohabitation agreements** (contract-law instruments);
- **Joint property ownership** documented carefully;
- **Trust** arrangements;
- **Wills** making proper provision (and updating on
  separation).

These are **not** as robust as marriage-equivalent
protections but provide some structure.

## Reform pipeline

A Jersey cohabitation regime has been **discussed** but
no firm legislative proposals are in force as of May
2026. Pressure for reform comes from:

- **Long-term cohabitants** facing inadequate remedies on
  separation;
- **Surviving cohabitants** without succession rights;
- **Comparative analysis** with Scotland (Family Law
  (Scotland) Act 2006), Ireland (Civil Partnership and
  Certain Rights and Obligations of Cohabitants Act
  2010), and other jurisdictions.

## Practical recommendations

For cohabiting couples:

- **Document** financial arrangements (joint accounts,
  property ownership, contributions);
- **Write a will** — cohabitants have no automatic
  succession rights;
- **Consider a cohabitation agreement** — particularly
  before significant joint investment;
- **Title** property carefully — joint or sole, with
  documented agreement on contributions;
- **Discuss** what would happen on separation — frankly,
  in writing.

For separating cohabitants:

- **Document** contributions, agreements, expectations;
- **Specialist advice** early;
- **Mediation** as cost-effective first step;
- **Consider** unjust enrichment and Schedule 1 routes;
- **Schedule 1** for children-related claims always
  available where applicable.

## Pitfalls

- **Assuming "common-law marriage"** — does not exist in
  Jersey law;
- **Long-term reliance** on a partner without legal
  protection;
- **Contributing** to a property in another's sole name
  without documenting expectations;
- **No will** — surviving cohabitant inherits nothing on
  intestacy.
