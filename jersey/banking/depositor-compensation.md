# Banking Depositor Compensation Scheme — Jersey

The **Banking Business (Depositors Compensation) (Jersey)
Regulations 2009** establish Jersey's **deposit compensation
scheme** — providing limited protection to retail depositors of
failed Jersey banks.

## Scope

The scheme covers:

- **Retail depositors** (individuals and certain small entities)
  with eligible deposits in a **Jersey-licensed bank**;
- **Maximum compensation** is prescribed in the Regulations
  (historically up to **£50,000 per depositor per bank**);
- **Excluded** are large corporates, other banks, and certain
  professional depositors;
- The scheme applies only to **Jersey-bank failures** — not to
  failures of foreign banks operating offshore branches.

## Funding

The scheme is funded through:

- **Industry levies** on participating banks;
- **Risk-based contributions** in some configurations;
- **States of Jersey backstop** if the levy is inadequate.

The detailed funding mechanism is in the Regulations.

## Process on a bank failure

If a Jersey bank fails:

- The **Compensation Board** is activated;
- Depositors **submit claims**;
- The Board **verifies** entitlement;
- **Compensation** is paid up to the prescribed maximum;
- The Board takes a **subrogated claim** against the bank's
  estate.

## Comparison

The Jersey scheme is **less generous** than:

- The UK FSCS (£85,000 per depositor in 2020s);
- The EU Deposit Guarantee Scheme regime (€100,000).

This reflects:

- **Smaller industry** and corresponding levy capacity;
- **Higher-end client mix** of Jersey banks (most retail
  depositors are typically below the threshold anyway);
- **Different regulatory positioning**.

## Why Jersey banking is still attractive

Despite the lower scheme cap:

- Jersey banks are typically **subsidiaries of well-capitalised
  parent groups**, reducing the risk of failure;
- **Private banking** clients often diversify across multiple
  institutions and jurisdictions;
- **Reputational and parent-group support** is high;
- The Jersey banking sector has been **stable** through global
  financial crises.
