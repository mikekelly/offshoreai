# Article 113C — False Statements to Auditors

Article 113C of the **[Companies (Jersey) Law 1991][cjl]** makes
it an **offence** to make **false statements** to auditors or to
**obstruct** their investigations.

## What Article 113C covers

It is an **offence** to:

- **knowingly or recklessly** make **false statements** to
  auditors; or
- **obstruct** auditor investigations.

Company officers must provide:

- **truthful information**;
- **complete access** to records necessary for audit purposes.

(See the consolidated text at [jerseylaw.je][cjl].)

## Why criminalised

The audit only works if the auditor gets **complete and accurate
information**. Officers who:

- **Lie to auditors** about material matters;
- **Hide records** from auditor inspection;
- **Mislead** about the substance of transactions;
- **Otherwise frustrate** the audit process;

undermine the auditor's ability to give a meaningful report. The
Article makes this **criminal**.

## Mental element

The offence requires **knowledge or recklessness**:

- **Knowingly** — the officer knew the statement was false;
- **Recklessly** — the officer was indifferent to whether the
  statement was true.

Innocent inaccuracies are **not** caught — the auditor's job is
to identify these through normal audit procedure.

## Penalty

Penalty in Schedule 1 of the Companies Law — typically fine,
imprisonment, or both.

## Pitfalls

- **Aggressive answers** to auditor questions can drift into
  recklessness territory. Be careful.
- **Withholding documents** on confidentiality grounds — the
  auditor's access is statutory; confidentiality is not a valid
  reason to withhold from the company's own auditor.
