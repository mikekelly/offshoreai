# Article 115A — Consequences of Unlawful Distribution

Article 115A of the **[Companies (Jersey) Law 1991][cjl]**
specifies the **consequences** when a distribution is made in
breach of [Article 115](distribution-restrictions.md) —
typically requiring **repayment** by the recipient members and
exposing directors to personal liability.

## What Article 115A says

Members who **received distributions violating Article 115**
remain **liable to repay** such amounts to the company, **subject
to defences** regarding **knowledge** and **receipt
circumstances**.

(See the consolidated text at [jerseylaw.je][cjl].)

## The repayment obligation

A member who received an unlawful distribution is **liable to
repay** the amount received. The repayment:

- Goes to the **company** (which has been wronged);
- Is treated as a **debt** of the member;
- May be enforced by **civil proceedings**;
- Can be claimed by a **Liquidator** if the company has
  subsequently entered wind-up.

## Defences

The Article preserves defences regarding:

- **Knowledge** — a member who received the distribution **without
  knowing or having reason to know** it was unlawful may have a
  defence in some circumstances;
- **Receipt circumstances** — the specific facts of the receipt
  may affect the strength of the claim against the member.

The defences are **narrow** in practice. Members of close
companies (who are typically aware of the distribution's
substantive solvency context) cannot rely on these defences.
Members of more arm's-length structures (e.g., listed-company
investors who couldn't know the directors had not signed a
proper solvency statement) may have a stronger defence.

## Director personal liability

Directors who **authorised the unlawful distribution**:

- May be **personally liable** to the company for the amount;
- Face **disqualification** under
  [Article 78](disqualification-orders.md);
- May face **criminal liability** for fraudulent solvency
  statements;
- Are exposed in any **subsequent insolvency** of the company —
  the Liquidator typically pursues directors for unlawful
  distributions as a matter of routine.

## In wind-up

A company that goes into wind-up after making unlawful
distributions:

- The **Liquidator** examines past distributions for compliance;
- **Unlawful distributions** are clawed back as a priority
  (members repay);
- **Directors face personal claims** alongside the member
  claims.

This pattern — unlawful distribution + insolvency + director
claims — is one of the most common Liquidator-litigation
scenarios.

## Practical implications

For a company **considering a distribution**:

- The **solvency-statement procedure** must be followed
  properly;
- Documentation should be **contemporaneous and thorough**;
- If solvency is **doubtful**, the distribution should be
  reduced or deferred — not the procedure cut short.

For a member receiving a distribution:

- In a **close company**, the member is on inquiry — ask whether
  the solvency analysis is sound;
- In a **listed or public-company** context, the member typically
  has the defence of reasonable ignorance.

## Pitfalls

- **Ignoring the post-distribution exposure** — directors may
  face claims years after the distribution if the company
  later becomes insolvent.
- **Member defences over-estimated** — particularly in close
  companies, member-knowledge defences are limited.
- **Liquidator scrutiny** — assume the wind-up Liquidator will
  examine past distributions; ensure documentation supports
  the solvency analysis.
