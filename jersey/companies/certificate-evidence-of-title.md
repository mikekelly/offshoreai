# Article 51 — Certificate to Be Evidence of Title

Article 51 of the **[Companies (Jersey) Law 1991][cjl]** establishes
the **evidentiary effect** of a properly-issued share certificate
— it is **prima facie evidence of title** but not conclusive
against the company.

## What Article 51 says

A properly-issued certificate constitutes **prima facie evidence
of the holder's title** to the shares specified.

However, it is **not conclusive proof** against the company's
own claims.

(See the consolidated text at [jerseylaw.je][cjl].)

## Prima facie vs conclusive

The distinction matters:

- **Prima facie evidence**: the certificate is **accepted as
  sufficient** in the absence of contrary evidence. A holder
  can rely on the certificate to prove title in most
  transactions.
- **Conclusive proof**: the certificate would be **unchallengeable**.
  Article 51 stops short of this.

The Article 51 standard means:

- **Third parties dealing with the holder** can rely on the
  certificate as evidence of the holder's title;
- The **company can challenge** the certificate's validity if
  it has evidence of error (e.g., the underlying transfer was
  not properly registered, the certificate was issued in error,
  the holder is not in fact on the register).

## Where the register prevails

If there is a discrepancy between the **certificate** and the
**register**, the **register** prevails as the canonical record.
The certificate is evidence; the register is the legal record.

This means a certificate that says X holds 100 shares is good
evidence — but if the register shows X holds 50 shares (and the
discrepancy is genuine), X is the member of 50 shares for
legal purposes.

## Lost or stolen certificates

Where a certificate is lost or stolen, the member can request
a **replacement** subject to the Article 46 declaration and
typically an indemnity. The replacement carries the same
evidentiary weight; the lost original loses any practical
significance (and is typically cancelled in the company's
records).

## Modern alternative — uncertificated shares

For uncertificated shares under
[Article 51A](uncertificated-securities.md), there is no physical
certificate. The **register entry itself** is the canonical
record of title, with similar evidential weight.

## Pitfalls

- **Treating the certificate as conclusive** — it's prima
  facie, not conclusive. Always cross-check the register for
  important transactions.
- **Out-of-date certificate** — if the register has been
  updated since the certificate was issued (e.g., a partial
  disposal not reflected in a returned certificate), the
  register is canonical.
