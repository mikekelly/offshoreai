# Article 26 — Membership of Holding Company (Cross-Holdings Prohibited)

Article 26 of the **[Companies (Jersey) Law 1991][cjl]** prohibits
a subsidiary from being a **member of its own holding company**.
The rule prevents circular cross-holdings within a group.

## What Article 26 says

A body corporate **cannot generally be a member of its own holding
company**. Any **allotment or transfer of shares** from a holding
body to its subsidiary is **void**.

### Exception 1 — pre-1992 holdings

Subsidiaries that **held membership before 30 March 1992** may
**continue as members**, but:

- **cannot vote** at company meetings; and
- **cannot acquire additional shares** in the holding body,
  **except** through **capitalisation of reserves**.

### Exception 2 — fiduciary capacities

The prohibition **does not apply** when the subsidiary acts in
the capacity of:

- **personal representative** (e.g., as executor of an estate); or
- **trustee** (where the trust property includes shares of the
  holding body).

In those cases, the subsidiary holds the shares **in trust** for
others, not for its own beneficial interest.

### Nominees

The restrictions apply equally to **nominees acting for
subsidiaries** — the prohibition cannot be circumvented by
interposing a nominee.

(See the consolidated text at [jerseylaw.je][cjl].)

## Why this rule exists

The prohibition prevents **circular cross-holdings** that would
allow management to entrench itself by directing the subsidiary
to vote the parent's own shares. The mischief:

- Parent A owns 100% of Subsidiary B;
- Subsidiary B owns shares in Parent A;
- B's shares in A are effectively voted by A's management (who
  control B);
- A's external shareholders are diluted in voting power without
  any economic mechanism justifying it.

By voiding the allotment or transfer, the Law makes the
cross-holding **legally impossible** going forward.

## Grandfathered pre-1992 holdings

The 30 March 1992 grandfathering preserves pre-existing
cross-holdings (which existed under the pre-1991 regime) but:

- **disenfranchises** them (no voting); and
- **freezes** them (no new acquisitions except via reserves
  capitalisation, which is essentially a free issue).

This is a typical transitional rule — old structures preserved but
not allowed to grow.

## Fiduciary-capacity exception

The fiduciary exception reflects the **substance** of the
holding. Where a subsidiary holds shares **as trustee** for
others, the subsidiary doesn't have **beneficial control** of the
shares — the trust beneficiaries do. Cross-holding concerns don't
apply.

A typical fiduciary scenario:

- Subsidiary B is a Jersey trust-company business (TCB);
- A trust administered by B holds shares of Parent A;
- B (as trustee) is the registered holder;
- This is permitted under Article 26 because B is acting in trust
  capacity, not for its own benefit.

## Pitfalls

- **Inadvertent cross-holding** in a group restructuring. M&A
  transactions sometimes throw up a situation where the target
  group includes shares of the acquiring company; restructure
  to eliminate before completion.
- **Nominee structures** can't circumvent Article 26 — the
  prohibition runs through nominees.
- **Distinguishing trustee from holding-in-own-right** — the
  trust capacity must be substantively real; nominal trusts to
  evade Article 26 won't work.
