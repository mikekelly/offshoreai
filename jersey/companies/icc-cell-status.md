# Article 127YD — Status of Cells

Article 127YD of the **[Companies (Jersey) Law 1991][cjl]**
clarifies the **legal status of an incorporated cell** — the
hallmark feature that distinguishes the ICC from the PCC.

## What Article 127YD covers

An incorporated cell is:

- A **separate legal person**;
- A **company** within the meaning of the Companies Law (with
  all the consequent rights and obligations);
- **Distinct** from the cell company (the ICC) of which it is a
  cell;
- Subject to its **own** memorandum and articles, directors,
  members, accounting records, etc. — though with some shared
  features under Article 127YDA;
- **Liable for its own debts** independently of the ICC and of
  other cells of the ICC.

The cell **continues to exist** until it is wound up or
otherwise dissolved in the normal way.

(See the consolidated text at [jerseylaw.je][cjl].)

## Why this matters

This article is the **legal foundation** of the ICC structure.
The fact that each cell is a **separate company**:

- Provides **structural segregation** of liabilities — cell A's
  creditors have no claim on cell B's assets (and the structural
  protection is the strongest available in Jersey law, since it
  is straightforward "separate-legal-person" segregation);
- Allows each cell to have **different members, directors, and
  governance**;
- Makes the cell **transferable** (subject to Article 127YI);
- Allows the cell to **convert** into a standalone company
  (Article 127YH).

This contrasts with the **PCC** where cells are statutory
compartments within a single legal entity — segregation is by
operation of law (Article 127YT) rather than by separate legal
personality.
