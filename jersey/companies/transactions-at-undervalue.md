# Article 176 — Transactions at an Undervalue

Article 176 of the **[Companies (Jersey) Law 1991][cjl]** is one
of the **central claw-back provisions** of the Jersey insolvency
regime: transactions at an undervalue entered into before
liquidation can be **unwound** by the liquidator.

## What Article 176 covers

A transaction is at an **undervalue** where the company entered
into it:

- As a **gift** or transaction for no consideration; or
- For consideration whose value, in money or money's worth, is
  **significantly less** than the value provided by the company.

The transaction is **vulnerable** to claw-back if:

- It was entered into **during the relevant period** before
  commencement of winding up (typically **5 years for connected
  persons**, **6 months for unconnected**);
- The company was **insolvent** at the time or became insolvent
  in consequence (or, for connected-person transactions, this is
  presumed).

The Royal Court may, on the liquidator's application, make such
order as it thinks fit to **restore** the position to what it
would have been if the company had not entered into the
transaction — typically:

- **Restoration of the property** to the company;
- **Payment of the value** by the recipient;
- **Compensation** for benefits received.

(See the consolidated text at [jerseylaw.je][cjl].)

## Why this matters

Transactions at undervalue are a **common form of pre-insolvency
asset stripping**:

- **Owner-directors** transferring company assets to themselves
  or related entities;
- **Group transfers** of valuable assets to solvent affiliates;
- **Sham transactions** with associated parties.

Article 176 lets the liquidator **recover these assets** for the
creditor body — turning back the clock on pre-insolvency
extraction.

## The "connected persons" presumption

For transactions with **connected persons** (defined in Article
176B), the presumption of insolvency makes claw-back substantially
easier. The transferee bears the **burden** of showing the
company was solvent and remained solvent.

This reflects the **policy view** that transactions with
insiders are inherently suspect when the company subsequently
becomes insolvent.
