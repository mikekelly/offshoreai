# Article 115ZA — Court Order Regarding Distributions

Article 115ZA of the **[Companies (Jersey) Law 1991][cjl]** allows
the Royal Court to **declare that a distribution was made in
compliance with Article 115** — even where there was a procedural
deficiency in the solvency-statement compliance.

## What Article 115ZA says

Where a distribution's legality is questioned, the **Court may
declare** it was **made in compliance with Article 115
restrictions** — provided the company **satisfied the solvency
requirements** at the time of the distribution.

(See the consolidated text at [jerseylaw.je][cjl].)

## When this is used

The Article addresses scenarios where:

- A distribution **was actually made on a solvency basis** —
  the company was solvent and could pay its debts as they fell
  due;
- But there was a **procedural deficiency** — e.g., the
  solvency statement wasn't formally signed by every director,
  the documentation was lost, the statement was signed late,
  or a formality was missed;
- A **third party** (often a creditor or member) is asking
  whether the distribution can be challenged.

Article 115ZA lets the Royal Court look at the **substantive
solvency position** and, if satisfied that the company was
genuinely solvent, **confirm the distribution** despite the
procedural shortfall.

## What the company must show

To obtain the Article 115ZA declaration, the company must
demonstrate:

- **The company was solvent** at the time of the distribution
  (i.e., the substantive Article 115 test was met);
- **Post-distribution**, the company could meet debts as they
  fell due;
- The procedural deficiency was **genuine** (a mistake or
  oversight) rather than a cover for substantive insolvency.

## What the order does

The Court's order:

- **Confirms** the distribution as lawful;
- **Cures** the procedural deficiency;
- **Forecloses** subsequent challenges based on the procedural
  issue;
- **Allows the company to record** the distribution as
  authorised.

The order is **discretionary** — the Court can refuse if it
considers the deficiency too serious or the substantive solvency
unproven.

## Procedure

Application by **Representation** in the Royal Court (Samedi
Division). Supporting evidence typically includes:

- Contemporaneous **financial records** showing solvency;
- **Directors' subsequent affidavits** explaining the position;
- **Auditor or accountant evidence** on the solvency analysis;
- Documentation of what **was done procedurally** (if anything).

## Pitfalls

- **Treating Article 115ZA as a routine cure-all** — it isn't.
  Get the procedure right first; reserve Article 115ZA for
  genuine inadvertent errors.
- **Lack of contemporaneous evidence** — the court needs to see
  that solvency genuinely existed at the time. Retrospective
  reconstruction is weak.
