# Article 170 — Powers and Duties of Liquidator

Article 170 of the **[Companies (Jersey) Law 1991][cjl]** is the
**central article** specifying the **powers and duties** of a
liquidator in a creditors' winding up.

## What Article 170 covers

The liquidator has power to:

- **Take possession** of the company's property;
- **Sell** the company's assets (movable and immovable);
- **Bring and defend** legal proceedings in the name of the
  company;
- **Carry on business** so far as necessary for the beneficial
  winding up;
- **Borrow** on the security of the company's assets where
  necessary;
- **Compromise** claims by and against the company;
- **Disclaim** onerous property (Article 171);
- **Investigate** the company's affairs and the conduct of its
  officers;
- **Distribute** the proceeds to creditors and members in
  accordance with the prescribed waterfall;
- **Engage** professional advisers.

Duties include:

- **Act in good faith** in the interests of creditors and (when
  satisfied) members;
- **Maintain accounts** and records of the liquidation;
- **Report** to creditors and (where required) to the JFSC;
- **Apply** to the Court where directions are needed;
- **Make returns** to the registrar as prescribed.

Some powers require **liquidation committee** or **Court
sanction** — typically major transactions, litigation
commitments, and remuneration changes.

(See the consolidated text at [jerseylaw.je][cjl].)

## Why this matters

Article 170 is the **operational engine** of the creditors'
winding up. Without these powers, the liquidator could not
function:

- **Asset realisation** requires sale powers;
- **Litigation** is often essential to recovery;
- **Distribution** requires actually paying creditors;
- **Investigation** uncovers claw-back opportunities and
  potential criminal conduct.

The **balance** between liquidator powers and committee /
court oversight is calibrated to give the liquidator
operational autonomy on routine matters while requiring
authorisation for material decisions.
