# Article 2A — Further Provisions on Subsidiaries and Holding Bodies

Article 2A of the **[Companies (Jersey) Law 1991][cjl]** supplements
the basic [Article 2](subsidiary-and-holding-body.md) definitions
with operational rules on:

- **attribution** of shareholdings (when shares held indirectly count
  to a parent);
- **fiduciary holdings** (trustees, custodians, nominees);
- **non-voting and weighted-voting** instruments;
- **multi-tier** group structures.

(For the verbatim text see [jerseylaw.je][cjl].)

## Attribution of holdings

Article 2A clarifies that, for the Article 2 voting-rights test:

- **shares held through nominees** for a parent are attributed to
  the parent;
- **shares held by subsidiaries** are attributed to the ultimate
  parent for the purposes of testing whether a further-removed
  company is itself a subsidiary;
- **shares held under a power of attorney or proxy** may, depending
  on the terms, be attributed to the holder of the power.

This avoids easy structuring-around of the subsidiary tests by
inserting intermediary holders.

## Fiduciary holdings — trustees, custodians

A trustee holding shares **for trust purposes** is not normally
treated as a "holding body" of the company whose shares it holds.
The Article carves out fiduciary holdings to preserve the
distinction between **beneficial control** and **fiduciary
holding-of-title**.

Similarly:

- **custodians** holding shares as bare custodians for clients
  are not "holding bodies";
- **nominees** holding for an undisclosed principal are not
  "holding bodies" — but the **principal** may be.

## Non-voting and weighted-voting instruments

The Article addresses unusual capital structures:

- **non-voting shares** generally do not contribute to the
  Article 2 voting-rights threshold;
- **weighted-voting shares** contribute the voting power they
  actually carry, not just one vote per share;
- **convertible securities** are typically not counted for
  Article 2 purposes until conversion (subject to deemed-conversion
  rules in specific provisions).

## Multi-tier groups

A multi-tier group — parent A, intermediate holding B, operating
company C — is structured through Article 2A so that:

- B is a subsidiary of A (directly held);
- C is a subsidiary of B (directly held);
- C is also a subsidiary of A (indirectly held through B), by
  attribution.

This is the standard **chain attribution** rule, and it makes the
**ultimate parent** the holding body of every entity in the chain.

## Pitfalls

- **Structuring intent.** Tax and regulatory characterisation
  sometimes turn on whether a particular entity is a subsidiary
  for Companies Law purposes. The Article 2 / 2A rules are not
  the only test — tax authorities and regulators may apply their
  own definitions, sometimes broader.
- **Fiduciary carve-out limits.** The trustee carve-out applies to
  the trustee. The **beneficiary** of the trust may still be a
  holding body if the beneficial interest amounts to control —
  the Royal Court has been alert to substance-over-form arguments
  in this area.
