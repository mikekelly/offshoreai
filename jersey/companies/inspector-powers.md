# Article 129 — Powers of Inspectors

Article 129 of the **[Companies (Jersey) Law 1991][cjl]** sets
out the **powers of inspectors** appointed under Article 128.

## What Article 129 covers

An inspector may:

- **Examine on oath** officers, employees, agents, advisers,
  and others connected with the company;
- **Require production** of books, records, accounts, contracts,
  correspondence, and other documents;
- Visit the company's **premises** and inspect its operations;
- Investigate the affairs of any **related body corporate**
  (subsidiary, holding company, or fellow subsidiary) where
  necessary;
- Require **explanations** of documents or transactions;
- Take **copies** of any records examined.

The inspector's powers are **enforceable by the Royal Court** —
non-cooperation can be punished as **contempt** in addition to
the offences under Articles 133 and 134.

(See the consolidated text at [jerseylaw.je][cjl].)

## Why this matters

These are **strong investigative powers** broadly equivalent to
those given to:

- The **Serious Fraud Office** in England under Section 2 of the
  Criminal Justice Act 1987;
- **Companies Act inspectors** under the UK Companies Act.

The powers exist because the alternatives — voluntary
cooperation, civil discovery — are too weak to uncover concerted
wrongdoing.

The **investigation of related bodies** is important: corporate
fraud often hides behind subsidiary or fellow-group entities,
and an inspector confined to one company would be easily
defeated.
