# Article 101 — Resolution Passed at Adjourned Meeting

Article 101 of the **[Companies (Jersey) Law 1991][cjl]** addresses
the **validity of resolutions** passed when a general meeting is
**adjourned** and reconvenes.

## What Article 101 covers

Resolutions adopted when meetings **reconvene** retain
**validity**.

An **adjourned meeting's** resolutions carry the **same legal
weight** as those from the **initial session**.

(See the consolidated text at [jerseylaw.je][cjl].)

## Why this matters

Meetings sometimes adjourn for **practical reasons**:

- Quorum not reached (lack of members present);
- Insufficient time to consider business;
- Need for additional information;
- Procedural problems requiring resolution before continuing.

When the meeting **reconvenes** at a later date:

- Resolutions passed at the **reconvened session** are **as
  valid** as if passed at the original meeting;
- The **continuity** of the meeting is preserved;
- The **notice** for the original meeting typically covers the
  reconvened session (subject to deed-specific rules).

## Effective date

For purposes such as filing under
[Article 100](filing-of-resolutions.md), the **effective date**
of a resolution passed at an adjourned meeting is typically the
**date the resolution was actually passed** (i.e., the
reconvened-session date), not the original meeting date.

## Adjournment vs new meeting

The Article distinguishes:

- An **adjourned meeting** — the same meeting continued at a
  later date;
- A **new meeting** — a separately-noticed and convened
  meeting.

Adjournments don't require fresh notice in the same way new
meetings do (subject to the deed).

## Pitfalls

- **Inadequate adjournment record** — the original meeting's
  adjournment should be documented in the minutes.
- **Adjourned meeting differing in substance** — if the
  reconvened session goes substantially beyond what the
  original meeting noticed, fresh notice may be required.
