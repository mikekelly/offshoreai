# Article 47 — Rectification of Share Register

Article 47 of the **[Companies (Jersey) Law 1991][cjl]** is the
**Royal Court's power** to order **rectification** of a Jersey
company's register of members when entries are incorrect or
defective.

## What Article 47 says

The **Royal Court may order register corrections** where entries
are **incorrect or defective**.

This provides a **judicial remedy** for registration errors.

(See the consolidated text at [jerseylaw.je][cjl].)

## When rectification is needed

Common scenarios:

- **Wrong name** entered for a member (typo, misspelling);
- **Wrong shareholding** number recorded;
- **Failure to register** a valid transfer (e.g., the board
  refused improperly);
- **Improper registration** of a transfer that should not have
  been accepted (e.g., transfer without complying with deed
  pre-emption);
- **Historical errors** discovered on subsequent audit or
  investigation;
- **Disputed entries** where two parties claim to be the proper
  registered holder.

## Procedure

An application is made by:

- A **member** (or person claiming to be a member);
- The **company itself**;
- A **person aggrieved** by an entry or omission.

The application is by **Representation** in the Royal Court
(Samedi Division for civil matters). The Court has wide
discretion to:

- Order **specific corrections** to the register;
- Direct the company to **register or de-register** a person;
- Make **consequential orders** (e.g., on past dividends paid to
  incorrectly-registered persons).

## Substantive grounds

The Royal Court's approach to register-rectification applications:

- The applicant must show **the entry is wrong**;
- The Court considers **what the correct entry should be** (on
  the evidence);
- The Court applies **conflict-of-laws** principles where the
  underlying transaction is foreign-governed;
- The Court has **discretion** to refuse rectification where
  third-party rights would be unjustly affected.

## Interaction with deed restrictions

A common type of dispute: a transferee claims the company
**should have** registered them, but the board refused on
deed-based grounds (pre-emption not complied with, board
discretion exercised). The Court considers:

- Was the deed-based ground **valid and properly invoked**?
- Was the board's exercise **in good faith and for proper
  purpose**?
- Has the transferee **fully complied** with deed requirements?

If the board's refusal was improper, the Court may order
registration.

## Pitfalls

- **Delay in seeking rectification** — long-standing errors
  become harder to unwind, especially if third-party rights
  intervene.
- **Mixing rectification with broader claims** — Article 47 is
  for register correction; substantive disputes about the
  underlying transaction may need separate civil proceedings.
