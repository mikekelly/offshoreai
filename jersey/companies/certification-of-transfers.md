# Article 43 — Certification of Transfers

Article 43 of the **[Companies (Jersey) Law 1991][cjl]** permits
the company to issue **certificates of transfer** — interim
evidence that a transfer has been received and is in process,
short of full registration.

## What Article 43 says

A company **may issue certificates** confirming **receipt of a
transfer**.

These certificates serve as **evidence that the transfer was
submitted for registration**.

They **do not guarantee completion** of the transfer.

(See the consolidated text at [jerseylaw.je][cjl].)

## Why this matters

A certified transfer is useful where:

- The **transferee needs evidence** of the transfer pending full
  registration (e.g., for onward sale, financing collateral, or
  proof of ownership);
- The **registration process takes time** — board approval,
  pre-emption, deed requirements — and the transferee shouldn't
  have to wait for all of these before showing the transfer is
  in train;
- A **part of a larger transaction** needs interim documentation
  before completion.

## What the certificate evidences

The Article 43 certificate evidences:

- That **the transferee has delivered the transfer** to the
  company;
- That the company **has received** it;
- That the company is **processing** it (subject to deed
  compliance).

It does **not** evidence:

- That registration is **completed** (which requires entry on the
  register);
- That registration **will be** completed (deed-compliance might
  fail);
- The **substantive validity** of the underlying transfer.

## Practical use

Article 43 certificates are most commonly issued in:

- **Listed companies** where same-day or near-same-day transfer
  proof is needed for market settlement;
- **Stock-lending arrangements** where interim evidence supports
  the lending transaction;
- **Pledged-share arrangements** where the lender needs evidence
  of the transfer for security purposes.

Most private Jersey companies don't routinely issue
certification-of-transfer certificates — they handle the transfer
on a more contained timeline without needing interim evidence.

## Pitfalls

- **Treating the certificate as evidence of full registration** —
  it's not. The transferee should still confirm registration via
  the register itself.
- **Issuing certificates for transfers that may fail
  registration** — if the company knows the transfer may be
  rejected (e.g., board approval contested), it should consider
  whether the certificate is appropriate.
