# FJL 2009 Article 5 — Effect of Incorporation

Article 5 specifies the **legal consequences** of establishment
under Article 4.

## The provision

On the issue of the certificate of establishment, the foundation
is:

- A **body corporate** with **separate legal personality**;
- Capable of **suing and being sued** in its own name;
- Capable of **owning property** in its own name;
- Capable of **contracting** in its own name;
- **Distinct** from its founder, council members, guardian, and
  beneficiaries.

The foundation has **perpetual succession** — its existence is
not affected by changes in council membership, retirement of
the qualified member, or death of the founder.

## Why this matters

The legal-personality feature is the foundation's **defining
contrast with a trust**:

| Feature | Trust | Foundation |
|---|---|---|
| Legal personality | None — trust is a relationship | Yes — body corporate |
| Owner of assets | Trustee in trust capacity | Foundation itself |
| Can sue / be sued | Trustee sues / is sued | Foundation sues / is sued in own name |
| Identity on register | Not registered | Registered (charter public) |
| Affected by trustee changes | Substantively (succession matters) | No — foundation is the constant |

## Implications for transactions

Because the foundation has its own legal personality:

- **Property transferred to the foundation** belongs to the
  foundation, not to any council member or the founder;
- **Foundation contracts** are obligations of the foundation,
  not of its officers (subject to Articles 17 / 21);
- **Litigation** is brought by or against the foundation in its
  own name (not by the council);
- **Foreign-law characterisation** of the foundation as a
  separate entity is well supported for most onshore tax /
  regulatory purposes.
