# FJL 2009 Article 37 — Firewall (Foreign Claims Excluded)

Article 37 is the **firewall provision** of the FJL 2009 —
the analogue for foundations of Article 9 of the Trusts
(Jersey) Law 1984.

## The provision

Where a Jersey foundation is governed by Jersey law, **no
matter** specified in Article 36 (validity, construction,
administration, etc.) shall be **determined by reference to**
the law of any other jurisdiction.

In particular, the following do **not affect** the foundation
or its assets:

- **Foreign forced-heirship** claims (legitime, réserve
  héréditaire);
- **Foreign matrimonial-regime** claims to community property;
- **Foreign judgments** purporting to override the charter /
  regulations;
- **Foreign personal-laws** claims based on residence,
  domicile, or nationality of any beneficiary or founder.

The Royal Court of Jersey **shall not recognise or enforce**
such claims to the extent they conflict with Article 36's
applicable-law allocation.

## Why this matters

Jersey foundations (like Jersey trusts) are used in part for
**asset-protection** purposes — to insulate family wealth from:

- **Forced-heirship regimes** in civil-law jurisdictions
  (e.g., French réserve, Spanish legitima, Sharia
  inheritance);
- **Community-property** claims (e.g., Spanish bienes
  gananciales, Louisiana community);
- **Foreign-divorce settlements** seeking to attach
  foundation assets directly.

Article 37 makes clear that **Jersey law alone** determines
these matters for a Jersey-law foundation.

## Limits

The firewall does **not** prevent:

- **Personal liability** of a founder / beneficiary to a
  foreign forced-heir creditor under the foreign judgment —
  the foreign creditor may have rights *against the
  individual*, just not *against the foundation*;
- **Asset-tracing** through criminal channels (mutual legal
  assistance, sanctions, AML);
- **Public-policy** override by the Royal Court in extreme
  cases (fraud, sham, criminality);
- **Tax claims** by foreign tax authorities through proper
  treaty channels.

## Sham foundations

A foundation that is a **sham** (the council members do not
intend to manage in accordance with the charter; the founder
retains substantial undocumented control) does **not** benefit
from firewall protection:

- The Royal Court can **set aside** the foundation as a sham;
- **Beneficial ownership** can be attributed to the founder /
  controller;
- **Foreign creditors** can reach the assets through Jersey
  proceedings.

The substance of arrangements matters — not just the
paperwork.

## Relationship to Article 9 of the Trusts Law

Article 37 of the FJL and Article 9 of the Trusts Law are
broadly analogous:

| Feature | TJL Article 9 | FJL Article 37 |
|---|---|---|
| Scope | Jersey trusts | Jersey foundations |
| Forced-heirship exclusion | Yes | Yes |
| Matrimonial exclusion | Yes | Yes |
| Foreign-judgment refusal | Yes | Yes |
| Public-policy carve-out | Implicit | Implicit |
| Sham doctrine | Yes (Court can set aside) | Yes (Court can set aside) |

The trust firewall has more **case law** developed around it
(*Crociani* etc.). The foundation firewall is less litigated
but the principles are expected to track.
