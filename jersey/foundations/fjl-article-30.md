# FJL 2009 Article 30 — Information Rights of Beneficiary

Article 30 sets the **default information regime** for
foundation beneficiaries — and it is **markedly narrower**
than the trust regime under *Schmidt v Rosewood*.

## The provision

A beneficiary's right to information from the foundation is:

- **Limited to** what the charter / regulations grant;
- **Subject to** the council's discretion to refuse where
  disclosure would be **contrary to the interests of the
  foundation** or its objects;
- **Subject to** confidentiality obligations the council owes
  to other persons;
- **Subject to** prior court order if applied for.

The Law does **not** create a general right of beneficiary
disclosure. The default is **no right**.

## Charter / regulations regime

Most well-drafted foundations make some provision:

- **Annual statement** of assets to beneficiaries (in
  pure-discretionary structures);
- **Account information** on request, subject to council
  discretion;
- **Notification** of distributions to other beneficiaries (or
  expressly not);
- **Right** to receive copies of charter (already public) and
  regulations (private).

## Contrast with trusts

| Feature | Trust beneficiary | Foundation beneficiary |
|---|---|---|
| Schmidt v Rosewood supervisory information | Yes (default) | No (not by default) |
| Article 29 disclosure (trustee accounting) | Yes (subject to court control) | No (subject to council / charter) |
| Right to call for accounts | Generally yes | Only if granted |
| Right to identify other beneficiaries | Court-controlled | None by default |
| Letter of wishes inspection | Strong presumption against | No general right |

## Court application

A beneficiary who **needs information** can apply to the Royal
Court under Article 46:

- The Court has a **supervisory jurisdiction** akin to its
  trust jurisdiction;
- The Court will **balance** the beneficiary's interest in
  information against the foundation's interest in
  confidentiality;
- Outcomes are **fact-specific** — there is no presumption in
  the beneficiary's favour as there is under *Schmidt*.

## Drafting practice

Practitioners should:

- **Address information rights expressly** in the regulations;
- **Specify** the standard (e.g., "the council shall provide
  annual accounts subject to confidentiality of third-party
  information");
- **Provide** a **dispute mechanism** for disputes about
  disclosure (e.g., mediation or council vote with guardian
  consent);
- **Consider** information rights of **classes** of
  beneficiaries (e.g., adult vs minor; current vs remoter).

## Practical consequences

For **family-office** structures:

- Foundations are **attractive** for privacy-conscious
  families — the no-default-information feature contrasts
  with *Schmidt*;
- But foundations are **less attractive** for families wanting
  beneficiary engagement — bespoke drafting is needed;
- The **guardian** is often the principal information conduit
  — receiving full information and exercising consent rights
  on the family's behalf.
