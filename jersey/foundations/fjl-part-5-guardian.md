# FJL 2009 Part 5 — Guardian (residual)

Articles 22-23 (the establishment and function of the
guardian) have their own deep files. This cluster covers the
remaining Part 5 provisions (Articles 24-25).

## Article 24 — Appointment, removal, retirement of guardian

A guardian is **appointed**:

- By the **charter or regulations** at establishment;
- By **resolution** of the council (where the regulations so
  permit);
- By a **person specified** in the charter or regulations
  (e.g., the founder, a surviving spouse);
- By **court order** in default of mechanism (Article 45).

A guardian is **removed**:

- By the **regulations'** mechanism;
- By **court order** (Article 45) for breach of duty or
  inability to act;
- By **resignation** of the guardian (with reasonable notice
  and a successor in place).

A guardian may **retire**:

- By **notice** to the council;
- Subject to **transition** — a successor must be appointed
  before retirement takes effect, or the council must apply to
  the Court if no successor is forthcoming.

## Article 25 — Information rights

The guardian is entitled, on reasonable request, to:

- **Inspect** records, minutes, and accounting books;
- **Receive** copies of resolutions, accounts, and other
  documents;
- **Receive** notice of council meetings (where the
  regulations so provide);
- **Be consulted** on matters per the regulations;
- **Be informed** of material developments.

The guardian's information right is **broader** than that of
beneficiaries (Article 30, which is typically constrained) —
reflecting the supervisory role.

## Consent vs information

A guardian's role can be set on a spectrum:

| Approach | Guardian role | Council burden |
|---|---|---|
| Pure information | Receives reports, no consent right | Council manages freely |
| Reserved consents | Council needs guardian consent for specified matters | Material decisions require coordination |
| Quasi-direction | Guardian effectively directs material decisions | Council acts on guardian direction (problematic — see below) |

The third approach is **not recommended** — the Law's design
contemplates guardian as **supervisor**, not director. A
guardian who effectively directs the council may be treated as
a **shadow council member** with the duties (and liabilities)
of a council member without the formal status.

## Multiple guardians

The Law does not preclude **multiple guardians**, though one
guardian is standard. Multi-guardian arrangements work via:

- **Joint** decisions on consents;
- **Majority** decisions where the regulations so provide;
- **Specific allocations** of consent rights between guardians.
