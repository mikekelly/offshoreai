# Freedom of Information (Jersey) Law 2011 — Overview

## What the law does

The **Freedom of Information (Jersey) Law 2011** (FOI
2011) gives **any person** a statutory right to request
information held by Jersey **public authorities**. It:

- Establishes a **default right of access** to recorded
  information held by **Scheduled Public Authorities**
  (SPAs);
- Lists **exemptions** — absolute and qualified;
- Provides for **public-interest tests** for qualified
  exemptions;
- Establishes **internal review** as the first appeal
  layer;
- Gives the **Information Commissioner** (JOIC) the
  second appeal role;
- Provides for **Royal Court** appeal as the final
  layer.

The Law sits alongside the **Data Protection (Jersey)
Law 2018** — FOI provides access to public information,
DPL governs handling of personal data.

## Scheduled Public Authorities

Only SPAs are subject to FOI. The schedule includes:

- **Government departments** (Health and Community
  Services, Customer and Local Services, etc.);
- **States Assembly** services and committees;
- **Police** authorities;
- **Parishes** in some respects;
- **Specific** statutory bodies and regulators;
- **Specific** non-ministerial offices.

Bodies **not** on the schedule are not subject to FOI —
even if they perform public-facing functions. This is a
notable difference from the broader UK Freedom of
Information Act 2000.

## The right of access

Any person can:

- **Request information** from an SPA;
- **In any format** (typically written or email);
- **Without** stating a reason (the requester's identity
  / purpose is generally irrelevant);
- **At low cost** — typically free up to a threshold,
  with fees for substantial requests.

The SPA must:

- **Acknowledge** within a prescribed time;
- **Respond substantively** within **20 working days**
  (with limited extensions);
- **Disclose** the information unless an exemption
  applies;
- **State reasons** if refusing.

## Exemptions

The FOI 2011 provides two types of exemption:

### Absolute exemptions

Information is **absolutely exempt** — no public-interest
test, no disclosure:

- **Personal data** of the requester (which is dealt with
  via DPL subject-access rights);
- **Personal data** of others (where disclosure would
  contravene DPL principles);
- Information **prohibited from disclosure** by another
  enactment;
- **Court documents** in specified circumstances;
- **Legal professional privilege** in narrow contexts.

### Qualified exemptions

Information **may** be exempt where disclosure would, or
would be likely to, prejudice specified interests —
subject to a **public-interest test**:

- **Prevention, detection, or investigation of crime**;
- **Apprehension or prosecution** of offenders;
- **Administration of justice**;
- **Assessment or collection** of tax or duty;
- **Operation of immigration controls**;
- **Maintenance** of security in prisons;
- **Proper supervision** of financial services;
- **JFSC functions**;
- **Commercial interests** (third-party);
- **Free and frank advice** within government;
- **International relations**;
- **Defence and national security** (mostly UK-coordinated);
- **Health and safety**;
- **Specific** policy-related exemptions.

For a qualified exemption to apply, the public authority
must show:

- The exemption **engages** (the kind of prejudice
  applies);
- The **public interest in withholding outweighs** the
  public interest in disclosing.

The public-interest test is the **balancing exercise** at
the heart of contested FOI requests.

See [`foi-exemptions.md`](foi-exemptions.md).

## The Information Commissioner

The Jersey Office of the Information Commissioner (JOIC):

- **Supervises** FOI compliance;
- **Issues guidance** to public authorities;
- **Receives** complaints from requesters;
- **Hears appeals** from internal-review outcomes;
- **Issues decision notices** that are binding on the
  public authority;
- **Annual report** to the States Assembly.

The JOIC's primary role on FOI is to fulfil the **second
stage** of the appeals process — see
[`foi-procedure-and-appeals.md`](foi-procedure-and-appeals.md).

## Appeals route

Three layers:

1. **Internal review** by the public authority itself;
2. **JOIC decision** — appeal to the Information
   Commissioner;
3. **Royal Court** — final appeal on points of law.

The Royal Court's decision is **final**.

## Cost and practical operation

- **Most FOI requests** are answered free;
- **Substantial requests** may attract a fee (calculated
  by hours of work above a threshold);
- **Vexatious or repeated** requests can be refused;
- **Requests can be refused** if compliance would exceed
  the cost threshold.

## Interaction with DPL

- **Personal data of the requester** — dealt with via
  DPL subject access (not FOI);
- **Personal data of others** — typically absolutely
  exempt from FOI, but disclosure may be lawful under
  DPL if a basis exists;
- **Anonymised data** about others may be disclosable;
- **Both Laws** are administered by the JOIC.

## Recent and ongoing reforms

- A 2025 **interim review** of the FOI 2011 was
  consulted on by Jersey Finance and others;
- Proposals include expanding the list of SPAs and
  strengthening the appeals route;
- **No major statutory revision** has yet been enacted as
  of May 2026.

## Practical recommendations

For a requester:

- **Identify the right SPA** — only scheduled bodies are
  subject to FOI;
- **Specific request** — narrow, specific requests are
  faster and harder to refuse on cost grounds;
- **Clear language** — describe what you want, not the
  legal basis;
- **Track timelines** — 20 working days is the target;
- **Escalate** through internal review → JOIC → Royal
  Court if needed;
- **Engage** Citizens Advice / specialist support for
  contested cases.

For a public authority:

- **Acknowledge promptly**;
- **Apply exemptions** carefully — wrong claims can be
  overturned by JOIC;
- **Public-interest test** for qualified exemptions —
  document the balancing exercise;
- **Disclose** in part where possible (with redactions);
- **Engage** the JOIC for guidance on novel issues.
