# Norman Customary Law — Jersey

Jersey's legal system has its roots in the **customary law of
Normandy**. The Island formed part of the Duchy of Normandy
until 1204, after which Norman customary law continued to apply
in Jersey while diverging from continental Normandy's civil-law
development.

## Historical context

- **Until 1204**, Jersey was part of Normandy under the Duke /
  King of England (the same person from 1066);
- **From 1204**, the Channel Islands remained loyal to King John
  but continental Normandy passed to the King of France;
- Jersey's customary law **continued** under English Crown
  protection, becoming a distinct tradition;
- The **Coutume de Normandie** (Norman customary law,
  compilation completed in the 16th century) is the principal
  historical-customary source;
- Subsequent Jersey **statute and case law** have heavily
  developed the tradition.

## Where customary law still applies

Customary law is most active in:

### Immovable property

- **Passation** — the public ceremony before the Royal Court at
  which heritable contracts (real-property transfers) are
  executed;
- **Hypothecs** — Jersey-law security over immovable property;
- **Tenure-of-land** concepts — *fief*, *seigniory*, etc.,
  though much modernised;
- **Customary rules** about boundary, easement, water rights.

### Succession

- **Légitime** — fixed shares for spouse and children in some
  categories of estate (now substantially reformed by the **Wills
  and Successions (Jersey) Law 1993**);
- **Movable / immovable** estate distinction with different
  succession rules historically;
- **Customary widow's rights** modified by statute.

### Procedure

- **Process** of summoning, service, and certain ancient
  procedural mechanics in the Royal Court;
- **Vicage** and other ancient officers' roles in court
  administration.

## Where customary law has receded

For modern commercial questions:

- **Companies law** is wholly statutory (Companies Law 1991);
- **Trusts law** is wholly statutory (Trusts Law 1984), though
  case law adds substantial gloss;
- **Tax law** is wholly statutory;
- **Financial-services regulation** is wholly statutory.

Customary law is **rarely controlling** in these areas. But the
**institutional context** — Royal Court, Bailiff, Jurats —
preserves the customary-law tradition even where the substantive
law is modern statute.
