# Judicial Review in Jersey

## What judicial review is

**Judicial review** is the Royal Court's supervisory
jurisdiction over **administrative decisions** of public
authorities — challenging the legality, rationality, and
procedural fairness of public-law decisions.

## Grounds

Standard administrative-law grounds apply:

### Illegality

- **Acting beyond statutory power** (ultra vires);
- **Misinterpreting** the relevant legislation;
- **Failing** to apply mandatory considerations;
- **Considering** irrelevant matters.

### Irrationality

- A decision **no reasonable** authority could have made
  (Wednesbury unreasonableness);
- Decisions **disproportionate** to their objective (where
  proportionality applies, e.g., ECHR contexts);
- Decisions **inconsistent** with the authority's own
  policy.

### Procedural impropriety

- **Bias** or apparent bias;
- **Breach** of natural justice (audi alteram partem);
- **Failure** to give reasons where required;
- **Failure** to consult where required;
- **Procedural** unfairness more generally.

### Legitimate expectation

- Substantive or procedural expectations created by past
  practice or statements;
- Frustration without proper consideration.

## Who can be reviewed

Bodies subject to judicial review include:

- The **Council of Ministers** and individual Ministers;
- **Government departments** and officials;
- The **JFSC** for regulatory decisions;
- The **Comptroller of Revenue** for tax assessments
  (subject to specific appeal routes);
- The **States Assembly** (in some respects);
- **Other public bodies** exercising public functions.

Bodies **not** subject to judicial review:

- Purely **private** bodies acting in private capacity;
- The **Royal Court** itself (subject to appeal, not review);
- The **Sovereign** (some Crown acts).

## Standing

Applicants must have **sufficient interest** in the decision:

- **Directly affected** persons clearly have standing;
- **Public interest** standing is recognised in appropriate
  cases;
- **Pressure groups** may have standing for environmental,
  human rights matters;
- **Standing** is not the principal barrier in most cases.

## Procedure

Judicial review proceedings:

- Commenced by **representation** to the Royal Court;
- **Leave** to apply for review is required;
- **Promptness** is required (typically within 3 months of the
  decision);
- **Affidavit** evidence with disclosure;
- **Hearing** before the Royal Court.

## Remedies

The Royal Court may grant:

- **Certiorari / quashing** — setting aside the decision;
- **Mandamus / mandatory order** — requiring action;
- **Prohibition** — preventing action;
- **Declaration** — declaring the law;
- **Damages** in limited cases.

## Specific contexts

### Regulatory decisions

- JFSC **enforcement** action subject to review (alongside
  statutory appeals);
- JFSC **registration** decisions subject to statutory
  appeal mainly, but residual review available;
- Comptroller of Revenue **tax assessment** subject to
  statutory appeal mainly.

### Immigration / residency

- Decisions on **housing qualifications** and **work
  permissions** are subject to review.

### Planning

- **Planning permission** decisions reviewable on standard
  grounds.

### Public-sector employment

- **Dismissal** and disciplinary decisions of public-sector
  employees reviewable, alongside employment-tribunal
  remedies.

## Relationship with appeals

Many decisions have **statutory appeal** routes (JFSC,
Comptroller of Revenue, planning). Where statutory appeal is
available:

- Appeal is **preferred** route;
- Judicial review **available** for matters outside the
  appeal scope (legality not normally captured by appeal);
- Some matters can be **both** appealed and reviewed.
