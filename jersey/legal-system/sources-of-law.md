# Sources of Law — Jersey

Jersey law draws from **four main sources**, with a hierarchy and
interplay that practitioners and courts apply consistently.

## The hierarchy

1. **Statute** — Laws enacted by the States Assembly,
   sanctioned by Order in Council, and registered by the Royal
   Court. **Highest authority**.
2. **Binding case law** — decisions of the Privy Council in
   Jersey appeals and the Jersey Court of Appeal that bind the
   Royal Court.
3. **Persuasive case law** — Royal Court decisions (which bind
   no one but inform later decisions), and the highly persuasive
   decisions of the English Supreme Court and Court of Appeal.
4. **Customary law** — Jersey's Norman-customary-law tradition,
   surviving most actively in property, succession, and certain
   procedural matters.

## Statute

The **States Assembly** enacts Laws (Lois) — formally **Projets
de Loi** when proposed, becoming Laws on sanction by Order in
Council and registration in the Royal Court.

The principal statute corpus is at **jerseylaw.je**. Major Laws
include:

- Companies (Jersey) Law 1991;
- Trusts (Jersey) Law 1984;
- Foundations (Jersey) Law 2009;
- Income Tax (Jersey) Law 1961;
- Financial Services (Jersey) Law 1998;
- Bankruptcy (Désastre) (Jersey) Law 1990.

Below Laws sit **Regulations** (made by the States) and **Orders**
(made by Ministers) — subordinate legislation.

## Case law

Jersey is a **case-law-influenced** jurisdiction, not a pure
civil-law codification jurisdiction. Royal Court decisions:

- **Are reasoned written judgments** published on jerseylaw.je;
- **Are persuasive** on later Royal Court decisions but do not
  formally bind it;
- **Are binding** if from the Court of Appeal or Privy Council
  in Jersey appeals.

The Trusts Law (Article 1) **expressly recognises** that the
Law is not a codification, leaving room for case-law development.

## English authority

English decisions are **highly persuasive** for Jersey courts:

- The Royal Court regularly cites English Supreme Court and
  Court of Appeal decisions;
- Where Jersey statute mirrors English statute, English
  jurisprudence guides Jersey interpretation;
- **Important divergences** must be consciously identified —
  e.g. the Article 47B–H mistake regime which is **wider** than
  English *Pitt v Holt*.

## Customary law

Jersey's **Norman customary law** survives most actively in:

- **Immovable property** — *passation*, *hypothecs*, customary
  rules about succession;
- **Succession** — *légitime*, though modified by the Wills and
  Successions (Jersey) Law 1993;
- **Procedure** — some procedural concepts and remedies have
  customary-law roots.

For most modern commercial questions, **statute dominates** and
customary law fills gaps.
