# Royal Court — Jersey

The **Royal Court** is Jersey's superior court of first instance and
appellate court for the Magistrate's Court. It exercises both civil and
criminal jurisdiction, sits in different formations ("Numbers")
depending on the work in hand, and is presided over by the
[Bailiff](bailiff.md) (or in the Bailiff's absence, the Deputy
Bailiff). Final appeals from the Royal Court lie first to the
[Jersey Court of Appeal](court-of-appeal.md) and ultimately to the
[Judicial Committee of the Privy Council](privy-council.md).

## Composition

The Royal Court is composed of:

- The **Bailiff** (presiding judge), and the **Deputy Bailiff** (who
  may sit in the Bailiff's place); plus
- the **Jurats** — a body of lay judges elected for life (until the
  age of 72) by an Electoral College, who serve as judges of fact and
  participate in sentencing.

([gov.je — Members of the Royal Court][members])

> The Royal Court is composed of the Bailiff, Deputy Bailiff and
> Jurats... The Bailiff is the judge of law and of procedure whereas
> the Jurats are the judges of fact and pass sentence.
>
> — gov.je

This division — **judge of law** (the Bailiff or Deputy Bailiff)
versus **judges of fact** (the Jurats) — is a distinctive feature of
the Jersey system, with roots in Norman customary practice. It is
preserved in modern criminal procedure: the Jurats determine guilt and
join in sentencing, while the Bailiff rules on points of law,
admissibility, and procedure.

For more on the Jurats — election, qualification, age limits, current
membership — see [jurats.md](jurats.md).

## The three principal "Numbers"

The Royal Court sits in different formations. The three principal ones
are:

### 1. Samedi Division (single-judge civil court)

The **Samedi Division** is the Royal Court's civil and administrative
day-to-day forum — named after the historical Saturday sittings. It is
typically constituted by the Bailiff or Deputy Bailiff sitting alone
(or, increasingly, by a **Master of the Royal Court** for case
management and lower-value civil work). It hears:

- Civil claims of value above the Magistrate's Court limit;
- Trust applications, including Article 51 directions applications
  under the Trusts (Jersey) Law 1984 and Article 47 set-aside
  applications;
- Probate and grants of representation;
- Company applications under the Companies (Jersey) Law 1991 (e.g.,
  schemes of arrangement, just-and-equitable winding-up petitions);
- Administrative-law / judicial-review matters.

### 2. Inferior Number

The **Inferior Number** is constituted by the Bailiff or Deputy
Bailiff **sitting with at least two Jurats**. It hears criminal trials
and sentences where the available sentence is no greater than four
years' imprisonment, and certain civil business where a finder of
fact is required. ([gov.je][members])

### 3. Superior Number

The **Superior Number** is constituted by the Bailiff or Deputy
Bailiff sitting with **at least five Jurats**. ([gov.je][members])
It hears:

- Criminal sentencing where the available sentence is **more than
  four years' imprisonment**;
- Certain constitutional and high-importance matters reserved for the
  fuller bench;
- Appeals from the Inferior Number on questions of fact in criminal
  matters.

## Civil and probate jurisdiction

In its civil capacity the Royal Court is the principal forum for
trust, company, succession and commercial litigation in Jersey. It
also exercises the original probate jurisdiction (grants of
representation) — historically through the *Ecclesiastical Court of
the Dean of Jersey* for personal-estate probate, but in modern
practice through the Royal Court Probate Division.

For the kinds of application that the offshore-finance industry
brings to the Royal Court most often:

- **Trust** — Article 51 directions, Article 47 set-aside, blessing of
  momentous decisions, removal and appointment of trustees. See the
  [trusts section](../trusts/index.md).
- **Company** — schemes of arrangement (Part 18A), mergers
  confirmations (Part 18B), continuance applications (Part 18C),
  unfair-prejudice petitions (Part 20). See
  [companies/companies-law-1991.md](../companies/companies-law-1991.md).
- **Insolvency** — *désastre* applications, creditors' winding-up
  petitions, recognition of foreign insolvency proceedings.

## Criminal jurisdiction

The Royal Court hears the more serious criminal cases that are beyond
the **Magistrate's Court** sentencing power. The choice of *Inferior*
or *Superior* Number sitting depends on the sentence the Court is
considering, with the four-year cut-off as the formal threshold.
([gov.je][members])

Prosecutions are typically brought by the Crown Officers — the
Attorney General and Solicitor General — or by their Crown Advocates.

## Appeals

- From the **Magistrate's Court** to the Royal Court (Inferior Number
  for criminal appeals, Samedi for civil).
- From the **Royal Court** to the **[Jersey Court of Appeal](court-of-appeal.md)**.
- From the Court of Appeal to the **[Judicial Committee of the Privy
  Council](privy-council.md)**, with leave.

## Court rules

The Royal Court's procedure is governed by the **Royal Court Rules**,
made under primary Law and published by the Judicial Greffe. The
Rules have been the subject of recent review (see gov.je's
"Royal Court Rules review" page) and should be checked for the
current version when running a procedural point.

## Where to find the current bench

The current Bailiff and Deputy Bailiff, the Master of the Royal Court,
and the Jurats are listed by name on the gov.je
[Members of the Royal Court page][members]. The list is updated when
the Crown makes a new judicial appointment.
