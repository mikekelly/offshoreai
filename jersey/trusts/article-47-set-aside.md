# Article 47 — Set-Aside for Mistake (Trusts (Jersey) Law 1984)

The mistake jurisdiction in Jersey trust law is **statutory**, set out
in **Articles 47B to 47H of the [Trusts (Jersey) Law 1984][tjl]**.
The regime was introduced by the **Trusts (Amendment No. 6) (Jersey)
Law 2013** to put on a clear statutory footing the Royal Court's
previous case-law-developed approach to mistake (and to replace the
*Hastings-Bass* doctrine, which Jersey had already taken in a wider
direction than the English courts). This page describes the regime,
the wide statutory definition of "mistake", the three-limb test the
Royal Court applies, and how Jersey's approach **diverges from**
English law after *Pitt v Holt* [2013] UKSC 26.

For the user-facing question ("we made a mistake on a previous
distribution — can we undo it?"), see
[`use-cases/trust-officer/article-47-set-aside-for-mistake.md`](../use-cases/trust-officer/article-47-set-aside-for-mistake.md).

## Naming conventions and a warning

The label **"Article 47"** is ambiguous in Jersey trust law because
the Article cluster covers more than one jurisdiction:

| Provision | What it does |
|---|---|
| **Article 47** | Variation of trust terms by Court approval (on behalf of minors, unascertained persons, unborn beneficiaries). |
| **Article 47A** | Court redirection of property to alternative purposes for charitable / non-charitable purpose trusts that have failed or become unsuitable. |
| **Article 47B** | **Interpretation** for the Articles 47C–47H regime — including the wide statutory definition of "mistake". |
| **Articles 47C–47H** | The **modern mistake jurisdiction** — set-aside of transfers, dispositions, and exercises of fiduciary powers made under qualifying mistake. |

When users (and even practitioners) say "Article 47" they almost
always mean the **Articles 47B–47H regime**. The historical Variation
of Trusts Jersey jurisdiction at Article 47 simpliciter is rarely
in issue in modern practice. Cite the **sub-Article** in formal work.

## The mistake regime — Articles 47B to 47H

### Article 47B — what is a "mistake"

Article 47B defines "mistake" widely for the purposes of the regime.
The defined ground covers a mistake as to:

- the **effect** of a transaction;
- its **consequences** (including, expressly, its tax consequences);
- any **advantage** the person believed they would gain from the
  transaction; and
- any **fact existing before or at the time of** the disposition or
  power-exercise.

([Mondaq — Mistake in Jersey Trust Law][mondaq]; [Lexology / Ogier —
Setting aside subsequent transfers][lexology])

Article 47B also excludes **testamentary dispositions** from the
scope of "transfer or other disposition of property to a trust" —
the mistake jurisdiction over wills sits separately.

### Article 47E — set-aside of a transfer of property *to* a trust

A transferor (or someone acting on their behalf, with appropriate
standing) may apply for the Court to declare a transfer or disposition
of property to a trust **voidable** on the ground that it was made
under mistake. If the Court so declares, the property returns to the
transferor (subject to any conditions the Court imposes).

This is the most-used of the modern mistake Articles in practice,
because it captures the common pattern of a settlor settling assets
into a trust and then discovering an unforeseen tax or other
consequence.

### Article 47G — set-aside of an exercise of a fiduciary power

A trustee, protector, beneficiary or other appropriate person can
apply for the Court to declare the **exercise of a fiduciary power**
in relation to a trust voidable on the ground that the power was
exercised under mistake. Setting aside reverses the power-exercise
and restores the position immediately prior.

This Article captures, for example, a trustee's appointment of capital
out of the trust that the trustee would not have made had it
appreciated a tax consequence; a protector's consent given under a
misapprehension about facts; a settlor's exercise of a reserved power
to vary the class of beneficiaries that produced unintended results.

### Articles 47C, 47D, 47F, 47H

The intervening Articles handle the related operational mechanics —
who has standing, what the consequences of a declaration are,
limitations on relief, and the relationship with third parties whose
position is affected.

## The three-limb test the Royal Court applies

The Royal Court applies a **three-limb test** before granting relief
under Articles 47E or 47G (per *S & T Trusts* [2015] JRC259 and the
authorities that have followed). The applicant must show:

1. **There was a mistake** within the wide Article 47B definition.
2. **But for the mistake, the transfer or power-exercise would not
   have been made** in the form it was.
3. **The mistake was of so serious a character that it would be just**
   for the Court to make a declaration.

([Viberts — S & T Trusts summary][viberts]; [Carey Olsen — No
re-writing history][co])

A failure on any one limb defeats the application. The most common
limb to fail in practice is limb 2 — the applicant struggles to show
that they would not have entered the transaction at all, as opposed
to entering a *different* transaction.

## Divergence from English law (*Pitt v Holt*)

The English Supreme Court in **[*Pitt v Holt; Futter v Futter* [2013]
UKSC 26][pitt]** drew a distinction between:

- a **"mistake"** (an erroneous belief about a relevant fact or law),
  which can ground rescission; and
- **"mere causative ignorance"** or **"inadequate deliberation"**,
  which generally cannot.

The Royal Court of Jersey has, *under the statutory regime*,
**rejected** that distinction:

> Under Jersey law, when considering applications under Article 47E,
> the jurisdiction to give statutory relief requires the Court to
> decide whether there was any mistake; in Jersey, the manner of the
> mistake is irrelevant and it is therefore inappropriate to make the
> distinctions applied under English law.

(Royal Court reasoning summarised at [Viberts — S & T Trusts][viberts]
and [Carey Olsen][co].)

The Jersey regime is therefore **wider** than the English. A Jersey-
law trust set up to mitigate the risk of future tax-or-other-rule
changes that the adviser cannot foresee benefits from this width,
because a future "we didn't think about that" position is more likely
to be remediable in the Royal Court than in the English High Court.

## Cases — successful and unsuccessful

### Successful

- **In re S & T Trusts [2015] JRC259** — set-aside granted on
  unforeseen tax consequences; the case in which the three-limb test
  was formulated. ([Viberts][viberts])
- A line of subsequent Royal Court decisions has applied the test in
  varied fact patterns — see the commentaries collected at [Carey
  Olsen][co], [Bedell Cristin][bc] and [Ogier][lexology].

### Unsuccessful

- **In the Matter of the M Settlement** — Royal Court declined to
  permit a partial rescission of a transaction, on the basis that
  the proposed relief was substantively a re-writing of history
  rather than an unwinding. ([Collas Crill — M Settlement][m])

- Walkers' summary of recent declined applications notes the Court's
  reluctance to treat a **disappointed expectation of a *future* tax
  advantage** (as distinct from an unforeseen tax *charge*) as a
  qualifying mistake. ([Walkers — Recent Mistake Cases][walkers])

## Effect of a declaration

Where the Court declares a transaction voidable and grants relief:

- The transaction is **void *ab initio***, in the usual case (the
  Court has flexibility to fashion relief). Property returns to the
  transferor; power-exercise is unwound.
- **Third-party rights** acquired in good faith and for value before
  the declaration are typically protected.
- **Foreign tax consequences** that have crystallised will only be
  unwound to the extent the foreign revenue authority recognises the
  Royal Court's rescission. **UK HMRC** has historically accepted
  Jersey set-aside as restoring the *status quo ante* for inheritance
  tax and capital gains tax purposes; **US IRS** practice is more
  cautious and fact-dependent; specific cross-border tax advice is
  always required.

## Relationship with Article 11 (mistake invalidating the trust as a
whole)

A mistake going to the **very intention to settle** — i.e., the
settlor never validly intended to create a trust at all — is
addressed by **Article 11** of the Trusts Law (trust invalid for
mistake), not by Articles 47C–H. The two doors are distinct and
mutually exclusive. ([Mondaq][mondaq])

## Relationship with the Article 51 directions jurisdiction

If the proposed remedy is not setting aside a transaction but
**directing the trustee** to take a remedial step (e.g., requesting a
distribution back), the right vehicle is sometimes an **Article 51
application for directions**, not an Article 47 set-aside. See
[`article-51-directions.md`](article-51-directions.md). In a
borderline case the trustee will often seek both reliefs in a single
Representation.

## Practical practice points

- The **applicant** is typically the **settlor** (in 47E cases) or the
  **trustee** (in 47G cases). Beneficiaries can also apply where
  they have standing.
- The application is by **Representation** in the Royal Court, Samedi
  Division, supported by affidavit evidence of the state of mind at
  the relevant time. (See
  [`../legal-system/royal-court.md`](../legal-system/royal-court.md).)
- **Contemporaneous documentary evidence** of the mistake is critical
  — file notes, advice given, e-mail correspondence. A reconstruction
  affidavit alone, years after the event, is weak.
- **Cross-border** declarations almost always need parallel
  cross-border tax analysis before they are useful; the Jersey
  declaration is necessary but not sufficient.
