# Article 15 — Duration of a Jersey Trust

Article 15 of the **[Trusts (Jersey) Law 1984][tjl]** provides that a
Jersey trust may **continue in existence for an unlimited period**.
There is **no rule against perpetuities** for Jersey trusts (subject
to certain pre-2006 considerations).

## What Article 15 says

A Jersey trust may "continue in existence for an unlimited period",
with no perpetuities rules applying to trusts created after the
relevant date.

(See the consolidated text at [jerseylaw.je][tjl].)

## Why this matters

Most common-law jurisdictions impose some form of **rule against
perpetuities** that limits the period during which a trust can
continue. The classic English rule (a "life in being plus 21 years")
is restrictive; modern statutory replacements (e.g., the 80-year
period under the UK's Perpetuities and Accumulations Act 1964, or
125 years under the 2009 Act) are more generous but still finite.

Jersey, by contrast, permits **perpetual trusts** for trusts created
after the 2006 amendments. The practical consequences:

- **Multi-generational dynasty trusts** are workable as a matter of
  Jersey law.
- **Long-horizon orphan structures** (e.g., purpose trusts holding
  PTCs or SPVs over many decades) do not face a Jersey-law lapse
  date.
- **Family-business succession structures** can be designed to
  outlive any specifiable generation.

## Trusts created before the 2006 amendments

The pre-2006 position was different: Jersey trusts created before the
relevant date were subject to a statutory perpetuity period
(historically 100 years). For these legacy trusts:

- the perpetuity period still bites, and the trustee should know
  the date of expiry;
- the trust deed may have included a power to extend or to
  re-domicile; review carefully;
- where the deed is unhelpful, an Article 47 variation application
  can sometimes be used to extend duration consistent with the
  settlor's evident intention.

## Termination short of duration

A perpetual-duration capacity is not a perpetual-duration *requirement*.
A Jersey trust may terminate:

- by **exhaustion** of trust property;
- by the **expiry** of a fixed duration period stated in the deed;
- by the **exercise of a power to terminate** (often included in the
  deed);
- by **Royal Court order** on application (Article 51 directions or
  Article 47 variation);
- by **distribution-out** of all assets to the beneficiaries on
  unanimous consent of all the beneficiaries (the *Saunders v
  Vautier* doctrine, which the Royal Court applies subject to all
  beneficiaries being of full age and consenting).

For the detail of termination see [`termination-of-trust.md`](termination-of-trust.md)
*(planned)*.

## Tax planning consequences

Long-duration Jersey trusts attract scrutiny from foreign tax
authorities:

- **UK inheritance tax.** The 10-year periodic charge and exit
  charges under the UK relevant-property regime apply to
  long-duration UK-tax-relevant trusts regardless of Jersey-law
  duration.
- **US GST tax.** The US generation-skipping transfer tax regime
  imposes its own multi-generational analysis; Jersey-law perpetual
  duration is one input but not determinative.
- **OECD/G20 Pillar Two.** For trusts holding shares in in-scope
  multinational groups, the Pillar Two analysis may engage at the
  underlying-entity level; the Jersey-law duration is not directly
  in scope but the structural perpetuity may matter to
  characterisation.

## Pitfalls

- **Conflating duration with "no termination ever".** A
  perpetual-duration trust can be terminated for good cause. The
  capacity for perpetual duration is permissive, not mandatory.
- **Saunders v Vautier consent.** All beneficiaries (not just
  fixed-interest beneficiaries) must consent to a *Saunders v
  Vautier* termination; for trusts with unborn or contingent
  beneficiaries the doctrine is unavailable without their
  representation (typically a guardian ad litem and Royal Court
  blessing).
