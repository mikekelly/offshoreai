# Article 57 — Limitation of Actions or Prescription

Article 57 of the **[Trusts (Jersey) Law 1984][tjl]** is the
**limitation** regime for trust-law claims. It sets out time limits
for breach-of-trust actions, an absolute longstop, and a fraud
exception that runs indefinitely.

## What Article 57 says

### Fraud exception

> No period of limitation or prescription shall apply to an action
> brought against a trustee in respect of any **fraud** to which the
> trustee was a party or to which the trustee was privy.

### General breach-of-trust limitation — 3 years

Actions for breach of trust may be brought within **3 years** from
the **earlier** of:

- the date **final accounts were delivered** to the beneficiary; or
- the date the beneficiary **first learned** of the breach.

### Enforcer claims — 3 years

An **enforcer** of a non-charitable purpose trust has the same
**3-year** window — from delivery of final accounts or knowledge of
the breach.

### Trustee suing former trustee — 3 years

A trustee may sue a **former trustee** within **3 years** of the
date that former trustee **ceased office**.

### Absolute longstop — 21 years

Regardless of any other limitation period, **no action may proceed
beyond 21 years** after the breach occurred.

### Hague Convention exception

These limitation rules do **not apply** to foreign trusts governed
by jurisdictions where the **1984 Hague Convention on Trusts**
applies.

(See the consolidated text at [jerseylaw.je][tjl].)

## The 3-year/21-year structure

The Jersey limitation regime is **distinctive** and tighter than
many comparator jurisdictions:

| Feature | Jersey | England (Limitation Act 1980 + LPA 2007) | Many US states |
|---|---|---|---|
| Standard breach-of-trust limitation | 3 years | 6 years (s.21(3) LA 1980) | 3–6 years |
| Starts from | Earlier of final accounts / discovery | Date of cause of action | Varies |
| Absolute longstop | 21 years | None (subject to laches) | 10–30 years |
| Fraud / dishonest assistance | No limitation | No limitation (s.21(1)(a)) | Varies |

The **3-year window** is short — beneficiaries who suspect a
breach must act quickly. The **discovery rule** softens this:
where the beneficiary did not know and could not have known, time
starts only on discovery.

## "Final accounts" trigger

Delivery of **final accounts** to the beneficiary is one of the two
triggers for the 3-year clock. This makes the routine practice of
**delivering accounts** a significant trigger event in trust
practice:

- A trustee that delivers thorough final accounts (typically on
  termination of the trust or on retirement) **starts the clock**
  against beneficiary claims for matters disclosed in the accounts.
- A trustee that delivers thin or evasive accounts does not
  necessarily start the clock — the discovery trigger may keep the
  matter live.
- A trustee that **does not** deliver accounts at all leaves the
  clock running on the discovery trigger only.

Best practice: **deliver substantive final accounts** at the end of
the trustee's tenure, with sufficient detail that beneficiaries
have the information to identify any breach.

## "First learned" trigger

The discovery trigger fires when the beneficiary **first learned**
of the breach. The Royal Court's approach:

- **subjective knowledge** by the beneficiary starts the clock;
- **mere suspicion** is generally not enough — the beneficiary must
  have actual awareness of the facts that constitute the breach;
- **constructive knowledge** (what the beneficiary could have known
  by reasonable inquiry) may also start the clock in some cases —
  the case law is more open than the strict subjective standard.

For minor and unascertained beneficiaries the clock typically
**does not start** until they reach the age of majority or become
ascertained.

## Fraud exception — no limitation

Where the trustee was a **party to or privy to fraud**, no
limitation runs. This is consistent with the irreducible-core
analysis in [Article 30](breach-of-trust-liability.md): fraud is the
most egregious breach and is protected against the time-bar
mechanism.

"Privy to" is broader than "party to":

- a **party** is a direct participant in the fraud;
- **privy** captures a trustee who had knowledge of the fraud and
  did not act to prevent or remedy it.

The fraud exception is **substantively important** in long-tail
trust cases — a fraud committed decades ago can still be the basis
for a present-day claim.

## The 21-year longstop

Regardless of other rules, **no action proceeds beyond 21 years**
after the breach. This applies even where:

- the beneficiary did not discover the breach until decades after
  it occurred;
- the beneficiary was a minor or unascertained for most of the
  period;
- the breach was deliberately concealed (subject to the fraud
  exception above).

The longstop provides **finality** — at some point trustee
exposure ends.

The 21-year period reflects historical English law on equitable
limitation, adapted to Jersey.

## Hague Convention exception

The exception for foreign trusts under the **Hague Convention on the
Law Applicable to Trusts and on their Recognition (1985)** reflects
the convention's framework for cross-border trust recognition. Where
the trust's proper law is a Convention jurisdiction (UK, Australia,
Canada — subject to ratification details), the **foreign law's
limitation regime** applies rather than the Jersey 3/21 regime.

In practice the impact is narrow: most non-Jersey trust-related
litigation in the Royal Court is in the Article 49 context
(foreign trust enforceability), and the limitation question is
typically resolved by reference to the foreign proper law's own
regime.

## Pitfalls

- **Quick 3-year clock.** Beneficiaries that delay after receiving
  final accounts may lose claims. The trustee's strategic
  interest is in delivering thorough final accounts; the
  beneficiary's strategic interest is in scrutinising them
  promptly.
- **Outgoing trustee's 21-year exposure.** A retiring trustee
  faces up to 21 years of potential exposure for past breaches.
  Security under [Article 43A](trustee-security.md) and indemnity
  packages should anticipate this.
- **Knowledge attribution to beneficiaries.** Where minor
  beneficiaries have parents or guardians, knowledge attribution
  is complex — knowledge of the guardian may or may not be
  attributed to the beneficiary depending on the relationship.
- **Cross-border claims.** A claim brought in a foreign court may
  apply a different limitation regime. Forum selection matters.
