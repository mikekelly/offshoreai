# Article 33 — Constructive Trustee

Article 33 of the **[Trusts (Jersey) Law 1984][tjl]** is the
**constructive trust** provision. It captures the equitable doctrine
that someone who profits from a breach of trust holds the profits on
constructive trust for the beneficiaries, subject to the protection
of bona fide purchasers for value.

## What Article 33 says

- A person who **profits from a breach of trust** becomes a
  **constructive trustee** of those profits.
- The exception is the **bona fide purchaser for value without
  notice** (often shortened to "BFPV without notice" or "*equity's
  darling*").
- The constructive trustee must **surrender the property** to the
  rightful owners (i.e., the trust).
- The Article **preserves other circumstances** in which a
  constructive trust may arise — Article 33 is not an exhaustive
  codification.

(See the consolidated text at [jerseylaw.je][tjl].)

## Why this matters

Article 33 is the statutory basis for the **proprietary remedy** in
breach-of-trust cases. Whereas
[Article 30](breach-of-trust-liability.md) provides a **personal
remedy** against the breaching trustee (compensation), Article 33
provides a **proprietary remedy** against the recipient of
misappropriated trust property — the third party "knowingly receives"
or "dishonestly assists" the breach.

Proprietary remedies are powerful because:

- they **track the property** through subsequent transactions
  (subject to bona fide purchaser defences);
- they **rank ahead** of the recipient's general creditors in
  insolvency;
- they enable **recovery of profits** the recipient has made on
  the property, not just its original value.

## The two equitable bases — knowing receipt and dishonest assistance

The case law in Jersey (and persuasively from England) recognises
two flavours of liability that ground constructive trusteeship under
the Article 33-type analysis:

### Knowing receipt

A person who **receives trust property knowing** (or having reason
to know) that it has been transferred in breach of trust holds the
property on constructive trust. The mental element is **awareness**
that the property is trust property and that the transfer is wrongful
— a continuum from actual knowledge through wilful blindness to
reasonable inquiry that would have revealed the breach.

### Dishonest assistance

A person who **dishonestly assists** in a breach of trust is
personally liable, even if they did not receive trust property. The
mental element is **dishonesty** — by the standards of an ordinary
honest person in the defendant's position.

The Privy Council's articulation in *Royal Brunei Airlines Sdn Bhd v
Tan* [1995] 2 AC 378 and the House of Lords / UK Supreme Court line
in *Twinsectra v Yardley* [2002] UKHL 12 and *Barlow Clowes v
Eurotrust* [2005] UKPC 37 has been treated as persuasive in Jersey,
applied in the *Esteem* and subsequent Royal Court cases on the
broader fraud-and-trust margin.

## The bona fide purchaser defence

The classic "**equity's darling**" — someone who:

- gives **value** for the property (not a gift);
- acts in **good faith** (no actual or constructive knowledge of
  the breach);
- has **no notice** of the trust (actual, constructive, or imputed).

A BFPV takes the property free of the trust. The trustee's claim is
then back to a **personal claim** against the breaching trustee or
against any other person liable on dishonest-assistance grounds.

## "Other constructive-trust circumstances"

Article 33 expressly preserves the broader equitable jurisdiction.
Other circumstances in which Jersey recognises constructive trusts:

- **trustee-de-son-tort** — a person who acts as trustee without
  appointment is treated as a constructive trustee;
- **profits from fiduciary office** — a fiduciary who profits in
  breach holds the profits on constructive trust (the *Boardman v
  Phipps* and *Bray v Ford* line);
- **commercial circumstances** — certain commercial agreements give
  rise to constructive trusts (e.g., money paid for a purpose that
  fails — the *Quistclose* line);
- **other equitable scenarios** — the doctrine is open-textured.

## Recovery — surrender and tracing

A constructive trustee under Article 33 must **surrender the
property** to the rightful owners. Where the property has been
**dissipated** or **transformed**, the beneficiaries' remedies
include:

- **tracing** — following the value through substitutions and
  mixtures, recovering proceeds where they remain identifiable;
- **personal claim** against the constructive trustee for the value
  surrendered.

The interplay of tracing rules (common-law and equitable tracing) is
complex; the Royal Court applies the standard equitable doctrine,
informed by the leading English authorities including *Foskett v
McKeown* [2001] 1 AC 102.

## Pitfalls

- **Confusing recipient liability with assister liability.**
  Knowing receipt requires *receipt* of trust property; dishonest
  assistance does not. The remedies and mental elements differ.
- **Bona fide purchaser unsubstantiated.** A defendant claiming
  BFPV status must establish the elements; the burden is real.
  In particular, "constructive notice" is broader than many
  defendants assume — knowledge a reasonable person would have
  acquired by reasonable inquiry counts.
- **Article 33 is not the only route.** The Article preserves the
  broader doctrine; pleading should not be limited to Article 33.
