# Article 59 — Saving Provisions

Article 59 of the **[Trusts (Jersey) Law 1984][tjl]** preserves
**existing rights, powers, and jurisdictions** that the modern Law
otherwise might have impliedly displaced.

## What Article 59 says

The Article expressly preserves:

- existing powers of the **Viscount**, **tuteurs**, and **attorneys**;
- **previous trusts and transactions** — retaining their original
  validity;
- the Royal Court's powers regarding **fraud**, **matrimonial
  matters**, and **trust variation** as those powers existed
  independently of the Trusts Law.

(See the consolidated text at [jerseylaw.je][tjl].)

## Why these savings matter

### Powers of the Viscount

The **Viscount of Jersey** is the senior executive officer of the
Royal Court with specific functions in:

- **Désastre** proceedings (Jersey's customary-law insolvency
  procedure);
- enforcement of certain Court orders;
- specific historical functions including trust-related custody
  in particular circumstances.

Article 59 confirms that the modern Trusts Law does not displace
these pre-existing powers; they continue alongside the statutory
trust framework.

### Tuteurs and attorneys

A **tuteur** is the Jersey customary-law equivalent of a guardian
for a minor or person under incapacity. An **attorney** under a
power of attorney has authority granted by the principal.

Both roles intersect with trusts:

- a **tuteur** may act on behalf of a minor beneficiary;
- an **attorney** may act on behalf of a settlor or trustee under a
  power of attorney.

Article 59 confirms these customary-law and contract-law authorities
continue unaffected by the Trusts Law.

### Previous trusts and transactions

The most significant saving: **trusts and transactions that occurred
before the Trusts Law's commencement retain their original validity**.
A trust validly created under pre-1984 Jersey law remains valid even
where its terms or formation would not fit cleanly within the modern
statutory framework.

This protects:

- **family settlements** of long standing whose original drafting
  pre-dates 1984;
- **trustee acts** taken under pre-1984 trust law that the modern
  Law might otherwise question;
- **historical compromises and arrangements** that operated on the
  pre-1984 understanding.

### Court powers regarding fraud, matrimonial, variation

The Royal Court has had inherent jurisdiction over fraud,
matrimonial matters affecting trusts, and trust variation since
long before 1984. Article 59 confirms these inherent jurisdictions
survive — the Trusts Law's statutory provisions on these matters
**supplement** the inherent jurisdiction rather than replacing it.

This is particularly significant for:

- **fraud-on-a-power** doctrine — survives alongside the modern
  Articles 47B–H mistake regime;
- **matrimonial jurisdiction** in trust contexts — preserved
  alongside any Royal Court powers under the Matrimonial Causes
  (Jersey) Law 1949 and subsequent legislation;
- **inherent variation jurisdiction** — preserved alongside the
  statutory Article 47 variation power.

## Pitfalls

- **Assuming statute is exhaustive.** Article 59 is the express
  signal that the Trusts Law is **not** a complete codification —
  pre-existing customary-law rights, powers, and jurisdictions
  remain. Practitioners should be alert to customary-law avenues
  in particular cases.
- **Old-deed validity.** A pre-1984 trust may be valid under the
  saving even if it would not satisfy modern requirements. Don't
  challenge validity on modern grounds alone.
