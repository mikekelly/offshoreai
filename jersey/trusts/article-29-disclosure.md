# Article 29 — Disclosure

Article 29 of the **[Trusts (Jersey) Law 1984][tjl]** is the
**statutory disclosure** provision. It governs the trustee's duty to
provide information to beneficiaries and others — and, importantly,
the categories of trustee material that need *not* be disclosed.

Article 29 sits alongside (and is supplemented by) the **supervisory
jurisdiction** of the Royal Court applied in *Schmidt v Rosewood*. The
two together form Jersey's information-rights framework; see
[`beneficiary-rights.md`](beneficiary-rights.md) for the full
treatment of the supervisory dimension.

## What Article 29 says

### What the trust terms can do

The trust terms may:

- **grant disclosure rights** (express entitlement of named persons
  to receive information);
- **determine the extent** of disclosure;
- **impose disclosure duties** on the trustee.

### What is disclosable as of right

The following may request **account-related documents**:

- **beneficiaries** (other than charities — charitable trusts have
  a different framework);
- **named charities** (where the charity is a named beneficiary);
- **enforcers** (in respect of non-charitable purpose trusts).

### When the trustee may refuse

The trustee **may refuse** disclosure where refusing **protects the
beneficiaries' interests**.

### What the trustee need not disclose

The trustee is **not required** to disclose:

- the trustee's **deliberations**;
- the **reasoning** behind trustee decisions; or
- **proposed exercises** of powers.

### Court oversight

The Royal Court may **order disclosure** (to a specified extent) on
application of an interested person.

(See the consolidated text at [jerseylaw.je][tjl].)

## How Article 29 and *Schmidt v Rosewood* interact

Article 29 is **statutory** and gives certain categories of person a
default right to **account-related** information. *Schmidt v Rosewood*
is the **supervisory** jurisdiction of the Royal Court over disclosure
generally.

The two interact as follows:

- For **accounts**, Article 29 establishes the default position —
  beneficiaries (and enforcers and named charities) may request
  them; the trustee may refuse only on the "protecting beneficiaries"
  ground.
- For **other materials** — the deed, letters of wishes, deliberations
  — *Schmidt v Rosewood* and Jersey's adapted presumptions govern.
  See [`beneficiary-rights.md`](beneficiary-rights.md) for the
  presumption table by document type.
- The **Article 29(5) Court order** route operates in parallel with
  the Royal Court's *Schmidt*-jurisdiction order route. In practice
  applications cite both.

## "Protecting beneficiaries' interests" — the refusal ground

Article 29 lets the trustee refuse to disclose where doing so
"protects beneficiaries' interests". The phrase imports the
balancing exercise the Royal Court conducts under *Schmidt*:

- a refusal that genuinely protects the beneficiaries as a whole
  (e.g., refusing to give a beneficiary specific portfolio details
  that the beneficiary would use to manipulate the market) is
  defensible;
- a refusal motivated by trustee convenience or by protection of
  the trustee against beneficiary criticism is not.

The trustee bears the burden of articulating the protection ground
if a refusal is challenged.

## "Deliberations, reasoning, and proposed exercises"

The trustee is **not required** to disclose:

- **deliberations** — internal discussions between trustees
  considering a decision;
- **reasoning** — the rationale for a past or proposed decision;
- **proposed exercises** — decisions not yet taken.

The *Re Londonderry's Settlement* [1965] Ch 918 logic survives in
this provision: requiring trustees to disclose their reasoning would
chill the decision-making process and expose the trustees to
litigation harassment.

This does **not** mean the trustee can stonewall a beneficiary about
material decisions. The trustee should disclose:

- **that** a decision has been taken (or is being considered);
- the **outcome** of past decisions where they affect beneficial
  interests;
- **information necessary** for the beneficiary to evaluate their
  position.

What the trustee withholds is the **reasoning chain** behind the
outcome.

## Foreign court / regulator requests

Article 29 governs disclosure to **beneficiaries and similar**. It
does not govern requests for trust information from foreign courts
or regulators. Those are dealt with under:

- the **firewall** ([Article 9](firewall.md)) — for foreign
  judgments inconsistent with Jersey law;
- **mutual-legal-assistance** frameworks — for criminal-law and
  AML/CFT requests;
- the **DPJL 2018** — for personal-data requests;
- ordinary **disclosure mechanisms** in Royal Court litigation.

See the use-case sketch at
[`use-cases/trust-officer/index.md`](../use-cases/trust-officer/index.md)
under "information and disclosure" for the related questions.

## Application to the Court — Article 29(5)

An interested person — including a beneficiary, a trustee, or
(presumably) an enforcer — may apply to the Royal Court for an
**order** as to the extent of disclosure. The application is by
Representation under [Article 51](article-51-directions.md). The
Court will conduct the *Schmidt*-style balancing exercise and order
disclosure to whatever extent and with whatever safeguards it
considers appropriate.

## Pitfalls

- **Refusing without articulating a ground.** Article 29's refusal
  permission requires the protection-of-beneficiaries justification.
  A bare refusal is not within Article 29 and invites a Court
  application.
- **Disclosing deliberations under pressure.** Trustees sometimes
  feel pressured into disclosing reasoning to placate a difficult
  beneficiary. Article 29 protects the reasoning from disclosure;
  the trustee should consider whether disclosure is appropriate
  on the facts, not concede it as a matter of habit.
- **Confusing Article 29 with the deed's express disclosure
  clause.** The deed may give specific beneficiaries enhanced
  disclosure rights; those override the Article 29 defaults.
- **AML/CFT disclosure cross-currents.** A trustee responding to a
  beneficiary information request must also consider AML/CFT
  obligations (suspicious activity reporting; sanctions
  notifications) which may constrain what can be said to the
  beneficiary and when.
