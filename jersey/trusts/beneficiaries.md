# Article 10 — Beneficiaries of a Trust

Article 10 of the **[Trusts (Jersey) Law 1984][tjl]** is the
**identifiability** rule for beneficiaries and the statutory basis for
discretionary additions and exclusions and for conditions on benefit.

## What Article 10 says

A beneficiary must be **identifiable**:

- **by name**; or
- **by ascertainability** by reference to a class, or a relationship
  to some person.

The trust terms may also:

- **add or exclude** beneficiaries (subject to the deed's powers);
- **impose conditions** on the receipt of benefit by a beneficiary.

(See the consolidated text at [jerseylaw.je][tjl].)

## What this means in practice

### Identifiability — the certainty-of-objects rule

Article 10 codifies the common-law "certainty of objects" requirement.
A trust whose beneficiaries cannot be ascertained — directly by name
or indirectly by reference to a definable class — fails for
uncertainty.

Permissible class definitions include:

- "the issue of X" (a relationship class);
- "the descendants of Y born before [date]" (a defined cohort);
- "the employees of Z and their dependants" (an employment-relationship
  class);
- "the children of X and Y, whether or not yet born or ascertained"
  (cross-refers Article 2's accommodation of unascertained and
  unborn beneficiaries).

Impermissibly vague: "the friends of X" (no objective test for
membership), "people who deserve help" (subjective and unbounded).

### Conditions on benefit

A trust may make a beneficiary's interest conditional on a future
event — e.g., reaching a stated age, completing a degree, marrying
within a stated faith. Conditions that are contrary to Jersey public
policy (e.g., conditions in terrorem against marriage at large) are
typically unenforceable.

### Power to add and exclude beneficiaries

A deed may give the trustee (or another office-holder) the power to
add or exclude beneficiaries. The Royal Court treats these as
fiduciary powers — they must be exercised in good faith, within their
proper purpose, and not for the conferral of personal benefit on the
power-holder. A protector exercising a power to add a beneficiary is
in the same fiduciary position.

The power to exclude is particularly significant in practice in two
contexts:

- **US-person planning** — a beneficiary who becomes a US person may
  be excluded to avoid the trust becoming subject to US tax
  reporting and grantor-trust attribution rules.
- **Family disputes** — a settlor may pre-empt a foreseeable family
  dispute by granting the trustee a power to exclude a problematic
  branch of the family.

## Relationship with other Articles

- [Article 2](existence-of-a-trust.md) — accommodates beneficiaries

  to disclaim.
- [Article 12](purpose-trusts.md) — non-charitable purpose trusts
  exist *without* beneficiaries, supervised by an enforcer instead.

  rules for closed and open classes.

## Pitfalls

- **Drafting "class" beneficiaries without a closing date** —
  produces ascertainability problems when distributions are due.
  Modern practice closes classes at a stated future event or date.
- **Conditional interests in conflict with public policy** — for
  example, a condition that the beneficiary divorce a particular
  spouse is likely unenforceable.
- **Power to add beneficiaries** — must be drafted to fit the
  fiduciary-power framework. A power "to add any person the trustee
  thinks fit" is broad; the Royal Court has applied the
  *proper-purpose* doctrine to limit such powers in practice.
