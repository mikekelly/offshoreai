# Article 16 — Number of Trustees

Article 16 of the **[Trusts (Jersey) Law 1984][tjl]** is the **minimum
number** rule. It sets a floor on trustee numbers and tells the
remaining trustees what they can and cannot do while below the floor.

## What Article 16 says

A trust must have **at least one trustee**. The trust does **not
fail** if the actual number of trustees drops below the required
minimum (typically because of resignation or death). While trustee
numbers are below the floor:

- a **new appointment must be made promptly** to restore the
  required number;
- existing trustees may only act to **preserve trust property** —
  not to make distributions, exercise dispositive powers, or take
  decisions that affect beneficial interests.

(See the consolidated text at [jerseylaw.je][tjl].)

## Practical implications

### One-trustee trusts

Jersey permits sole-trustee trusts. In professional practice this
typically means a Jersey-regulated trust company business acting as
sole trustee. Family-office structures sometimes use a **private
trust company (PTC)** as sole trustee — the PTC itself a single
corporate entity but with multiple natural-person directors.

A sole-trustee structure is operationally simpler (no co-trustee
co-ordination, no Article 22 unanimity issues) but concentrates risk
in one fiduciary. For high-value or complex trusts, families often
prefer a co-trustee model.

### What "preserving trust property" allows below the floor

When a co-trustee resigns or dies and the trustee number drops below
the deed's required minimum, the remaining trustee(s) can:

- **continue holding** the trust property;
- **safeguard** it from loss (insurance, security, ordinary asset
  maintenance);
- **continue receiving** income on the trust property and bank it;
- **defend** the trust property against claims;
- **make appointments** to restore the trustee number.

They cannot:

- make **distributions** to beneficiaries;
- **change investments** (other than for preservation);
- exercise **dispositive powers** (additions, exclusions, advancements);
- take other decisions that materially affect beneficial interests.

### Restoring the number — interaction with Article 17

Where the deed gives a power of appointment of new trustees, that
power is the primary mechanism. Where the deed is silent or the
appointor cannot act, [Article 17](trustee-appointment-out-of-court.md)
supplies a statutory power of appointment.

If no appointment can be made out of court, an application may be
made to the Royal Court under
[Article 51](article-51-directions.md) (or, where there is no resident
trustee at all, under [Article 44](court-appointed-resident-trustee.md)
*(planned)*).

## Why the deed's "required minimum" often exceeds one

A deed will frequently require **two or three trustees** for trusts
above a stated value, or where the family wants a balance between
professional and lay trustees. The Article 16 floor of one is the
statutory minimum, not a recommended structure for substantial trusts.

## Pitfalls

- **Distributions below the floor.** A distribution made while
  trustee numbers are below the floor is voidable and the trustee
  is exposed personally. The right response to an unexpected
  vacancy is **stop, preserve, appoint** — not "process the
  distribution and worry about it later".
- **PTC director vacancies.** A PTC acting as trustee has only one
  legal personality, so a director vacancy on the PTC's board is
  not the same as a trustee vacancy under Article 16. But it may
  trigger Article 22 / Article 28 issues — see
  [`corporate-trustee-resolution.md`](corporate-trustee-resolution.md).
