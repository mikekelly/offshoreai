# Article 13 — Enforcers of Non-Charitable Purpose Trusts

Article 13 of the **[Trusts (Jersey) Law 1984][tjl]** establishes the
**enforcer** office. An enforcer is required for every
non-charitable purpose trust under [Article 12](purpose-trusts.md):
without beneficiaries, somebody must have standing to enforce the
purposes against the trustee, and the enforcer is that person.

## What Article 13 says

An **enforcer**:

- must **enforce the trust in relation to its non-charitable
  purposes**;
- **cannot simultaneously serve as trustee** of the same trust.

(See the consolidated text at [jerseylaw.je][tjl].)

## Who can be an enforcer

The Trusts Law does not specify qualifications. Practical
considerations:

- The enforcer is typically a **professional fiduciary** — often a
  Jersey-regulated trust company business unrelated to the trustee
  — for orphan-structure NCPTs. The independence from the trustee
  is the substantive requirement.
- For **family-office NCPTs**, the enforcer is often a trusted family
  adviser or an independent professional, again unrelated to the
  trustee.
- **A natural person** may act as enforcer; corporate enforcers are
  also permitted.
- The enforcer **must not be the trustee** — this is the only firm
  statutory bar.

## The enforcer's duties

The duty is to **enforce the trust in relation to its non-charitable
purposes**. In practice this entails:

- monitoring the trustee's compliance with the purposes;
- requiring the trustee to take action consistent with the purposes
  where the trustee fails to do so;
- bringing applications to the Royal Court where necessary to compel
  performance;
- consenting (where the deed requires consent) to acts of the trustee
  that might otherwise be outside the purposes;
- supervising the trustee's record-keeping in relation to the
  purposes.

The Royal Court has supervisory jurisdiction over the enforcer's
performance of the function, parallel to its supervisory jurisdiction
over trustees.

## Enforcer's relationship with the trustee

The enforcer's office is **fiduciary**. The enforcer owes duties:

- to the **non-charitable purposes** of the trust;
- to **comply with the deed**;
- to **act in good faith** and not for personal benefit.

The enforcer does **not** have the trustee's powers — the trustee
holds and disposes of trust property. The enforcer's role is
supervisory and enforcement-oriented, not administrative.

## Standing to apply to the Royal Court

The enforcer has standing to apply to the Royal Court under
[Article 51](article-51-directions.md) for directions on the proper
performance of the trust's purposes. The enforcer is also a proper
party to be convened where the trustee applies to the Court on a
purpose-related matter.

## Pitfalls

- **Trustee-affiliated enforcer.** The substantive independence
  requirement should be observed; an enforcer that is part of the
  trustee's group risks the Royal Court treating the appointment
  as inadequate.
- **No succession provision.** A deed that names a sole enforcer
  without providing for succession is fragile — see
  [`enforcer-resignation-removal.md`](enforcer-resignation-removal.md)
  for the Article 14 mechanics.
- **Conflating enforcer and protector.** A **protector** (named in
  most family discretionary trusts) is a different fiduciary office,
  applicable to beneficiary trusts as well as purpose trusts. An
  enforcer is specific to non-charitable purpose trusts.
- **Fee structure.** Enforcer fees are typically modest because the
  role is more supervisory than administrative; deeds should
  contemplate the enforcer's right to fees and indemnity from the
  trust fund.
