# Article 44 — Court Appointment of Resident Trustee

Article 44 of the **[Trusts (Jersey) Law 1984][tjl]** is the
**resident-trustee fallback**. Where there is **no trustee resident
in Jersey**, a beneficiary can apply to the Royal Court to appoint
an additional **Jersey-resident trustee**.

## What Article 44 says

> Where there is no trustee resident in Jersey a beneficiary may
> apply to the court for the appointment of a person nominated for
> the purpose in the application, who shall be a person who resides
> in Jersey, as an additional trustee.

The Court may make the appointment upon being satisfied that:

- **notice has been served** on existing trustees; and
- the **nominated person is willing to act**.

(See the consolidated text at [jerseylaw.je][tjl].)

## Why this matters

A Jersey trust without a Jersey-resident trustee can become
operationally problematic:

- the **Article 5 jurisdiction** of the Royal Court over the trust
  rests in part on Jersey residence of a trustee — without a
  Jersey-resident trustee, the jurisdictional hook narrows;
- **regulatory contact points** (JFSC supervision of trust-company
  business, FATF requirements for beneficial-ownership reporting)
  benefit from a Jersey-resident professional trustee;
- **practical administration** — Jersey banking, custody, and legal
  arrangements are simplified by having a Jersey-resident trustee;
- the trust's **firewall protection** under
  [Article 9](firewall.md) — though not formally dependent on a
  resident trustee — is more robust where the Royal Court is
  unambiguously the natural forum.

Article 44 lets a beneficiary force the addition of a Jersey-resident
trustee where the current arrangement has drifted to all-foreign
trustees.

## Standing — beneficiary application

The Article gives **beneficiary standing** to apply. This is broader
than the existing-trustees' standing under
[Article 17](trustee-appointment-out-of-court.md): a beneficiary
need not wait for the trustees to nominate a successor or for the
trustees to act under a deed power. The beneficiary can take the
initiative.

The applying beneficiary nominates the proposed appointee. The Court
controls whether to make the appointment.

## Procedural requirements

The Article expressly requires:

- **notice** to existing trustees — so that the existing trustees can
  appear and oppose if they wish;
- **willingness** of the nominated person — they must consent in
  advance.

In practice the application is by **Representation** in the Royal
Court (Samedi Division), supported by:

- an **affidavit** describing the trust, the current trustee
  arrangement, and the reasons for seeking a Jersey-resident trustee;
- the **nominee's written consent** to act;
- the **deed and any amendments**;
- evidence of **service** on existing trustees.

## Discretion of the Court

The Court is not bound to make the appointment. It will consider:

- whether a Jersey-resident trustee is genuinely needed (some trusts
  are administered effectively by foreign trustees and the
  beneficiary's application may be motivated by something other than
  good administration);
- whether the **nominee is suitable** — typically a Jersey-regulated
  TCB rather than a natural person;
- the **existing trustees' position** — they may consent or oppose;
- the **trust's interests** as a whole.

Where the Court considers a resident trustee is not needed but some
other intervention is, the Court may direct otherwise — for example,
ordering the existing trustees to retain a Jersey-resident
administrator without becoming a trustee.

## Relationship with other appointment routes

Article 44 sits alongside:

- [Article 17](trustee-appointment-out-of-court.md) — out-of-court
  appointment by trustees, last remaining trustee, or personal
  representative;
- the **deed's appointment mechanism** — typically vested in the
  settlor, protector, or continuing trustees;
- [Article 51](article-51-directions.md) — general directions
  jurisdiction, which can be used to order trustee appointment
  for cause.

Article 44 is specifically for the **resident-trustee-absent**
scenario at beneficiary initiative. Other appointment scenarios use
the deed mechanism or Article 51 directions.

## Pitfalls

- **Premature application.** A beneficiary considering an Article 44
  application should first ask whether the deed mechanism can solve
  the problem — typically faster and cheaper.
- **Nominee unsuitability.** Proposing a connected party as
  resident trustee invites refusal; the nominee should be
  independent and suitably regulated.
- **Strategic use against existing trustees.** Article 44 should not
  be used as a tactical step in a beneficiary's broader dispute
  with the existing trustees; the Court will look at substance.
