# Article 50 — Application of Part 4

Article 50 of the **[Trusts (Jersey) Law 1984][tjl]** is the
**scope-of-Part-4** provision. It extends Part 4's general
provisions to **both Jersey and foreign trusts**:

> This Part of this Law shall apply to a Jersey trust and, to the
> extent that the context admits, shall apply to a foreign trust.

(See the consolidated text at [jerseylaw.je][tjl].)

## What this means

Part 4 of the Trusts Law (Articles 50–57) contains **general
provisions** that the legislature wanted to apply broadly:

- [Article 51](article-51-directions.md) — Court directions
  jurisdiction;
- Article 52 — execution of instruments by Court order;
- Article 53 — payment of costs;
- Article 54 — trustee's estate and trust property in insolvency;
- Article 55 — protection of persons dealing with the trustee
  (BFPV);
- Article 57 — limitation of actions.

Each of these provisions applies to **Jersey trusts** as a matter
of course, and to **foreign trusts** to the extent the context
admits.

## "To the extent that the context admits"

This qualifier acknowledges that some Part 4 rules apply naturally
to foreign trusts (e.g., the protection of persons dealing with the
trustee, the Court's directions jurisdiction in Jersey-touching
matters), while others may be context-specific:

- **Article 51 directions** are routinely available for
  Jersey-resident trustees of foreign trusts;
- **Article 54** on trustee insolvency applies where the trustee is
  bankrupt in Jersey, regardless of the trust's proper law;
- **Article 57 limitation** applies to Jersey-litigated claims;
  the trust's proper law may have its own limitation regime that
  governs claims in foreign courts.

## Why this matters

Article 50's bridging function means a **Jersey-resident trustee of
a foreign trust** has access to Royal Court directions, costs
relief, and other procedural supports — even though the substantive
trust law is foreign. The combination of
[Article 5 jurisdiction](court-jurisdiction-over-trusts.md) and
Article 50 extension makes the Royal Court a workable forum for
many foreign-trust applications.
