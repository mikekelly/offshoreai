# Article 42 — Failure or Lapse of Interest

Article 42 of the **[Trusts (Jersey) Law 1984][tjl]** is the
**resulting trust** provision in default form. Where a trust interest
**lapses**, the trust **terminates**, or the **beneficiaries cease to
exist**, the trustee holds the property on resulting trust for the
**settlor absolutely** (or the settlor's personal representative).

## What Article 42 says

Where:

- an **interest lapses**;
- a **trust terminates**; or
- **beneficiaries cease to exist**;

the property is **held by the trustee for the settlor absolutely**
(or for the settlor's personal representative if the settlor is
deceased).

Applications under this Article may be made by the **Attorney
General** (where charitable or public-interest purposes are
engaged).

(See the consolidated text at [jerseylaw.je][tjl].)

## How Article 42 fits with other Articles

Article 42 is the **default destination** rule when a trust runs out
of beneficiaries or interests. It sits alongside:

- [Article 36 class interests](class-interests.md) — when a class
  closes without members, the income accumulates pending a member
  or class closure; if the class closes without ever having members,
  Article 42 takes over;
- [Article 43 termination](termination-of-trust.md) — termination
  procedurally; Article 42 sets where the property goes when no
  destination is specified;
- [Article 47A](court-purpose-trust-redirection.md) —
  cy-près-style redirection by the Royal Court for charitable and
  non-charitable purpose trusts when the original purpose fails.

## The default resulting trust

The Article 42 default is a **resulting trust** to the settlor (or
the settlor's personal representative). This is the equity-law
default that applies whenever:

- the deed does not specify a default beneficiary on failure of
  the primary interests;
- the deed's specified default has itself failed.

The default is **rebuttable** by:

- **express deed terms** providing a different default — most
  modern deeds include a "**default beneficiary**" or "**default
  charity**" clause that displaces Article 42;
- **the operation of [Article 47A](court-purpose-trust-redirection.md)
 ** for purpose trusts where the Court can redirect the
  property to alternative purposes consistent with the settlor's
  intent.

## When the settlor is deceased

If the settlor has died, the property reverts to the **personal
representative** — i.e., the executor or administrator of the
settlor's estate. Practical consequences:

- the property re-enters the **settlor's estate** for inheritance
  and succession purposes;
- the **succession rules** of the settlor's domicile (or relevant
  jurisdiction) may now apply to the property;
- **forced-heirship rules** that the firewall under
  [Article 9](firewall.md) had protected against during the trust's
  existence may now bite on the reverted property in the settlor's
  estate;
- **inheritance tax / equivalent** in the settlor's home
  jurisdiction may be triggered by the reversion.

The "settlor deceased" branch of Article 42 is therefore particularly
sensitive in cross-border planning: a default reversion to the
settlor's estate may undo much of the planning that the trust was
designed to achieve.

## Attorney General applications

The Article expressly mentions the **Attorney General** as a
potential applicant. The AG's role is particularly relevant for:

- **charitable trusts** that have failed — the AG may apply for
  redirection (under [Article 47A](court-purpose-trust-redirection.md)
 ) or for Article 42 default treatment;
- **non-charitable purpose trusts** where the purpose has failed
  and there is a public-interest dimension;
- **publicly-significant trusts** where the AG considers the
  Court's intervention is warranted.

## Drafting practice

Modern Jersey trust deeds almost always include a **default
beneficiary** clause to displace Article 42. Common defaults:

- a **specified charity** or **class of charities**;
- a **named family branch** as ultimate default;
- the **settlor's estate** (effectively reinstating Article 42 by
  drafting, but with the trustee's powers continuing to govern the
  transition);
- a **purpose trust** with a defined alternative purpose if the
  primary purposes fail.

A deed without a default is exposed to Article 42's resulting trust
— and to the cross-border consequences described above.

## Pitfalls

- **No default in the deed.** Article 42's resulting trust may
  unwind the firewall protection that the trust was designed to
  provide. Always include a default.
- **Inadequate purpose-trust drafting.** For a non-charitable
  purpose trust, the deed should include a fallback charitable or
  alternative-purpose direction; reliance on Article 47A redirection
  by the Court is more uncertain.
- **Class closure without members.** A class of "issue" that never
  produces issue triggers Article 42 when the class closes. The
  deed should anticipate.
- **Cross-border default reversion.** A reversion to a settlor's
  estate in a forced-heirship jurisdiction may be the worst possible
  outcome for the settlor's planning. Test the default in the
  cross-border context.
