# Article 21 — Duties of Trustee

Article 21 of the **[Trusts (Jersey) Law 1984][tjl]** is the **core
duties** provision. It sets out the **statutory baseline** of trustee
obligations on top of which the deed and the equitable jurisdiction
of the Royal Court layer further duties. It is the foundation for the
breach-of-trust liability in [Article 30](breach-of-trust-liability.md)
*(planned)*.

## What Article 21 says

A trustee must act:

- **with due diligence**, as would a **prudent person**;
- **to the best of the trustee's ability and skill**; and
- observing **the utmost good faith**.

Operational duties:

- **administer the trust per its terms**;
- **reasonably preserve and enhance** trust property value;
- **not profit** from the position except with **court approval** or
  the **express terms** of the trust permitting it;
- **maintain accurate accounts**;
- **keep trust property separate** from personal assets; and
- ensure **enforcers are appointed** for non-charitable purpose trusts.

(See the consolidated text at [jerseylaw.je][tjl].)

## The standard of care

Article 21's "prudent person" standard is **objective**, but the
"to the best of the trustee's ability and skill" element imports a
**subjective uplift** for professional trustees:

- An ordinary lay trustee is held to the standard of a prudent
  person of average competence.
- A **professional trustee** — a JFSC-regulated trust-company
  business — is held to the standard of a prudent person *with the
  trustee's actual skill set*. The professional trustee cannot
  point to lack of expertise as a defence in an area where it holds
  itself out as expert.

This dual structure (objective floor, professional uplift) maps to
the common-law standard articulated in cases such as *Re Whiteley*
(1886) 33 ChD 347 and *Bartlett v Barclays Bank* [1980] Ch 515 in
England, which the Royal Court treats as persuasive.

## "Utmost good faith"

The "utmost good faith" standard is **higher than ordinary good
faith**. It imports the equity-court conception of the fiduciary as
someone who must:

- act exclusively in the beneficiaries' interests in the relevant
  decision;
- avoid conflicts of interest;
- not exploit information or opportunity acquired by reason of the
  fiduciary position;
- disclose, candidly and proactively, material facts and concerns.

This is the source of the "no-conflict" and "no-profit" rules that
saturate fiduciary practice.

## Specific operational duties

### Administer per the terms

The first duty is **compliance with the deed**. Where the deed is
silent, the trustee acts under the general statutory powers
([Article 24](trustee-powers.md)) consistent with the duty of
prudent administration.

### Preserve and enhance value

The trustee must take **reasonable steps to preserve** trust
property (insurance, security, ordinary maintenance) and **reasonable
steps to enhance** its value (prudent investment, considered
disposals).

The "reasonably" qualifier is significant: the duty is not absolute.
A trustee that takes a considered decision that turns out badly is
not in breach merely for the loss; the question is whether the
process was prudent.

### No profit without authority

The **no-profit rule** is one of the strictest of fiduciary rules. A
trustee cannot:

- charge fees for services unless the deed allows or the Court
  approves (the [Article 26](trustee-remuneration.md) statutory
  remuneration permission for professional trustees is the major
  exception);
- take a benefit from a transaction the trustee enters on behalf of
  the trust (e.g., commission from a counterparty);
- acquire property at an undervalue from the trust;
- compete with a business owned by the trust.

Where the deed expressly permits a profit (e.g., a fee schedule, or
permission for the trustee to engage its own affiliates as
investment manager), that permission must be **express and clear**;
the Royal Court reads such permissions strictly.

### Accounts and segregation

The trustee must:

- maintain **proper accounts** of the trust property and its
  performance; and
- keep trust property **segregated** from personal assets.

These duties underpin the [Schmidt v Rosewood](beneficiary-rights.md)
disclosure framework — without accounts, the trustee cannot give
the disclosure the supervisory jurisdiction may require.

### Enforcer for purpose trusts

For non-charitable purpose trusts, the trustee must ensure an
**enforcer** is in place — see [Articles 12](purpose-trusts.md) and
[13](enforcers.md).

## The deed cannot exclude all duties

The deed can **vary** trustee duties but cannot exclude them entirely.
A clause purporting to relieve the trustee from all liability for
breach is void as contrary to the irreducible core of trusteeship. A
"trustee exoneration clause" can validly exclude liability for
negligence (in some circumstances) but cannot exclude liability for
**dishonesty**, **wilful default**, or **gross negligence**.

The boundaries are sharper for **professional trustees** — the Royal
Court is sceptical of broad exoneration clauses for fees-charging
trustees, on the basis that a fees-charging trustee is being paid
precisely for the standard of care being excluded.

## Duty of confidentiality

Although not expressly listed in Article 21, the trustee owes a
**duty of confidentiality** to the trust — derived from the utmost
good faith standard and from the case law of the Royal Court. The
duty bounds the trustee's response to requests for information from
third parties (see [Schmidt v Rosewood / beneficiary-rights.md](beneficiary-rights.md))
and is the basis for the trustee's resistance to fishing-expedition
disclosure orders.

## Interaction with case law

Article 21 is the statutory baseline; the Royal Court has expanded
on each element through case law. Standard authorities the trustee
should know:

- *In Re Esteem Settlement* [2003] JLR 188 — sham analysis; the
  trustee's duty to maintain real segregation.
- *Crociani v Crociani* [2014] UKPC 40 — jurisdiction clauses;
  trustee's duty to defend the proper forum where appropriate.
- *Schmidt v Rosewood Trust Ltd* [2003] UKPC 26 — beneficiary
  information rights; the trustee's duty to handle disclosure
  appropriately.
- The Royal Court line on *Public Trustee v Cooper* applications —
  the trustee's duty to seek Court blessing for momentous decisions
  (see [`article-51-directions.md`](article-51-directions.md)).

## Pitfalls

- **Treating Article 21 as the whole of trustee duty.** Article 21
  is the floor; the deed and the equitable jurisdiction add to it.
- **Profits in disguise.** A trustee receiving "marketing fees" or
  "referral fees" from counterparties to the trust is in breach of
  the no-profit rule unless the fees are within an express
  authorisation.
- **Skill defences.** A professional trustee cannot disclaim
  professional skill it holds itself out as having.
