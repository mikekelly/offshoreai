# Article 36 — Class Interests

Article 36 of the **[Trusts (Jersey) Law 1984][tjl]** addresses
**class interests** — beneficiaries identified by reference to a
class rather than by name. Three rules apply:

1. A class **closes** when no further person can become a member.
2. **Women over 55** are deemed incapable of bearing children for
   class-closing purposes.
3. Where **no class members exist** at any time, the income
   **accumulates** until a class member arises or the class closes.

(See the consolidated text at [jerseylaw.je][tjl].)

## Why this matters

Class-based beneficial interests are ubiquitous in family trusts.
Examples:

- "**the children of the settlor**" — a class that closes on the
  settlor's death (assuming the settlor cannot have further children);
- "**the issue of the settlor**" — a class that may remain open for
  decades through successive generations;
- "**the employees of [a named company]**" — a class that fluctuates
  with hiring and departures.

Article 36 gives the trustee operational rules for managing class
interests over time.

## Rule 1 — when a class closes

A class **closes** when it is no longer possible for any other person
to become a member. The closing date depends on the class:

- "children of X" closes on X's death (subject to posthumous-birth
  considerations and modern assisted-reproduction questions);
- "grandchildren of X" closes when all of X's children have died
  (subject to the same considerations);
- "employees of [company]" closes when no further hires can be made
  (typically on company dissolution).

Closing matters because it **fixes the membership** of the class for
the purposes of distribution. Until the class closes, the trustee
must account for the **possibility of further members**.

## Rule 2 — women over 55 presumed past childbearing

The Article includes a **statutory presumption** that women over 55
are incapable of bearing children for class-closing purposes. This
is a pragmatic operational rule, derived from English authorities
(notably *Re Pettifor* [1966] Ch 257), to allow trustees to close
classes without indefinitely waiting for the theoretical possibility
of further births.

The presumption is **rebuttable** in unusual cases (modern
assisted-reproductive techniques can produce children in
post-natural-fertility women), but the practical effect is to permit
the trustee to treat a woman over 55 as **past childbearing** for
class purposes absent specific evidence to the contrary.

This rule has not been updated to address modern fertility
technology and is occasionally challenged on those grounds. Where a
specific case involves recent or ongoing fertility treatment, the
trustee should not rely on the presumption without further
consideration.

## Rule 3 — no class members; income accumulates

Where a class has **no current members** — e.g., "the children of X"
when X has not yet had any children, or "the surviving issue of X"
when all members have died and the class remains open for further
births — the **income accumulates** until either:

- a **member arises** (a child is born, an issue comes into
  existence); or
- the **class closes** without any members (failure of the class —
  see [`failure-or-lapse.md`](failure-or-lapse.md) for the
  consequences).

The accumulation is a **statutory default**. The deed can override
it (e.g., by providing for income to pass to a default beneficiary
or class while the primary class is empty).

## Interaction with other Articles

- [Article 38](accumulation-and-advancement.md) provides a separate
  framework for **accumulation under the deed's terms**. Article 36's
  no-members accumulation is a default; Article 38 governs
  expressed accumulation regimes.
- [Article 42](failure-or-lapse.md) addresses the case where a class
  closes without members and the interest **fails**. The default
  result is that property is held for the settlor or the settlor's
  personal representative.
- [Article 23 impartiality](trustee-impartiality.md) applies to the
  trustee's administration of class interests — particularly
  significant where the trust has both a current class and a
  successor class.

## Drafting practice

A well-drafted family trust will:

- **define each class** clearly with a stated closing event;
- **specify the consequences** of class closure (default class,
  charity, reversion to settlor's estate);
- **address contingencies** — what happens if a class closes
  without members; what happens if there are unforeseen members
  (e.g., a child born from modern assisted reproduction whose
  status as "issue" is debatable);
- consider **modern reproductive technology** in defining "issue"
  and "children" — surrogate children, donor-conceived children,
  adopted children, posthumous children.

## Pitfalls

- **Over-reliance on the over-55 presumption** in modern cases. A
  trustee that closes a class without considering current
  reproductive technology faces a small but real risk of being
  challenged.
- **Accumulation without authority.** The Article 36 accumulation
  arises only where no class member exists. If the trustee is
  accumulating in other circumstances (e.g., during minority),
  the authority is under [Article 38](accumulation-and-advancement.md)
  and the deed.
- **Posthumous and assisted-reproduction children.** The traditional
  "children of X" definition may not cleanly capture modern family
  patterns. Drafting should be deliberate.
