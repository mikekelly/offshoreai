# Article 30 — Liability for Breach of Trust

Article 30 of the **[Trusts (Jersey) Law 1984][tjl]** is the **liability**
provision. It sets out the trustee's exposure for breach of trust and
the principal defences and limits on liability — including the
**irreducible core** of liability that cannot be excluded by deed.

## What Article 30 says

### Personal liability

A trustee is liable for:

- breaches **committed** by the trustee;
- breaches **concurred in** by the trustee.

The trustee must compensate for:

- **loss** or depreciation in the value of trust property;
- **lost gains** the trust would have made absent the breach.

Multiple breaches **cannot be offset** against each other — gains from
a profitable breach do not cancel losses from a separate breach.

### Predecessor and discovered breaches

A trustee is **not responsible** for breaches committed by
**predecessor** trustees, **but** must take steps to **remedy
breaches** the trustee discovers in the trust's history.

### Co-trustee liability

Co-trustees are **jointly liable** unless a non-acting co-trustee was
**unaware** of the misconduct (and could not reasonably have been
aware).

### Beneficiary release

Beneficiaries may **relieve a trustee from liability** if they have:

- **capacity** to consent; and
- **full knowledge** of the relevant facts.

### Exoneration clauses — the irreducible core

Exculpatory clauses in the deed **cannot** protect against:

- **fraud**;
- **wilful misconduct**; or
- **gross negligence**.

(See the consolidated text at [jerseylaw.je][tjl].)

## The standard remedy — compensation for loss and lost gains

The two limbs of Article 30 compensation are:

1. **Loss** — the difference between the trust's actual position and
   where it would have been had the breach not occurred. For asset
   losses, this is typically the diminution in value plus
   transactional costs the trust incurred.
2. **Lost gains** — what the trust would have gained absent the
   breach. This requires the Royal Court to do a counterfactual
   reconstruction; the trustee in a contested case may face
   significant evidential exposure on what the trust "would have"
   earned in a prudent alternative scenario.

The "no offset" rule means a trustee that has been unusually
successful in one area cannot claim credit for those gains when
defending a breach in another. Each breach is assessed on its own.

## Discovered breaches by predecessors

A trustee who steps into a trust and discovers historic breaches by
prior trustees has a **duty to act**. Options:

- **Sue the prior trustee** for compensation if recovery is realistic.
- **Apply to the Royal Court** under
  [Article 51](article-51-directions.md) for directions on the
  proper course (including Beddoe protection if litigation is
  contemplated).
- **Notify beneficiaries** if appropriate and consistent with the
  *Schmidt v Rosewood* framework (see
  [`beneficiary-rights.md`](beneficiary-rights.md)).

A trustee who **discovers and ignores** a historic breach risks
becoming complicit — the duty to remedy carries forward, and inaction
can produce its own breach claim.

## Co-trustee liability and the unaware co-trustee

The default is joint liability. The escape is **lack of awareness**
(actual or reasonable). A co-trustee who:

- attended the board meeting that approved the impugned transaction;
- received minutes or correspondence putting the matter on notice;
- had the means but did not enquire,

cannot rely on the lack-of-awareness defence. The defence is for the
genuinely sidelined or deceived co-trustee, not for the inattentive.

Practical hardening:

- **document dissent in writing** — see
  [`co-trustees-acting-together.md`](co-trustees-acting-together.md)
  and Article 22;
- **resign** in serious cases — but note Article 19's restrictions
  on simultaneous resignation;
- **seek Article 51 directions** if continued co-trusteeship is
  untenable.

## Beneficiary release

A trustee may obtain a release from breach claims by **informed
beneficiary consent**. The requirements are strict:

- the beneficiary must have **capacity** (adult, mentally competent);
- the beneficiary must have **full knowledge** of the relevant
  facts — full and frank disclosure by the trustee;
- the release should be in **writing**;
- all **affected beneficiaries** should be releasing (a single
  beneficiary's release does not bind the class).

Release is the standard mechanism on a clean exit when the trustee
retires and the beneficiaries are content. It is **not available**
for unborn or unascertained beneficiaries — which is why momentous
decisions are normally pre-blessed by the Royal Court under Article
51 (see
[`distribution-decisions-and-court-blessing.md`](../use-cases/trust-officer/distribution-decisions-and-court-blessing.md)).

## The irreducible core — fraud, wilful misconduct, gross negligence

This is the **most-litigated** part of Article 30 in practice. Deeds
routinely include exoneration clauses excluding liability for
negligence; the Royal Court enforces those, subject to limits:

- **Fraud** — never excludable.
- **Wilful misconduct** — never excludable. This includes
  reckless indifference to whether an act is a breach.
- **Gross negligence** — never excludable in Jersey. (English law's
  position on whether gross negligence can be excluded is contested;
  Jersey is clear that it cannot.)
- **Negligence below the gross threshold** — excludable by clear
  language, but the Royal Court reads exoneration clauses strictly
  against the trustee, particularly a fee-charging professional
  trustee.

The Jersey position on **gross negligence** is firmer than English
law's, and is one of the policy distinctives of the Jersey trust
regime. A professional trustee cannot exclude liability for gross
negligence even with the clearest deed wording.

## Court relief — Article 45

Even where a breach is found, the Royal Court has a discretion under
[Article 45](relief-from-personal-liability.md) *(planned)* to relieve
the trustee from personal liability where the trustee acted **honestly
and reasonably and ought fairly to be excused**. This is a
case-by-case discretionary safety valve, not a routine outcome.

## Limitation

Breach-of-trust claims are subject to a limitation period under
[Article 57](limitation-and-prescription.md) *(planned)*. The starting
point is typically the date of the breach (or, for concealed
breaches, the date of discovery).

## Pitfalls

- **Over-reliance on exoneration clauses.** Even a well-drafted
  exoneration clause does not protect against gross negligence in
  Jersey. The trustee's principal protection is doing the work
  properly.
- **Settling a breach with one beneficiary.** A trustee who settles
  a claim with one beneficiary without engaging others remains
  exposed to claims from the others.
- **Discovered breaches and silence.** A trustee that finds a
  historic breach and does nothing inherits exposure on the duty-to-
  remedy point.
- **PTC director liability.** A breach by a PTC acting as trustee is
  a breach by the PTC. Whether the PTC's directors are personally
  liable is governed by separate corporate-law and fiduciary-duty
  rules, not Article 30 directly; but in practice the trust's
  beneficiaries often look for routes to reach the directors
  personally.
