# Article 12 — Trusts for Non-Charitable Purposes

Article 12 of the **[Trusts (Jersey) Law 1984][tjl]** is the
gateway for **non-charitable purpose trusts** (NCPTs). Jersey was an
early-mover in offshore jurisdictions in permitting NCPTs, and
Article 12 is the legal basis. The provision permits a Jersey trust
to exist for a non-charitable purpose without any beneficiaries,
provided the deed appoints an **enforcer** to enforce the purpose.

## What Article 12 says

A trust for non-charitable purposes is **valid** if it provides for
the appointment of an **enforcer** in relation to its non-charitable
purposes.

(See the consolidated text at [jerseylaw.je][tjl].)

The Article works alongside:

- **Article 2(b)** — a trust may exist for purposes that are not for
  the benefit only of the trustee;
- **Article 11(b)** — a non-charitable purpose trust is invalid if it
  lacks a beneficiary or qualifying enforcer;
- **Article 13** — the office of enforcer is established;
- **Article 14** — resignation and removal of the enforcer.

## Why NCPTs matter

NCPTs are a workhorse vehicle in offshore-finance practice. Common
uses:

### 1. Orphan structures in commercial transactions

A purpose trust whose purpose is to hold the shares of a special-
purpose vehicle is the standard "orphan" structure in:

- securitisations (the SPV's shares are held by a charitable or
  non-charitable purpose trust so the SPV is not consolidated into
  any party's accounts);
- aviation and shipping finance (an asset-owning SPV is "orphaned"
  to hold the aircraft or ship under a leasing structure);
- structured finance generally, where neither the originator nor the
  investor wants the SPV on their balance sheet.

### 2. Holding a private trust company (PTC)

A Jersey PTC's shares are commonly held by a purpose trust whose
purpose is to facilitate the trusteeship of the family's trusts. This
removes the PTC shares from the family's personal estate while
allowing the PTC to act as trustee.

### 3. Family-governance purpose trusts

Some family-office structures use NCPTs to hold long-term assets for
**purposes** rather than for beneficiaries — for example, to maintain
a family heritage asset or to support specific philanthropic
objectives that don't qualify as charitable.

### 4. STAR-like applications

Jersey NCPTs are functionally similar to (though not identical with)
the Cayman STAR trust regime. Where a structure is being chosen
between Jersey NCPT and Cayman STAR, the differences turn on:

- the **identity and qualifications of the enforcer** (Jersey requires
  an enforcer; STAR requires an enforcer too but the regimes differ
  on who can act);
- **redomiciliation** options;
- **regulatory and tax treatment** of the underlying entities.

## Enforcer — the key feature

An NCPT must have an **enforcer** (Article 13). Without an enforcer
the trust fails Article 11 validity. The enforcer's role is to
enforce the trust in relation to its non-charitable purposes, in the
absence of beneficiaries with standing to bring an enforcement claim.

- The enforcer **cannot simultaneously serve as trustee** of the same
  trust ([Article 13][tjl]).
- The enforcer **may resign or be removed** under [Article 14][tjl].
- The Royal Court has supervisory jurisdiction over the enforcer's
  performance of the function.

For detail see [`enforcers.md`](enforcers.md).

## Permissible purposes

The Trusts Law does not exhaustively list permissible non-charitable
purposes, but the case law and practice have settled on certain
requirements:

- the purpose must be **sufficiently certain** to be enforceable —
  vague "for the benefit of the world" purposes fail;
- the purpose must be **lawful** — not contrary to Jersey criminal
  law, AML/CFT obligations, or public policy;
- the purpose must be **specific** enough that the enforcer can
  meaningfully police adherence.

Purposes that satisfy these tests include:

- "to hold the shares of [a named SPV] for the purposes of [a defined
  transaction]";
- "to facilitate the orderly succession of [a named family business]"
  (provided the deed defines this with adequate specificity);
- "to maintain and exhibit [a specified heritage asset]".

## Duration

Like other Jersey trusts, an NCPT may continue **for an unlimited
period** — see [Article 15](duration.md). There is no rule against
perpetuities for trusts created after the 2006 amendments.

## Failure / cy-près-style redirection

If the purpose of an NCPT becomes impossible or impracticable, the
Royal Court has power under [Article 47A](court-purpose-trust-redirection.md)
 to redirect the property to alternative purposes
consistent with the settlor's intent.

## Pitfalls

- **No enforcer = invalid.** A deed that purports to create an NCPT
  but omits the enforcer fails Article 11. The trustee may apply
  to the Royal Court for directions and the Court may appoint an
  enforcer; this should be cleaned up in drafting.
- **Trustee-as-enforcer.** Article 13 forbids the same person from
  being trustee and enforcer of the same NCPT. A common mistake in
  group-structuring is to nominate the trustee's affiliated entity
  as enforcer; the rule against trustee-as-enforcer should be
  observed substantively, not just formally.
- **Foreign tax characterisation of orphan structures.** An "orphan"
  Jersey purpose trust is often treated under foreign-tax rules in
  a way that depends on detailed structure — e.g., the US "check the
  box" regime for foreign entities, UK rules on company residence
  and substance, etc. Foreign tax modelling is essential.
- **Charity-or-purpose label.** A trust that is in substance
  charitable should be drafted as a charitable trust (with the
  benefits of the Charities (Jersey) Law 2014 framework) rather than
  shoe-horned into Article 12.

## Comparison with foundations

A Jersey **foundation** under the
[Foundations (Jersey) Law 2009](../foundations/foundations-law-2009.md)
also accommodates purpose-only objects without beneficiaries. The
choice between an NCPT and a foundation in a given case typically
turns on:

- whether the client wants a **body corporate** (foundation) or a
  **trust** (NCPT) — foundations are corporate;
- the desired **governance shape** — foundations have councils,
  guardians and qualified members; NCPTs have trustees and an
  enforcer;
- **registration and publicity** — foundations are registered with a
  public charter; NCPTs are private documents;
- **tax characterisation** — different home jurisdictions may treat
  foundations and trusts differently.
