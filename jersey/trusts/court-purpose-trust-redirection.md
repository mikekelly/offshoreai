# Article 47A — Court Redirection for Failed Charitable / Purpose Trusts

Article 47A of the **[Trusts (Jersey) Law 1984][tjl]** is the
**cy-près** jurisdiction in Jersey form. Where trust property is
held for **charitable or non-charitable purposes** and the purposes
have failed, become impossible, or otherwise cease to apply
appropriately, the Royal Court may **redirect** the property to
alternative purposes consistent with the settlor's original intent.

## What Article 47A says

Where trust property is held for **charitable or non-charitable
purposes** and specified circumstances apply (failure, impossibility,
ceased applicability), the Court may declare the property shall be
held for "**such other charitable or non-charitable purpose**" as
the Court considers consistent with the settlor's original
intention.

(See the consolidated text at [jerseylaw.je][tjl].)

## Cy-près — origin and concept

The doctrine of **cy-près** (Norman French "as near") is the
equity-court mechanism for **rescuing** a purpose trust whose
purpose can no longer be carried out. Rather than letting the trust
fail entirely, the Court redirects the property to **the nearest
practicable alternative purpose** consistent with the settlor's
intent.

Article 47A codifies this for Jersey, applying both to **charitable**
purpose trusts (the traditional cy-près domain) and to
**non-charitable** purpose trusts under [Article 12](purpose-trusts.md)
(a Jersey-specific extension).

## When the jurisdiction is engaged

The Court may redirect where the original purpose:

- has **failed** — e.g., the purpose has been fully achieved and
  funds remain; or the beneficiary or purpose class has ceased to
  exist;
- has **become impossible** — e.g., the named institution to be
  supported has been dissolved;
- has **become unsuitable** — e.g., the original purpose is now
  obsolete (a trust to maintain a museum that has since merged
  with another institution under a new name);
- has **lost its rationale** — the underlying social context that
  made the purpose meaningful has changed.

## The "consistent with settlor's intention" test

The Court's task is to find a **redirected purpose** that:

- is **as close as practicable** to the original purpose;
- preserves the **general charitable or settlor intention**;
- is **practically achievable**.

The Court will consider:

- the **stated original purpose** in the deed;
- any **wider purpose** evident from the deed (e.g., a general
  charitable intent);
- the **settlor's known views** (where evidenced — letters of
  wishes, contemporaneous correspondence, family testimony);
- **practical considerations** — what redirected purposes are
  achievable with the trust's resources.

## Charitable vs non-charitable redirection

For **charitable** trusts, the cy-près redirection is well-established
and the Court has broad scope to redirect to any charitable purpose
consistent with the settlor's intent. The **Attorney General**
typically participates in the application.

For **non-charitable purpose trusts** (under
[Article 12](purpose-trusts.md)), Article 47A's extension of the
cy-près-style power is more constrained — the redirection must still
be to a purpose, but it can be non-charitable. The **enforcer** is
typically the applicant; the AG may participate where there is a
public-interest dimension.

## Application — procedure

The application is by **Representation** in the Royal Court (Samedi
Division), with:

- **trust deed and amendments**;
- **affidavit evidence** of failure / impossibility / unsuitability
  of the original purpose;
- **proposed redirected purpose** with rationale;
- **evidence of the settlor's general intent** where available;
- **expert evidence** where the proposed redirection requires
  technical assessment (e.g., charitable-status analysis under the
  Charities (Jersey) Law 2014);
- the **enforcer's position** (for non-charitable purpose trusts);
- the **Attorney General's position** (for charitable trusts).

## Relationship with Article 42

[Article 42](failure-or-lapse.md) is the **default resulting
trust** rule — when a trust fails, the property reverts to the
settlor or the settlor's personal representative. Article 47A
**displaces** Article 42 for purpose trusts where the Court is
willing to redirect: rather than reverting, the property is
redirected to an alternative purpose.

The settlor's drafted intent often **prefers redirection over
reversion** — particularly where reversion would expose the property
to forced-heirship claims in the settlor's home jurisdiction. A
well-drafted purpose trust will signal this preference in the
deed.

## Practical drafting

Modern Jersey purpose trusts often include **express failure
provisions** that pre-empt the Article 47A application:

- **alternative purpose** specified in the deed (so if the primary
  purpose fails, the alternative kicks in by deed rather than by
  Court application);
- **default charity** named in the deed for any unallocated assets;
- **specific direction** to the trustee on the manner of
  redirection if the primary purpose becomes unworkable.

Article 47A then operates only where the deed's own mechanism has
failed or is inadequate.

## Pitfalls

- **Inadequate evidence of settlor intent.** Where the proposed
  redirection is a stretch from the original purpose, the Court
  will need solid evidence of the wider settlor intent.
- **No enforcer for non-charitable purpose trusts.** A purpose
  trust without a valid enforcer is failing for that reason —
  the Court will likely appoint an enforcer before considering
  redirection.
- **Reversion preference.** In rare cases the deed may direct
  that on failure the property revert to the settlor — Article 47A
  redirection is then unavailable.
