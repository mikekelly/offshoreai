# Article 52 — Execution of Instruments by Order of the Court

Article 52 of the **[Trusts (Jersey) Law 1984][tjl]** is the
**substitute-execution** provision. Where a person ordered by the
Court to execute a document **refuses or fails** to do so, the Court
can direct that the document be executed by **someone else** the
Court nominates.

## What Article 52 says

When someone fails to comply with a Court order to execute documents,
"**the court may... order that the conveyance, assignment, or other
document... shall be executed... by such person as the court
nominates**".

(See the consolidated text at [jerseylaw.je][tjl].)

## Why this matters

A Court order requiring a person to execute a trust-related document
— typically a vesting deed, transfer instrument, or deed of
appointment — is sometimes ignored by a recalcitrant trustee or
former trustee. Article 52 lets the Court **bypass** the
recalcitrance: the Court appoints someone (often the **Master of the
Royal Court** or the **Viscount of Jersey**) to execute the document
on behalf of the defaulting person.

Common scenarios:

- a **resigning trustee** refuses to execute the vesting deed for
  property transfer to the successor;
- a **removed trustee** refuses to sign off final accounts;
- a **party in a beneficiary dispute** refuses to execute a settlement
  instrument the Court has approved;
- a **uncooperative consent-holder** under the deed refuses to give
  consent that the Court has ruled should be given.

## Practical effect

The document executed under an Article 52 order is **effective as
if** the defaulting party had executed it themselves. Third parties
(banks, registries, counterparties) can rely on the Court-ordered
execution.

Article 52 is the **practical complement** to the Court's substantive
jurisdiction under [Article 51](article-51-directions.md): the
Court can direct a result, and Article 52 ensures the result can be
implemented even if the defaulting party will not co-operate.

## Pitfalls

- **Notice and opportunity to comply.** Article 52 follows on a
  Court order; the defaulting party must have had notice and the
  opportunity to comply. Skipping straight to substitute execution
  is procedurally suspect.
- **Foreign-element documents.** A Jersey Court order requiring
  execution of a document under foreign law may not be honoured by
  foreign registries — Article 52 cures the Jersey-side defect but
  not the foreign-side.
