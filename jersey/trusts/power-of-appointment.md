# Article 39 — Power of Appointment

Article 39 of the **[Trusts (Jersey) Law 1984][tjl]** is the
**power-of-appointment** provision. The trust terms may give the
trustee — or another office-holder — power to **appoint or assign**
trust property or any interest in trust property to a beneficiary or
potential beneficiary.

## What Article 39 says

The trust terms may authorise a **trustee or other person** to
"**appoint or assign all or any part of the trust property or any
interest in the trust property**" to:

- any **beneficiary**; or
- any **potential beneficiary**.

(See the consolidated text at [jerseylaw.je][tjl].)

## Why this matters

A **power of appointment** is the operational core of a **discretionary
trust**. The trustee starts with the broad class of beneficiaries and
exercises discretion to direct property to specific class members at
specific times in specific amounts. Without a power of appointment,
a "discretionary" trust cannot meaningfully operate.

## Forms of power of appointment

### Special vs general

- A **special power of appointment** is exercisable only in favour
  of a defined class (the beneficiaries).
- A **general power of appointment** is exercisable in favour of
  anyone, including the power-holder. General powers are rare in
  modern offshore practice because they are typically treated by
  tax authorities as quasi-ownership of the trust property.
- A **hybrid power** (intermediate) is exercisable in favour of
  anyone *except* a defined excluded class (typically the
  power-holder and their estate).

### Held by trustee vs other office-holder

- **Trustee-held powers** are typically fiduciary, exercised by the
  trustee under the trust deed.
- **Settlor-held powers** are reserved powers under
  [Article 9A](reserved-powers.md); they may be fiduciary or
  personal depending on the deed.
- **Protector-held powers** are typically fiduciary.

### Special-purpose appointment powers

Modern deeds often include specific appointment powers:

- **power to appoint capital** in favour of a stated beneficiary;
- **power to appoint to a sub-trust** (creating a new trust within
  the existing trust);
- **power to appoint on terms** (creating a new beneficial interest
  with specified conditions);
- **power to add or exclude beneficiaries** (which is technically a
  power affecting the class rather than a power of appointment in
  the narrow sense, but often grouped together in deeds).

## Fiduciary nature and proper-purpose constraints

Where the power is held by a fiduciary, the standard fiduciary
constraints apply:

- exercise in **good faith**;
- for the **proper purpose** for which the power was conferred —
  not for a collateral purpose;
- consistent with the **fiduciary duties** owed (including
  impartiality under [Article 23](trustee-impartiality.md));
- **without conflict of interest**.

A misuse of a power of appointment — for example, the power-holder
appointing property to themselves under a hybrid power, or appointing
under collusion with a beneficiary to defeat another beneficiary's
interest — is liable to be **set aside** under
[Articles 47G/H](article-47-set-aside.md) or under the broader
equitable doctrine of fraud on a power.

## Exercise — formalities

The deed will normally specify:

- **who exercises** the power (trustee, protector, settlor);
- **the form** — typically a written deed of appointment;
- **any consents** required (e.g., protector consent to a
  trustee's appointment of capital);
- **the timing** — when the appointment takes effect (immediate, on
  a future event, revocably);
- **the revocability** — whether the appointment can be revoked
  before vesting.

A defectively-exercised power may be **void** (no effect) or
**voidable** (set aside on application). The distinction matters for
third parties and for the "innocent" beneficiary who received under
the impugned appointment.

## Cross-reference to Article 47G/H

Where an appointment is exercised under **mistake**, the modern
remedy is **set-aside under Articles 47G/47H**. The Royal Court
applies the three-limb mistake test from *S & T Trusts*; see
[`article-47-set-aside.md`](article-47-set-aside.md).

## Tax consequences of appointment

Exercising a power of appointment can trigger tax events:

- **UK** — appointment from a relevant-property trust may trigger
  an IHT exit charge; appointment to a UK-resident beneficiary
  brings the beneficiary into the income-and-CGT net;
- **US** — appointment to a US person engages US-tax-reporting
  consequences (FBAR, Form 3520) and may produce throwback-tax
  treatment;
- **Other** — civil-law jurisdictions may treat the appointment as a
  gift or as a deemed distribution.

The trustee should pre-model the tax position before exercising the
power.

## Pitfalls

- **Exceeding the scope.** A power "to appoint to the children of X"
  cannot be exercised in favour of X's grandchildren without express
  authority. Read the scope carefully.
- **Improper-purpose exercise.** An appointment for an extraneous
  purpose (e.g., to put assets beyond a creditor's reach) is liable
  to attack. Document the proper purpose.
- **Tax-trigger appointments.** Appointing without pre-modelling
  tax consequences is a frequent source of subsequent Article 47
  set-aside applications. Model first.
- **Revocability silence.** A deed silent on revocability of
  appointments may leave the trustee unable to undo a mistaken
  appointment without applying to the Royal Court.
