# Article 18 — Prohibition of Renunciation After Acceptance

Article 18 of the **[Trusts (Jersey) Law 1984][tjl]** governs the
**acceptance** of trusteeship and forbids a trustee from
**renouncing** the office once it has been accepted.

## What Article 18 says

- **No-one is bound to accept** the office of trustee.
- A person who **performs acts consistent with trusteeship** is
  **deemed to have accepted** the office — acceptance can be by
  conduct as well as by express assent.
- A nominated trustee who has **not yet accepted** may **disclaim**
  by **written notice** to the settlor or to existing trustees,
  within a reasonable time of becoming aware of the nomination.
- The Royal Court may grant **relief from an unwanted appointment**
  where the settlor is unavailable and there are no other trustees
  to receive a disclaimer.

(See the consolidated text at [jerseylaw.je][tjl].)

## Why this rule matters

The rule serves the **certainty** of trust administration. Once a
person has accepted the office and started acting as trustee, they
hold the trust property and owe duties to the beneficiaries; allowing
them to renounce mid-stream would create gaps in administration and
expose the trust to loss.

The line between **acceptance** and **declination** is therefore
significant — and it can be crossed by conduct, not just by express
statement.

## What counts as "acts consistent with trusteeship"

The Royal Court takes a contextual view. Examples of acts that have
been treated as acceptance by conduct:

- **opening a bank account** in the name of the trustee for the
  trust;
- **executing investment instructions** on trust property;
- **receiving distributions of income** as trustee;
- **signing trustee minutes** approving past or planned acts;
- **liaising with beneficiaries** in the capacity of trustee.

Acts that do **not** typically constitute acceptance:

- preliminary discussions about whether to accept;
- reviewing the trust deed for the purpose of deciding whether to
  accept;
- attending a meeting to receive information about the trust before
  deciding.

## Disclaim by written notice — timing

A nominated trustee who decides not to accept must disclaim **within
a reasonable time** of becoming aware of the nomination. The reason
is to avoid administrative limbo. The written notice should be
delivered to:

- the **settlor** (if alive and competent); or
- the **existing trustees** (if any).

A nominated trustee who has not yet been formally appointed but has
begun to act is treated as having accepted under the conduct rule —
disclaim before acting.

## Court relief from unwanted appointment

Where a person has been nominated as trustee but the settlor is
unavailable (deceased, lost capacity, untraceable) and there are no
existing trustees to receive the notice, the Royal Court can be
asked to grant relief from the unwanted appointment. The application
is by Representation under [Article 51](article-51-directions.md)
and is friendly proceedings.

## Once accepted — Article 19 governs exit

A trustee who has accepted may only **resign** or be **removed** in
accordance with [Article 19](trustee-resignation-and-removal.md).
The "renunciation after acceptance" prohibition in Article 18 means
that an accepted trustee cannot simply walk away; they must follow the
resignation procedure, which preserves continuity.

## Pitfalls

- **Inadvertent acceptance.** A person nominated as trustee who is
  considering whether to accept should be careful to take no act in
  a "trustee" capacity until the decision is made.
- **Trust-company onboarding.** A trust-company business proposed as
  successor trustee should not deal with the trust property — even
  for safekeeping purposes — before the formal acceptance step is
  complete.
- **Drafting nomination clauses.** Settlors should consider whether
  to allow nominations to lapse if not accepted within a stated
  period (e.g., 30 days) — this provides a clean default without
  needing Article 18 disclaim mechanics.
