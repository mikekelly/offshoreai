# Article 9 — The Jersey Firewall

**Article 9** of the **[Trusts (Jersey) Law 1984][tjl]** is the
**firewall**. It is the single most important provision in the Trusts
Law for cross-border wealth-planning purposes. The firewall instructs
the Royal Court to apply **Jersey law** — and only Jersey law — to
questions about the validity, capacity-to-settle, administration and
beneficial-rights aspects of a Jersey trust, and to refuse to give
effect to foreign rules (notably forced-heirship and matrimonial-
regime rules) that would otherwise constrain the settlor's freedom to
settle.

Article 9's current form derives from the **Trusts (Amendment No. 4)
(Jersey) Law 2006**, which strengthened the firewall by extending it
to foreign judgments inconsistent with the Article and by clarifying
the position on capacity.

## What Article 9 says

The Article is structured as a series of paragraphs each doing a
specific firewall job:

### Paragraph (1) — the headline rule

Any question concerning:

- the **validity** of the trust (and of any transfer or other
  disposition of property to it),
- its **interpretation**,
- the **capacity of the settlor** (in relation to the trust),
- the **administration** of the trust,
- the **powers** of trustees and others,
- **foreign-court exercises of variation powers**, and
- the **beneficial rights** under the trust,

> shall be determined in accordance with the law of Jersey and no
> rule of foreign law shall affect such question.

— Article 9(1), Trusts (Jersey) Law 1984 (per the consolidated text at
[jerseylaw.je][tjl])

### Paragraph (2) — disregard of foreign non-trust regimes

The Article 9 determination proceeds **without consideration of
whether or not any foreign law prohibits or does not recognise the
concept of a trust**, and **without consideration of whether the trust
avoids or defeats rights, claims, or interests conferred by any
foreign law**. ([Article 9(2)][tjl])

This is the heart of the firewall: even where a foreign jurisdiction
treats the settlor's disposition into a Jersey trust as ineffective
(because the jurisdiction does not recognise trusts) or as a fraud on
forced-heirship rules, Jersey law as applied by the Royal Court will
not give effect to that foreign view.

### Paragraph (2A) — savings for ownership, formalities and
testamentary law

The firewall does **not**:

- validate a disposition where the settlor had no ownership or no
  power to dispose;
- affect Jersey's recognition of other jurisdictions' laws regarding
  ownership or formalities of property transfer; or
- validate testamentary dispositions that are invalid under the
  testator's domicile law.

([Article 9(2A)][tjl])

This paragraph addresses the standard concerns that critics of trust
firewalls raise — Article 9 cannot turn theft into title, cannot
override the local-law formalities for transferring real estate, and
cannot cure an invalid will.

### Paragraph (3) — *légitime* and Jersey domicile

Jersey's own *légitime* rules (forced-heirship under Jersey customary
law) apply only if the settlor is domiciled in Jersey. ([Article
9(3)][tjl])

### Paragraph (3A) — Jersey conflict of laws excluded

Jersey conflict-of-laws rules (other than Article 9 itself) do **not**
apply to the questions captured by Article 9. ([Article 9(3A)][tjl])

This is a technical but important provision: it prevents an argument
that Jersey *renvoi* sends the question back to the foreign law.
Article 9 is a self-contained choice-of-law rule.

### Paragraphs (4)–(7) — foreign judgments, *donner et retenir ne
vaut*, and definitions

- **Foreign judgments inconsistent with Article 9 are unenforceable
  in Jersey**. ([Article 9(4)][tjl]) This is the strong-form
  firewall: even if a foreign court has ruled that a transfer into
  the Jersey trust is invalid, that judgment cannot be enforced in
  Jersey if it conflicts with the Article 9 rules.
- **The Jersey customary rule against *donner et retenir ne vaut*
  does not apply.** This is the rule that one cannot validly give
  away property while still retaining control of it — historically a
  problem for reserved-powers planning. Disapplying it within the
  trust context complements Article 9A.
- **Definitions** of "foreign", "heirship rights", "*légitime*", and
  "personal relationship" are provided. ([Article 9(7)][tjl])

## How the firewall is used in practice

The firewall is the structural answer to a series of standard
cross-border attacks on a Jersey trust:

1. **Forced heirship in civil-law jurisdictions.** A settlor with
   children resident in (e.g.) France or Italy may face local-law
   *réserve héréditaire* rules that reserve a fixed share of the
   estate to certain heirs. A transfer into a Jersey trust during
   the settlor's lifetime may be attacked by the heirs after the
   settlor's death as a *fraudulent disposition* that should be
   notionally returned to the estate for the calculation of the
   reserve. Article 9 refuses to give effect to these rules in
   Jersey.
2. **Matrimonial regimes.** Community-property regimes, where the
   non-settlor spouse has a co-ownership interest in matrimonial
   property under the local law, can be the basis for an attack on
   a transfer into a Jersey trust. Again, Article 9 refuses to
   apply the foreign matrimonial regime.
3. **Foreign divorce orders against trust assets.** Where a foreign
   divorce court orders the transfer of trust assets as part of a
   divorce settlement, Article 9(4) renders the order unenforceable
   in Jersey to the extent inconsistent with Article 9.
4. **Foreign creditor claims** founded on local-law clawback or
   anti-avoidance regimes (e.g., German *Pflichtteilsergänzung*).
   Article 9 again applies.

## Limits

Despite the firewall, a Jersey trust is not immune in cross-border
practice. Common limits:

- **Personal jurisdiction over the trustee or beneficiary in the
  foreign forum.** A foreign court may have jurisdiction *in personam*
  over a beneficiary who is resident there, and may order that
  beneficiary to take steps in relation to trust property. Article 9
  does not bind the foreign court; it binds the Royal Court of Jersey
  and Jersey-located assets. See [Crociani — sketch](use-cases/trust-officer/index.md)
  on the limits of Jersey jurisdiction clauses.
- **Asset location.** Trust assets located in the foreign jurisdiction
  may be reached by the foreign court directly — the firewall cannot
  protect what is not in Jersey or held by a Jersey-located
  intermediary.
- **Domicile of the settlor in Jersey.** Article 9(3) preserves Jersey
  *légitime* where the settlor is Jersey-domiciled. A Jersey-domiciled
  settlor cannot escape Jersey forced-heirship rules merely by
  settling assets into a Jersey trust.
- **Capacity at the personal-law level.** Article 9 does not cure a
  settlor's lack of capacity under their personal law to dispose of
  property; capacity for the purposes of the trust is governed by
  Jersey law under Article 9(1), but the settlor's underlying right
  to dispose may still be impaired by their personal-law capacity
  rules.

## Interaction with Article 9A (reserved powers)

Article 9A complements the firewall by stating that the reservation
by the settlor of a wide menu of powers (revocation, advancement,
direction of trustees, etc.) does not invalidate the trust. Without
Article 9A, the *donner et retenir ne vaut* / *substance over form*
arguments against reserved-powers trusts would gain traction; with
it, a Jersey reserved-powers trust is structurally robust. See
[`reserved-powers.md`](reserved-powers.md).

## Practical drafting

- The trust deed should make an **express choice of Jersey law** as
  the proper law (engaging Article 4), so that the firewall under
  Article 9 is engaged.
- The deed should not include provisions that effectively re-import
  foreign rules — e.g., a clause requiring the trustee to make
  distributions in accordance with the laws of succession of the
  settlor's home country. Such a clause can undercut the firewall
  by the back door.
- Where the family is exposed to a forced-heirship jurisdiction,
  consider parallel **substantive lifetime planning** in that
  jurisdiction (e.g., notarial *pacte de famille*) rather than
  relying on the firewall alone.

## Pitfalls

- **Foreign judgments may be enforced through other gateways.**
  Article 9(4) renders foreign judgments inconsistent with Article 9
  unenforceable in Jersey — but a foreign judgment may still be
  enforced in the foreign jurisdiction against foreign-located assets
  or against beneficiaries in personam.
- **Firewall is not a bar to ordinary breach-of-trust claims.**
  Where the dispute is between trustee and beneficiary on ordinary
  trust-law grounds (breach of duty, accounting, removal), Article 9
  does not protect the trustee — it protects the trust against
  *foreign-law* attack, not against *Jersey-law* claims.
- **Sham analysis.** A trust that fails the [Article 2](existence-of-a-trust.md)
  existence test (because the settlor in substance retained
  beneficial ownership) is not protected by Article 9 — a sham is
  not a trust at all and the firewall does not engage.
- **Public policy.** Jersey public policy can in principle override
  Article 9 in egregious cases (e.g., trust for criminal purposes);
  this is rarely invoked but is the residual safety valve.
