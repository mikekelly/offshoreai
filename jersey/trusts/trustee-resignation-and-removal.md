# Article 19 — Resignation or Removal of Trustee

Article 19 of the **[Trusts (Jersey) Law 1984][tjl]** is the **exit**
provision: it governs how a trustee leaves office. Three routes are
recognised — **resignation**, **removal**, and **termination by trust
terms**.

## What Article 19 says

### Resignation

- A trustee who is **not the sole trustee** may resign by giving
  **written notice** to **co-trustees**. The resignation takes
  effect on delivery.
- **Simultaneous resignations** that would leave the trust with no
  trustee have **no effect** — the resignations are void and the
  trustees remain in office.
- A **sole trustee cannot resign** if doing so would leave the trust
  with no trustee.

### Cessation by other routes

A trustee also ceases to hold office where:

- the **Court removes** the trustee (typically under [Article 51](article-51-directions.md));
- a **resignation** under the deed's terms takes effect;
- the **trust terms** otherwise specify (e.g., automatic
  termination on the trustee ceasing to be Jersey-resident).

### Co-operation on succession

A trustee who ceases to hold office must **co-operate** in vesting
trust property in the successor trustees and in completing the
administrative transition.

(See the consolidated text at [jerseylaw.je][tjl].)

## Practical exit procedures

A clean trustee exit involves more than the Article 19 mechanics:

1. **Written notice** to co-trustees (and to any other named
   recipients in the deed — settlor, protector).
2. **Identification of the successor** trustee, where the deed
   provides for replacement.
3. **Vesting deed or transfer documentation** for assets that
   require positive transfer (e.g., foreign-registered shares, real
   estate). Jersey trust property vests automatically by operation
   of [Article 17](trustee-appointment-out-of-court.md), but
   foreign-located property requires local steps.
4. **Indemnity and release.** A retiring trustee will typically
   require an indemnity from the trust fund and a release from the
   continuing trustees / beneficiaries for past acts. The deed often
   provides the framework; the Royal Court has historically been
   slow to require beneficiaries to grant a release without good
   reason.
5. **Accounts and handover.** Final trustee accounts; transfer of
   records, minutes, correspondence; AML/CFT records (these must be
   retained for the statutory period regardless of trusteeship
   change).
6. **Regulatory notifications.** A Jersey-regulated trust company
   business changing its role as trustee may have JFSC reporting
   obligations under the [TCB Code of Practice](../financial-regulation/codes-of-practice.md)
   *(stub)*.

## Removal

The trust deed will typically provide for **removal** of a trustee
by the settlor (during lifetime), by a protector, or by a stated
majority of beneficiaries. Where the deed is silent or the named
mechanism cannot be invoked, the Royal Court has power under
[Article 51](article-51-directions.md) to remove a trustee for
cause. Recognised grounds include:

- breach of trust;
- conflict of interest;
- incapacity;
- breakdown in working relationship with co-trustees or
  beneficiaries that materially impairs administration;
- prolonged failure to act.

Removal is the equitable jurisdiction's most-used response to a
trustee that the Court considers cannot continue. It is distinct
from a breach-of-trust claim, which seeks compensation rather than
removal.

## Sole-trustee resignation problem

A **sole trustee** cannot resign without arranging a successor. The
sole trustee must therefore:

- procure a replacement trustee under the deed's appointment power
  (or under [Article 17](trustee-appointment-out-of-court.md));
- transfer property to the new trustee;
- only then resign.

If a replacement cannot be procured, the sole trustee must apply to
the Royal Court for directions and (potentially) Court appointment
of a successor.

## The outgoing trustee's continuing position

[Article 34](outgoing-trustee.md) *(planned)* deals specifically
with the **position of an outgoing trustee** — including continuing
liability for acts during trusteeship, the indemnity from the trust
fund, and the trustee's right to documents and records for the
defence of future claims. Article 19 governs *how* the trustee exits;
Article 34 governs the *legal status* of an exited trustee.

## Pitfalls

- **Simultaneous resignation trap.** A board of two trustees who
  both purport to resign at the same time, without identifying a
  successor, achieves nothing — the resignations are void by
  Article 19. The trustees remain in office and remain liable.
- **Pre-mature transfer.** Transferring trust property to a
  successor before the deed's notice / approval mechanism has been
  completed can produce vesting and authority gaps.
- **Inadequate indemnity / release.** A retiring trustee that
  accepts a thin indemnity from the trust fund may face personal
  exposure if substantial future claims emerge for acts during
  trusteeship.
- **Foreign-property transfer.** Don't assume automatic vesting
  works for property registered in a foreign jurisdiction; check
  the local-law requirements for changing registered title.
