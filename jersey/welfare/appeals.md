# Appeals Against Welfare Decisions

## The three stages

If you disagree with a CLS decision (Income Support
refusal, benefit calculation, work-expectation sanction,
overpayment claim, etc.), the appeal route is:

1. **Internal review** by CLS — a senior officer
   re-examines the decision;
2. **Social Security Tribunal** — an independent tribunal
   established by statute;
3. **Royal Court** — on points of law only.

Each stage has **strict time limits**.

## Stage 1 — Internal review

The first step. Procedure:

- **Written request** for review, stating your reasons;
- **Within prescribed time** (typically 28 days of the
  original decision);
- **Senior officer** at CLS re-examines;
- **Outcome** — original decision confirmed, varied, or
  reversed.

The review is **not** independent of CLS — it's an internal
check on the original officer's decision. It is the
necessary precursor to a tribunal appeal.

## Stage 2 — Social Security Tribunal

If the internal review does not resolve, you can appeal
to the **Social Security Tribunal**:

- **Independent** statutory body;
- **Composed of** a legally-qualified chair plus members
  with relevant experience;
- **Hears** appeals on both income support and
  contributory-scheme decisions;
- **Powers** — confirm, vary, or reverse the decision;
- **Free** to the appellant.

Procedure:

- **Notice of appeal** within time limit (typically 28
  days of the internal review);
- **Statement of case** by the appellant;
- **CLS response**;
- **Documentary evidence**;
- **Oral hearing** in most cases (sometimes paper-only by
  agreement);
- **Decision** with reasons.

The Tribunal is informal — claimants commonly represent
themselves, though specialist advocates or Citizens
Advice representatives can attend. Legal representation
is not usually publicly funded for welfare appeals.

## Stage 3 — Royal Court

Limited to **points of law**:

- **Misapplication** of the statute or regulations;
- **Procedural unfairness**;
- **Irrationality** in the public-law sense;
- **Insufficient reasons**;
- **Exceeding jurisdiction**.

The Royal Court is **not** a re-hearing — it tests the
legality of the Tribunal's decision.

Procedure:

- **Notice of appeal** with strict time limit;
- **Legal representation** strongly recommended;
- **Hearing** before a Royal Court judge;
- **Decision** — typically a quashing order requiring
  the Tribunal to redetermine;
- **Costs risk** in some cases.

## Common appeal grounds

- **Wrong calculation** of needs / resources;
- **Wrong application** of disregards or asset thresholds;
- **Wrong** determination of work-expectation status;
- **Sanctions** imposed without proper basis;
- **Overpayment recovery** in disputed circumstances;
- **Residence test** disputed;
- **Disability** assessment disputed.

## What to do

If you receive an adverse CLS decision:

1. **Read the decision letter carefully** — it explains
   the reasons and rights of review/appeal;
2. **Note the deadline** for review request (typically 28
   days);
3. **Gather** documentation supporting your case;
4. **Write a clear** review request explaining what you
   think is wrong and why;
5. **Engage** Citizens Advice Jersey or a specialist
   advocate if appropriate;
6. **Be specific** — generic complaints carry less
   weight than focused legal / factual challenges;
7. **Keep records** of all correspondence;
8. **Continue receiving benefit** during appeal if
   possible (an adverse decision being appealed does not
   automatically restore the prior position).

## Time limits — strict

Missing the deadline is typically fatal to the appeal:

- **Internal review**: typically 28 days from original
  decision;
- **Tribunal appeal**: typically 28 days from internal
  review outcome;
- **Royal Court**: short window from Tribunal decision.

In **exceptional circumstances** a late appeal may be
allowed, but the threshold is high. Don't rely on it.
