# Q: I've just been arrested. What are my rights at the police station?

## The short answer

Under the **Police Procedures and Criminal Evidence
(Jersey) Law 2003** (PPCE 2003) and its Codes of Practice,
your headline rights are:

| Right | Position |
|---|---|
| **Legal advice** | Free, 24/7 duty solicitor or own choice; can be delayed only in very serious cases |
| **Inform someone of your arrest** | Yes; one person; police phone at public expense |
| **Inform consulate** | If a foreign national |
| **Right to silence** | Yes; caution: "You are not obliged to say anything..." |
| **Custody record** | Notice of rights in writing; copy on release |
| **Interpreter** | If you don't speak English well enough |
| **Appropriate adult** | If juvenile or vulnerable |
| **Medical attention** | If needed |
| **Detention limit** | **24 hours** without charge; extendable to 36 hours by inspector for serious offences; up to 96 hours by Royal Court warrant |
| **Rest periods** | At least 8 continuous hours in any 24-hour period |

**Most important practical advice**: **Ask for a solicitor
immediately** and **don't answer questions** until you've
spoken to one.

## The proper answer

### 1. Right to free legal advice — exercise it

You have the **absolute right** to consult a solicitor or
advocate:

- **Free of charge** through the duty-solicitor scheme;
- **Available 24/7**;
- **In private** — privileged communications;
- **Before any interview** — interviews should not begin
  until you have had the opportunity for advice.

This right can be **delayed** only on specific statutory
grounds in **very serious cases** (terrorism,
evidence-preservation in defined high-threshold offences),
authorised by a senior officer. Routine delay is not
permitted.

If you change your mind about wanting legal advice mid-
interview, **questioning must stop** and you must be
offered access to a solicitor.

**Don't decline this right** to look cooperative — it's
the single most important safeguard you have.

### 2. Right to inform someone

You can have one person told of your arrest and your
location:

- **Family member, friend, or other**;
- **Police make the call** at public expense;
- **As soon as practicable**.

This can be delayed in serious cases (specific evidence-
preservation grounds), authorised at inspector level.

### 3. Right to silence — and the caution

When you are arrested, and again at the start of any
interview, you will be cautioned:

> "You are not obliged to say anything unless you wish to
> do so, but what you say may be put into writing and
> given in evidence."

You can:

- **Say nothing**;
- **Answer some questions** and not others;
- **Give a prepared statement** through your solicitor
  and decline questions;
- **Speak freely** (typically inadvisable without legal
  advice).

**Adverse inferences** from silence at police station
interview are narrower in Jersey than in some UK
contexts.

### 4. Detention time limits

| Stage | Limit | Authorised by |
|---|---|---|
| Initial | **24 hours** | Custody officer |
| Extension (serious offences) | **36 hours** | Police inspector |
| Warrant of further detention | up to **96 hours total** | Royal Court (Magistrate) |
| Beyond 96 hours | Must charge or release |

You must be **brought before a court** (or released)
within these limits. Detention beyond is unlawful.

The custody officer must **review** your detention at
prescribed intervals (typically every 6 hours initially)
to confirm grounds still exist.

### 5. Interview procedure

If interviewed:

- **Recording** — Code E requires open audio recording;
- **Caution** at the start of each session;
- **Solicitor present** if you've requested;
- **No oppression** — raising voice, intimidation,
  prolonged questioning all prohibited;
- **Breaks** at appropriate intervals;
- **Fitness** — police must not interview you if
  intoxicated, unwell, or unable to follow questions;
- **Language** — interpreter required if needed.

### 6. Custody record

The **custody record** is the comprehensive log of every
event during your detention. You can:

- **Ask to see it** during detention;
- **Receive a copy** on release.

Keep it carefully — it's critical evidence for any
subsequent challenge to procedural fairness.

### 7. Search at the station

Police may search you and your property:

- **Personal search** for items facilitating escape or
  relevant evidence;
- **Strip search** only by same-sex officer in private,
  with proper recording;
- **Intimate samples** — specific consent / authorisation;
- **Non-intimate samples** (fingerprints, mouth swabs) —
  in defined circumstances.

### 8. After interview / detention

Possible outcomes:

- **Released without charge** (no further action, or
  released pending further inquiry);
- **Released on bail** to return on a specified date;
- **Charged** and bailed to court;
- **Charged** and detained until court (overnight
  typically, longer in serious cases).

If charged, you'll appear at the **Magistrate's Court**
or **Royal Court** depending on the offence.

## Practical advice

### Before anything else

- **Stay calm** — agitated reactions can complicate
  things;
- **Identify yourself** truthfully — refusal to give
  your name is itself an offence in many contexts.

### Once at the station

1. **Ask for a solicitor immediately** — by name if you
   have one, or duty solicitor;
2. **Use your phone call** to inform someone;
3. **Read the notice of rights** carefully;
4. **Don't answer substantive questions** until you've
   spoken to a solicitor;
5. **Cooperate** with procedural matters (photographing,
   fingerprinting, custody booking);
6. **Note** times, names, and conversations as best you
   can.

### During interview

1. **Insist** on having your solicitor present;
2. **Decline to answer** if your solicitor advises (a
   "no comment" interview is a legitimate option);
3. **Take breaks** if you need them;
4. **Don't sign** anything without legal advice;
5. **Listen for** the caution at each session.

### On release

1. **Get a copy of the custody record**;
2. **Note any concerns** about procedural fairness while
   fresh;
3. **Follow up with your solicitor** on any next steps;
4. **Comply scrupulously** with any bail conditions.

## If your rights have been breached

If you believe a right was breached:

- **Tell your solicitor** at first opportunity;
- **Note the time and details**;
- **Complaint** through formal channels post-release;
- **Material exclusion** of evidence at any subsequent
  trial may be sought;
- **Civil claim** possible for serious breaches.
