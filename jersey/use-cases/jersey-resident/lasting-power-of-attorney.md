# Q: How do I set up a Lasting Power of Attorney in Jersey?

## The short answer

A **Lasting Power of Attorney** (LPA) is a legal
instrument under the **Capacity and Self-Determination
(Jersey) Law 2016** that lets you nominate trusted
people (attorneys) to make decisions on your behalf if
you lose capacity in the future.

Two types are available:

| LPA type | Covers |
|---|---|
| **Health and welfare** | Medical treatment, where you live, care arrangements, life-sustaining treatment (if expressly authorised) |
| **Property and finance** | Banking, paying bills, managing investments, property decisions, charitable gifts, daily expenses |

You can have **both**. Both must be made while you have
capacity, signed with witnesses and a capacity
certificate, and **registered at the Royal Court
Protection Division** before they can be used.

This is fundamentally different from an ordinary Power
of Attorney under the 1995 Law — that **falls away** if
you lose capacity. The LPA is designed for exactly that
moment.

## The proper answer

### Why you need an LPA

Without an LPA, if you lose capacity:

- **Bank accounts** become inaccessible to your family;
- **Bills** may go unpaid;
- **Property** cannot be managed or sold;
- **Medical decisions** are made by clinicians under the
  best-interests framework, not by your chosen people;
- **Your family** must apply to the Royal Court for a
  **delegate appointment** — slower, more expensive,
  more restrictive.

The LPA prevents all this. It's the single most important
piece of incapacity planning most people can do.

### Step 1 — Choose your attorneys

Decisions to make:

- **How many** attorneys? One, two, or more?
- **Who?** Spouse, adult children, friends, professionals?
- **How should they decide?** Jointly (unanimity
  required), severally (each can act alone), or a mix?
- **Replacement attorneys** if a primary cannot act?

Common patterns:

- **Married couple**: spouse plus adult child as backup;
- **Mixed**: family member plus solicitor for property /
  finance;
- **Multiple children**: jointly for major decisions,
  severally for routine matters.

Each attorney must:

- Be **18+** and **capacitous**;
- **Consent** to the appointment;
- **Not be bankrupt** (for property and finance LPA).

### Step 2 — Decide on scope and conditions

For each LPA, decide:

- **Specific restrictions** (e.g. "no sale of the family
  home without consultation");
- **Specific permissions** (e.g. "authorise refusal of
  life-sustaining treatment");
- **When it kicks in** — for property/finance LPA, either
  immediately on registration or only on capacity loss;
- **Notice to relatives** if required.

### Step 3 — Complete the form

Use the prescribed forms from the Royal Court Protection
Division. Key elements:

- **Donor identification**;
- **Type of LPA**;
- **Attorneys named** and roles;
- **Restrictions and instructions**;
- **Certificate of capacity** by an appropriate person
  (often the GP or a solicitor);
- **Witnesses** to all signatures.

### Step 4 — Sign

- **Donor signs** with witness;
- **Each attorney signs** with witness, indicating
  consent;
- **Certificate of capacity** completed;
- **All signatures** in the prescribed sequence.

### Step 5 — Register at the Royal Court

This is **mandatory** before the LPA can be used:

- **Submit** the LPA to the Royal Court Protection
  Division;
- **Pay the registration fee**;
- **Notice** to family members / persons-to-be-notified
  (typically a 4-week objection period);
- **Registration date** issued;
- **LPA operative** from that date.

### Step 6 — Keep it safe and inform people

- **Keep the original** safe;
- **Tell your attorneys** where it is;
- **Provide certified copies** to your bank, GP, etc., as
  needed;
- **Review periodically** (every few years) to update if
  circumstances change.

## Cost

Indicative (varies):

- **DIY**: capacity certificate cost + Court fee;
- **Solicitor-drafted**: from £500-£2,000 per LPA;
- **Both types together via solicitor**: typically
  £1,000-£3,000.

Cheap relative to the cost of a contested delegate
application later.

## Specific situations

### "I'm in my 30s — is this premature?"

No. Capacity can be lost at any age (accident, sudden
illness, mental-health crisis). The classic case for an
early-life LPA is the property-and-finance LPA enabling
a spouse to operate joint affairs if one partner is
incapacitated by serious illness.

### "I have an English LPA — does it work in Jersey?"

Not automatically. An English LPA may need a separate
process to operate over Jersey assets — typically a
**mirror order** or fresh Jersey registration.
Specialist cross-border advice essential.

### "What if I lose capacity before signing?"

Then the LPA route is closed. The alternative is a
**court-appointed delegate** — see
[`../../health-and-capacity/capacity-and-self-determination-law-2016.md`](../../health-and-capacity/capacity-and-self-determination-law-2016.md).
This is slower, more expensive, and more restrictive than
having an LPA in place.

### "I want my LPA to authorise refusal of
life-sustaining treatment"

This requires **explicit authorisation** in the LPA —
default LPAs don't extend to life-sustaining-treatment
decisions. Specific drafting needed. Consider whether
this should be in the LPA or in a separate **Advance
Decision to Refuse Treatment** (typically more
appropriate for specific medical scenarios).

### "Can I revoke an LPA?"

Yes — while you have capacity. Written revocation,
witnessed, with notice to attorneys and the Court. After
capacity loss, only the Court can revoke (typically on
safeguarding grounds).

## What to do tomorrow morning

1. **Decide** in principle whether you want one or both
   LPA types;
2. **Identify** potential attorneys and discuss with
   them;
3. **Engage** a solicitor or use the Court's
   self-help materials — for most people a solicitor is
   worth the modest fee;
4. **Talk to your GP** about the capacity certificate;
5. **Discuss your wishes** with your chosen attorneys —
   what would you want them to do?
6. **Complete the forms**;
7. **Register** at the Royal Court;
8. **Store** safely and tell attorneys where.

## Pitfalls

- **Delaying** "until you need it" — capacity loss can be
  sudden;
- **Choosing attorneys** without honest conversation
  about their willingness and ability;
- **Forgetting** the registration step — unregistered LPA
  is unusable;
- **Not including** life-sustaining-treatment authorisation
  if wanted;
- **Drafting** ambiguous restrictions that complicate
  use;
- **Failing** to provide for replacement attorneys;
- **Cross-border** assets without specialist advice.
