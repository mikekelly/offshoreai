# Filing a Suspicious Activity Report (SAR)

## The question

You have received an internal disclosure suspecting money
laundering. When and how do you escalate to the Jersey
Financial Intelligence Unit (FIU)?

## The short answer

If you (as MLRO) **suspect** that property is criminal property
or that a person is engaged in money laundering or terrorist
financing, you **must** file a **SAR** with the FIU **as soon as
practicable**. Failure to do so is an **offence**.

The SAR is filed **electronically** through the FIU's reporting
system, contains prescribed information, and may include a
**consent request** for the transaction to proceed.

## The proper answer

### When to suspect

"Suspicion" is a **low threshold** — significantly less than
"belief":

- **More than a fleeting thought** but **less than balance of
  probabilities**;
- A **reasonable basis** to suspect, given the facts known;
- Often expressed as a **"red flag"** triggering closer review.

Common triggers:

- **Unusual transaction patterns** inconsistent with the
  customer's known activity;
- **Reluctance** to provide CDD information;
- **Suspicious counterparties** (sanctions matches, negative
  news);
- **Pass-through** transactions with no apparent economic
  purpose;
- **Jurisdictional anomalies** (transactions through high-risk
  jurisdictions);
- **Source-of-funds inconsistencies**.

### Process — your role

As MLRO:

1. **Receive internal disclosure** from staff member;
2. **Investigate** — review CDD, transaction history,
   counterparties;
3. **Assess** — does the matter rise to "suspicion"?
4. **Document** your assessment and reasoning;
5. **Decide**: file SAR or close internal investigation
   (recorded with reasons);
6. If filing: prepare and **submit SAR** to the FIU;
7. **Manage** the customer relationship after SAR:
   - Continue **without tipping off** the subject;
   - Apply for **consent** for specific transactions if needed.

### SAR content

A typical SAR includes:

- **Reporting institution** identity;
- **Subject of the SAR** — customer identity and CDD details;
- **Suspicious activity** described factually;
- **Transaction details** — amounts, dates, counterparties;
- **Reasons for suspicion** — the analytical basis;
- **Documents** supporting the SAR (KYC docs, transaction
  records);
- **Consent request** if applicable (see below).

### Consent regime

If the suspicion arises **before completing** a transaction,
you may request **FIU consent** to proceed:

- **Submit the SAR** with consent request;
- **Statutory deadline** — FIU has prescribed time to respond
  (typically 7 working days);
- If FIU **consents** (or doesn't refuse in time): proceed;
- If FIU **refuses**: do not proceed; further restraint may
  follow.

The consent regime allows the customer relationship to continue
without tipping off the subject.

### Tipping off

After filing a SAR (or even before, if disclosure to the
subject would prejudice an investigation):

- **Do not disclose** to the subject that a SAR has been or
  will be filed;
- **Do not disclose** that an investigation is contemplated;
- **Tipping off** is a serious **criminal offence**.

This can create awkward customer-management situations — the
customer asks why their transaction is delayed and you cannot
honestly answer. Stock-phrase explanations and professional
handling are part of the MLRO's craft.

### After the SAR

Post-SAR:

- **Document** all subsequent customer interactions;
- **Continue ongoing monitoring** of the relationship;
- **Consider exit** of the relationship (consent for exit may
  be needed in some cases);
- **Cooperate** with any FIU or police follow-up.

## What to do next

For a current internal disclosure:

1. **Investigate** the disclosure facts.
2. **Document** your analysis.
3. **Decide** to file or close, with reasons recorded.
4. If filing: **submit SAR** through the FIU portal.
5. **Manage** the relationship post-SAR avoiding tipping off.

For programme readiness:

- **Train staff** in disclosure procedures;
- **Document** the internal-escalation pathway;
- **Maintain** the SAR-filing capability.

## Pitfalls

- **Setting the suspicion bar too high**: SARs should be filed
  on **suspicion**, not certainty.
- **Tipping off in conversation**: a careless explanation to
  the customer can be a criminal offence.
- **Not filing on internal pressure**: the MLRO must file when
  the threshold is met regardless of business pressure.
- **Lack of documentation**: undocumented decisions to **not**
  file expose the MLRO and the firm.
