# Record-Keeping Retention Periods

## The question

How long must AML / CFT and other compliance records be
retained?

## The short answer

- **CDD records**: at least **5 years from end of business
  relationship**;
- **Transaction records**: at least **5 years from transaction
  date**;
- **SAR records**: at least **5 years from filing**;
- **Training records**: at least **5 years**;
- **General compliance records**: typically **5–10 years**
  depending on Code of Practice.

Some categories (tax-related, regulatory filings) have
**longer** retention. **Litigation hold** can extend retention
beyond minima.

## The proper answer

### Statutory minima

The Money Laundering Order 2008 prescribes:

- **CDD records**: 5 years from end of business relationship;
- **Transaction records**: 5 years from transaction date;
- **Sanctions-screening records**: 5 years.

The Code of Practice may set longer:

- **Trust company records**: often **5 years from trust
  termination** (or longer);
- **Fund records**: 5–10 years post-fund termination;
- **Banking records**: 6 years typical (banking-business norm).

### Practical retention

In practice, many firms retain:

- **Customer files**: as long as the relationship plus 6 years
  post-exit;
- **Transaction records**: 6–10 years;
- **SAR and related records**: 5–7 years post-filing;
- **Training records**: 7 years;
- **Board minutes** and key governance: indefinite or 20+
  years.

### Format

Records may be kept in:

- **Electronic** form (most common modern practice);
- **Paper** form (legacy or specific categories);
- **Cloud-stored** with appropriate security;
- **Off-site backup** for resilience.

The Code of Practice expects records to be:

- **Retrievable** within reasonable time;
- **Readable** by JFSC and other authorities;
- **Authenticated** (tamper-evident if electronic);
- **Backed up** against loss.

### Specific categories

#### CDD records

- **Identification documents** (passport copies, etc.);
- **Verification evidence**;
- **Source-of-wealth narratives and supporting docs**;
- **PEP / sanctions screening results**;
- **Risk assessments and approval records**;
- **Ongoing-monitoring records**.

#### Transaction records

- **Transaction details** (date, amount, counterparty,
  purpose);
- **Supporting documentation** for material transactions;
- **Account statements**;
- **Settlement records**.

#### SAR records

- **The SAR itself**;
- **Internal-disclosure records** leading to SAR;
- **Investigation records**;
- **FIU correspondence**;
- **Consent records** if applicable.

#### Regulatory records

- **JFSC correspondence**;
- **Filing copies** (annual returns, BO filings, CRS / FATCA);
- **Audit / inspection records**;
- **Self-report submissions**.

### Litigation hold

Where litigation is pending or threatened:

- **Hold** all relevant records beyond normal retention;
- **Suspend** normal destruction schedules;
- **Identify** custodians of potentially relevant records;
- **Document** the hold.

### Destruction

When retention periods expire:

- **Destroy** in secure manner;
- **Document** the destruction;
- **Apply hold** if subject to litigation / investigation;
- **Specific category preservation** may extend beyond
  destruction date.

## What to do next

For programme:

1. **Map** retention periods for each record category;
2. **Implement** retention schedule in records system;
3. **Document** the retention policy;
4. **Apply** litigation holds when needed;
5. **Audit** compliance.

For an active situation:

- Verify retention of relevant records;
- Apply hold if litigation / investigation pending;
- Address destruction of expired records.

## Pitfalls

- **Premature destruction**: especially under litigation hold;
- **Inadequate metadata**: records exist but unretrievable;
- **Format obsolescence**: old electronic formats unreadable;
- **Custodian gaps**: departing staff records lost.
