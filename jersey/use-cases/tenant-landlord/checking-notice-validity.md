# Tenant Q: I've been served a notice — is it valid?

## The question

Your landlord has served you a notice telling you to leave.
How do you know whether it's valid, what your rights are,
and what to do?

## The short answer

**Don't panic and don't move out automatically.** A notice
is not by itself an eviction order. Even a valid notice
needs to be followed by a **Petty Debts Court order**
before the landlord can lawfully take possession — and a
defective notice can be challenged.

Run through this quick checklist:

| Check | Valid notice |
|---|---|
| Written and signed? | Yes |
| Delivered properly? | Yes |
| Identifies you, the property, and the date? | Yes |
| Period correct? | Yes (1 month tenant; 3 months landlord justified; 12 months landlord no-fault; 7 days breach) |
| Ground stated (where required)? | Yes |
| Date in the future, not already expired? | Yes |

If any of those is **no**, the notice is at risk of being
invalid — which can buy you significant time and may
defeat the landlord's eviction application altogether.

## The proper answer

### Notice period — is it the right length?

| Notice type | Minimum period |
|---|---|
| Tenant's notice (any tenancy, post-2025) | **1 month** |
| Landlord — justified ground (e.g. breach following 7-day cure, sale, owner-occupation, redevelopment) | **3 months** |
| Landlord — no-fault | **12 months** |
| Landlord — fixed-term expiry (non-renewal) | **3 months before end** |
| Breach notice (e.g. unpaid rent) | **7 days to cure** then court application |

If your landlord has served a **3-month notice without a
justified ground**, the notice is invalid. The landlord
must either re-serve on a justified ground (and prove it)
or convert to a 12-month no-fault notice.

### Form — is it written and signed?

The notice must be:

- **Written** (a phone call or text message isn't enough);
- **Signed** by the landlord or authorised agent;
- **Specific** about the property and parties;
- **Delivered** by the method specified in your agreement —
  typically personal delivery or recorded post. Email is
  only valid if your agreement permits it.

A notice posted through your door (no signature, no
delivery proof) is challengeable.

### Ground — is one stated where required?

For a **3-month justified notice**, the landlord must
state a specific ground:

- Breach by you (typically following the 7-day procedure);
- Owner-occupation (landlord or close family member moving
  in);
- Sale with vacant possession;
- Substantial redevelopment;
- End of employment-linked accommodation.

For a **12-month no-fault notice**, no specific ground is
required — but the **discrimination and retaliation
overlays still apply** (see below).

### Date — is it in the future, with the right period?

The termination date stated must be:

- **At least** the minimum period after the date you
  received the notice (not the date it was sent);
- **Not** retrospective;
- **Specific** to a calendar date.

### Procedural defects — what's fatal?

- Wrong period (too short);
- Not signed;
- Not delivered in an accepted way;
- Wrong tenant named;
- Wrong property;
- Past date.

The landlord will usually have to **start over** with a
fresh, valid notice — which restarts the clock.

## Specific situations

### "I've been served a 3-month notice and no ground is stated"

The notice is **defective**. A 3-month notice requires a
justified ground. The landlord should be giving 12
months' no-fault notice instead. You can:

- Write to the landlord pointing out the defect;
- Continue paying rent and remain in occupation;
- Resist any eviction application on the basis of the
  defective notice.

### "I've been served a 12-month notice immediately after I complained about repairs"

This may be **retaliatory** and challengeable. The
Discrimination Law 2013 doesn't directly cover
retaliation (unless on a protected characteristic), but
the Petty Debts Court will scrutinise the timing. If you
have a protected characteristic and the eviction relates
to it, you have a parallel discrimination claim.

Document:

- When you raised the complaint and how;
- The landlord's response;
- The timing of the notice;
- Any other tenants who haven't been served.

### "I've been served a 7-day breach notice for rent I don't owe"

If you dispute the arrears:

- Reply in writing immediately;
- Document why the amount is wrong (e.g. payment receipts,
  set-off for landlord disrepair);
- Pay any **undisputed** part in full;
- Prepare to defend at the Petty Debts Court if the
  landlord proceeds.

### "The notice was served while I was on holiday"

Service is usually effective on the date of **delivery**,
not the date of **receipt** by you personally. If the
notice was posted through the door or by recorded post in
your absence, the clock typically started then. Get
advice quickly — you may have less time than you think.

### "The notice mentions a ground but it's not true"

The landlord must be able to **prove** the ground at
court. If, for example, the landlord said "owner
occupation" but actually plans to re-let, the court can
refuse the order — and the tenant may have a damages
claim if the landlord proceeds and then re-lets to a
third party.

## What to do tomorrow morning

1. **Keep the notice** — original copy, plus envelope if
   it came by post;
2. **Note the date** of receipt and how it was delivered;
3. **Compare** the notice against the checklist above;
4. **Continue paying rent** — refusing rent triggers a
   separate breach claim and undermines your position;
5. **Engage** with Citizens Advice Jersey, JACS, or a
   Jersey advocate — fees may be modest for an initial
   review;
6. **Write to the landlord** pointing out any defects in
   the notice — sometimes this resolves the matter;
7. **Plan ahead** — even valid notices give you weeks or
   months to find alternative accommodation. Use the
   time.
