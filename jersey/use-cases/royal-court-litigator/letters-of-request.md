# Letters of Request for Evidence in Jersey

## The question

A **foreign court** has issued a **letter of request** for
evidence to be taken in Jersey from a Jersey-resident witness
or about Jersey-held documents. How is this handled?

## The short answer

Letters of request (also called **letters rogatory**):

- **Hague Evidence Convention 1970** governs (Jersey is a
  party);
- **Foreign court** issues letter of request to Jersey;
- **Channel**: Attorney General as central authority;
- **Royal Court** considers and orders evidence-taking;
- **Witness compelled** to give evidence;
- **Privilege and confidentiality** safeguards apply.

For Jersey litigators: act for the Jersey witness or for the
party initiating the request.

## The proper answer

### Hague Evidence Convention

Jersey is party to the **Hague Convention on the Taking of
Evidence Abroad in Civil or Commercial Matters 1970**:

- **Foreign court** sends letter of request through central
  authority;
- **Jersey Attorney General** is the central authority;
- **Royal Court** executes the request;
- **Standard procedural** framework.

### Chapter 5 of the Royal Court Rules

Specific Jersey procedural rules for foreign evidence-taking:

- **Application** to Royal Court by Jersey-counsel for the
  foreign party;
- **Hearing** to consider scope and execution;
- **Order** for evidence-taking;
- **Witness examination** before commissioner or master.

### Procedure

#### Initial request

- **Foreign court** issues letter of request;
- **Routed through** diplomatic / central-authority channels
  to Jersey AG;
- **AG forwards** to Royal Court.

#### Royal Court hearing

- **Counsel for foreign party** applies for execution;
- **Jersey witness** can object / make submissions;
- **Court considers**:
  - **Hague Convention compliance**;
  - **Specific** Jersey-law objections;
  - **Privilege**;
  - **Public-policy** considerations;
  - **Scope** of evidence sought.

#### Order

- **Specific witness** named;
- **Topics** of examination;
- **Documents** to be produced;
- **Place and time** of examination;
- **Officer** to take evidence (commissioner, master).

#### Examination

- **Commissioner** or **master** presides;
- **Foreign counsel** (or Jersey-counsel acting) may examine;
- **Witness** under oath;
- **Transcript** made;
- **Privilege** can be asserted at examination.

### What can be ordered

#### Witness examination

- **Jersey-resident** witness;
- **Oral evidence**;
- **Cross-examination** sometimes;
- **Transcript** for foreign court.

#### Document production

- **Specified documents**;
- **Jersey-resident** custodians;
- **Subject to** privilege.

#### Objections grounds

- **Privilege** (legal professional, journalistic, banker-
  customer in some cases);
- **Public policy**;
- **Scope** too broad;
- **Confidentiality** in trust matters (Article 9
  considerations);
- **Witness incapacity** or unavailability.

### Trust-context considerations

For Jersey trustee asked to give evidence:

- **Article 9 firewall** may limit scope;
- **Schmidt-Rosewood** principles for trust documents;
- **Trustee confidentiality** to beneficiaries;
- **Application for directions** sometimes appropriate before
  responding.

### Common scenarios

#### English Family Division evidence

- **English court** dealing with matrimonial / family matter;
- **Jersey trustee or beneficiary** has evidence;
- **Letter of request** through Hague Convention;
- **Article 9** considerations for trust evidence.

#### US discovery in Jersey

- **US court** seeking discovery;
- **Hague Convention** application;
- **Jersey witness / records**;
- **Privilege** considerations.

#### Commercial litigation

- **Foreign commercial court**;
- **Jersey-based witness** to specific transaction;
- **Standard** Hague Convention process.

#### Criminal evidence

- **Foreign criminal court**;
- **Hague Convention on Mutual Assistance in Criminal Matters**;
- **Different procedural** framework;
- **AG** central role.

### Cost

- **Application** through Jersey counsel: £10k–50k+;
- **Examination** session costs;
- **Translation** if needed;
- **Foreign party** typically bears cost.

### Strategic for opposing party

If you act for the Jersey witness / party:

- **Object** to overbroad requests;
- **Assert privilege** appropriately;
- **Article 9** for trust matters;
- **Cost protection** for unnecessary requests;
- **Witness protection**.

### Strategic for requesting party

If you're the foreign party:

- **Specific** request — broad fishing disfavoured;
- **Privilege-clean** drafting;
- **Engage Jersey counsel** familiar with process;
- **Coordinate** with foreign-court timeline.

## What to do next

For an active letter of request:

1. **Receive** through AG or directly;
2. **Engage** Jersey counsel;
3. **Apply** to Royal Court for execution;
4. **Manage** examination logistics;
5. **Coordinate** with foreign-court timeline.

## Pitfalls

- **Hague Convention non-compliance** of foreign request;
- **Privilege gaps** in execution;
- **Article 9** considerations under-played;
- **Cost** misalignment.
