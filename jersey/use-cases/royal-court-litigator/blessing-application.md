# Blessing of a Momentous Decision — Public Trustee v Cooper Category 2

## The question

The trustee has a **momentous decision** to make (large
distribution, sale of significant asset, irrevocable
restructure). The trustee wants the Royal Court to **bless** the
decision before acting. How?

## The short answer

A **Category 2 blessing application** under *Public Trustee v
Cooper*:

- **Trustee proposes** a discretionary decision within its
  powers;
- **Decision is momentous** (consequential, irrevocable,
  significant);
- **Trustee retains** decision-making authority;
- **Court blesses** if the decision is one a **reasonable
  trustee could properly make**;
- **Blessing protects** trustee from later challenge.

Procedure: **Representation** under Article 51, **affidavit**
evidence, beneficiary involvement, **hearing**, reasoned
judgment.

## The proper answer

### What is a Category 2 application

The four Public Trustee v Cooper categories:

- **Category 1**: vires — is the proposed action within
  trustee's powers?
- **Category 2**: blessing of momentous decision — trustee has
  power, but wants court comfort;
- **Category 3**: trustee deadlock — court resolves;
- **Category 4**: trustee conflict — court directs.

**Category 2** is the most common — the trustee has authority
to act but the decision's significance warrants court blessing.

### When to seek a blessing

#### Common triggers

- **Large distribution** to a beneficiary;
- **Sale** of significant asset (operating business, real
  estate);
- **Investment** of substantial fund proceeds;
- **Restructuring** of trust property;
- **Irrevocable** decisions with long-term consequences.

#### Common threshold

- **Materiality** — affects significant portion of trust value;
- **Irrevocability** — cannot easily be undone;
- **Beneficiary dispute** or risk of dispute;
- **Adviser caution** about exposure.

### The test

The Court applies the **reasonable trustee** test:

- The Court considers whether the decision is one a **reasonable
  trustee could properly make**;
- **Not** the Court's own preferred decision;
- **Trustee's discretion respected**;
- **Reasonableness** assessed in light of trust purposes,
  beneficiary interests, alternatives considered.

The Court will not bless a decision that:

- **No reasonable trustee** would make;
- **Outside trust purposes**;
- **Self-dealing** or improper;
- **Reached without proper consideration**.

### Application content

#### Representation

- **Identifies** the trustee and trust;
- **Outlines** the decision to be made;
- **Prays** for blessing.

#### Affidavit

The critical document — comprehensive:

- **Trust background** and history;
- **Beneficiary identities** and interests;
- **The decision** being proposed in detail;
- **Alternative options** considered;
- **Reasons** for the proposed decision;
- **Professional advice** received;
- **Beneficiary consultation** undertaken;
- **Risks** of the decision;
- **Why blessing is sought**.

The Court's blessing rests on the affidavit content.

#### Supporting documents

- **Trust deed**;
- **Relevant correspondence**;
- **Professional advice** (legal, tax, valuation);
- **Beneficiary statements** if any.

### Beneficiary involvement

#### Service

- **Beneficiaries** typically served;
- **Representative beneficiary** for minors / unborn /
  unascertained;
- **Court-appointed** representative often.

#### Beneficiary position

- **Support** the proposed decision (typical);
- **Neutral** (don't oppose but no positive endorsement);
- **Oppose** (the Court hears the opposition).

In **uncontested blessings**, beneficiaries support and the
hearing is brief.

In **contested blessings**, both sides present arguments.

### Hearing

#### Uncontested

- **Brief hearing** (sometimes under an hour);
- **Trustee's counsel** presents the case;
- **Beneficiary** consents read;
- **Court** considers and blesses or refuses.

#### Contested

- **Longer hearing**;
- **All parties** present arguments;
- **Sometimes** cross-examination of trustee deponent;
- **Reserved judgment** typical.

### Blessing or refusal

#### If blessing granted

- **Order** issued;
- **Trustee proceeds** with the decision;
- **Protection** against later challenge;
- **Beneficiaries bound** by the blessing.

#### If blessing refused

- **Trustee reconsiders**;
- **May modify** and reapply;
- **May proceed without blessing** but at risk;
- **May appeal**.

### Cost

#### Trustee cost

- **From trust fund** on indemnity basis;
- **Beddoe protection** if separate application made;
- **Standard** treatment.

#### Beneficiary cost

- **Helpful participation**: from trust fund;
- **Unreasonable opposition**: personal cost order possible.

### Common scenarios

#### Distribution to one beneficiary at expense of others

- **Momentous**;
- **Other beneficiaries** consulted;
- **Court** blesses if reasonable.

#### Sale of family business

- **Significant economic decision**;
- **Letters of wishes** considered;
- **Beneficiary input**;
- **Court blesses** sound commercial decision.

#### Restructure of trust assets

- **Significant operational change**;
- **Professional advice** central;
- **Court blesses** if reasonable.

#### Tax-driven restructure

- **Major tax implications**;
- **Tax advice** central;
- **Beneficiary impact** considered;
- **Court blesses** sound tax planning.

## What to do next

For an active application:

1. **Confirm** the decision is within trustee's powers
   (Category 1 if vires uncertain);
2. **Document** the decision-making process;
3. **Engage** Jersey Advocate;
4. **Draft** Representation and affidavit;
5. **File** with Royal Court;
6. **Serve** beneficiaries / representatives;
7. **Attend** hearing;
8. **Implement** the blessed decision.

## Pitfalls

- **Inadequate affidavit**: the Court can only act on what's
  presented;
- **No alternatives**: showing only one option weakens the
  application;
- **Beneficiary involvement gaps**: failing to consult / serve;
- **Premature** application — without proper consideration first.
