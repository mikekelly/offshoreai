# Private Trust Company (PTC) — When Right?

## The question

The family wants a **Private Trust Company (PTC)** as trustee of
the family trust(s), rather than a professional TCB. When is a
PTC the right vehicle?

## The short answer

A PTC works well when:

- The family wants **direct control** over trustee decisions;
- The family is **large or complex** enough to support
  professional governance internally;
- **Multiple related trusts** can share the PTC's governance
  infrastructure;
- **Continuity** beyond any specific service provider is
  needed;
- Family is willing to bear the **administrative cost** of
  running a PTC.

A PTC is **less appropriate** when:

- The family is small / simple;
- Professional independence is valued more than control;
- Cost-efficiency matters more than governance flexibility;
- The family lacks capable internal governance.

## The proper answer

### What a PTC is

A **Private Trust Company** is a Jersey company **incorporated
specifically to act as trustee** of one or more family trusts:

- **Owned indirectly** (typically by a foundation or
  non-charitable purpose trust holding the PTC shares orphan-
  style);
- **Board of directors** comprising **family members,
  professional advisers, and a regulated TCB**;
- **Operates as trustee** of the family trust(s);
- **Pays a regulated TCB** for administrative support.

### The PTC structure

A typical PTC structure:

```
[Foundation OR purpose trust] (orphan vehicle)
    |
    owns PTC shares
    |
[PTC] (Jersey company)
    |
    acts as trustee of
    |
[Family Trust(s)]
    |
    holds
    |
[Family wealth structures]
```

The **orphan vehicle** holds the PTC shares so that the family
doesn't directly own the trustee (which would create
substance / control issues).

### JFSC PTC exemption

PTCs **acting only for trusts of a single family** can typically
operate under a **JFSC exemption** rather than full TCB
registration:

- **Family-only client base** (specifically defined);
- **Reduced regulatory burden** but still some oversight;
- **No fees** from external clients;
- Family-PTC exemption is the **principal regulatory benefit**
  of the PTC model.

If the PTC **acts for unrelated families**, it becomes a full
TCB and full registration / fees apply.

### Why use a PTC

#### Direct control

The family can directly populate the PTC board:

- **Family members** as directors;
- **Trusted advisers** as directors;
- **One or two professional / TCB representatives** for
  governance.

This gives **decision-making proximity** that a third-party TCB
doesn't.

#### Multiple trust sharing

A PTC can be trustee of **multiple family trusts** under one
governance umbrella:

- **Family resilience** trust for adverse events;
- **Distribution** trust for living expenses;
- **Investment** trust for wealth accumulation;
- **Specific-purpose** trusts for individual family members.

Shared PTC governance is **more efficient** than multiple TCBs.

#### Continuity

A PTC, owned by an orphan vehicle, provides **continuity
beyond any specific service provider**:

- The PTC can change administrators while remaining the trustee;
- The orphan vehicle persists across generations;
- The family's governance arrangement is **structurally stable**.

### Why not use a PTC

#### Cost

A PTC has substantial **fixed administrative costs**:

- Jersey company filings;
- Board governance (meetings, minutes, decisions);
- Regulated-TCB support fees (still required);
- Plus the orphan vehicle's costs.

For small / simple families, **direct TCB trusteeship is
cheaper**.

#### Governance burden

A PTC requires **family governance capacity**:

- Capable family directors;
- Decision-making discipline;
- Compliance awareness;
- Regular meetings.

Families without governance capacity struggle.

#### Regulatory exposure

The PTC and its directors face **regulatory exposure** more
directly than would be the case with an independent TCB:

- Director personal liability;
- Fit-and-proper expectations of family directors;
- Compliance burden on the family.

### Typical sizing

PTCs work well for families with:

- **Multi-generational** wealth (3+ generations);
- **Multiple trusts** to be administered;
- **Operating business** wealth alongside investment portfolios;
- **Substantial** asset base (often £100m+ to justify costs);
- **Engaged family principals** willing to serve as directors.

For smaller families, **independent TCB trusteeship** is more
typical.

## What to do next

For a family considering a PTC:

1. **Assess** family governance capacity;
2. **Model** the cost (PTC + orphan vehicle + TCB support);
3. **Identify** trusted family directors and advisers;
4. **Engage** Jersey counsel for structure design;
5. **Apply** for JFSC exemption registration;
6. **Establish** the orphan vehicle + PTC;
7. **Transfer** trusts to PTC trusteeship;
8. **Operate** the governance ongoing.

## Pitfalls

- **Family member capacity**: directors must understand their
  fiduciary duties;
- **Drift to wider client base**: accepting non-family work
  ends the exemption;
- **Inadequate professional support**: PTCs still need
  regulated-TCB-grade administration;
- **Succession of family directors**: planned transitions over
  generations.
