# Removal of a Co-Trustee — Threshold

## The question

You want to **remove** a co-trustee — perhaps for non-cooperation,
incapacity, breach of duty, or simply because the trust deed
permits and it's appropriate. What's the threshold?

## The short answer

Removal routes:

- **Trust deed power**: many trusts give the **settlor, a
  protector, or other named person** a power to remove
  trustees;
- **Article 21 / 22**: the Royal Court has statutory power to
  remove trustees on application of beneficiaries / co-trustees
  / others with interest;
- **Inherent jurisdiction**: the Royal Court has inherent
  jurisdiction over trusts (long-established).

The **substantive threshold** for Court-ordered removal is the
**trust's best interests** — a serious matter requiring solid
evidence, but not requiring proof of breach.

## The proper answer

### Routes to removal

#### 1. Deed-based removal

If the trust deed gives a power of removal:

- **Identify** who holds the power (settlor / protector /
  appointor);
- **Exercise** in accordance with the deed's terms;
- **Power is fiduciary** — must be exercised in good faith and
  for the trust's benefit;
- **Document** the reasons and procedure followed.

This is the cleanest route where available.

#### 2. Article 21 — Statutory power

Article 21 of the Trusts (Jersey) Law 1984 gives the Royal
Court power to remove a trustee. Grounds include:

- **Breach of duty**;
- **Conflict of interest**;
- **Incapacity** (mental or physical);
- **Persistent failure** to perform duties;
- **General unsuitability** for the role;
- **Loss of beneficiary confidence** (in some circumstances).

The Court applies a **best-interests-of-the-trust** test —
focusing on whether the trustee's continued tenure prejudices
the trust's beneficiaries.

#### 3. Article 22 / inherent jurisdiction

Article 22 governs **discharge of trustees** more broadly; the
Court's inherent jurisdiction supplements.

### The substantive test

In **Re A Trust** and similar cases, the Royal Court has
articulated the test:

- The Court is concerned with the **welfare of the
  beneficiaries** and the **proper administration** of the trust;
- **Friction** alone is not enough;
- Need not be **proof of misconduct** — unfitness is sufficient;
- But **clear evidence** of unsuitability or breakdown is
  required.

### Common scenarios

#### Long-running co-trustee dispute

- Two trustees disagree fundamentally on strategy;
- Beneficiaries suffer from the deadlock;
- One or both may be removed.

#### Trustee misconduct / breach

- Evidence of self-dealing, missing accounts, improper
  distributions;
- Stronger ground for removal.

#### Trustee incapacity

- Health issues preventing performance;
- Routine but should be handled with sensitivity.

#### Loss of confidence

- Beneficiaries no longer trust the trustee;
- Higher bar — bare allegations don't suffice.

### Procedural steps

For Article 21 application:

1. **Application by Representation** by beneficiary or
   co-trustee;
2. **Evidence** by affidavit setting out the grounds;
3. **Service** on the trustee being removed and on
   beneficiaries;
4. **Hearing** — often contested;
5. **Order** for removal and appointment of replacement.

For deed-based removal:

1. **Identify** power-holder and verify their authority;
2. **Document** the reasons;
3. **Execute** the removal deed;
4. **Notify** the outgoing trustee, successor, and
   counterparties;
5. **Handover** records and assets.

### The outgoing trustee's position

A removed trustee:

- **Ceases to be trustee** on removal;
- **Retains** indemnity rights from trust for past acts within
  scope (subject to Court direction);
- May **face professional consequences** (JFSC, reputational);
- **Should engage Jersey counsel** if removal is contested.

## What to do next

1. **Identify** the appropriate removal route (deed-based or
   Court);
2. **Document** the grounds in writing;
3. **Engage Jersey counsel**;
4. **Consider** mediated resolution if friction is the issue;
5. **Proceed** with deed-based or Article 21 removal;
6. **Implement** handover to successor.

## Pitfalls

- **Improper exercise of deed power**: a removal in bad faith
  is voidable;
- **Removal for personal reasons**: must be for trust's
  benefit, not the removing-party's preference;
- **Inadequate evidence**: Court will not remove on thin
  grounds;
- **Replacement planning**: don't remove without a successor
  in place.
