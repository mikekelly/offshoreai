# "Can I make this distribution, or do I need Court blessing?"

## The short answer

Most trustee decisions need no court involvement at all — the trustee
just makes the decision under its express powers in the deed.
But where the decision is **momentous** for the trust, the standard
prudent course in Jersey is to apply to the **Royal Court** under
**Article 51** of the [Trusts (Jersey) Law 1984][tjl] for a
**"blessing"** of the decision before the trustee acts. The blessing
is not approval of the merits — it is the Court's confirmation that
the decision has been formed properly, so that the trustee is
protected from a later challenge by a beneficiary saying the trustee
acted irrationally or in conflict.

The Jersey Royal Court applies the **four-category framework from
[*Public Trustee v Cooper* [1999] 12 WLUK 603][ptvc]** and, within
that, the **three-limb test from *In Re S Settlement*** for category-2
("momentous decision") cases:

1. The trustee's decision was formed **in good faith**;
2. It is a decision **a reasonable trustee, properly instructed,
   could have reached**; and
3. It is **not vitiated by an actual or potential conflict of
   interest**.

If all three limbs are satisfied, the Court blesses the decision and
the trustee acts. ([Stewarts — Royal Court blesses trustee
decision][stewarts]; [Mourant — Trustee Decision-Making and the Role
of the Court][mourant])

---

## When you must / should bring an Article 51 application

There is no statutory list. The case law and practice converge on the
following triggers:

- The decision will **fundamentally change the character of the
  trust** — e.g., division of the trust into separate funds for
  different family branches; or
- The decision involves **alienation of a major / unique asset** —
  sale of the principal trust asset, sale of a family home, sale of
  the family company; or
- The decision will **substantially diminish or exhaust the trust
  fund** — a large distribution, an in-specie distribution, a
  termination distribution; or
- The decision involves **adding to or removing from the class of
  beneficiaries** in a material way; or
- The decision is **opposed by a beneficiary, protector, or
  enforcer**, and the trustee judges the opposition serious enough
  that the trustee will be sued for proceeding without Court cover;
  or
- The trustee has a **potential conflict of interest** (e.g., the
  trustee or a connected person is a beneficiary or counter-party);
  or
- The decision is **funding hostile litigation** by or against the
  trust ("Beddoe" application — see below).

Examples drawn from public commentary on Jersey decisions include
"the sale or disposal of a major trust asset, the removal of a
beneficiary, the payment of trust moneys to a spouse to fund their
divorce settlement, the refusal to submit to the jurisdiction of a
foreign court or provide information to parties to foreign divorce
proceedings between the settlor and their spouse, or the ultimate
'winding up' of a trust." ([Mourant][mourant])

If none of these triggers are present, the standard course is **do
not apply** — the trustee bears the cost of the application and the
Court will sometimes decline to bless a decision that the Court
considers the trustee should simply have taken without it.

## The four *Public Trustee v Cooper* categories

The Royal Court analyses any application to the trust supervisory
jurisdiction by reference to **which of four categories** the
application falls into. The categories were set out by Hart J in
*Public Trustee v Cooper* and are now standard:

| Category | What the trustee is asking |
|---|---|
| **1. Construction** | Whether, on its proper interpretation, the trust deed permits a proposed course of action. |
| **2. Blessing of a momentous decision** | The trustee has the requisite power and has decided how to use it, but wants the Court to bless the decision before acting because the decision is momentous. |
| **3. Surrender of discretion** | The trustees surrender their discretion to the Court because they are disabled from acting (deadlock, conflict of interest). |
| **4. Challenge to a past exercise** | Someone (typically a beneficiary) challenges a past exercise of a power as *ultra vires* or made for an improper purpose. |

([Voisin Law — Blessing momentous decisions by Trustees][voisin];
[Mourant][mourant])

For a trust officer thinking about a future distribution, the
question is almost always whether the file is a **category 2**
application. If it is, the Court applies the *S Settlement* three-limb
test above. If the trustee instead **surrenders its discretion** to
the Court (category 3), the Court itself decides — but the trustee
does not get the Court's protection-by-blessing because the trustee
hasn't taken a decision.

## What the Court is and is not doing

The Court is *not* deciding what the right answer is. In a blessing
application:

- The Court does **not** substitute its own decision for the
  trustee's.
- The Court does **not** assess whether the decision is *the best*
  decision the trustee could have made.
- The Court does **not** consider issues of merit beyond rationality.

The Court *is* confirming that the trustee has made a decision
properly — i.e., in good faith, within the range of decisions a
reasonable trustee could reach, and without a vitiating conflict.
This is enough to protect the trustee from a later claim by a
beneficiary asserting the same decision was irrational.

Recent practice in the Royal Court shows the Court taking a careful
look at family context where the trust is a discretionary family
trust: the family dynamics matter when judging whether a decision is
within the range of reasonable. ([Carey Olsen — Family context is key
in blessing applications (Re Z Trust)][co-z]; [Stewarts][stewarts])

## What to file (procedure)

The application is made by **Representation** to the Royal Court under
[Article 51][tjl], typically heard in the **Samedi Division** by the
Bailiff or Deputy Bailiff sitting alone (see
[../../legal-system/royal-court.md](../../legal-system/royal-court.md)).
The Representation will:

- be supported by a **trustee affidavit** explaining the trust,
  the proposed decision and the reasons for it;
- exhibit the **trust deed**, any letters of wishes, and the
  trustee's minutes recording the decision (or proposed decision);
- where the trustee is asking the Court to bless a litigation step,
  exhibit a **lawyer's opinion** on the merits of the proposed
  step ("Beddoe" practice — see [Dickinson Gleeson — Beddoe
  applications][dg-beddoe]);
- **convene** as Respondents the persons whose interests are
  affected — typically the principal beneficiaries; for minor and
  unborn beneficiaries the Court will appoint a **guardian ad litem**.

Costs in Article 51 proceedings are typically borne by the trust
fund on a trustee-indemnity basis, *because the proceedings are
"friendly"* — they are not breach-of-trust litigation. See
[../../trusts/article-51-directions.md](../../trusts/article-51-directions.md)
for the full procedural picture and citation chain.

## Pitfalls

- **Asking the Court to bless a decision you have not yet taken.** If
  the trustee has not actually decided, the application is in
  reality a category 3 surrender of discretion — and the Court may
  refuse to deal with it as a blessing.
- **A conflict of interest.** The Court will not bless if there is
  an unresolved conflict. Manage the conflict (independent decision,
  appointment of an additional trustee, formal recusal) before the
  application, not after.
- **Inadequate disclosure to convened beneficiaries.** A beneficiary
  who is convened is entitled to information sufficient to make
  meaningful submissions; suppressing material weakens the
  protection a blessing confers.
- **Forum-shopping risk.** A foreign court may not respect a Royal
  Court blessing where the proposed decision has cross-border
  effects — see [Crociani](crociani-question.md) on Jersey
  jurisdiction clauses *(stub)*. Take advice on whether parallel
  protection is needed.
- **Using Article 51 for hostile litigation.** Article 51 is not the
  proper vehicle to prosecute or defend a breach-of-trust claim
  against the trustee. ([Baker & Partners — Jersey Trust
  Litigation][bp]; [Dickinson Gleeson][dg-beddoe])
