# Service of Foreign Proceedings in Jersey

## The question

You need to **serve foreign court proceedings** on a Jersey-
resident person or company. How?

## The short answer

Service in Jersey of **foreign proceedings**:

- **Civil/commercial**: under **Hague Service Convention 1965**
  (Jersey is a party);
- **Through Jersey Attorney-General** as central authority;
- **Direct service** sometimes permitted (Article 10 Hague —
  but Jersey's reservations apply);
- **Process servers** in Jersey for direct service;
- **Address**: the registered office of a Jersey company; or
  the residential address of a natural person.

**Jersey Advocate involvement** helpful for compliant service.

## The proper answer

### Hague Service Convention

Jersey is a party to the **Hague Convention on the Service
Abroad of Judicial and Extrajudicial Documents in Civil or
Commercial Matters 1965**.

#### Central authority

- **Jersey Attorney-General** is the central authority;
- Foreign-court requests routed through;
- **Process** of service by appropriate Jersey method;
- **Certificate** of service returned to foreign court.

#### Process

1. **Foreign court** issues service request to Jersey AG;
2. **AG arranges** service through bailiff / process server;
3. **Service** effected on Jersey-resident defendant;
4. **Certificate** of service issued and returned.

Standard but **takes time** (often months).

#### Article 10 (direct service)

- Hague Convention Article 10 allows direct service in
  specified ways;
- **Jersey's reservations** affect available methods;
- **Direct postal service** generally not permitted to / from
  Jersey;
- **Through judicial officers** sometimes permitted.

### Non-Hague countries

For service from countries not party to Hague Service Convention:

- **Letters rogatory** through diplomatic channels;
- **Slower** and more procedural;
- **Compliance** with foreign-jurisdiction service rules.

### Service on Jersey companies

For service on a Jersey-registered company:

- **Registered office** is the address for service (Companies
  Law Article 72);
- **Direct service** at registered office typically valid;
- **Foreign-court** typically can effect through Hague Service
  Convention.

### Service on individuals

- **Residential address** is the typical service point;
- **Personal service** required for some proceedings;
- **Process server** in Jersey can effect.

### Recognition of foreign service

For foreign service to **operate in Jersey courts** subsequently:

- **Service** must have been **proper** under foreign rules;
- **Hague Convention compliance** preferred;
- **Direct service** may face challenge if not compliant.

### Foreign judgment service

Service of foreign judgments for recognition in Jersey:

- **Apply** to Royal Court for recognition / enforcement;
- **Judgment** must be from a recognised court;
- **Comity principles** typically applied;
- **Article 9** (firewall) may limit recognition for trust-
  related judgments.

### Anti-suit injunctions

If foreign proceedings should be in Jersey:

- **Apply** to Royal Court for anti-suit injunction;
- **Jersey jurisdiction clause** in underlying documents helps;
- **Crociani** principles support enforcement of Jersey
  jurisdiction clauses;
- **Court considers** appropriateness of order.

### Practical considerations

- **Use Hague Service Convention** where available — cleaner;
- **Engage Jersey counsel** for compliance;
- **Allow time** — months for Hague service;
- **Verify service** before relying on foreign-court orders.

## What to do next

For active service:

1. **Identify** appropriate service method (Hague / direct);
2. **Engage** Jersey counsel for guidance;
3. **Issue** through correct channel;
4. **Obtain** certificate of service;
5. **Use** in foreign proceedings.

## Pitfalls

- **Non-Hague-compliant service**: may be invalid;
- **Timeline underestimate**: Hague service is slow;
- **Service-of-foreign-judgment** different from service of
  proceedings — separate process;
- **Article 9 considerations** for trust-related matters.
