# Q: I was caught speeding 50 in a 30 zone. What happens?

## The question

You've been stopped (or caught by a speed-measurement
device) doing 50 mph in a 30 mph zone in Jersey. What's
the process, what's the penalty, and how should you
handle it?

## The short answer

You're **20 mph over** the limit — that's a substantial
excess. The procedural and substantive position:

| Aspect | Position |
|---|---|
| Forum | **Magistrate's Court** (not Parish Hall — 15 mph+ over the limit is automatically out of Parish Hall scope) |
| Likely fine | **£300-£400** range under the Sentencing Guidelines |
| Endorsement | Almost certain |
| Disqualification | **Likely** for 20 mph excess; the Magistrate has discretion |
| Process | Charge / summons → first appearance → plea → (trial if not guilty) → sentence |
| Right to representation | Yes — engage a Jersey advocate for a serious case like this |
| Timeline | Several weeks from summons to disposition |

## The proper answer

### Forum: Magistrate's Court, not Parish Hall

Because the excess speed is **15 mph or more over the
limit**, your case is automatically **excluded from
Parish Hall Enquiry** disposition. The Centenier of the
parish where the offence occurred will not deal with this
at the Parish Hall — instead, the matter is referred
directly to the **Magistrate's Court** by summons.

This means:

- **No £200 ceiling** on the fine;
- **Formal court proceedings**, with all the usual
  procedural rights;
- **Sentencing exposure** up to the full statutory
  maximum;
- **Disqualification** is on the table.

### Likely fine

The **Magistrate's Court Sentencing Guidelines** map
excess speed against zone limit to suggest fine bands.
For **20 mph over** the limit in a 30 mph zone:

- Indicative fine band: **£300-£400**;
- May go higher with aggravating factors (weather,
  pedestrians, time of day, accident risk);
- May go lower with strong mitigation (clean record,
  prompt admission, medical context).

### Endorsement and disqualification

For 20 mph excess:

- **Endorsement** of the licence is essentially
  automatic;
- **Disqualification** is a real prospect — the Sentencing
  Guidelines say "endorse / consider disqualification" or
  "disqualify" at this level.

Whether you are disqualified depends on:

- **Your driving record** — prior endorsements weigh
  heavily;
- **Aggravating circumstances** of the offence;
- **Mitigation** — clean record, prompt cooperation,
  employment / family consequences.

A first-time offender with a clean record may avoid
disqualification with strong mitigation. A repeat
offender will almost certainly be disqualified.

### Evidential corroboration

Note that under Jersey law, a speeding conviction **cannot
rest on a single witness's opinion alone**. The
prosecution needs:

- A **calibrated speed-measurement device** reading
  (radar, laser, average-speed camera); or
- **Two witness corroboration**; or
- Your **admission**.

If the only evidence is one officer's visual estimate,
that's not sufficient. Ask whether the measurement device
was used and request its calibration certificate.

### Procedure

1. **Summons** is served on you — sets a court date;
2. **First appearance** — short, you confirm identity
   and confirm whether you have legal representation;
3. **Plea entered** — guilty or not guilty;
4. If **guilty**: sentencing may be the same day or
   adjourned;
5. If **not guilty**: trial is set, evidence presented,
   verdict;
6. **Sentence** if convicted.

### Mitigation — what helps

- **Admit promptly** if guilty (saves court time, shows
  remorse, attracts the standard reduction);
- **Clean licence** history;
- **Character references** from employer / community;
- **Consequences** evidence — employment dependent on
  licence, family caring responsibilities;
- **Medical or emergency** context (rare, but real);
- **Specialist advocate** to present mitigation
  professionally.

### Defences — what doesn't help

- "I was only just over the limit" — 50 in a 30 is 20
  over;
- "Everyone else was speeding" — not a defence;
- "I didn't see the sign" — only narrowly available if
  signage was genuinely deficient;
- "I had to get to X" — necessity defence is very narrow.

## What to do tomorrow morning

1. **Don't ignore** the summons or any correspondence —
   non-attendance escalates.
2. **Collect documents** — vehicle and licence details,
   driving history, insurance.
3. **Get specialist advice** — Jersey has a small number
   of motoring-law specialist advocates. Fees for
   representation are usually less than the cost of a
   disqualification's secondary impact.
4. **Request** the prosecution evidence — speed reading,
   device calibration, officer statements.
5. **Consider** whether to enter a guilty plea early
   (typically attracts a 1/3 reduction) or contest.
6. **Prepare mitigation** — references, employment
   evidence, character statements.
7. **Plan financially** — fine, possible costs, possible
   insurance impact.
8. **Plan logistically** — if disqualified, how will you
   get to work, manage family commitments?

## Common mistakes

- **Self-representing** in a 20-mph-excess case — false
  economy;
- **Failing to engage** with the procedure — escalates;
- **Trying to talk your way out** at the roadside —
  rarely works, can produce statements used against you;
- **Underestimating** the disqualification risk;
- **Ignoring** the impact on insurance — premium hike
  often exceeds the fine over multi-year period.
