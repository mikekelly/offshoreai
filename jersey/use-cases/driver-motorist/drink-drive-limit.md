# Q: What's the drink-drive limit in Jersey?

## The short answer

Jersey's drink-drive limits are:

- **80 mg of alcohol per 100 ml of blood** (BAC 0.08%);
- **35 micrograms of alcohol per 100 ml of breath**;
- **107 mg of alcohol per 100 ml of urine**.

These are the **historic UK limits**, still applicable in
England, Wales and Northern Ireland. They are **higher
than Scotland's** (50 mg / 100 ml blood, which equates to
22 micrograms / 100 ml breath). Drivers used to Scottish
or many European limits should not assume Jersey's lower
limit applies.

## The proper answer

### How testing works

If a Jersey police officer (States or Honorary) suspects
you've been drinking and driving, the procedure is:

1. **Stop** and assessment;
2. **Roadside screening breath test** — a screening
   breathalyser at the roadside;
3. **Arrest** if the screening test fails or you refuse;
4. **Evidential breath test** at the police station — a
   more accurate device; OR
5. **Blood or urine sample** — typically where breath
   testing is unsuitable;
6. **Charge** if you're over the limit.

The screening test isn't evidence by itself — it's there
to justify arrest. The **evidential** test is what
supports prosecution.

### Refusing a test

Refusing to provide a specimen **without reasonable
excuse** is a separate offence carrying penalties as
serious as drink-driving itself — mandatory disqualification,
fine, possible imprisonment. "Reasonable excuse" is very
narrow — medical inability is essentially the only
realistic ground.

### Conversion between blood, breath, urine

Roughly:

- 80 mg / 100 ml blood ≈ 35 μg / 100 ml breath ≈
  107 mg / 100 ml urine.

The breath limit is most commonly used because of breath
testing's practicality.

### What this means for a driver

Different drinks affect different people differently
depending on weight, sex, food consumption, time, and
metabolism. As a very rough indication:

- A medium-sized man might reach the limit after
  approximately 4 units (about 2 standard pints of normal-
  strength beer or 2 large glasses of wine);
- A medium-sized woman might reach the limit after
  approximately 3 units;
- These figures are **indicative only** and easily
  exceeded in real conditions.

The only safe approach is **don't drink and drive at all**.

### Morning-after

Alcohol clears at roughly **1 unit per hour**. A heavy
evening can leave a driver still over the limit well into
the next morning. Common cases involve people who feel
"fine" the next morning but are still over the limit.

### Penalties

For a first offence:

- **Mandatory disqualification**: minimum 12 months
  (typically more for higher readings);
- **Fine**: up to £2,000;
- **Imprisonment**: up to 6 months for serious cases;
- **Endorsement**;
- **Re-test** before licence restored in some cases;
- **Insurance impact**: severe;
- **Travel impact**: some countries restrict entry.

For repeats, penalties escalate sharply — multi-year
disqualifications, higher fines, custodial sentences more
likely.

### Forum

Drink-driving **cannot** be dealt with at Parish Hall
Enquiry. It always goes to **Magistrate's Court** (or
Royal Court for the most serious cases including those
involving death or serious injury).

### Defences

Substantive defences (specialist advice essential):

- **Identity** — were you the driver?
- **Procedural defect** — breath / blood test procedure
  not properly followed (calibration, timing, two-specimen
  rule, statutory protections);
- **Hip flask** — alcohol consumed *after* driving but
  before the test (narrow; requires medical evidence);
- **Public place** — Jersey-specific narrow defence in
  some cases.

### Drug-driving

The drink-driving regime is **separate** from drug-driving
— see [`../../road-traffic/drug-driving.md`](../../road-traffic/drug-driving.md).
Currently Jersey relies on **impairment evidence** for
drug-driving (not statutory limits). A reform proposal in
February 2026 would introduce statutory limits and
roadside drug testing, but is not yet enacted.

## What to do

If you're considering driving after drinking:

- **Don't.** Even modest amounts can put you near or over
  the limit.
- **Plan transport** — taxi, designated driver, public
  transport.
- **Stay overnight** — sleep it off rather than drive
  back.
- **Morning after** — be cautious; alcohol clears slowly.

If stopped after drinking:

- **Cooperate** with the test procedure;
- **Don't refuse** — refusal is its own serious offence;
- **Don't make admissions** to the officer beyond what's
  required by the procedure;
- **Request legal advice** as soon as practicable;
- **Note** the procedural details (timing, machine ID,
  officer name).

If charged:

- **Engage specialist legal advice immediately** —
  Jersey advocates who specialise in motoring offences
  can spot procedural defences invisible to a generalist;
- **Don't plead** until you've taken advice;
- **Prepare for substantial consequences** including
  disqualification.
