# States Assembly — Jersey's Legislature

The **States Assembly** (formerly "the States of Jersey") is Jersey's
unicameral parliament. It is the legislative body of a self-governing
[Crown Dependency](crown-dependency.md) and is responsible for primary
legislation, the Annual Government Plan (which sets taxation and
spending), and the holding to account of the executive
[Council of Ministers](council-of-ministers.md).

## Composition — current term (elected at the 2022 general election)

For the 2022 general election Jersey moved to a new electoral system
under which the Assembly comprises **49 elected members**, made up of:

- **37 Deputies** elected from **nine new electoral constituencies**
  across the Island; and
- **12 Connétables** — one elected from each of the [twelve civil
  parishes](parishes.md), serving ex officio as members of the Assembly.

([statesassembly.je — How members are elected][how-elected];
[londonoffice.gov.je][london])

The role of **Senator** — a States Member elected on an island-wide
mandate — was abolished for the 2022 election as part of the reform.

### Senators returning at the 2026 election

Following a subsequent decision of the Assembly, **the role of Senator
returns at the 2026 elections**. From 2026, Islanders will be able to
vote for:

- the **Connétable** of their Parish;
- between **two and four Deputies** representing their local constituency;
  and
- a **Senator** representing the whole Island.

(Confirmed at: [statesassembly.je][how-elected]; and the same site's
"about" pages.) The exact final number of Senators and the recalibrated
constituency map for 2026 should be checked against the current
electoral law before relying on this file for advice about candidacy or
voting.

## Non-elected members

In addition to the elected members, the constitution of the States
Assembly includes the following non-elected members or attendees, who
have specific functions in the chamber but (in the modern practice)
limited or no voting rights on most matters:

- The **Bailiff** (or in the Bailiff's absence, the **Deputy Bailiff**),
  who **presides** over the Assembly. See [bailiff.md](bailiff.md).
- The **Lieutenant-Governor** of Jersey, the King's representative —
  attends but does not vote. See
  [lieutenant-governor.md](lieutenant-governor.md).
- The **Dean of Jersey** — the senior clergyman of the Established
  Church on the Island.
- The **Attorney General** and the **Solicitor General** — the Crown
  Officers responsible for legal advice to the Crown and the States.

([statesassembly.je — About the Assembly][about])

## Elections

- The Assembly's elected members serve **four-year terms**.
- Elections take place island-wide on a fixed cycle.
- Most members sit as **independents**; political parties play a smaller
  role in Jersey politics than in the UK. As at 2026 only two parties
  hold seats — **Reform Jersey** and the **Jersey Liberal Conservatives**.
  ([londonoffice.gov.je][london])

For the detailed franchise, residency requirement to vote, and the
official voter-registration system, see the gov.je elections pages and
[vote.je](https://www.vote.je/the-states-assembly/).

## What the Assembly does

The States Assembly:

1. **Adopts primary legislation.** Bills are introduced as **Projets de
   Loi**. Once adopted, a Projet is forwarded for **Royal Sanction**.
   On the grant of Royal Sanction by **Order in Council** of the Privy
   Council, and registration by the Royal Court (the customary act of
   *enregistrement*), the Projet becomes a **Law of Jersey** with its
   stated commencement date. This Privy Council route is the formal
   acknowledgment that Jersey legislates under the Crown rather than
   under the UK Parliament — see [crown-dependency.md](crown-dependency.md).
2. **Adopts the Annual Government Plan**, which sets income-tax,
   GST and other revenue-raising measures and authorises departmental
   spending for the year. See [tax/index.md](../tax/index.md).
3. **Adopts subordinate legislation** that is required by primary Law
   to be brought to the Assembly (typically by way of *Regulations* —
   contrast with *Orders*, which are made by the relevant Minister
   under a delegated power and laid before the Assembly).
4. **Holds the executive to account** through Ministerial questions,
   debate, and scrutiny panels.

## Procedural authority

The Assembly's procedural framework is set out in:

- The **Standing Orders of the States of Jersey** (published by the
  States Greffe and amended from time to time by the Assembly itself),
  setting out how debates are conducted, how Bills are progressed and
  how votes are taken. ([statesassembly.je — Rules][rules])
- The **States of Jersey Law 2005**, the constitutional statute that
  recreated the Assembly under its modern name and established the
  ministerial system of government.

## How this fits together

- The Assembly **legislates**: see this page and
  [crown-dependency.md](crown-dependency.md) for the constitutional
  context.
- The Assembly's **Ministers** form the executive: see
  [council-of-ministers.md](council-of-ministers.md).
- The Assembly's Laws are interpreted and applied by the
  [Royal Court](../legal-system/royal-court.md), and ultimately by the
  [Court of Appeal](../legal-system/court-of-appeal.md) and the
  Judicial Committee of the Privy Council.
- The Assembly is **distinct from** the parish assemblies, which deal
  with parish-level matters under the
  [Connétables](parishes.md).
